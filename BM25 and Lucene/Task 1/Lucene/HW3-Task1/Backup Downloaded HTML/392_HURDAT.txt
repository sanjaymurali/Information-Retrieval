<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">HURDAT</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">
					Jump to:					<a href="#mw-head">navigation</a>, 					<a href="#p-search">search</a>
</div>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><p>The <a href="/wiki/National_Hurricane_Center" title="National Hurricane Center">National Hurricane Centers</a> HURricane DATabases (HURDAT) are two separate <a href="/wiki/Database" title="Database">databases</a> that contain details on <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone" title="Tropical cyclone">tropical cyclones</a>, that have occurred within the <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Ocean" title="Atlantic Ocean">Atlantic Ocean</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone_basins" title="Tropical cyclone basins">Eastern Pacific Ocean</a> since either 1851 or 1949.</p>
<p>The Eastern Pacific database was originally compiled at the NHC during 1976, to help with the initialization with two <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone_forecast_model" title="Tropical cyclone forecast model">tropical cyclone forecast models</a>. Initially tracks for the Central Pacific region and tracks for tropical depressions, that did not develop into tropical storms or hurricanes were not included within the database. Over the next few years tracks were archived best track data from the Eastern Pacific Hurricane Center (EPHC) were archived by the NHC on an annual basis. During 1982 the NHC started to include data on Central Pacific tropical storms and hurricanes within the database, before they took over the responsibility for issuing advisories during 1988. The format of the Eastern Pacific database was subsequently significantly changed during 2013 to include non-synoptic best track times and non-developing tropical depressions.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Atlantic_hurricane_database">The Atlantic hurricane database</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=HURDAT&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: The Atlantic hurricane database">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>During 1959, a technical paper was published by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_States_Weather_Bureau" title="United States Weather Bureau">United States Weather Bureau</a>, which consolidated several sources of records in to a single publication.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TP36_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-TP36-1">[1]</a></sup> These sources included annual summaries that had been published in the Monthly Weather Review at various times since 1922, unpublished materials from the Hurricane forecast offices and other studies on hurricanes and hurricane climatology back to around 1886.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TP36_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-TP36-1">[1]</a></sup> While combining the sources, position errors of over 100 miles (160 km) were found for several hurricanes shown in more than one source.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TP36_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-TP36-1">[1]</a></sup> Therefore, the positions of all of the systems that were considered to have tropical characteristics, were compared with the historical weather maps of the daily synoptic series.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TP36_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-TP36-1">[1]</a></sup> The most reliable positions and intensities were then plotted on a series of annual track charts, before being reviewed by the hurricane forecast centres, Extended Forecast Section and the <a href="/wiki/National_Hurricane_Research_Project" title="National Hurricane Research Project">National Hurricane Research Project</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TP36_1-4"><a href="#cite_note-TP36-1">[1]</a></sup> The most accurate and consistent locations from the reviews were then plotted on the maps and published.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TP36_1-5"><a href="#cite_note-TP36-1">[1]</a></sup> This dataset was subsequently updated during 1965, which extended the dataset back to 1871 and forwards to 1963 based on additional material.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TP55_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-TP55-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>At around this time, <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">NASA</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Apollo_program" title="Apollo program">Apollo space programme</a> requested data, on the climatological impacts of tropical cyclones on launches of space vehicles at the <a href="/wiki/Kennedy_Space_Center" title="Kennedy Space Center">Kennedy Space Center</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Prob_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Prob-3">[3]</a></sup> The basic data was taken by the authors from the National Weather Records North Atlantic Tropical Cyclone deck number 988, which was updated and corrected to include data from 1886–1968.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Prob_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-Prob-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1886-1977_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-1886-1977-4">[4]</a></sup> As a result of this work, a requirement for a computerized tropical cyclone database at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/National_Hurricane_Centre" title="National Hurricane Centre">National Hurricane Centre</a> <small>(NHC)</small> was realised, which led to the prediction of tropical cyclone motion out through 72 hours.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1886-1977_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-1886-1977-4">[4]</a></sup> Over the next few years, HURDAT was extensively revised, by both the NHC and the National Climatic Center, before it was published at irregular intervals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Over the next couple of decades, it became obvious that the database needed to be revised because it was incomplete, contained significant errors, or did not reflect the latest scientific understanding regarding the interpretation of past data.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Charles J Neumann subsequently documented several of these problems and obtained a grant, to start addressing them in a programme that was eventually called the Atlantic Hurricane Database Re-analysis Project.</p>
<p>During the 1990s Jose Fernandez-Partagas led efforts to document previously undocumented tropical cyclones from the mid 1800s until the early 1900s, by using lists of previous hurricanes, books and newspapers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> After his death in 1997, the results of his efforts were built upon by the Atlantic reanalysis team, before being checked by NHC's Best Track Committee and added in to the Atlantic HURDAT in 2001 and 2003.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p><br/>
In 2013, the archives format was significantly changed to include non-synoptic best track times, non-developing tropical depressions and wind radii.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HURDAT2ATL_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-HURDAT2ATL-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Eastern_and_Central_Pacific_hurricane_database">The Eastern and Central Pacific hurricane database</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=HURDAT&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: The Eastern and Central Pacific hurricane database">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Eastern Pacific best track database was initially compiled on magnetic tape in 1976 for the seasons between 1949 and 1975, at the NHC to help with the development of two <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone_forecast_model" title="Tropical cyclone forecast model">tropical cyclone forecast models</a>, which required tracks of past cyclones as a base for its predictions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Stat_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-Stat-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Comp_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-Comp-14">[14]</a></sup> The database was based on records held by the United States Navy and were interpolated from 12 hourly intervals to 6 hourly intervals based on a scheme devised by Hiroshi Akima in 1970.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EPAC_Book_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-EPAC_Book-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Intra_Seasonal_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-Intra_Seasonal-16">[16]</a></sup> Initially tracks for the Central Pacific region and tracks for tropical depressions that did not develop into tropical storms or hurricanes were not included within the database.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Intra_Seasonal_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-Intra_Seasonal-16">[16]</a></sup> After the database had been created Arthur Pike of the NHC made some internal adjustments, while in 1980 a review was made by Arnold Court under contract from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_States_National_Weather_Service" title="United States National Weather Service">United States National Weather Service</a> and resulted in additions and/or modifications to 81 tracks in the database.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EPAC_Book_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-EPAC_Book-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1949-1983_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-1949-1983-17">[17]</a></sup> Between 1976 - 1987, the NHC archived best track data from the <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Pacific_Hurricane_Center" title="Eastern Pacific Hurricane Center">Eastern Pacific Hurricane Center</a> <small>(EPHC)</small>, and in 1982 started including information on Central Pacific tropical storms and hurricanes started to be included in the database based on data from the <a href="/wiki/Joint_Typhoon_Warning_Center" title="Joint Typhoon Warning Center">Joint Typhoon Warning Center</a> and research done by Samuel Shaw of the <a href="/wiki/Central_Pacific_Hurricane_Center" title="Central Pacific Hurricane Center">Central Pacific Hurricane Center</a> <small>(CPHC)</small> in 1981.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Stat_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-Stat-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EPAC_Book_15-2"><a href="#cite_note-EPAC_Book-15">[15]</a></sup> The format of the database was completely revised by the NHC during 1984, so that the format could resemble the Atlantic database before they took over the warning responsibility from the EPHC for the Eastern Pacific during 1988.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EPAC_Book_15-3"><a href="#cite_note-EPAC_Book-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1949-1983_17-1"><a href="#cite_note-1949-1983-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> During 2008 and 2013 several revisions were made to the database to extend tracks in land, based on reports in the <a href="/wiki/Mariners_Weather_Log" title="Mariners Weather Log">Mariners Weather Log</a> and extrapolation of the tracks since the EPHC stopped issuing advisories on systems before they made landfall.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EPAC_Metadata_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-EPAC_Metadata-19">[19]</a></sup> The archives format was significantly changed during 2013 to include non-synoptic best track times, non-developing tropical depressions and wind radii.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HURDAT2_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-HURDAT2-20">[20]</a></sup> During February 2016, the NHC released the <a href="/wiki/1959_Mexico_hurricane" title="1959 Mexico hurricane">1959 Mexico hurricane</a>'s reanalysis, which was the first system to be reassessed, using methods developed for the Atlantic reanalysis process.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reanalysis_projects">Reanalysis projects</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=HURDAT&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Reanalysis projects">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>After the HURDAT databases were created; it became obvious over the next couple of decades that HURDAT needed to be revised because it was incomplete, contained significant errors, or did not reflect the latest scientific understanding regarding the interpretation of past data.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2013, the archives format was significantly changed to include non-synoptic best track times, non-developing tropical depressions and wind radii.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HURDAT2ATL_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-HURDAT2ATL-12">[12]</a></sup> During March 2014, the Atlantic HURDAT was updated with the results of the reanalysis for the seasons between 1946 and 1950, with nine tropical storms added to the database.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-metadata_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-metadata-23">[23]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> Hurricane Camille's reanalysis was expedited and published during April 2014, after the National Hurricane Center management realised a need to answer the question: "Which is the strongest hurricane to have struck the United States?"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Camille_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-Camille-25">[25]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> During 2015 and 2016, HURDAT was revised with the results of the reanalysis for the seasons between 1951 and 1955, with 12 new tropical storms added to the database.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-metadata_23-1"><a href="#cite_note-metadata-23">[23]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2015_RA_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-2015_RA-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
