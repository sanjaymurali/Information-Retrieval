<p>The <b>Potomac River</b> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="'p' in 'pie'">p</span><span title="/ə/: 'a' in 'about'">ə</span><span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'t' in 'tie'">t</span><span title="/oʊ/: 'o' in 'code'">oʊ</span><span title="'m' in 'my'">m</span><span title="/ə/: 'a' in 'about'">ə</span><span title="'k' in 'kind'">k</span></span>/</a></span><small class="nowrap metadata"> (<span class="unicode haudio"><span class="fn"><span style="white-space:nowrap"><a href="/wiki/File:GT_Potomac.ogg" title="About this sound"><img alt="About this sound" data-file-height="20" data-file-width="20" height="11" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/11px-Loudspeaker.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/17px-Loudspeaker.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/22px-Loudspeaker.svg.png 2x" width="11"/></a> </span><a class="internal" href="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/a/a2/GT_Potomac.ogg" title="GT Potomac.ogg">listen</a></span></span>)</small></span>) is located along the mid-<a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Ocean" title="Atlantic Ocean">Atlantic Ocean</a> coast of the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> and flows into the <a href="/wiki/Chesapeake_Bay" title="Chesapeake Bay">Chesapeake Bay</a>. The <a href="/wiki/River" title="River">river</a> (main stem and North Branch) is approximately 405 miles (652 km) long,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NHD_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-NHD-1">[1]</a></sup> with a <a href="/wiki/Drainage_basin" title="Drainage basin">drainage area</a> of about 14,700 square miles (38,000 km<sup>2</sup>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-basin-facts_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-basin-facts-2">[2]</a></sup> In terms of area, this makes the Potomac River the fourth largest river along the Atlantic coast of the United States and the 21st largest in the United States. Over 5 million people live within the Potomac <a href="/wiki/Drainage_basin" title="Drainage basin">watershed</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geography">Geography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Potomac_River&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Geography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The river forms part of the borders between <a href="/wiki/Maryland" title="Maryland">Maryland</a> and <a href="/wiki/Washington,_D.C." title="Washington, D.C.">Washington, D.C.</a>, on the left descending bank and <a href="/wiki/West_Virginia" title="West Virginia">West Virginia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Virginia" title="Virginia">Virginia</a> on the river's right descending bank. The majority of the lower Potomac River is part of the State of Maryland. Exceptions include a small tidal portion within the District of Columbia, and the border with Virginia being delineated from "point to point" (thus various bays and shoreline indentations lie in Virginia). Except for a small portion of its headwaters in West Virginia, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/North_Branch_Potomac_River" title="North Branch Potomac River">North Branch Potomac River</a> is considered part of Maryland to the low water mark on the opposite bank. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/South_Branch_Potomac_River" title="South Branch Potomac River">South Branch Potomac River</a> lies completely within the state of West Virginia except for its <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Headwaters" title="Headwaters">headwaters</a>, which lie in Virginia.</p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Potomac_River_in_District_of_Columbia_IMG_4720.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3240" data-file-width="4320" height="188" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7b/Potomac_River_in_District_of_Columbia_IMG_4720.JPG/250px-Potomac_River_in_District_of_Columbia_IMG_4720.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7b/Potomac_River_in_District_of_Columbia_IMG_4720.JPG/375px-Potomac_River_in_District_of_Columbia_IMG_4720.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7b/Potomac_River_in_District_of_Columbia_IMG_4720.JPG/500px-Potomac_River_in_District_of_Columbia_IMG_4720.JPG 2x" width="250"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Potomac_River_in_District_of_Columbia_IMG_4720.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The Potomac River in <a href="/wiki/Washington,_D.C." title="Washington, D.C.">Washington, D.C.</a>, with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rosslyn,_Arlington,_Virginia" title="Rosslyn, Arlington, Virginia">Rosslyn, Arlington, Virginia</a> in the background</div>

<p>The Potomac River runs 405 miles (652 km) from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fairfax_Stone" title="Fairfax Stone">Fairfax Stone</a> in West Virginia on the <a href="/wiki/Allegheny_Plateau" title="Allegheny Plateau">Allegheny Plateau</a> to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Point_Lookout,_Maryland" title="Point Lookout, Maryland">Point Lookout</a>, Maryland, and drains 14,679 square miles (38,020 km<sup>2</sup>). The length of the river from the junction of its North and South Branches to Point Lookout is 302 miles (486 km).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NHD_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-NHD-1">[1]</a></sup> The average flow is 10,800 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cubic_feet_per_second" title="Cubic feet per second">ft³/s</a> (306 m³/s). The largest flow ever recorded on the Potomac at Washington, D.C., was in March 1936 when it reached 425,000 ft³/s (12,000 m³/s). The lowest flow ever recorded at the same location was 600 ft³/s (17 m³/s) in September, 1966.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-basin-facts_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-basin-facts-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>The river has two sources. The source of the North Branch is at the Fairfax Stone located at the junction of <a href="/wiki/Grant_County,_West_Virginia" title="Grant County, West Virginia">Grant</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tucker_County,_West_Virginia" title="Tucker County, West Virginia">Tucker</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Preston_County,_West_Virginia" title="Preston County, West Virginia">Preston</a> counties in <a href="/wiki/West_Virginia" title="West Virginia">West Virginia</a>. The source of the South Branch is located near <a href="/wiki/Hightown,_Virginia" title="Hightown, Virginia">Hightown</a> in northern <a href="/wiki/Highland_County,_Virginia" title="Highland County, Virginia">Highland County</a>, Virginia. The river's two branches converge just east of <a href="/wiki/Green_Spring,_West_Virginia" title="Green Spring, West Virginia">Green Spring</a> in <a href="/wiki/Hampshire_County,_West_Virginia" title="Hampshire County, West Virginia">Hampshire County</a>, West Virginia, to form the Potomac.</p>
<p>Once the Potomac drops from the <a href="/wiki/Piedmont_(United_States)" title="Piedmont (United States)">Piedmont</a> to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coastal_Plain" title="Coastal Plain">Coastal Plain</a> at <a href="/wiki/Little_Falls_(Potomac_River)" title="Little Falls (Potomac River)">Little Falls</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tides" title="Tides">tides</a> further influence the river as it passes through Washington, D.C. and beyond. <a href="/wiki/Salinity" title="Salinity">Salinity</a> in the Potomac River <a href="/wiki/Estuary" title="Estuary">Estuary</a> increases thereafter with distance downstream. The estuary also widens, reaching 11 statute miles (17 km) wide at its mouth, between <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Point_Lookout,_Maryland" title="Point Lookout, Maryland">Point Lookout</a>, Maryland, and Smith Point, Virginia, before flowing into the Chesapeake Bay.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Potomac_River&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:View_of_Potomac_River_from_Mount_Vernon.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1285" data-file-width="2958" height="87" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6a/View_of_Potomac_River_from_Mount_Vernon.jpg/200px-View_of_Potomac_River_from_Mount_Vernon.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6a/View_of_Potomac_River_from_Mount_Vernon.jpg/300px-View_of_Potomac_River_from_Mount_Vernon.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6a/View_of_Potomac_River_from_Mount_Vernon.jpg/400px-View_of_Potomac_River_from_Mount_Vernon.jpg 2x" width="200"/></a>

<p>"Potomac" is a European spelling of <i>Patowmeck</i>, the <a href="/wiki/Algonquian_languages" title="Algonquian languages">Algonquian</a> name of a Native American village, perhaps meaning "something brought".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bright2004_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bright2004-3">[3]</a></sup> Native Americans had different names for different parts of the river, calling the river above <a href="/wiki/Great_Falls_(Potomac_River)" title="Great Falls (Potomac River)">Great Falls</a> <i>Cohongarooton</i>, meaning "honking geese"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Achenbach2004_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Achenbach2004-5">[5]</a></sup> and "Patawomke" below the fall, meaning "river of swans".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> The spelling of the name has taken many forms over the years from "Patawomeke" (as on <a href="/wiki/John_Smith_(explorer)" title="John Smith (explorer)">Captain John Smith</a>'s map) to "<a href="/wiki/Patawomeck" title="Patawomeck">Patawomeck</a>", "Patowmack", and numerous other spellings in the 18th century and now "Potomac".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Achenbach2004_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-Achenbach2004-5">[5]</a></sup> The river's name was officially decided upon as Potomac by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Board_on_Geographic_Names" title="Board on Geographic Names">Board on Geographic Names</a> in 1931.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>The river itself is at least two million years old, likely extending back ten to twenty million years before present when the Atlantic Ocean lowered and exposed coastal sediments along the fall line. This included the area at Great Falls, which eroded into its present form during recent glaciation periods.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-USGS_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-USGS-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tleft">

<p>The Potomac River brings together a variety of cultures throughout the watershed from the coal miners of upstream West Virginia to the urban residents of the nation's capital and, along the lower Potomac, the watermen of Virginia's Northern Neck.</p>
<p>Being situated in an area rich in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/American_history" title="American history">American history</a> and American heritage has led to the Potomac being nicknamed "the Nation's River." <a href="/wiki/George_Washington" title="George Washington">George Washington</a>, the first <a href="/wiki/President_of_the_United_States" title="President of the United States">President of the United States</a>, was born in, surveyed, and spent most of his life within the Potomac basin. All of Washington, D.C., the nation's <a href="/wiki/Capital_city" title="Capital city">capital city</a>, also lies within the watershed. The 1859 siege of <a href="/wiki/Harpers_Ferry,_West_Virginia" title="Harpers Ferry, West Virginia">Harper's Ferry</a> at the river's <a href="/wiki/Confluence" title="Confluence">confluence</a> with the <a href="/wiki/Shenandoah_River" title="Shenandoah River">Shenandoah</a> was a precursor to numerous epic battles of the <a href="/wiki/American_Civil_War" title="American Civil War">American Civil War</a> in and around the Potomac and its tributaries, such as the 1861 Battle of Ball's Bluff and the 1862 Battle of Shepherdstown. General <a href="/wiki/Robert_E._Lee" title="Robert E. Lee">Robert E. Lee</a> crossed the river, thereby invading the North and threatening Washington, D.C., twice in campaigns climaxing in the battles of <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Antietam" title="Battle of Antietam">Antietam</a> (September 17, 1862) and <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Gettysburg" title="Battle of Gettysburg">Gettysburg</a> (July 1–3, 1863). Confederate General <a href="/wiki/Jubal_Early" title="Jubal Early">Jubal Early</a> crossed the river in July 1864 on his attempted raid on the nation's capital. The river not only divided the Union from the Confederacy, but also gave name to the Union's largest army, the <a href="/wiki/Army_of_the_Potomac" title="Army of the Potomac">Army of the Potomac</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>

<a href="/wiki/Harpers_Ferry,_West_Virginia" title="Harpers Ferry, West Virginia">Harpers Ferry</a>, <a href="/wiki/West_Virginia" title="West Virginia">West Virginia</a> at the confluence of the Potomac and <a href="/wiki/Shenandoah_River" title="Shenandoah River">Shenandoah</a> rivers</div>

<p>The <a href="/wiki/Patowmack_Canal" title="Patowmack Canal">Patowmack Canal</a> was intended by George Washington to connect the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tidewater_region_of_Virginia" title="Tidewater region of Virginia">Tidewater region</a> near <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Georgetown,_District_of_Columbia" title="Georgetown, District of Columbia">Georgetown</a> with <a href="/wiki/Cumberland,_Maryland" title="Cumberland, Maryland">Cumberland</a>, Maryland. Started in 1785 on the Virginia side of the river, it was not completed until 1802. Financial troubles led to the closure of the <a href="/wiki/Canal" title="Canal">canal</a> in 1830. The <a href="/wiki/Chesapeake_and_Ohio_Canal" title="Chesapeake and Ohio Canal">Chesapeake and Ohio Canal</a> operated along the banks of the Potomac in Maryland from 1831 to 1924 and also connected Cumberland to Washington, D.C.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> This allowed freight to be transported around the <a href="/wiki/Rapids" title="Rapids">rapids</a> known as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Great_Falls_of_the_Potomac_River" title="Great Falls of the Potomac River">Great Falls of the Potomac River</a>, as well as many other, smaller rapids.</p>
<p>Washington, D.C. began using the Potomac as its principal source of <a href="/wiki/Drinking_water" title="Drinking water">drinking water</a> with the opening of the <a href="/wiki/Washington_Aqueduct" title="Washington Aqueduct">Washington Aqueduct</a> in 1864, using a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Water_intake" title="Water intake">water intake</a> constructed at Great Falls.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ways_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ways-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>In March 1996 an agreement was signed which made the Potomac a "sister river" of the <a href="/wiki/Arakawa_River_(Kanto)" title="Arakawa River (Kanto)">Ara River</a>, one of the principal rivers of <a href="/wiki/Tokyo" title="Tokyo">Tokyo</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Potomac_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-Potomac-12">[12]</a></sup> President <a href="/wiki/Bill_Clinton" title="Bill Clinton">Bill Clinton</a> designated the Potomac as one of the <a href="/wiki/American_Heritage_Rivers" title="American Heritage Rivers">American Heritage Rivers</a> in 1998.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Water_supply_and_water_quality">Water supply and water quality</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Potomac_River&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Water supply and water quality">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>An average of approximately 486 million US gallons (1,840,000 m<sup>3</sup>) of water per day is withdrawn daily in the Washington area for <a href="/wiki/Water_supply" title="Water supply">water supply</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-basin-facts_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-basin-facts-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Anacostia_River_1936_flood.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="572" data-file-width="740" height="170" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/01/Anacostia_River_1936_flood.jpg/220px-Anacostia_River_1936_flood.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/01/Anacostia_River_1936_flood.jpg/330px-Anacostia_River_1936_flood.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/01/Anacostia_River_1936_flood.jpg/440px-Anacostia_River_1936_flood.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>

<p>As a result of damaging floods in 1936 and 1937, the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Army_Corps_of_Engineers" title="United States Army Corps of Engineers">Army Corps of Engineers</a> proposed a series of <a href="/wiki/Dam" title="Dam">dams</a> that were intended to regulate the river and to provide a more reliable water supply. One dam was to be built at <a href="/wiki/Little_Falls_(Potomac_River)" title="Little Falls (Potomac River)">Little Falls</a>, just north of Washington, backing its pool up to Great Falls. Just above Great Falls, a much larger dam was proposed whose reservoir would extend to Harpers Ferry.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ffls1_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-ffls1-14">[14]</a></sup> Several other dams were proposed for the Potomac and its tributaries. When detailed studies were issued by the Corps in the 1950s, they met sustained opposition, led by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._Supreme_Court" title="U.S. Supreme Court">U.S. Supreme Court</a> Justice <a href="/wiki/William_O._Douglas" title="William O. Douglas">William O. Douglas</a>, resulting in the plans' abandonment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> The only dam project that did get built was <a href="/wiki/Jennings_Randolph_Lake" title="Jennings Randolph Lake">Jennings Randolph Lake</a> on the North Branch. The Corps built a supplementary water intake for the Washington Aqueduct at Little Falls in 1959.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Scott_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-Scott-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1940 <a href="/wiki/United_States_Congress" title="United States Congress">Congress</a> passed a law authorizing creation of an <a href="/wiki/Interstate_compact" title="Interstate compact">interstate compact</a> to coordinate water quality management among states in the Potomac basin. Maryland, West Virginia, Pennsylvania, Virginia, and the District of Columbia agreed to establish the <a href="/wiki/Interstate_Commission_on_the_Potomac_River_Basin" title="Interstate Commission on the Potomac River Basin">Interstate Commission on the Potomac River Basin</a>. The compact was amended in 1970 to include coordination of water supply issues and land use issues related to water quality.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ICPRB_timeline_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-ICPRB_timeline-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>Beginning in the 19th century, with increasing <a href="/wiki/Mining" title="Mining">mining</a> and <a href="/wiki/Agriculture" title="Agriculture">agriculture</a> upstream and urban <a href="/wiki/Sewage" title="Sewage">sewage</a> and <a href="/wiki/Surface_runoff" title="Surface runoff">runoff</a> downstream, the <a href="/wiki/Water_quality" title="Water quality">water quality</a> of the Potomac River deteriorated. This created conditions of severe <a href="/wiki/Eutrophication" title="Eutrophication">eutrophication</a>. It is said that President <a href="/wiki/Abraham_Lincoln" title="Abraham Lincoln">Abraham Lincoln</a> used to escape to the highlands on summer nights to escape the river's stench. In the 1960s, with dense green <a href="/wiki/Algae" title="Algae">algal</a> blooms covering the river's surface, President <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lyndon_Johnson" title="Lyndon Johnson">Lyndon Johnson</a> declared the river "a national disgrace" and set in motion a long-term effort to reduce <a href="/wiki/Water_pollution" title="Water pollution">pollution</a> from <a href="/wiki/Sewage" title="Sewage">sewage</a> and restore the beauty and ecology of this historic river. One of significant pollution control projects at the time was the expansion of the <a href="/wiki/Blue_Plains_Advanced_Wastewater_Treatment_Plant" title="Blue Plains Advanced Wastewater Treatment Plant">Blue Plains Advanced Wastewater Treatment Plant</a>, which serves Washington and several surrounding communities.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> Enactment of the 1972 <a href="/wiki/Clean_Water_Act" title="Clean Water Act">Clean Water Act</a> led to construction or expansion of additional <a href="/wiki/Sewage_treatment" title="Sewage treatment">sewage treatment</a> plants in the Potomac watershed. Controls on <a href="/wiki/Phosphorus" title="Phosphorus">phosphorus</a>, one of the principal contributors to eutrophication, were implemented in the 1980s, through sewage plant upgrades and restrictions on phosphorus in detergents.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ICPRB_timeline_17-1"><a href="#cite_note-ICPRB_timeline-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>By the end of the 20th century, there was notable success, as massive algal blooms vanished and recreational fishing and boating rebounded. Still, the aquatic <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Habitat_(ecology)" title="Habitat (ecology)">habitat</a> of the Potomac River and its tributaries remain vulnerable to eutrophication, <a href="/wiki/Heavy_metals" title="Heavy metals">heavy metals</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pesticide" title="Pesticide">pesticides</a> and other toxic chemicals, over-fishing, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alien_(biology)" title="Alien (biology)">alien species</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Pathogen" title="Pathogen">pathogens</a> associated with <a href="/wiki/Fecal_coliform" title="Fecal coliform">fecal coliform</a> <a href="/wiki/Bacteria" title="Bacteria">bacteria</a> and <a href="/wiki/Shellfish" title="Shellfish">shellfish</a> diseases. In 2005 two federal agencies, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/US_Geological_Survey" title="US Geological Survey">US Geological Survey</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fish_and_Wildlife_Service" title="Fish and Wildlife Service">Fish and Wildlife Service</a>, began to identify fish in the Potomac and tributaries that exhibited "intersex" characteristics, as a result of <a href="/wiki/Endocrine_disruptor" title="Endocrine disruptor">endocrine disruption</a> caused by some form of pollution.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> On November 13, 2007, the Potomac Conservancy, an environmental group, issued the river a grade of "D-plus", citing high levels of pollution and the reports of "<a href="/wiki/Intersex" title="Intersex">intersex</a>" fish.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> Since then, the river has improved with a reduction in nutrient runoff, return of fish populations and land protection along the river. As a result, the same group issued a grade of "B-minus" in 2016. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Legal_issues">Legal issues</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Potomac_River&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Legal issues">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>For 400 years Maryland and Virginia have disputed control of the Potomac and its North Branch, since both states' original <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Colonial_charter" title="Colonial charter">colonial charters</a> grant the entire river rather than half of it as is normally the case with boundary rivers. In its first state <a href="/wiki/Constitution" title="Constitution">constitution</a> adopted in 1776, Virginia ceded its claim to the entire river but reserved free use of it, an act disputed by Maryland. Both states acceded to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Compact_of_1785" title="Compact of 1785">Compact of 1785</a> and the 1877 Black-Jenkins Award which grants Maryland the river bank-to-bank from the low water mark on the Virginia side, while permitting Virginia full <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Riparian_rights" title="Riparian rights">riparian rights</a> short of obstructing navigation.</p>
<p>From 1957 to 1996, the Maryland Department of the Environment (MDE) routinely issued permits applied for by Virginia entities concerning use of the Potomac. However, in 1996 the MDE denied a permit submitted by the <a href="/wiki/Fairfax_County_Water_Authority" title="Fairfax County Water Authority">Fairfax County Water Authority</a> to build a water intake 725 feet (220 m) offshore, citing potential harm to Maryland's interests by an increase in Virginia sprawl caused by the project. After years of failed appeals within the Maryland government's appeal processes, in 2000 Virginia took the case to the <a href="/wiki/Supreme_Court_of_the_United_States" title="Supreme Court of the United States">Supreme Court of the United States</a>, which exercises original jurisdiction in cases between two states. Maryland claimed Virginia lost its riparian rights by acquiescing to MDE's permit process for 63 years (MDE began its permit process in 1933). A <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Special_Master" title="Special Master">Special Master</a> appointed by the Supreme Court to investigate recommended the case be settled in favor of Virginia, citing the language in the 1785 Compact and the 1877 Award. On December 9, 2003, the Court agreed in a 7-2 decision.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p>The original charters are silent as to which branch from the upper Potomac serves as the boundary, but this was settled by the 1785 Compact. When West Virginia <a href="/wiki/West_Virginia#Separation_from_Virginia" title="West Virginia">seceded</a> from Virginia in 1863, the question of West Virginia's succession in title to the lands between the branches of the river was raised, as well as title to the river itself. Claims by Maryland to West Virginia land north of the South Branch (all of <a href="/wiki/Mineral_County,_West_Virginia" title="Mineral County, West Virginia">Mineral</a> and <a href="/wiki/Grant_County,_West_Virginia" title="Grant County, West Virginia">Grant</a> Counties and parts of <a href="/wiki/Hampshire_County,_West_Virginia" title="Hampshire County, West Virginia">Hampshire</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hardy_County,_West_Virginia" title="Hardy County, West Virginia">Hardy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tucker_County,_West_Virginia" title="Tucker County, West Virginia">Tucker</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pendleton_County,_West_Virginia" title="Pendleton County, West Virginia">Pendleton</a> Counties) and by West Virginia to the Potomac's high-water mark were rejected by the Supreme Court in two separate decisions in 1910.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="North_Branch_Potomac_River">North Branch Potomac River</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Potomac_River&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: North Branch Potomac River">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>The source of the <b>North Branch Potomac River</b> is at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fairfax_Stone" title="Fairfax Stone">Fairfax Stone</a> located at the junction of <a href="/wiki/Grant_County,_West_Virginia" title="Grant County, West Virginia">Grant</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tucker_County,_West_Virginia" title="Tucker County, West Virginia">Tucker</a> and <a href="/wiki/Preston_County,_West_Virginia" title="Preston County, West Virginia">Preston</a> counties in West Virginia.</p>
<p>From the Fairfax Stone, the North Branch Potomac River flows 27 miles (43 km) to the man-made <a href="/wiki/Jennings_Randolph_Lake" title="Jennings Randolph Lake">Jennings Randolph Lake</a>, an <a href="/wiki/Reservoir" title="Reservoir">impoundment</a> designed for flood control and emergency water supply. Below the dam, the North Branch cuts a serpentine path through the eastern Allegheny Mountains. First, it flows northeast by the communities of <a href="/wiki/Bloomington,_Maryland" title="Bloomington, Maryland">Bloomington</a>, <a href="/wiki/Luke,_Maryland" title="Luke, Maryland">Luke</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Westernport,_Maryland" title="Westernport, Maryland">Westernport</a> in Maryland and then on by <a href="/wiki/Keyser,_West_Virginia" title="Keyser, West Virginia">Keyser</a>, West Virginia to <a href="/wiki/Cumberland,_Maryland" title="Cumberland, Maryland">Cumberland</a>, Maryland. At Cumberland, the river turns southeast. 103 miles (166 km) downstream from its source,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NHD_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-NHD-1">[1]</a></sup> the North Branch is joined by the South Branch between <a href="/wiki/Green_Spring,_West_Virginia" title="Green Spring, West Virginia">Green Spring</a> and <a href="/wiki/South_Branch_Depot,_West_Virginia" title="South Branch Depot, West Virginia">South Branch Depot</a>, West Virginia from whence it flows past <a href="/wiki/Hancock,_Maryland" title="Hancock, Maryland">Hancock</a>, Maryland and turns southeast once more on its way toward <a href="/wiki/Washington,_D.C." title="Washington, D.C.">Washington, D.C.</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Chesapeake_Bay" title="Chesapeake Bay">Chesapeake Bay</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="South_Branch_Potomac_River">South Branch Potomac River</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Potomac_River&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: South Branch Potomac River">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>The <b>South Branch Potomac River</b> has its <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Headwaters" title="Headwaters">headwaters</a> in northwestern <a href="/wiki/Highland_County,_Virginia" title="Highland County, Virginia">Highland County</a>, Virginia near <a href="/wiki/Hightown,_Virginia" title="Hightown, Virginia">Hightown</a> along the eastern edge of the <a href="/wiki/Allegheny_Front" title="Allegheny Front">Allegheny Front</a>. After a river distance of 139 miles (224 km),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NHD_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-NHD-1">[1]</a></sup> the mouth of the South Branch lies east of <a href="/wiki/Green_Spring,_West_Virginia" title="Green Spring, West Virginia">Green Spring</a> in <a href="/wiki/Hampshire_County,_West_Virginia" title="Hampshire County, West Virginia">Hampshire County</a>, West Virginia where it meets the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/North_Branch_Potomac_River" title="North Branch Potomac River">North Branch Potomac River</a> to form the Potomac.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="South_Branch_nomenclature">South Branch nomenclature</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Potomac_River&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: South Branch nomenclature">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Native_Americans_in_the_United_States" title="Native Americans in the United States">Native Americans</a> of the region, and thus the earliest white settlers, referred to the South Branch Potomac River as the <b>Wappatomaka</b>. Variants throughout the river's history included <b>Wappatomica River</b>, <b>Wapacomo River</b>, <b>Wapocomo River</b>, <b>Wappacoma River</b>, <b>Wappatomaka River</b>, <b>South Branch of Potowmac River</b>, and <b>South Fork Potomac River</b>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<p>Places settled in the South Branch valley bearing variants of "Wappatomaka" include <a href="/wiki/Wappocomo_(Romney,_West_Virginia)" title="Wappocomo (Romney, West Virginia)">Wappocomo farm</a> built in 1774 and the <a href="/wiki/Unincorporated_area" title="Unincorporated area">unincorporated</a> hamlet of <a href="/wiki/Wappocomo,_West_Virginia" title="Wappocomo, West Virginia">Wappocomo</a> (sometimes spelled Wapocomo) at <a href="/wiki/Hanging_Rocks" title="Hanging Rocks">Hanging Rocks</a>, both north of <a href="/wiki/Romney,_West_Virginia" title="Romney, West Virginia">Romney</a> on <a href="/wiki/West_Virginia_Route_28" title="West Virginia Route 28">West Virginia Route 28</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="South_Branch_headwaters_and_course">South Branch headwaters and course</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Potomac_River&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: South Branch headwaters and course">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The exact location of the South Branch's source is northwest of Hightown along <a href="/wiki/U.S._Route_250" title="U.S. Route 250">U.S. Route 250</a> on the eastern side of Lantz Mountain (3,934 ft) in Highland County. From Hightown, the South Branch is a small meandering <a href="/wiki/Stream" title="Stream">stream</a> that flows northeast along Blue Grass Valley Road through the communities of <a href="/wiki/New_Hampden,_Virginia" title="New Hampden, Virginia">New Hampden</a> and <a href="/wiki/Blue_Grass,_Virginia" title="Blue Grass, Virginia">Blue Grass</a>. At <a href="/wiki/Forks_of_Waters,_Virginia" title="Forks of Waters, Virginia">Forks of Waters</a>, the South Branch joins with Strait Creek and flows north across the Virginia/West Virginia border into <a href="/wiki/Pendleton_County,_West_Virginia" title="Pendleton County, West Virginia">Pendleton County</a>. The river then travels on a northeastern course along the western side of Jack Mountain (4,045 ft), followed by Sandy Ridge (2,297 ft) along <a href="/wiki/U.S._Route_220" title="U.S. Route 220">U.S. Route 220</a>. North of the confluence of the South Branch with Smith Creek, the river flows along Town Mountain (2,848 ft) around <a href="/wiki/Franklin,_West_Virginia" title="Franklin, West Virginia">Franklin</a> at the junction of U.S. Route 220 and <a href="/wiki/U.S._Route_33" title="U.S. Route 33">U.S. Route 33</a>. After Franklin, the South Branch continues north through the <a href="/wiki/Monongahela_National_Forest" title="Monongahela National Forest">Monongahela National Forest</a> to <a href="/wiki/Upper_Tract,_West_Virginia" title="Upper Tract, West Virginia">Upper Tract</a> where it joins with three sizeable streams: Reeds Creek, Mill Run, and Deer Run. Between Big Mountain (2,582 ft) and Cave Mountain (2,821 ft), the South Branch bends around the Eagle Rock (1,483 ft) outcrop and continues its flow northward into <a href="/wiki/Grant_County,_West_Virginia" title="Grant County, West Virginia">Grant County</a>. Into Grant, the South Branch follows the western side of Cave Mountain through the 20-mile (32 km) long <a href="/wiki/Smoke_Hole_Canyon" title="Smoke Hole Canyon">Smoke Hole Canyon</a>, until its confluence with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/North_Fork_South_Branch_Potomac_River" title="North Fork South Branch Potomac River">North Fork</a> at <a href="/wiki/Cabins,_West_Virginia" title="Cabins, West Virginia">Cabins</a>, where it flows east to <a href="/wiki/Petersburg,_West_Virginia" title="Petersburg, West Virginia">Petersburg</a>. At Petersburg, the South Branch is joined with the <a href="/wiki/South_Branch_Valley_Railroad" title="South Branch Valley Railroad">South Branch Valley Railroad</a>, which it parallels until its mouth at <a href="/wiki/Green_Spring,_West_Virginia" title="Green Spring, West Virginia">Green Spring</a>.</p>

<p>In its eastern course from Petersburg into <a href="/wiki/Hardy_County,_West_Virginia" title="Hardy County, West Virginia">Hardy County</a>, the South Branch becomes more navigable allowing for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Canoes" title="Canoes">canoes</a> and smaller river vessels. The river splits and forms a series of large islands while it heads northeast to <a href="/wiki/Moorefield,_West_Virginia" title="Moorefield, West Virginia">Moorefield</a>. At Moorefield, the South Branch is joined by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/South_Fork_South_Branch_Potomac_River" title="South Fork South Branch Potomac River">South Fork South Branch Potomac River</a> and runs north to <a href="/wiki/Old_Fields,_West_Virginia" title="Old Fields, West Virginia">Old Fields</a> where it is fed by Anderson Run and Stony Run. At <a href="/wiki/McNeill,_West_Virginia" title="McNeill, West Virginia">McNeill</a>, the South Branch flows into <a href="/wiki/The_Trough" title="The Trough">the Trough</a> where it is bound to its west by <a href="/wiki/Mill_Creek_Mountain" title="Mill Creek Mountain">Mill Creek Mountain</a> (2,119 ft) and to its east by Sawmill Ridge (1,644 ft). This area is the habitat to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bald_eagles" title="Bald eagles">bald eagles</a>. The Trough passes into <a href="/wiki/Hampshire_County,_West_Virginia" title="Hampshire County, West Virginia">Hampshire County</a> and ends at its confluence with Sawmill Run south of <a href="/wiki/Glebe,_West_Virginia" title="Glebe, West Virginia">Glebe</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sector,_West_Virginia" title="Sector, West Virginia">Sector</a>. The South Branch continues north parallel to <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=South_Branch_River_Road&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="South Branch River Road (page does not exist)">South Branch River Road</a> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/County_road" title="County road">County Route</a> 8) toward <a href="/wiki/Romney,_West_Virginia" title="Romney, West Virginia">Romney</a> with a number of historic plantation farms adjoining it. En route to Romney, the river is fed by Buffalo Run, <a href="/wiki/Mill_Run_(South_Branch_Potomac_River)" title="Mill Run (South Branch Potomac River)">Mill Run</a>, McDowell Run, and <a href="/wiki/Mill_Creek_(South_Branch_Potomac_River)" title="Mill Creek (South Branch Potomac River)">Mill Creek</a> at <a href="/wiki/Vanderlip,_West_Virginia" title="Vanderlip, West Virginia">Vanderlip</a>. The South Branch is traversed by the <a href="/wiki/Northwestern_Turnpike" title="Northwestern Turnpike">Northwestern Turnpike</a> (<a href="/wiki/U.S._Route_50_in_West_Virginia" title="U.S. Route 50 in West Virginia">U.S. Route 50</a>) and joined by Sulphur Spring Run where it forms <a href="/wiki/Valley_View_Island" title="Valley View Island">Valley View Island</a> to the west of town. Flowing north of Romney, the river still follows the eastern side of Mill Creek Mountain until it creates a horseshoe bend at <a href="/wiki/Wappocomo,_West_Virginia" title="Wappocomo, West Virginia">Wappocomo</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Hanging_Rocks" title="Hanging Rocks">Hanging Rocks</a> around the George W. Washington plantation, <a href="/wiki/Washington_Bottom_Farm" title="Washington Bottom Farm">Ridgedale</a>. To the west of <a href="/wiki/Three_Churches,_West_Virginia" title="Three Churches, West Virginia">Three Churches</a> on the western side of <a href="/wiki/South_Branch_Mountain" title="South Branch Mountain">South Branch Mountain</a>, 3,028 feet (923 m), the South Branch creates a series of bends and flows to the northeast by <a href="/wiki/Springfield,_West_Virginia" title="Springfield, West Virginia">Springfield</a> through Blue's Ford. After two additional horseshoe bends (meanders), the South Branch flows under the old <a href="/wiki/Baltimore_and_Ohio_Railroad" title="Baltimore and Ohio Railroad">Baltimore and Ohio Railroad</a> mainline between <a href="/wiki/Green_Spring,_West_Virginia" title="Green Spring, West Virginia">Green Spring</a> and <a href="/wiki/South_Branch_Depot,_West_Virginia" title="South Branch Depot, West Virginia">South Branch Depot</a>, and joins the North Branch to form the Potomac.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="North_Fork_South_Branch_Potomac_River">North Fork South Branch Potomac River</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Potomac_River&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: North Fork South Branch Potomac River">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:North_Fork_South_Branch_Potomac_River.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2142" data-file-width="2856" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bf/North_Fork_South_Branch_Potomac_River.jpg/220px-North_Fork_South_Branch_Potomac_River.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bf/North_Fork_South_Branch_Potomac_River.jpg/330px-North_Fork_South_Branch_Potomac_River.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bf/North_Fork_South_Branch_Potomac_River.jpg/440px-North_Fork_South_Branch_Potomac_River.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>

The North Fork South Branch below <a href="/wiki/Seneca_Rocks" title="Seneca Rocks">Seneca Rocks</a> in Pendleton County, West Virginia</div>

<p>The <b>North Fork South Branch Potomac River</b>, 43.6 miles (70.2 km) long,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NHD_1-4"><a href="#cite_note-NHD-1">[1]</a></sup> forms just north of the Virginia/West Virginia border in <a href="/wiki/Pendleton_County,_West_Virginia" title="Pendleton County, West Virginia">Pendleton County</a> at the confluence of the <a href="/wiki/Laurel_Fork_(North_Fork_South_Branch_Potomac_River)" title="Laurel Fork (North Fork South Branch Potomac River)">Laurel Fork</a> and Straight Fork along Big Mountain 3,881 feet (1,183 m). From <a href="/wiki/Circleville,_West_Virginia" title="Circleville, West Virginia">Circleville</a>, the North Fork flows northeast through Pendleton County between the Fore Knobs 2,949 feet (899 m) to its west and the <a href="/wiki/River_Knobs_(West_Virginia)" title="River Knobs (West Virginia)">River Knobs</a>, 2,490 feet (759 m) to its east. At <a href="/wiki/Seneca_Rocks,_West_Virginia" title="Seneca Rocks, West Virginia">Seneca Rocks</a>, the North Fork is met by <a href="/wiki/Seneca_Creek_(North_Fork_South_Branch_Potomac_River)" title="Seneca Creek (North Fork South Branch Potomac River)">Seneca Creek</a>. From Seneca Rocks, the North Fork continues to flow northeast along the western edge of North Fork Mountain 3,389 feet (1033 m) into <a href="/wiki/Grant_County,_West_Virginia" title="Grant County, West Virginia">Grant County</a>. Flowing east through North Fork Gap, the North Fork joins the South Branch Potomac at the town of <a href="/wiki/Cabins,_West_Virginia" title="Cabins, West Virginia">Cabins</a>, west of <a href="/wiki/Petersburg,_West_Virginia" title="Petersburg, West Virginia">Petersburg</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="South_Fork_South_Branch_Potomac_River">South Fork South Branch Potomac River</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Potomac_River&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: South Fork South Branch Potomac River">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <b>South Fork South Branch Potomac River</b> forms just north of <a href="/wiki/U.S._Route_250" title="U.S. Route 250">U.S. Route 250</a> in <a href="/wiki/Highland_County,_Virginia" title="Highland County, Virginia">Highland County</a>, <a href="/wiki/Virginia" title="Virginia">Virginia</a> near <a href="/wiki/Head_Waters,_Virginia" title="Head Waters, Virginia">Head Waters</a> and flows 68.4 miles (110.1 km)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NHD_1-5"><a href="#cite_note-NHD-1">[1]</a></sup> north-northeastward to the South Branch Potomac River at <a href="/wiki/Moorefield,_West_Virginia" title="Moorefield, West Virginia">Moorefield</a> in <a href="/wiki/Hardy_County,_West_Virginia" title="Hardy County, West Virginia">Hardy County</a>, <a href="/wiki/West_Virginia" title="West Virginia">West Virginia</a>. From 1896 to 1929, it was named the <b>Moorefield River</b> by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Board_on_Geographic_Names" title="Board on Geographic Names">Board on Geographic Names</a> to avoid confusion with the South Branch.</p>

<p>The tidal or Lower Potomac River lies below the <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Seaboard_fall_line" title="Atlantic Seaboard fall line">Fall Line</a>. This stretch encompasses the Potomac from about one mile (2 km) below the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Washington,_DC" title="Washington, DC">Washington, DC</a> - <a href="/wiki/Montgomery_County,_Maryland" title="Montgomery County, Maryland">Montgomery County</a> line, just below the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Little_Falls_of_the_Potomac_River" title="Little Falls of the Potomac River">Little Falls of the Potomac River</a> where the tidal river begins, to <a href="/wiki/Chesapeake_Bay" title="Chesapeake Bay">Chesapeake Bay</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Fish">Fish</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Potomac_River&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Fish">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A variety of fish inhabit the Potomac, including <a href="/wiki/Bass_(fish)" title="Bass (fish)">bass</a>, <a href="/wiki/Muskellunge" title="Muskellunge">muskellunge</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pike_(fish)" title="Pike (fish)">pike</a>, <a href="/wiki/Walleye" title="Walleye">walleye</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Northern_snakehead" title="Northern snakehead">northern snakehead</a>, an invasive species resembling the native <a href="/wiki/Bowfin" title="Bowfin">bowfin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lamprey" title="Lamprey">lamprey</a>, and <a href="/wiki/American_eel" title="American eel">American eel</a>, was first seen in 2004.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> Although rare, <a href="/wiki/Bull_shark" title="Bull shark">bull sharks</a> can be found.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
