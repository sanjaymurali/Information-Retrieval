<p>The <b>Geophysical Fluid Dynamics Laboratory</b> (<b>GFDL</b>) is a laboratory in the <a href="/wiki/National_Oceanic_and_Atmospheric_Administration" title="National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration">National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration</a> (NOAA) <a href="/wiki/Office_of_Oceanic_and_Atmospheric_Research" title="Office of Oceanic and Atmospheric Research">Office of Oceanic and Atmospheric Research</a> (OAR). The current director is Dr. <a href="/wiki/Venkatachalam_Ramaswamy" title="Venkatachalam Ramaswamy">Venkatachalam Ramaswamy</a>. It is one of seven NOAA Research Laboratories (RLs).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>GFDL is engaged in comprehensive long lead-time research to expand the scientific understanding of the physical processes that govern the behavior of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Earth%27s_atmosphere" title="Earth's atmosphere">atmosphere</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Ocean" title="Ocean">oceans</a> as <a href="/wiki/Fluid_mechanics" title="Fluid mechanics">complex fluid systems</a>. These systems can then be modeled mathematically and their <a href="/wiki/Empirical_research" title="Empirical research">phenomenology</a> can be studied by computer simulation methods.</p>
<p>GFDL's accomplishments include the development of the first climate models to study global warming, the first comprehensive ocean prediction codes, and the first dynamical models with significant skill in hurricane track and intensity predictions. Much current research within the laboratory is focused around the development of Earth System Models for assessment of natural and human-induced climate change.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Major_accomplishments">Major accomplishments</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Geophysical_Fluid_Dynamics_Laboratory&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Major accomplishments">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul>
<li>The first global numerical simulations of the atmosphere — defining the basic structure of the numerical weather prediction and climate models that are still in use today throughout the world.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NOAA_Magazine_Article_on_GFDL_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-NOAA_Magazine_Article_on_GFDL-2">[2]</a></sup></li>
<li>The first numerical simulation of the world ocean.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NOAA_Magazine_Article_on_GFDL_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-NOAA_Magazine_Article_on_GFDL-2">[2]</a></sup></li>
<li>The initial definition and further elaborations of many of the central issues in global warming research, including water vapor feedback, polar amplification of temperature change, summer mid-continental dryness and cloud feedback.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NOAA_Magazine_Article_on_GFDL_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-NOAA_Magazine_Article_on_GFDL-2">[2]</a></sup></li>
<li>The first coupled atmosphere-ocean climate models and the first simulations of global warming using these models (including the above feedback processes and the potential weakening of the Atlantic overturning circulation).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NOAA_Magazine_Article_on_GFDL_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-NOAA_Magazine_Article_on_GFDL-2">[2]</a></sup></li>
<li>The development of a state-of-art hurricane model and its transfer to operations in the NOAA National Weather Service and the Navy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NOAA_Magazine_Article_on_GFDL_2-4"><a href="#cite_note-NOAA_Magazine_Article_on_GFDL-2">[2]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Scientific_groups">Scientific groups</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Geophysical_Fluid_Dynamics_Laboratory&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Scientific groups">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The GFDL has a diverse community of about 300 researchers, collaborators and staff, with many from Britain, India, China, Japan, France, etc. The laboratory is currently organized into several scientific groups (listed alphabetically below) as well as a large computer support group.</p>
<h3><span id="Atmospheric_physics,_chemistry_and_climate"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Atmospheric_physics.2C_chemistry_and_climate">Atmospheric physics, chemistry and climate</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Geophysical_Fluid_Dynamics_Laboratory&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Atmospheric physics, chemistry and climate">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Current head: Dr. Hiram Levy II</p>
<p>This group focuses on processes that affect the vertical structure of the atmosphere, such as convection and radiation. A particular focus is the role of aerosols in climate, both through direct effects on the radiative balance, and indirect effects on cloud physics.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Climate_and_ecosystems">Climate and ecosystems</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Geophysical_Fluid_Dynamics_Laboratory&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Climate and ecosystems">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Current head: Ron Stouffer</p>
<p>This group focuses on interactions between the physical climate and biogeochemical systems, both in the land and ocean. Development of GFDL's Earth System Model is centered in this group.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Climate_diagnostics">Climate diagnostics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Geophysical_Fluid_Dynamics_Laboratory&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Climate diagnostics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Current head: Dr. Ngar-Chung (Gabriel) Lau</p>
<p>This group focuses on comparing models with observations, developing observational datasets that can be compared with models, and using models to isolate key processes that regulate interannual variability in the ocean and atmosphere.</p>
<h3><span id="Climate_change,_variability_and_prediction"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Climate_change.2C_variability_and_prediction">Climate change, variability and prediction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Geophysical_Fluid_Dynamics_Laboratory&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Climate change, variability and prediction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Current head: Dr. Tom Delworth</p>
<p>The largest group in the lab centers on the development of numerical models for predictions and projections of climate on seasonal-to-centennial time scales. Major projects include the development of El Nino predictions for seasonal forecasting, and the production of IPCC-class climate models.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Oceans_and_climate">Oceans and climate</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Geophysical_Fluid_Dynamics_Laboratory&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Oceans and climate">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Current head: Dr. Robert Hallberg</p>
<p>This group focuses on the role played by the oceans in the large-scale climate system, and on developing state-of-the-art numerical codes to simulate the ocean.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Weather_and_atmospheric_dynamics">Weather and atmospheric dynamics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Geophysical_Fluid_Dynamics_Laboratory&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Weather and atmospheric dynamics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Current head: Dr. <a href="/wiki/Isaac_Held" title="Isaac Held">Isaac Held</a></p>
<p>This group focuses on the dynamics of the atmosphere, with particular emphasis on the interactions between waves and turbulence and the large-scale flow. Development of GFDL's hurricane model is supported from this group.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Facilities">Facilities</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Geophysical_Fluid_Dynamics_Laboratory&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Facilities">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The GFDL is located at <a href="/wiki/Princeton_University" title="Princeton University">Princeton University</a>'s Forrestal Campus in Princeton, NJ.</p>
<p>Since March 2011, the GFDL no longer possesses an on-site <a href="/wiki/Supercomputer" title="Supercomputer">supercomputer</a>. They instead utilize a <a href="/wiki/Massively_parallel" title="Massively parallel">massively parallel</a> <a href="/wiki/Cray" title="Cray">Cray</a> supercomputer with over 30,000 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Processor_core" title="Processor core">processor cores</a> which is currently located at <a href="/wiki/Oak_Ridge_National_Laboratory" title="Oak Ridge National Laboratory">Oak Ridge National Laboratory</a> in <a href="/wiki/Oak_Ridge,_Tennessee" title="Oak Ridge, Tennessee">Oak Ridge</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tennessee" title="Tennessee">Tennessee</a>. This contrasts from their previous systems architecture, which consisted of eight <a href="/wiki/Silicon_Graphics" title="Silicon Graphics">Silicon Graphics</a> <a href="/wiki/Altix" title="Altix">Altix</a> computers, each housing 1024 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Processor_core" title="Processor core">processor cores</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Hardware updates occur on average, every 18 months.</p>
<p>The GFDL has been utilizing High Performance Computing Systems for Numerical Modeling since the 1950s.</p>
