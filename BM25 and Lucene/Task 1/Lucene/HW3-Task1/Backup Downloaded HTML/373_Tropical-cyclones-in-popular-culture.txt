<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Tropical cyclones in popular culture</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>

<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><p>The appearances of <b>tropical cyclones in popular culture</b> spans many genres of media and encompasses many different plot uses.</p>
<p>It includes both <a href="/wiki/Fiction" title="Fiction">fictional</a> <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone" title="Tropical cyclone">tropical cyclones</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TropCyc_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-TropCyc-1">[a]</a></sup> and real ones used as the basis for a fictional work, and has proven to be of enough interest for the <a href="/wiki/National_Oceanic_and_Atmospheric_Administration" title="National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration">National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration</a> ("NOAA") to maintain a webpage on the topic.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-noaafaq_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-noaafaq-2">[1]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Purpose_in_fiction_and_literature">Purpose in fiction and literature</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tropical_cyclones_in_popular_culture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Purpose in fiction and literature">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Although many forms of natural disaster appear in fiction and literature, tropical cyclones serve a number of useful literary functions because they are both extraordinarily powerful and, to those who have some experience with them, their occurrence can be portended several days in advance. The NOAA page notes that:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>There is undeniable drama to hurricanes; their massive scale affecting the lives of thousands, the foreshadowing of impending doom, and their ponderous pace as they approach the shore. This has made them ideal plot elements in many fictional works.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-noaafaq_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-noaafaq-2">[1]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>The strength of the tropical cyclone has made it a device by which authors explain the upending of characters' lives, and even transformations of the personalities of those who live through such an event. Their somewhat hazy predictability also makes them a useful <a href="/wiki/MacGuffin" title="MacGuffin">MacGuffin</a>, an impetus for characters to set to action. In some instances, the storm provides cover for characters to engage in covert behavior.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_history_of_tropical_cyclones_in_literature">Early history of tropical cyclones in literature</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tropical_cyclones_in_popular_culture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Early history of tropical cyclones in literature">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>One of the earliest uses of a tropical cyclone as a plot device occurs in a <a href="/wiki/William_Shakespeare" title="William Shakespeare">William Shakespeare</a> play, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Tempest" title="The Tempest">The Tempest</a></i>, first performed in 1611 or 1612. There, a storm (raised by the sorcerer <a href="/wiki/Prospero" title="Prospero">Prospero</a>) blows key characters to the island to which Prospero had been exiled many years before. The theme is said to have been inspired by Shakespeare's knowledge of a real-life hurricane which had caused the shipwreck of the <i><a href="/wiki/Sea_Venture" title="Sea Venture">Sea Venture</a></i> in 1609 on the islands of <a href="/wiki/Bermuda" title="Bermuda">Bermuda</a>, while sailing toward <a href="/wiki/Virginia" title="Virginia">Virginia</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-noaafaq_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-noaafaq-2">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Edgar_Allan_Poe" title="Edgar Allan Poe">Edgar Allan Poe</a>, in his 1841 story, "<a href="/wiki/A_Descent_into_the_Maelstr%C3%B6m" title="A Descent into the Maelström">A Descent into the Maelström</a>", has the main character describe how "the most terrible hurricane that ever came out of the heavens" forced the boat manned by himself and his brothers into a gigantic <a href="/wiki/Whirlpool" title="Whirlpool">whirlpool</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[2]</a></sup> The trauma of surviving the storm and the whirlpool (and seeing the death of his brothers) is asserted to have a profound effect on the character, causing his hair to turn white. However, since the story is asserted to occur off the coast of <a href="/wiki/Norway" title="Norway">Norway</a>, it is unlikely that the event described could have fallen within the formal definition of a hurricane, as such storms form almost exclusively in the <a href="/wiki/Air_mass#Classification" title="Air mass">Maritime Tropical air masses</a> of <a href="/wiki/Tropics" title="Tropics">tropical</a> regions of the globe.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Joseph_Conrad" title="Joseph Conrad">Joseph Conrad</a>, in his acclaimed 1903 book <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Typhoon_(novel)" title="Typhoon (novel)">Typhoon</a></i>, uses a tropical cyclone as a more direct element of the story, centering the plot on a ship captain's stubborn insistence on going into the heart of such a storm.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-noaafaq_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-noaafaq-2">[1]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Works_predominantly_focused_on_the_occurrence_of_a_fictional_tropical_cyclone">Works predominantly focused on the occurrence of a fictional tropical cyclone</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tropical_cyclones_in_popular_culture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Works predominantly focused on the occurrence of a fictional tropical cyclone">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Books_and_plays">Books and plays</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tropical_cyclones_in_popular_culture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Books and plays">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<ul>
<li><b>(Unnamed)</b>: <i><b><a href="/wiki/A_High_Wind_in_Jamaica_(novel)" title="A High Wind in Jamaica (novel)">A High Wind in Jamaica</a></b></i> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_States_of_America" title="United States of America">U.S.</a> title: <i>The Innocent Voyage</i>). In this 1929 novel by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Richard_Hughes_(writer)" title="Richard Hughes (writer)">Richard Hughes</a>, horrific incidents are described from a child's point of view, beginning with the destruction of the family's house by a hurricane. "If Emily had known this was a Hurricane, she would doubtless have been far more impressed, for the word was full of romantic terrors...."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[3]</a></sup></li>
<li><b>(Unnamed)</b>: <i><b><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=In_Hazard&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="In Hazard (page does not exist)">In Hazard</a></b></i>, a 1938 novel by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Richard_Hughes_(writer)" title="Richard Hughes (writer)">Richard Hughes</a>. A single-screw turbine cargo steamer encounters a hurricane off the coast of Cuba.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-noaafaq_2-4"><a href="#cite_note-noaafaq-2">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[4]</a></sup> Reviewers compared it to <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Conrad" title="Joseph Conrad">Joseph Conrad</a>'s <i>Typhoon,</i> admired the weather descriptions, complained of "puppet-like" characters.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[7]</a></sup></li>
<li><b>(Unnamed)</b>: <i><b>The Mystery of the Double Double Cross</b></i> A 1982 novel by Mary Blount Christian prominently features a hurricane hitting Galveston, TX.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[9]</a></sup></li>
<li><b>Tropical Storm Barney</b>: The name of an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Weather_control" title="Weather control">artificially enhanced</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tropical_storm" title="Tropical storm">tropical storm</a> that strikes <a href="/wiki/Barbados" title="Barbados">Barbados</a> with winds in excess of 75 mph (121 km/h) in opening of the novel, <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Category_7:_The_Biggest_Storm_in_History&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Category 7: The Biggest Storm in History (page does not exist)">Category 7: The Biggest Storm in History</a></i>. The authors describe the storms <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rapid_deepening#Explosive_intensification" title="Rapid deepening">sudden intensification</a> as "...bloated menacing clouds exploding over the open ocean with the unholy force of a mid-air detonation."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[10]</a></sup> This description is the books <i><a href="/wiki/Leitmotif" title="Leitmotif">leitmotif</a></i> for a manipulated storm.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-noaafaq_2-5"><a href="#cite_note-noaafaq-2">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cat7_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-Cat7-12">[11]</a></sup></li>
<li><b>Hurricane Claude</b>: A team of scientists try to use an <a href="/wiki/Electromagnetic_pulse" title="Electromagnetic pulse">EMP burst</a> to destroy a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saffir-Simpson_Hurricane_Scale#Category_2" title="Saffir-Simpson Hurricane Scale">Category Two</a> hurricane following a track similar to that of the <a href="/wiki/1938_New_England_hurricane" title="1938 New England hurricane">Long Island Express</a>. Preceded by <b>Hurricane Barbara</b>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[12]</a></sup></li>
<li><b>Hurricane Faith</b>: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saffir-Simpson_Hurricane_Scale#Category_5" title="Saffir-Simpson Hurricane Scale">Category Five</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cape_Verde-type_hurricane" title="Cape Verde-type hurricane">Cape Verde hurricane</a> which hits <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York City</a> with full force, causing a <a href="/wiki/Storm_surge" title="Storm surge">storm surge</a> that topples the <a href="/wiki/Statue_of_Liberty" title="Statue of Liberty">Statue of Liberty</a> and inflicts severe damage on the rest of the city.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[13]</a></sup> The novels <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="Atlantic hurricane season">hurricane season</a> prior to the appearance of <b>Faith</b> is described by one of the characters as "Just five in six weeks since the start of the season. And every one has gone flatter than my wife's pancakes in less than a week".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Eric_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-Eric-15">[14]</a></sup> Of these storms, only hurricanes <b>Anthony</b><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[15]</a></sup>(<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saffir-Simpson_Hurricane_Scale#Category_1" title="Saffir-Simpson Hurricane Scale">Category One</a>),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[16]</a></sup> <b>Barbara</b>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[17]</a></sup> <b>Christopher</b><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[18]</a></sup> &amp; <b>Eric</b><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Eric_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-Eric-15">[14]</a></sup> are named in the novel.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[19]</a></sup></li>
<li><b>Hurricane Mabel</b>: The name of the storm in <a href="/wiki/Desmond_Bagley" title="Desmond Bagley">Desmond Bagley</a>'s 1966 novel <i>Wyatt's Hurricane</i>. The titular scientist predicts the hurricane will strike a small Caribbean island despite what the models show. Local rebels use it to help overthrow the island's dictator. <i>Mabel</i> is described at the start of the novel as having a central pressure of 870 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Millibars" title="Millibars">millibars</a> and an outside pressure of 1040 millibars and winds strong enough to classify it as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saffir-Simpson_Hurricane_Scale#Category_5" title="Saffir-Simpson Hurricane Scale">Category Five</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[20]</a></sup> Reference is also made in the novel to <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Ione" title="Hurricane Ione">Hurricane Ione</a> as proof of the <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone_track_forecasting" title="Tropical cyclone track forecasting">difficulties of forecasting a hurricanes course</a>. Preceded by <b>Hurricane Laura</b>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[21]</a></sup></li>
<li><b>Maria</b>: The name of the storm in <a href="/wiki/George_R._Stewart" title="George R. Stewart">George R. Stewart</a>'s bestselling 1941 novel, <i><a href="/wiki/Storm_(novel)" title="Storm (novel)">Storm</a>.</i> Although not, strictly speaking, a hurricane, the storm is highly notable for receiving a woman's name, the first widely known example of such personification. In the novel, a character referred to only as "the Junior Meteorologist" gives storms women's names as a private mental game. Stewart said that he was inspired by reading that "a certain meteorologist had even felt storms to be so personal that he had given them names."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-noaafaq_2-6"><a href="#cite_note-noaafaq-2">[1]</a></sup> Stewart's book in turn inspired <a href="/wiki/Lerner_and_Loewe" title="Lerner and Loewe">Lerner and Loewe</a>'s song "They Call the Wind Maria."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-noaafaq_2-7"><a href="#cite_note-noaafaq-2">[1]</a></sup> It is widely thought to have influenced <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._Navy" title="U.S. Navy">U.S. Navy</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Meteorologists" title="Meteorologists">meteorologists</a>, who gave female names to Pacific tropical storms during <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[22]</a></sup></li>
<li><b>Hurricane Omega</b>: The name of a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saffir-Simpson_Hurricane_Scale#Category_5" title="Saffir-Simpson Hurricane Scale">Category Five</a> hurricane threatening the east coast of the United States that is deflected by <a href="/wiki/Weather_modification" title="Weather modification">moving</a> the <a href="/wiki/Jet_stream" title="Jet stream">jet stream</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[23]</a></sup></li>
<li><b>Hurricane Simone</b>: The name of an artificially created storm that threatens to hit <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York City</a> with a strength of above <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saffir-Simpson_Hurricane_Scale#Category_5" title="Saffir-Simpson Hurricane Scale">Category Five</a> in the novel, <i>Category 7: The Biggest Storm in History</i>. While the storm is successfully <a href="/wiki/Project_Stormfury" title="Project Stormfury">disrupted</a> before it makes landfall, the storm surge it generates devastates the city and topples the <a href="/wiki/Statue_of_Liberty" title="Statue of Liberty">Statue of Liberty</a> into the <a href="/wiki/Hudson_River" title="Hudson River">Hudson</a>. Reference is made to Hurricanes <b><a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Mitch" title="Hurricane Mitch">Mitch</a></b> &amp; <b><a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Ivan" title="Hurricane Ivan">Ivan</a></b> as being the products of trial runs of the technology used to create <b>Simone</b>, which was originally developed and tested during the <a href="/wiki/1971_Pacific_typhoon_season" title="1971 Pacific typhoon season">1971 Pacific typhoon season</a> by the U.S. Government.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-noaafaq_2-8"><a href="#cite_note-noaafaq-2">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cat7_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-Cat7-12">[11]</a></sup></li>
</ul>

Nance. (page does not exist)">John J. Nance.</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-72"><a href="#cite_note-72">[70]</a></sup></li>
<li><b>Hurricane Simone</b>: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saffir-Simpson_Hurricane_Scale#Category_4" title="Saffir-Simpson Hurricane Scale">Category Four</a> hurricane that strikes <a href="/wiki/Florida" title="Florida">Florida</a> south of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sanibel_island" title="Sanibel island">Sanibel island</a> as a category three storm, triggering a chain of events that lead to a bloody shootout at an illegal geophysical laboratory in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Florida_Everglades" title="Florida Everglades">Florida Everglades</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-73"><a href="#cite_note-73">[71]</a></sup></li>

<li><b>(Various)</b>: <i>Hurricane Punch</i>, a comedic thriller by <a href="/wiki/Tim_Dorsey" title="Tim Dorsey">Tim Dorsey</a> in which a misanthropic serial killer and his bumbling partner-in-crime take an impromptu storm-chasing trek across <a href="/wiki/Florida" title="Florida">Florida</a> with a kidnapped journalist in tow.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-75"><a href="#cite_note-75">[73]</a></sup></li>

<li><b><a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Katrina" title="Hurricane Katrina">Hurricane Katrina (2005)</a></b>: <i><a href="/wiki/K-Ville_(TV_series)" title="K-Ville (TV series)">K-Ville</a></i> is an <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">American</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Television_drama" title="Television drama">television drama</a> centered on policing <a href="/wiki/New_Orleans" title="New Orleans">New Orleans</a> after the hurricane.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-102"><a href="#cite_note-102">[100]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Theatrical_films_2">Theatrical films</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tropical_cyclones_in_popular_culture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Theatrical films">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<ul>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/1991_Perfect_Storm" title="1991 Perfect Storm">1991 Perfect Storm</a></b>: The film adaptation of <a href="/wiki/Sebastian_Junger" title="Sebastian Junger">Sebastian Junger</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/The_Perfect_Storm_(film)" title="The Perfect Storm (film)">The Perfect Storm</a></i> is about the crew of the <a href="/wiki/Andrea_Gail" title="Andrea Gail">Andrea Gail</a> during the storm.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-103"><a href="#cite_note-103">[101]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Music_2">Music</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tropical_cyclones_in_popular_culture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Music">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<ul>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Cyclone_Tracy" title="Cyclone Tracy">Cyclone Tracy (1974)</a></b>: <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Bill_Cate&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Bill Cate (page does not exist)">Bill Cate</a>'s charity song "Santa Never made it into Darwin", in 1974 to raise money for the relief after the disaster.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-104"><a href="#cite_note-104">[102]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-105"><a href="#cite_note-105">[103]</a></sup></li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Cyclone_Tracy" title="Cyclone Tracy">Cyclone Tracy (1974)</a></b>: <a href="/wiki/Hoodoo_Gurus" title="Hoodoo Gurus">Hoodoo Gurus</a>' song "Tojo", from the 1984 album <a href="/wiki/Stoneage_Romeos" title="Stoneage Romeos">Stoneage Romeos</a>, personifying Tracy as callously justifying hitting <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Darwin_(city)" title="Darwin (city)">Darwin</a> by the fact that the city was spared from Japanese invasion during <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-106"><a href="#cite_note-106">[104]</a></sup></li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Katrina" title="Hurricane Katrina">Hurricane Katrina (2005)</a></b>: <a href="/wiki/Jimmy_Buffett" title="Jimmy Buffett">Jimmy Buffett</a>'s song <i><a href="/wiki/Take_the_Weather_with_You" title="Take the Weather with You">Breathe In, Breathe Out, Move On</a></i> describes the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Effect_of_Hurricane_Katrina_on_New_Orleans" title="Effect of Hurricane Katrina on New Orleans">effect of Hurricane Katrina on New Orleans</a> and the need to move on after a disaster.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-107"><a href="#cite_note-107">[105]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Green_Day" title="Green Day">Green Day</a> and <a href="/wiki/U2" title="U2">U2</a>'s charity song, "<b><a href="/wiki/The_Saints_Are_Coming" title="The Saints Are Coming">The Saints Are Coming</a></b>" in 2006 to raise money for the relief after the disaster. This song was originally sung by <b><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Skids" title="The Skids">The Skids</a></b>, who first recorded the song in 1978.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-108"><a href="#cite_note-108">[106]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Works_in_which_a_tropical_cyclone_is_a_key_event">Works in which a tropical cyclone is a key event</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tropical_cyclones_in_popular_culture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Works in which a tropical cyclone is a key event">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Theatrical_films_3">Theatrical films</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tropical_cyclones_in_popular_culture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Theatrical films">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<ul>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Great_New_England_Hurricane_of_1938" title="Great New England Hurricane of 1938">Great New England Hurricane of 1938</a>: In John M. Stahl's <a href="/wiki/When_Tomorrow_Comes_(film)" title="When Tomorrow Comes (film)">When Tomorrow Comes</a>, based on <a href="/wiki/James_M._Cain" title="James M. Cain">James M. Cain</a>'s idea for <i>The Root of His Evil</i> (called <i>The Modern Cinderella</i>, a name that was also used in some publicity material for Stahl's film) two lovers are trapped by a hurricane that causes major damage to the Northeast. The storm forces the two to stay the night together in a church, simultaneously ending a strike that <a href="/wiki/Irene_Dunne" title="Irene Dunne">Irene Dunne</a>'s character organized, bringing the lovers closer together, and prompting major conflicts with other characters. Although the storm is not named, the film was released in 1939, and the major hurricane that struck New England the year before would have been the storm most closely associated with it.</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Carmen" title="Hurricane Carmen">Hurricane Carmen (1974)</a></b>: A pivotal scene in <i><a href="/wiki/Forrest_Gump" title="Forrest Gump">Forrest Gump</a></i> occurs when Gump and his former commanding officer, Lieutenant Dan Taylor, ride out the storm in Gump's shrimping boat; having been the only such boat to remain at sea, theirs is the only one not wrecked by the storm, allowing an unwitting Gump to monopolize the shrimping industry and become a millionaire. During the storm, Lieutenant Dan - in an alcoholic depression since the loss of his legs in the Vietnam War - challenges God, who is embodied in the fury of the hurricane. After surviving the event, Lieutenant Dan finally makes peace with his fate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-109"><a href="#cite_note-109">[107]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Real_tropical_cyclones_impacting_popular_culture">Real tropical cyclones impacting popular culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tropical_cyclones_in_popular_culture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Real tropical cyclones impacting popular culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Iniki" title="Hurricane Iniki">Hurricane Iniki (1992)</a></b>: Filmmaker <a href="/wiki/Steven_Spielberg" title="Steven Spielberg">Steven Spielberg</a> and 130 cast and crew members of the movie <i><a href="/wiki/Jurassic_Park_(film)" title="Jurassic Park (film)">Jurassic Park</a></i>, were preparing for the final day of on-location shooting on the Hawaiian Island of <a href="/wiki/Kauai" title="Kauai">Kaua<span class="unicode">ʻ</span>i</a> when this storm hit. They safely rode out the hurricane in a hotel.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-spielberg_110-0"><a href="#cite_note-spielberg-110">[108]</a></sup></li>
<li><b><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hurricane_Katrina_(2005)" title="Hurricane Katrina (2005)">Hurricane Katrina (2005)</a></b>: The syndicated game show <i><a href="/wiki/Wheel_of_Fortune_(U.S._game_show)" title="Wheel of Fortune (U.S. game show)">Wheel of Fortune</a></i> was scheduled to tape three weeks of episodes in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/New_Orleans,_Louisiana" title="New Orleans, Louisiana">New Orleans</a> in August 2005 for broadcast during November sweeps. However, before they could tape week three (Family Week), Katrina barreled closer to New Orleans, and the third taping had to be cancelled so that the cast, staff and crew could strike the set and evacuate the area. The Family Week shows were eventually broadcast in May 2006 after being taped in <a href="/wiki/Culver_City,_California" title="Culver City, California">Culver City</a>. <a href="/wiki/Southwest_Airlines" title="Southwest Airlines">Southwest Airlines</a> sponsored the week of shows affected by Katrina, flying in the affected family teams originally scheduled to participate in New Orleans. Host <a href="/wiki/Pat_Sajak" title="Pat Sajak">Pat Sajak</a> received an award from the <a href="/wiki/American_Red_Cross" title="American Red Cross">American Red Cross</a> for his contributions to Katrina relief efforts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-111"><a href="#cite_note-111">[109]</a></sup></li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Wilma" title="Hurricane Wilma">Hurricane Wilma (2005)</a></b>: Wilma struck the Bahamas during the filming of <i><a href="/wiki/Pirates_of_the_Caribbean:_Dead_Man%27s_Chest" title="Pirates of the Caribbean: Dead Man's Chest">Pirates of the Caribbean: Dead Man's Chest</a></i>. The service roads were destroyed and several trailers turned over. The two principal ships, the <i><a href="/wiki/Black_Pearl" title="Black Pearl">Black Pearl</a></i> and <a href="/wiki/Flying_Dutchman_(Pirates_of_the_Caribbean)" title="Flying Dutchman (Pirates of the Caribbean)"><i>The Flying Dutchman</i></a>, were relatively undamaged and the cast and crew were evacuated on the Friday before the hurricane hit.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-112"><a href="#cite_note-112">[110]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
