<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Almaz</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>

<p>The <b>Almaz</b> (<a href="/wiki/Russian_language" title="Russian language">Russian</a>: <span lang="ru" xml:lang="ru">Алмаз</span>, "Diamond") program was a highly secretive <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet</a> military <a href="/wiki/Space_station" title="Space station">space station</a> program, begun in the early 1960s.</p>
<p>Three crewed military <a href="/wiki/Reconnaissance" title="Reconnaissance">reconnaissance</a> stations were launched between 1973 and 1976: <a href="/wiki/Salyut_2" title="Salyut 2">Salyut 2</a>, <a href="/wiki/Salyut_3" title="Salyut 3">Salyut 3</a> and <a href="/wiki/Salyut_5" title="Salyut 5">Salyut 5</a>. To cover the military nature of the program the three launched Almaz stations were designated as civilian <a href="/wiki/Salyut_programme" title="Salyut programme">Salyut space stations</a>. Salyut 2 failed shortly after achieving orbit, but Salyut 3 and Salyut 5 both conducted successful manned testing. Following Salyut 5, the Soviet Ministry of Defence judged in 1978 that the time consumed by station maintenance outweighed the benefits relative to automatic <a href="/wiki/Reconnaissance_satellite" title="Reconnaissance satellite">reconnaissance satellites</a>.</p>
<p>The space stations cores were known internally as OPS (<a href="/wiki/Russian_language" title="Russian language">Russian</a>: <span lang="ru" xml:lang="ru">ОПС</span>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/GRAU_index" title="GRAU index">GRAU index</a> 11F71 and 11F71B), from "Orbital Piloted Station" (<a href="/wiki/Russian_language" title="Russian language">Russian</a>: <span lang="ru" xml:lang="ru">Орбитальная Пилотируемая Станция</span>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RSWops_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-RSWops-1">[1]</a></sup> As part of the Almaz program, the Soviets developed several spacecraft for support roles—the <a href="/wiki/VA_spacecraft" title="VA spacecraft">VA spacecraft</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Functional_Cargo_Block" title="Functional Cargo Block">Functional Cargo Block</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/TKS_spacecraft" title="TKS spacecraft">TKS spacecraft</a>—which they planned to use in several combinations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RSWtks_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-RSWtks-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EAapos_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-EAapos-3">[3]</a></sup> The heritage of the Almaz program continues, with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/ISS" title="ISS">ISS</a> module <a href="/wiki/Zarya" title="Zarya">Zarya</a> being one example.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Development">Development</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Almaz&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Development">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vladimir_Chelomei" title="Vladimir Chelomei">Vladimir Chelomei</a> at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/OKB-52" title="OKB-52">OKB-52</a> design bureau promoted Almaz as a response to the US Air Force's <a href="/wiki/Manned_Orbiting_Laboratory" title="Manned Orbiting Laboratory">Manned Orbiting Laboratory</a> (MOL) project. MOL had been widely publicized in the US press in the early 1960s, which provided Chelomei plenty of material to use to lobby for a Soviet response.</p>
<p>The Almaz space station programme involved three major hardware components:</p>
<ul>
<li>Orbital Piloted Station (OPS, 11F71) module, forming the space station itself,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RSWops_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-RSWops-1">[1]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Functional_Cargo_Block" title="Functional Cargo Block">Functional Cargo Block</a> (FGB, 11F77), intended as resupply craft for the stations,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RSWtks_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-RSWtks-2">[2]</a></sup> and</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/VA_spacecraft" title="VA spacecraft">VA spacecraft</a> (11F74, known in the West as the <i>Merkur</i> spacecraft), intended as launch and return vehicle for the crews,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RSWtks_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-RSWtks-2">[2]</a></sup> and reusable for up to 10 flights.</li>
</ul>
<p>The OPS would have a maximum diameter of 4.15 metres (13.6 ft), a mass of roughly 20 tonnes (20 long tons; 22 short tons), and an internal habitable volume of 47.5 cubic metres (1,680 cu ft).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RSWops_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-RSWops-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Much like its <a href="/wiki/Manned_Orbiting_Laboratory" title="Manned Orbiting Laboratory">MOL</a>/<a href="/wiki/Project_Gemini" title="Project Gemini">Gemini</a> counterpart, the initial Almaz APOS space station design called for the launch of an Almaz-OPS space station and a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/VA_capsule" title="VA capsule">VA return capsule</a> containing its initial three man crew, mated together as OPS/VA atop Chelomei's UR-500 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Proton_rocket" title="Proton rocket">Proton rocket</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EAapos_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-EAapos-3">[3]</a></sup> As with MOL/Gemini, once in orbit the crew would access the lab through a hatch in the heat shield at the bottom of the VA capsule. After an extended stay of 30 to 60 days of military observation and photography the crew would return to Earth by way of a VA return vehicle.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RSWorigin_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-RSWorigin-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EAtks_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-EAtks-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>Unlike the American MOL design, the Soviets designed the Almaz to be recrewed and resupplied. For this, they created the <a href="/wiki/TKS_(spacecraft)" title="TKS (spacecraft)">TKS resupply craft</a> (11F72), which consisted of an FGB and a <a href="/wiki/VA_spacecraft" title="VA spacecraft">VA return craft</a> to carry the crew, also launched together on a Proton rocket. At the station, one <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spacecraft_docking" title="Spacecraft docking">docking port</a> would be available to receive the TKS craft once the previous crew had left the station in their VA capsule.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RSWtks_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-RSWtks-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>While the MOL was canceled in 1969, the Almaz program was integrated into the <a href="/wiki/Salyut_programme" title="Salyut programme">Salyut programme</a> and resulted in three flown space stations, two of which were crewed successfully. As "man-rating" the VA spacecraft and the Proton rocket took longer, the first phase called for the launch of three Almaz stations without the VA spacecraft, with the crew instead launched separately by Soyuz rocket in a modified Soyuz spacecraft. Plans called for the first three Almaz stations to be visited by three two-month-long expeditions each. This was realized fully by two missions and partially by one; however, the initial intention of launching Almaz APOS and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/TKS_spacecraft" title="TKS spacecraft">TKS spacecraft</a> together with its crew in <a href="/wiki/VA_spacecraft" title="VA spacecraft">VA spacecraft</a> would never materialize during the program, and neither would the TKS craft play its intended role as resupply craft. The Almaz APOS design, without VA spacecraft, would evolve into the Almaz OPS station cores of the Salyut programme.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RSWorigin_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-RSWorigin-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RSWhist_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-RSWhist-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Defense_measures">Defense measures</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Almaz&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Defense measures">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In addition to reconnaissance equipment, Almaz was equipped with a unique <a href="/wiki/Rikhter_R-23" title="Rikhter R-23">23mm Rikhter</a> (factory index 261P or 225P) rapid-fire cannon mounted on the forward belly of the station. This revolver cannon was modified from the tail-gun of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tu-22" title="Tu-22">Tu-22 bomber</a> and was capable of a theoretical rate of fire of 1800-2000 (up to 2600) rounds per minute. Each 168 gram (ammo 23-OFZ-D-R ) or 173 gram (ammo 23-OFZ-G-R) projectile flew at a speed of 850 m/s relative to the station. The cannon had a supply of 32 rounds and was tested at the end of the mission, when the station was operating in unmanned mode. To aim the cannon, which was on a fixed mounting, the entire station would be turned to face the threat.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> The Almaz series remain, to this day, the only armed, crewed military spacecraft ever flown.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Salyut_3" title="Salyut 3">Salyut 3</a>/OPS-2 conducted a successful remote test firing with the station unmanned due to concerns over excessive vibration and noise.</p>
<p>OPS-4 was to have featured two rockets instead of the aircraft cannon, but this system has not been shown publicly and may have never been fully manufactured despite it being used experimentally.</p>
<h2><span id="Orbital_Piloted_Stations_(OPS)"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Orbital_Piloted_Stations_.28OPS.29">Orbital Piloted Stations (OPS)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Almaz&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Orbital Piloted Stations (OPS)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Three Almaz OPS space stations were flown from 1973 to 1976 in the <a href="/wiki/Salyut_programme" title="Salyut programme">Salyut programme</a>: <a href="/wiki/Salyut_2" title="Salyut 2">Salyut 2</a> (OPS-1), <a href="/wiki/Salyut_3" title="Salyut 3">Salyut 3</a> (OPS-2) and <a href="/wiki/Salyut_5" title="Salyut 5">Salyut 5</a> (OPS-3). Five crewed Soyuz expeditions were flown to the Almaz space stations Salyut 3 and Salyut 5, with three reaching their stations and only two of the missions being considered fully successful at that time – the three crews that had reached their stations had manned Almaz stations for a total of 81 days when the program was ended.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RSWhist_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-RSWhist-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-astrospies_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-astrospies-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>Besides the three flown space stations OPS-1 to OPS-3, seven more spaceframes of Almaz space stations had been built when the program was cancelled: <a href="/wiki/OPS-4" title="OPS-4">OPS-4</a>, Almaz-205, Almaz-206, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Almaz-T&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Almaz-T (page does not exist)">Almaz-T</a>, Almaz-T2 (<a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Kosmos_1870&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Kosmos 1870 (page does not exist)">Kosmos 1870</a>), <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Almaz-1&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Almaz-1 (page does not exist)">Almaz-1</a> and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Almaz-2&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Almaz-2 (page does not exist)">Almaz-2</a> – with Almaz-T2 and Almaz-1 having successfully flown as repurposed unmanned radar-carrying reconnaissance satellites (see below). The partially outfitted hulls of Almaz-205 and Almaz-206 are today in the property of Excalibur Almaz, a company that plans to launch these as manned space stations (see below).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RSWprog_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-RSWprog-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Flown_Almaz_space_stations">Flown Almaz space stations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Almaz&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Flown Almaz space stations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Salyut_programme" title="Salyut programme">Salyut programme</a></div>
<h4><span id="OPS-1_(Salyut_2)"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="OPS-1_.28Salyut_2.29">OPS-1 (Salyut 2)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Almaz&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: OPS-1 (Salyut 2)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Salyut_2" title="Salyut 2">Salyut 2</a></div>
<p>The first Almaz station (OPS-1 or Almaz 101.1), announced as <a href="/wiki/Salyut_2" title="Salyut 2">Salyut 2</a>, it was launched on April 3, 1973. For purposes of military secrecy, it was publicly designated <a href="/wiki/Salyut_2" title="Salyut 2">Salyut 2</a> upon reaching orbit. A crew was prepared to fly to the station but an accident days after the launch left OPS-1 disabled and depressurized.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span id="OPS-2_(Salyut_3)"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="OPS-2_.28Salyut_3.29">OPS-2 (Salyut 3)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Almaz&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: OPS-2 (Salyut 3)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Salyut_3" title="Salyut 3">Salyut 3</a></div>
<p>OPS-2 (or Almaz 101.2), announced as <a href="/wiki/Salyut_3" title="Salyut 3">Salyut 3</a>, was launched on June 25, 1974. The crew of the <a href="/wiki/Soyuz_14" title="Soyuz 14">Soyuz 14</a> spacecraft spent 15 days aboard the station in July 1974. A second expedition was launched toward OPS-2 in August 1974, but failed to reach the station. The station successfully remotely test-fired an onboard <a href="/wiki/Aircraft" title="Aircraft">aircraft</a> <a href="/wiki/Autocannon" title="Autocannon">cannon</a> at a target satellite while the station was unmanned. Salyut-3 was deorbited in January 1975.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-astrospies_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-astrospies-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span id="OPS-3_(Salyut_5)"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="OPS-3_.28Salyut_5.29">OPS-3 (Salyut 5)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Almaz&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: OPS-3 (Salyut 5)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Salyut_5" title="Salyut 5">Salyut 5</a></div>
<p>OPS-3 (or Almaz 103), announced after launch as <a href="/wiki/Salyut_5" title="Salyut 5">Salyut 5</a>, entered orbit on June 22, 1976. It was visited by two crews in mid-1976 and late 1977. Salyut 5 was deorbited on 8 August 1977, and broke up as it reentered the Earth's atmosphere.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EA-S5_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-EA-S5-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Unflown_Almaz_space_stations">Unflown Almaz space stations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Almaz&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Unflown Almaz space stations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="OPS-4">OPS-4</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Almaz&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: OPS-4">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/OPS-4" title="OPS-4">OPS-4</a></div>
<p>The next Almaz station, <a href="/wiki/OPS-4" title="OPS-4">OPS-4</a>, was to be the first station launched with a three panel <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Mech-A&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Mech-A (page does not exist)">Mech-A</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Synthetic_Aperture_Radar" title="Synthetic Aperture Radar">Synthetic Aperture Radar</a> and a manned reusable Return Vehicle <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/TKS_Spacecraft" title="TKS Spacecraft">VA</a>, however the VA was replaced by a second TKS docking port. This station's <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Shchit-1&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Shchit-1 (page does not exist)">Shchit-1</a> 23 mm defense <a href="/wiki/Autocannon" title="Autocannon">cannon</a> was also to be replaced with an advanced Shchit-2 <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Space-to-space&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Space-to-space (page does not exist)">space-to-space</a> cannon. The Shchit-2 was reported to be a two projectile system, although no photographs of it have ever been published and it does not appear that this system was ever installed on the station. OPS-4 was grounded when the Almaz manned program was cancelled.</p>
<h2><span id="Almaz-T_(unmanned)"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Almaz-T_.28unmanned.29">Almaz-T (unmanned)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Almaz&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Almaz-T (unmanned)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Almaz_radar_satellite.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="155" data-file-width="332" height="103" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/06/Almaz_radar_satellite.svg/220px-Almaz_radar_satellite.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/06/Almaz_radar_satellite.svg/330px-Almaz_radar_satellite.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/06/Almaz_radar_satellite.svg/440px-Almaz_radar_satellite.svg.png 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Almaz_radar_satellite.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Almaz radar satellite (based on Almaz space station).</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Following cancellation of the program, the Almaz station was reconfigured as an unmanned heavy radar-carrying <a href="/wiki/Reconnaissance_satellite" title="Reconnaissance satellite">reconnaissance satellite</a>. Three such satellites were launched, two of which functioned successfully in orbit.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Almaz-T">Almaz-T</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Almaz&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Almaz-T">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><b>Almaz-T</b> - The first Almaz-T blasted off from Baikonur on October 29, 1986. It did not reach orbit due to the failure of the first and second stages of the Proton launcher to separate. The safety system then destroyed the vehicle.</p>
<h3><span id="Almaz-T2_(Kosmos_1870)"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Almaz-T2_.28Kosmos_1870.29">Almaz-T2 (Kosmos 1870)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Almaz&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Almaz-T2 (Kosmos 1870)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><b><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Kosmos_1870&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Kosmos 1870 (page does not exist)">Kosmos 1870</a></b> - On July 25, 1987, Almaz-T2, the second Almaz-T spacecraft, successfully reached orbit with an inclination 71.92 degrees toward the Equator and it was officially identified as Kosmos-1870. The spacecraft functioned for two years, providing <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Radar_imagery" title="Radar imagery">radar imagery</a> with a resolution down to 25 meters, until it was deorbited on July 30, 1989.</p>
<p>It was the first commercial radar satellite, according to Art Dula (chairman of <a href="/wiki/Excalibur_Almaz" title="Excalibur Almaz">Excalibur Almaz</a>), who worked in a company marketing the radar images gathered by the satellite.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mbEA1_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-mbEA1-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Almaz-1">Almaz-1</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Almaz&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Almaz-1">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><b><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Almaz-1&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Almaz-1 (page does not exist)">Almaz-1</a></b> - The third Almaz-T spacecraft was launched on March 31, 1991 under the name Almaz-1. After the launch a failure of the communications antenna designed to downlink the imagery via the <a href="/wiki/Luch_(satellite)" title="Luch (satellite)">Luch</a> relay satellite was noted. Also one of the solar panels failed to deploy completely, leaving the main radar panel of the spacecraft partially blocked. After 18 months of successful work the Almaz-1 was deorbited on October 17, 1992 over the Pacific Ocean.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (March 2014)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Almaz-2">Almaz-2</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Almaz&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Almaz-2">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><b><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Almaz-2&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Almaz-2 (page does not exist)">Almaz-2</a> (Almaz-1V)</b> - Not flown. It had a new radar that would have provided a resolution of 5 to 7 meters. In addition, an optical-electronic payload on the station would have been capable of producing imagery with a resolution of 2.5 – 4 meters.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Heritage">Heritage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Almaz&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Heritage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The heritage of the Almaz space station program continues until today, and can even be found today in the <a href="/wiki/International_Space_Station" title="International Space Station">International Space Station</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="DOS_space_station_cores">DOS space station cores</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Almaz&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: DOS space station cores">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Salyut_programme" title="Salyut programme">Salyut programme</a></div>
<p>The DOS space station core modules were based on the Almaz-OPS hull design, which was mated by <a href="/wiki/Sergei_Korolev" title="Sergei Korolev">Sergei Korolev</a>'s organization <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/OKB-1" title="OKB-1">OKB-1</a> with their own Soyuz-derived subsystems. OKB-1 was at that time in competition with the designer of the Almaz, Vladimir Chelomei's organization OKB-52, and was thereby able to short-cut the development time for the first space station and beat OKB-52, which had started design work much earlier.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>DOS space station cores derived since 1971 from the Almaz-OPS hull design include:</p>
<ul>
<li>DOS-1, which formed the basis for <a href="/wiki/Salyut_1" title="Salyut 1">Salyut 1</a> space station, in 1971 the first space station in the history of space flight</li>
<li>DOS-5 and DOS-6, the cores for the long lived <a href="/wiki/Salyut_6" title="Salyut 6">Salyut 6</a> and <a href="/wiki/Salyut_7" title="Salyut 7">Salyut 7</a> space stations</li>
<li>DOS-7, the <a href="/wiki/Mir_Core_Module" title="Mir Core Module">Mir Core Module</a> for the <a href="/wiki/Mir" title="Mir">Mir space station</a>, the first modular space station</li>
<li>DOS-8, the <a href="/wiki/Zvezda_(ISS_module)" title="Zvezda (ISS module)">Zvezda "Service Module"</a> for the <a href="/wiki/International_Space_Station" title="International Space Station">International Space Station</a>, as of 2012<sup class="plainlinks noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Almaz&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup> still in use</li>
</ul>
<h3><span id="Functional_Cargo_Block_(FGB)"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Functional_Cargo_Block_.28FGB.29">Functional Cargo Block (FGB)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Almaz&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Functional Cargo Block (FGB)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Functional_Cargo_Block" title="Functional Cargo Block">Functional Cargo Block</a></div>

<p>The modules based on the DOS design are not the only heritage of the Almaz program still in use: The habitat, propulsion and service module of the TKS spacecraft, the so-called Functional Cargo Block (FGB), went on to become the core of many Soviet and Russian space station modules.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RSWtks_2-4"><a href="#cite_note-RSWtks-2">[2]</a></sup> The FGB-based <a href="/wiki/Kvant-1" title="Kvant-1">Kvant-1</a> module of the Mir space station was the first space station module of its kind, and the <a href="/wiki/Zarya" title="Zarya">Zarya</a> Functional Cargo Block, which is as of 2017<sup class="plainlinks noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Almaz&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup> still in use on the <a href="/wiki/International_Space_Station" title="International Space Station">International Space Station</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Excalibur_Almaz">Excalibur Almaz</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Almaz&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Excalibur Almaz">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Excalibur_Almaz" title="Excalibur Almaz">Excalibur Almaz</a></div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Private_spaceflight" title="Private spaceflight">private spaceflight</a> company <a href="/wiki/Excalibur_Almaz" title="Excalibur Almaz">Excalibur Almaz</a> has bought the two partially completed Almaz-205 and Almaz-206 space station hulls from the Russian <a href="/wiki/NPO_Mashinostroyeniya" title="NPO Mashinostroyeniya">NPO Mashinostroyeniya</a> (the former OKB-52) with the stated intention to outfit and launch them.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RSWex_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-RSWex-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> The Almaz-205 module is similar to the OPS-2 of the Salyut 3 station, while the Almaz-206 is closer to the OPS-3 of the Salyut 5 station.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>In addition, Excalibur Almaz acquired four VA return capsule hulls (derived from the TKS/VA spacecraft) and plans to outfit and launch them as well: one is planned to be used in support of <a href="/wiki/Space_tourism" title="Space tourism">space tourism</a> while the other three capsules are reserved for scientific and commercial payloads. The needed development of propulsion systems for the VA capsule was reportedly delegated to an unnamed European organization as early as 2009.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RSWex_17-1"><a href="#cite_note-RSWex-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>Excalibur Almaz has as of January 2012<sup class="plainlinks noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Almaz&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup> postponed its first launch to 2015 to be able to include more lucrative deep space capabilities like <a href="/wiki/Asteroid_mining" title="Asteroid mining">asteroid mining</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WSJ120620_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-WSJ120620-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
