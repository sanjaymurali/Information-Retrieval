<p>An <b>Atlantic hurricane</b> or <b>tropical storm</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone" title="Tropical cyclone">tropical cyclone</a> that forms in the <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Ocean" title="Atlantic Ocean">Atlantic Ocean</a>, usually in the summer or fall. A hurricane differs from a <a href="/wiki/Cyclone" title="Cyclone">cyclone</a> or <a href="/wiki/Typhoon" title="Typhoon">typhoon</a> only on the basis of location.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HCT_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-HCT-1">[1]</a></sup> A hurricane is a <a href="/wiki/Storm" title="Storm">storm</a> that occurs in the <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Ocean" title="Atlantic Ocean">Atlantic Ocean</a> and northeastern <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Ocean" title="Pacific Ocean">Pacific Ocean</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Typhoon" title="Typhoon">typhoon</a> occurs in the northwestern Pacific Ocean, and a <a href="/wiki/Cyclone" title="Cyclone">cyclone</a> occurs in the south Pacific or <a href="/wiki/Indian_Ocean" title="Indian Ocean">Indian Ocean</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HCT_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-HCT-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Tropical cyclones can be categorized by intensity. <i>Tropical storms</i> have one-minute maximum sustained winds of at least 39 mph (34 knots, 17 m/s, 63 km/h), while <i>hurricanes</i> have one-minute maximum sustained winds exceeding 74 mph (64 knots, 33 m/s, 119 km/h).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> Most North Atlantic <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone" title="Tropical cyclone">tropical storms</a> and hurricanes form between June 1 and November 30.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> <a href="/wiki/National_Hurricane_Center" title="National Hurricane Center">National Hurricane Center</a> monitors the basin and issues reports, watches, and warnings about <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tropical" title="Tropical">tropical</a> weather systems for the North Atlantic Basin as one of the Regional Specialized Meteorological Centers for <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone" title="Tropical cyclone">tropical cyclones</a>, as defined by the <a href="/wiki/World_Meteorological_Organization" title="World Meteorological Organization">World Meteorological Organization</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WMO_RSMC_list_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-WMO_RSMC_list-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>In recent times, tropical disturbances that reach tropical storm intensity are named from a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lists_of_tropical_cyclone_names" title="Lists of tropical cyclone names">predetermined list</a>. Hurricanes that result in significant damage or casualties may <a href="/wiki/List_of_retired_Atlantic_hurricane_names" title="List of retired Atlantic hurricane names">have their names retired from the list</a> at the request of the affected nations in order to prevent confusion should a subsequent storm be given the same name.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> On average, in the North Atlantic basin (from 1966 to 2009) 11.3 named storms occur each season, with an average of 6.2 becoming hurricanes and 2.3 becoming major hurricanes (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saffir-Simpson_Hurricane_Scale#Category_3" title="Saffir-Simpson Hurricane Scale">Category 3</a> or greater).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Climatology" title="Climatology">climatological</a> peak of activity is around September 11 each season.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NHCTCclimo_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-NHCTCclimo-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>In March 2004, <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Catarina" title="Hurricane Catarina">Catarina</a> was the first hurricane-intensity tropical cyclone ever recorded in the Southern Atlantic Ocean. Since 2011, the <a href="/wiki/Brazilian_Navy" title="Brazilian Navy">Brazilian Navy Hydrographic Center</a> has started to use the same scale of the North Atlantic Ocean for tropical cyclones in the South Atlantic Ocean and assign names to those which reach 35 kn (65 km/h; 40 mph).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Steering_factors">Steering factors</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Atlantic_hurricane&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Steering factors">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Subtropicalridge2000091412.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="282" data-file-width="622" height="100" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8e/Subtropicalridge2000091412.jpg/220px-Subtropicalridge2000091412.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8e/Subtropicalridge2000091412.jpg/330px-Subtropicalridge2000091412.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8e/Subtropicalridge2000091412.jpg/440px-Subtropicalridge2000091412.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Subtropicalridge2000091412.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The subtropical ridge (in the Pacific) shows up as a large area of black (dryness) on this water vapor satellite image from September 2000</div>
</div>
</div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone" title="Tropical cyclone">Tropical cyclones</a> are steered by the surrounding flow throughout the depth of the <a href="/wiki/Troposphere" title="Troposphere">troposphere</a> (the atmosphere from the surface to about eight miles (12 km) high). <a href="/wiki/Neil_Frank" title="Neil Frank">Neil Frank</a>, former director of the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> <a href="/wiki/National_Hurricane_Center" title="National Hurricane Center">National Hurricane Center</a>, used the analogies such as "a leaf carried along in a stream" or a "brick moving through a river of air" to describe the way atmospheric flow affects the path of a hurricane across the ocean. Specifically, air flow around <a href="/wiki/High_pressure" title="High pressure">high pressure</a> systems and toward <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Low_pressure_area" title="Low pressure area">low pressure areas</a> influences hurricane tracks.</p>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/Tropics" title="Tropics">tropical latitudes</a>, tropical storms and hurricanes generally move westward with a slight tendency toward the north, under the influence of the <i><a href="/wiki/Subtropical_ridge" title="Subtropical ridge">subtropical ridge</a></i>, a high pressure system that usually extends east-west across the subtropics.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AOML_FAQ_G6_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-AOML_FAQ_G6-9">[9]</a></sup> South of the subtropical ridge, surface easterly winds (blowing from east to west) prevail. If the subtropical ridge is weakened by an upper <a href="/wiki/Trough_(meteorology)" title="Trough (meteorology)">trough</a>, a tropical cyclone may turn poleward and then <i>recurve</i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> or curve back toward the northeast into the main belt of the Westerlies. Poleward (north) of the subtropical ridge, westerly winds prevail and generally steer tropical cyclones that reach northern latitudes toward the east. The <a href="/wiki/Westerlies" title="Westerlies">westerlies</a> also steer <a href="/wiki/Extratropical_cyclone" title="Extratropical cyclone">extratropical cyclones</a> with their cold and warm fronts from west to east.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>

<p>Generally speaking, the intensity of a tropical cyclone is determined by either the storm's <a href="/wiki/Maximum_sustained_wind" title="Maximum sustained wind">maximum sustained winds</a> or lowest <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Barometric_pressure" title="Barometric pressure">barometric pressure</a>. The following table lists the most intense Atlantic hurricanes in terms of their lowest barometric pressure. In terms of wind speed, <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Allen" title="Hurricane Allen">Hurricane Allen</a> (in <a href="/wiki/1980_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="1980 Atlantic hurricane season">1980</a>) was the strongest Atlantic tropical cyclone on record, with maximum sustained winds of 190 mph (310 km/h). However, these measurements are suspect since instrumentation used to document wind speeds at the time would likely succumb to winds of such intensity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MostIntense_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-MostIntense-13">[13]</a></sup> Nonetheless, their central pressures are low enough to rank them among the strongest recorded Atlantic hurricanes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HURDAT_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-HURDAT-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>Owing to their intensity, the strongest Atlantic hurricanes have all attained Category 5 classification. <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Opal" title="Hurricane Opal">Hurricane Opal</a>, the strongest Category 4 hurricane recorded, intensified to reach a minimum pressure of 916 mbar (hPa; 27.05 inHg),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OpalTCR_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-OpalTCR-14">[14]</a></sup> a pressure typical of Category 5 hurricanes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SSHS-LSU_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-SSHS-LSU-15">[15]</a></sup> Nonetheless, the pressure remains too high to list Opal as one of the ten strongest Atlantic tropical cyclones.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HURDAT_12-2"><a href="#cite_note-HURDAT-12">[12]</a></sup> Presently, <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Wilma" title="Hurricane Wilma">Hurricane Wilma</a> is the strongest Atlantic hurricane ever recorded, after reaching an intensity of 882 mbar (hPa; 26.05 inHg) in October 2005;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MostIntense_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-MostIntense-13">[13]</a></sup> this also made Wilma the strongest tropical cyclone worldwide outside of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pacific_typhoon" title="Pacific typhoon">West Pacific</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EPAC_HURDAT_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-EPAC_HURDAT-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NIO_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-NIO-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gafilo_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-Gafilo-18">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BOM_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-BOM-19">[19]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wellington_BT_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-Wellington_BT-20">[20]</a></sup> where seven tropical cyclones have been recorded to intensify to lower pressures.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WPACBT_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-WPACBT-21">[21]</a></sup> However, this was later superseded by <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Patricia" title="Hurricane Patricia">Hurricane Patricia</a> in <a href="/wiki/2015_Pacific_hurricane_season" title="2015 Pacific hurricane season">2015</a> in the east Pacific, which had a pressure reading of 872 mbar. Preceding Wilma is <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Gilbert" title="Hurricane Gilbert">Hurricane Gilbert</a>, which had also held the record for most intense Atlantic hurricane for 17 years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MWRGilbert_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-MWRGilbert-22">[22]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/1935_Labor_Day_hurricane" title="1935 Labor Day hurricane">1935 Labor Day hurricane</a>, with a pressure of 892 mbar (hPa; 26.34 inHg), is the third strongest Atlantic hurricane and the strongest documented tropical cyclone prior to 1950.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HURDAT_12-3"><a href="#cite_note-HURDAT-12">[12]</a></sup> Since the measurements taken during Wilma and Gilbert were documented using <a href="/wiki/Dropsonde" title="Dropsonde">dropsonde</a>, this pressure remains the lowest measured over land.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DeanTCR_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-DeanTCR-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Rita" title="Hurricane Rita">Hurricane Rita</a> is the fourth strongest Atlantic hurricane in terms of barometric pressure and one of three tropical cyclones from 2005 on the list, with the others being Wilma and <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Katrina" title="Hurricane Katrina">Katrina</a> at first and sixth, respectively.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HURDAT_12-4"><a href="#cite_note-HURDAT-12">[12]</a></sup> However, with a barometric pressure of 895 mbar (hPa; 26.43 inHg), Rita is the strongest tropical cyclone ever recorded in the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_of_Mexico" title="Gulf of Mexico">Gulf of Mexico</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RitaReport_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-RitaReport-24">[24]</a></sup> Hurricanes Camille, <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Mitch" title="Hurricane Mitch">Mitch</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Dean" title="Hurricane Dean">Dean</a> share intensities for the seventh strongest Atlantic hurricane at 905 mbar (hPa; 26.73 inHg).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DeanTCR_23-1"><a href="#cite_note-DeanTCR-23">[23]</a></sup> Sharing tenth place for most intense Atlantic tropical cyclone are <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Ivan" title="Hurricane Ivan">Hurricane Ivan</a> and an unnamed hurricane from 1932, which both are listed to have deepened to a pressure as low as 910 mbar (hPa; 26.88 inHg).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HURDAT_12-5"><a href="#cite_note-HURDAT-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>Many of the strongest recorded tropical cyclones weakened prior to their eventual <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Landfall_(meteorology)" title="Landfall (meteorology)">landfall</a> or demise. However, three of the storms remained intense enough at landfall to be considered some of the strongest landfalling hurricanes – three of the eleven hurricanes on the list constitute the three most intense Atlantic landfalls in recorded history. The 1935 Labor Day hurricane made landfall at peak intensity, making it the most intense Atlantic landfall. Though it weakened slightly before its eventual landfall on the <a href="/wiki/Yucat%C3%A1n_Peninsula" title="Yucatán Peninsula">Yucatán Peninsula</a>, Hurricane Gilbert maintained a pressure of 900 mbar (hPa; 26.58 inHg) at landfall, making its landfall the second strongest. Similarly, Hurricane Dean made landfall on the peninsula, though it did so at peak intensity and with a higher barometric pressure; its landfall marked the third strongest in Atlantic hurricane history.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DeanTCR_23-2"><a href="#cite_note-DeanTCR-23">[23]</a></sup></p>

<p><a href="/wiki/Climatology" title="Climatology">Climatology</a> does serve to characterize the general properties of an average season and can be used as one of many other tools for making forecasts. Most storms form in warm waters several hundred miles north of the <a href="/wiki/Equator" title="Equator">equator</a> near the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Intertropical_convergence_zone" title="Intertropical convergence zone">Intertropical convergence zone</a> from <a href="/wiki/Tropical_wave" title="Tropical wave">tropical waves</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Coriolis_force" title="Coriolis force">Coriolis force</a> is usually too weak to initiate sufficient rotation near the equator.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AOML_FAQ_A15_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-AOML_FAQ_A15-26">[26]</a></sup> Storms frequently form in the warm waters of the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_of_Mexico" title="Gulf of Mexico">Gulf of Mexico</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Caribbean_Sea" title="Caribbean Sea">Caribbean Sea</a>, and the tropical <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Ocean" title="Atlantic Ocean">Atlantic Ocean</a> as far east as the <a href="/wiki/Cape_Verde" title="Cape Verde">Cape Verde</a> Islands, the origin of strong and long-lasting <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cape_Verde-type_hurricane" title="Cape Verde-type hurricane">Cape Verde-type hurricanes</a>. Systems may also strengthen over the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_Stream" title="Gulf Stream">Gulf Stream</a> off the coast of the eastern <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>, wherever water temperatures exceed 26.5 °C (79.7 °F).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AOML_FAQ_A15_26-1"><a href="#cite_note-AOML_FAQ_A15-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<p>Although most storms are found within tropical latitudes, occasionally storms will form further north and east from disturbances other than tropical waves such as <a href="/wiki/Cold_front" title="Cold front">cold fronts</a> and upper-level lows. These are known as baroclinically induced tropical cyclones.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> There is a strong correlation between Atlantic hurricane activity in the tropics and the presence of an <a href="/wiki/El_Ni%C3%B1o" title="El Niño">El Niño</a> or <a href="/wiki/La_Ni%C3%B1a" title="La Niña">La Niña</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Ocean" title="Pacific Ocean">Pacific Ocean</a>. El Niño events increase the wind shear over the Atlantic, producing a less-favorable environment for formation and decreasing tropical activity in the Atlantic basin. Conversely, La Niña causes an increase in activity due to a decrease in wind shear.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<p>According to the <a href="/wiki/Azores_High" title="Azores High">Azores High</a> hypothesis by <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Kam-biu_Liu&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Kam-biu Liu (page does not exist)">Kam-biu Liu</a>, an anti-phase pattern is expected to exist between the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_of_Mexico" title="Gulf of Mexico">Gulf of Mexico</a> coast and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atlantic_Seaboard" title="Atlantic Seaboard">North American Atlantic coast</a>. During the quiescent periods (3000–1400 BC, and 1000 AD to present), a more northeasterly position of the Azores High would result in more hurricanes being steered toward the Atlantic coast. During the hyperactive period (1400 BC to 1000 AD), more hurricanes were steered towards the Gulf coast as the Azores High was shifted to a more southwesterly position near the Caribbean.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Liu1999_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-Liu1999-29">[29]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LiuFearn2000_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-LiuFearn2000-30">[30]</a></sup> Such a displacement of the Azores High is consistent with paleoclimatic evidence that shows an abrupt onset of a drier climate in <a href="/wiki/Haiti" title="Haiti">Haiti</a> around 3200 <a href="/wiki/Radiocarbon_dating" title="Radiocarbon dating"><sup>14</sup>C</a> years BP,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Higuera-Gundy1999_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-Higuera-Gundy1999-31">[31]</a></sup> and a change towards more humid conditions in the <a href="/wiki/Great_Plains" title="Great Plains">Great Plains</a> during the late-<a href="/wiki/Holocene" title="Holocene">Holocene</a> as more moisture was pumped up the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mississippi_Valley" title="Mississippi Valley">Mississippi Valley</a> through the Gulf coast. Preliminary data from the northern Atlantic coast seem to support the Azores High hypothesis. A 3000-year proxy record from a coastal lake in <a href="/wiki/Cape_Cod" title="Cape Cod">Cape Cod</a> suggests that hurricane activity has increased significantly during the past 500–1000 years, just as the Gulf coast was amid a quiescent period of the last millennium.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Seasonal_variation">Seasonal variation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Atlantic_hurricane&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Seasonal variation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Climatologically speaking, approximately 97 percent of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclones" title="Tropical cyclones">tropical cyclones</a> that form in the North Atlantic <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclogenesis" title="Tropical cyclogenesis">develop</a> between the dates of June 1 and November 30 – dates which delimit the modern-day Atlantic hurricane season. Though the beginning of the annual hurricane season has historically remained the same, the official end of the hurricane season has shifted from its initial date of October 31. Regardless, on average once every few years a tropical cyclone develops <a href="/wiki/List_of_off-season_Atlantic_hurricanes" title="List of off-season Atlantic hurricanes">outside the limits of the season</a>;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Season_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-Season-32">[32]</a></sup> as of January 2016 there have been 66 tropical cyclones in the off-season, with the most recent being <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Alex_(2016)" title="Hurricane Alex (2016)">Hurricane Alex</a> in <a href="/wiki/2016_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="2016 Atlantic hurricane season">2016</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HURDAT_12-6"><a href="#cite_note-HURDAT-12">[12]</a></sup> The first tropical cyclone of the <a href="/wiki/1938_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="1938 Atlantic hurricane season">1938 Atlantic hurricane season</a>, which formed on January 3, became the earliest forming tropical storm and hurricane after <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_hurricane_reanalysis_project" title="Atlantic hurricane reanalysis project">reanalysis</a> concluded on the storm in December 2012.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Metadata_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-Metadata-33">[33]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Able_(1951)" title="Hurricane Able (1951)">Hurricane Able</a> in <a href="/wiki/1951_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="1951 Atlantic hurricane season">1951</a> was initially thought to be the earliest forming major hurricane – a tropical cyclone with winds exceeding 115 mph (185 km/h)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[nb 1]</a></sup> – however following post-storm analysis it was determined that Able only reached Category 1 strength which made <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Alma_(1966)" title="Hurricane Alma (1966)">Hurricane Alma</a> of <a href="/wiki/1966_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="1966 Atlantic hurricane season">1966</a> the new record holder; as it became a major hurricane on June 8.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HURDAT_12-7"><a href="#cite_note-HURDAT-12">[12]</a></sup> Though it developed within the bounds of the Atlantic hurricane season,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HURDAT_12-8"><a href="#cite_note-HURDAT-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Season_32-1"><a href="#cite_note-Season-32">[32]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Audrey" title="Hurricane Audrey">Hurricane Audrey</a> in <a href="/wiki/1957_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="1957 Atlantic hurricane season">1957</a> became the earliest developing <a href="/wiki/List_of_Category_4_Atlantic_hurricanes" title="List of Category 4 Atlantic hurricanes">Category 4 hurricane</a> on record after it reached the intensity on June 27.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Audrey_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-Audrey-36">[35]</a></sup> However, reanalysis from 1956 to 1960 by NOAA downgraded Audrey to a Category 3, making Hurricane Dennis of 2005 the earliest Category 4 on record on July 8, 2005.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[36]</a></sup> The earliest-forming <a href="/wiki/List_of_Category_5_Atlantic_hurricanes" title="List of Category 5 Atlantic hurricanes">Category 5 hurricane</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Emily_(2005)" title="Hurricane Emily (2005)">Emily</a>, reached the highest intensity on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saffir%E2%80%93Simpson_hurricane_wind_scale" title="Saffir–Simpson hurricane wind scale">Saffir–Simpson hurricane wind scale</a> on July 17, 2005.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EmilyTCR_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-EmilyTCR-38">[37]</a></sup></p>
<p>Though the official end of the Atlantic hurricane season occurs on November 30, the dates of October 31 and November 15 have also historically marked the official end date for the hurricane season.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Season_32-2"><a href="#cite_note-Season-32">[32]</a></sup> December, the only month of the year after the hurricane season, has featured the cyclogenesis of fourteen tropical cyclones.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HURDAT_12-9"><a href="#cite_note-HURDAT-12">[12]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Tropical_Storm_Zeta" title="Tropical Storm Zeta">Tropical Storm Zeta</a> in 2005 was the latest tropical cyclone to attain tropical storm intensity as it did so on December 30. However, the second <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Alice_(December_1954)" title="Hurricane Alice (December 1954)">Hurricane Alice</a> in <a href="/wiki/1954_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="1954 Atlantic hurricane season">1954</a> was the latest forming tropical cyclone to attain hurricane intensity. Both Zeta and Alice were the only two storms to exist in two calendar years – the former from 1954 to 1955 and the latter from 2005 to 2006.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AtlanticRecords_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-AtlanticRecords-39">[38]</a></sup> No storms have been recorded to exceed Category 1 hurricane intensity in December.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HURDAT_12-10"><a href="#cite_note-HURDAT-12">[12]</a></sup> In <a href="/wiki/1999_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="1999 Atlantic hurricane season">1999</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Lenny" title="Hurricane Lenny">Hurricane Lenny</a> reached Category 4 intensity on November 17 as it took an unprecedented west to east track across the Caribbean; its intensity made it the latest developing Category 4 hurricane, though this was well within the bounds of the hurricane season.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LateHurricanes_40-0"><a href="#cite_note-LateHurricanes-40">[39]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Hattie" title="Hurricane Hattie">Hurricane Hattie</a> (October 27-November 1, 1961) was initially thought to have been the latest forming Category 5 hurricane ever documented,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-C5Notables_41-0"><a href="#cite_note-C5Notables-41">[40]</a></sup> though reanalysis indicated that a <a href="/wiki/1932_Cuba_hurricane" title="1932 Cuba hurricane">devastating hurricane</a> in <a href="/wiki/1932_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="1932 Atlantic hurricane season">1932</a> reached such an intensity at a later date.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HURDAT_12-11"><a href="#cite_note-HURDAT-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Metadata_33-1"><a href="#cite_note-Metadata-33">[33]</a></sup> Consequently, this made the hurricane the latest developing tropical cyclone to reach all four Saffir–Simpson hurricane wind scale classifications past Category 1 intensity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HURDAT_12-12"><a href="#cite_note-HURDAT-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="June">June</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Atlantic_hurricane&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: June">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>

<p>The beginning of the hurricane season is most closely related to the timing of increases in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sea_surface_temperatures" title="Sea surface temperatures">sea surface temperatures</a>, <a href="/wiki/Convective_instability" title="Convective instability">convective instability</a>, and other thermodynamic factors.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GrayNov2005_42-0"><a href="#cite_note-GrayNov2005-42">[41]</a></sup> Although June marks the beginning of the hurricane season, generally little activity occurs during the month with an average of 1 <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone" title="Tropical cyclone">tropical cyclone</a> every 2 years. Tropical systems usually form in the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_of_Mexico" title="Gulf of Mexico">Gulf of Mexico</a> or off the east coast of the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NOAA_Book_212_43-0"><a href="#cite_note-NOAA_Book_212-43">[42]</a></sup></p>
<p>Since 1851, a total of 81 tropical storms and hurricanes formed in the month of June. During this period, two of these systems developed in the deep tropics east of the Lesser Antilles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NOAA_Book_212_43-1"><a href="#cite_note-NOAA_Book_212-43">[42]</a></sup> Since 1870, three major hurricanes have formed during June, most notably <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Audrey" title="Hurricane Audrey">Hurricane Audrey</a> in <a href="/wiki/1957_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="1957 Atlantic hurricane season">1957</a>. Audrey attained an intensity greater than that of any Atlantic tropical cyclone during June or July until Hurricanes Dennis and Emily of 2005.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NOAA_Book_27_44-0"><a href="#cite_note-NOAA_Book_27-44">[43]</a></sup> The easternmost forming storm during June, <a href="/wiki/1979_Atlantic_hurricane_season#Tropical_Storm_Ana" title="1979 Atlantic hurricane season">Tropical Storm Ana</a> in 1979, formed at 45°W.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NOAA_Book_212_43-2"><a href="#cite_note-NOAA_Book_212-43">[42]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="July">July</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Atlantic_hurricane&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: July">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Typical_North_Atlantic_Tropical_Cyclone_Formation_in_July.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="227" data-file-width="450" height="111" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/91/Typical_North_Atlantic_Tropical_Cyclone_Formation_in_July.png/220px-Typical_North_Atlantic_Tropical_Cyclone_Formation_in_July.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/91/Typical_North_Atlantic_Tropical_Cyclone_Formation_in_July.png/330px-Typical_North_Atlantic_Tropical_Cyclone_Formation_in_July.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/91/Typical_North_Atlantic_Tropical_Cyclone_Formation_in_July.png/440px-Typical_North_Atlantic_Tropical_Cyclone_Formation_in_July.png 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Typical_North_Atlantic_Tropical_Cyclone_Formation_in_July.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Typical locations and tracks in July</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Not much tropical activity occurs during the month of July, but the majority of hurricane seasons see the formation of one <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone" title="Tropical cyclone">tropical cyclone</a> during July. From an average of Atlantic tropical cyclone seasons from 1944 to 1996, the first tropical storm in half of the seasons occurred by 11 July, and a second formed by 8 August.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NHCTCclimo_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-NHCTCclimo-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>Formation usually occurs in the eastern <a href="/wiki/Caribbean_Sea" title="Caribbean Sea">Caribbean Sea</a> around the <a href="/wiki/Lesser_Antilles" title="Lesser Antilles">Lesser Antilles</a>, in the northern and eastern parts of the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_of_Mexico" title="Gulf of Mexico">Gulf of Mexico</a>, in the vicinity of the northern <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bahamas" title="Bahamas">Bahamas</a>, and off the coast of <a href="/wiki/The_Carolinas" title="The Carolinas">The Carolinas</a> and <a href="/wiki/Virginia" title="Virginia">Virginia</a> over the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_Stream" title="Gulf Stream">Gulf Stream</a>. Storms travel westward through the Caribbean and then either move towards the north and curve near the eastern coast of the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> or stay on a north-westward track and enter the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_of_Mexico" title="Gulf of Mexico">Gulf of Mexico</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HURDAT_12-13"><a href="#cite_note-HURDAT-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>Since 1851, a total of 105 tropical storms have formed during the month of July.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NOAA_Book_213_45-0"><a href="#cite_note-NOAA_Book_213-45">[44]</a></sup> Since 1870, ten of these storms reached major hurricane intensity. Only <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Emily_(2005)" title="Hurricane Emily (2005)">Hurricane Emily</a> of <a href="/wiki/2005_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="2005 Atlantic hurricane season">2005</a>, the strongest July tropical cyclone in the Atlantic basin, attained Category 5 hurricane status during July, making it the earliest Category 5 hurricane on record.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NOAA_Book_27_44-1"><a href="#cite_note-NOAA_Book_27-44">[43]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NOAA_Book_200_46-0"><a href="#cite_note-NOAA_Book_200-46">[45]</a></sup> The easternmost forming storm and longest lived during the month of July, <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Bertha_(2008)" title="Hurricane Bertha (2008)">Hurricane Bertha</a> in <a href="/wiki/2008_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="2008 Atlantic hurricane season">2008</a>, formed at 22.9°W and lasted 17 days.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NOAA_Book_203_47-0"><a href="#cite_note-NOAA_Book_203-47">[46]</a></sup></p>
<div style="clear:both;"></div>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="August">August</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Atlantic_hurricane&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: August">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Typical_North_Atlantic_Tropical_Cyclone_Formation_in_August.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="227" data-file-width="450" height="111" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2b/Typical_North_Atlantic_Tropical_Cyclone_Formation_in_August.png/220px-Typical_North_Atlantic_Tropical_Cyclone_Formation_in_August.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2b/Typical_North_Atlantic_Tropical_Cyclone_Formation_in_August.png/330px-Typical_North_Atlantic_Tropical_Cyclone_Formation_in_August.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2b/Typical_North_Atlantic_Tropical_Cyclone_Formation_in_August.png/440px-Typical_North_Atlantic_Tropical_Cyclone_Formation_in_August.png 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Typical_North_Atlantic_Tropical_Cyclone_Formation_in_August.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Typical locations and tracks in August</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Decrease in wind shear from July to August contributes to a significant increase of tropical activity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SHEARCLIMO_48-0"><a href="#cite_note-SHEARCLIMO-48">[47]</a></sup> An average of 2.8 Atlantic tropical storms develop annually in August. On average, four named tropical storms, including one hurricane, occur by August 30, and the first intense hurricane develops by 4 September.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NHCTCclimo_7-2"><a href="#cite_note-NHCTCclimo-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<div style="clear:both;"></div>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="September">September</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Atlantic_hurricane&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: September">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Typical_North_Atlantic_Tropical_Cyclone_Formation_in_September.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="227" data-file-width="450" height="111" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/48/Typical_North_Atlantic_Tropical_Cyclone_Formation_in_September.png/220px-Typical_North_Atlantic_Tropical_Cyclone_Formation_in_September.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/48/Typical_North_Atlantic_Tropical_Cyclone_Formation_in_September.png/330px-Typical_North_Atlantic_Tropical_Cyclone_Formation_in_September.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/48/Typical_North_Atlantic_Tropical_Cyclone_Formation_in_September.png/440px-Typical_North_Atlantic_Tropical_Cyclone_Formation_in_September.png 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Typical_North_Atlantic_Tropical_Cyclone_Formation_in_September.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Typical locations and tracks in September</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The peak of the hurricane season occurs in September and corresponds with low <a href="/wiki/Wind_shear" title="Wind shear">wind shear</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SHEARCLIMO_48-1"><a href="#cite_note-SHEARCLIMO-48">[47]</a></sup> and the warmest <a href="/wiki/Sea_surface_temperature" title="Sea surface temperature">sea surface temperatures</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[48]</a></sup> The month of September sees an average of 3 storms a year. By 24 September, the average Atlantic season features 7 named tropical storms, including 4 hurricanes. In addition, two major hurricanes occur on average by 28 September. Relatively few tropical cyclones make landfall at these intensities.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NHCTCclimo_7-3"><a href="#cite_note-NHCTCclimo-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<div style="clear:both;"></div>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="October">October</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Atlantic_hurricane&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: October">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Typical_North_Atlantic_Tropical_Cyclone_Formation_in_October.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="227" data-file-width="450" height="111" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/34/Typical_North_Atlantic_Tropical_Cyclone_Formation_in_October.png/220px-Typical_North_Atlantic_Tropical_Cyclone_Formation_in_October.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/34/Typical_North_Atlantic_Tropical_Cyclone_Formation_in_October.png/330px-Typical_North_Atlantic_Tropical_Cyclone_Formation_in_October.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/34/Typical_North_Atlantic_Tropical_Cyclone_Formation_in_October.png/440px-Typical_North_Atlantic_Tropical_Cyclone_Formation_in_October.png 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Typical_North_Atlantic_Tropical_Cyclone_Formation_in_October.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Typical locations and tracks in October.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The favorable conditions found during September begin to decay in October. The main reason for the decrease in activity is increasing <a href="/wiki/Wind_shear" title="Wind shear">wind shear</a>, although <a href="/wiki/Sea_surface_temperature" title="Sea surface temperature">sea surface temperatures</a> are also cooler than in September.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GrayNov2005_42-1"><a href="#cite_note-GrayNov2005-42">[41]</a></sup> Activity falls markedly with 1.8 cyclones developing on average despite a climatological secondary peak around 20 October.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[49]</a></sup> By 21 October, the average season features 9 named storms with 5 hurricanes. A third major hurricane occurs after 28 September in half of all Atlantic tropical cyclone seasons.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NHCTCclimo_7-4"><a href="#cite_note-NHCTCclimo-7">[7]</a></sup> In contrast to mid-season activity, the mean locus of formation shifts westward to the Caribbean and Gulf of Mexico, reversing the eastward progression of June through August.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HURDAT_12-14"><a href="#cite_note-HURDAT-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<div style="clear:both;"></div>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="November">November</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Atlantic_hurricane&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: November">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Typical_North_Atlantic_Tropical_Cyclone_Formation_in_November.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="227" data-file-width="450" height="111" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e8/Typical_North_Atlantic_Tropical_Cyclone_Formation_in_November.png/220px-Typical_North_Atlantic_Tropical_Cyclone_Formation_in_November.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e8/Typical_North_Atlantic_Tropical_Cyclone_Formation_in_November.png/330px-Typical_North_Atlantic_Tropical_Cyclone_Formation_in_November.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e8/Typical_North_Atlantic_Tropical_Cyclone_Formation_in_November.png/440px-Typical_North_Atlantic_Tropical_Cyclone_Formation_in_November.png 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Typical_North_Atlantic_Tropical_Cyclone_Formation_in_November.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Typical locations and tracks in November.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Wind shear from <a href="/wiki/Westerlies" title="Westerlies">westerlies</a> increases substantially through November, generally preventing cyclone formation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GrayNov2005_42-2"><a href="#cite_note-GrayNov2005-42">[41]</a></sup> On average, one tropical storm forms during every other November. On rare occasions, a major hurricane occurs. The few intense hurricanes in November include <a href="/wiki/1932_Cuba_hurricane" title="1932 Cuba hurricane">Hurricane "Cuba"</a> in late October and early November 1932 (the strongest November hurricane on record peaking as a Category 5 hurricane), <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Lenny" title="Hurricane Lenny">Hurricane Lenny</a> in mid-November 1999, <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Kate_(1985)" title="Hurricane Kate (1985)">Hurricane Kate</a> in late November 1985 which was the latest major hurricane formation on record until <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Otto" title="Hurricane Otto">Hurricane Otto</a> (a category 3 storm) of the 2016 hurricane season.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HURDAT_12-15"><a href="#cite_note-HURDAT-12">[12]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Paloma" title="Hurricane Paloma">Hurricane Paloma</a> was a very potent category 4 storm that made landfall in Cuba in early November 2008.</p>
<div style="clear:both;"></div>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="December_to_May">December to May</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Atlantic_hurricane&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: December to May">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/List_of_off-season_Atlantic_hurricanes" title="List of off-season Atlantic hurricanes">Off-season storms</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">

<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:North_Atlantic_Tropical_Cyclone_Climatology_by_Day_of_Year_Graph.PNG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Probability of a tropical cyclone of tropical storm or hurricane strength at a specific date, expressed as systems per 100 years</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Although the hurricane season is defined as beginning on June 1 and ending on November 30, there have been several off-season storms.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HURDAT_12-16"><a href="#cite_note-HURDAT-12">[12]</a></sup> Since 1870, there have been 32 off-season cyclones, 18 of which occurred in May. In the same time span, nine storms formed in December, two in April, and one each in January, February and March.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NOAA_Book_27_44-2"><a href="#cite_note-NOAA_Book_27-44">[43]</a></sup> During four years (<a href="/wiki/1887_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="1887 Atlantic hurricane season">1887</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NOAA_Book_82_51-0"><a href="#cite_note-NOAA_Book_82-51">[50]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/1953_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="1953 Atlantic hurricane season">1953</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NOAA_Book_148_52-0"><a href="#cite_note-NOAA_Book_148-52">[51]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/2003_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="2003 Atlantic hurricane season">2003</a>, and <a href="/wiki/2007_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="2007 Atlantic hurricane season">2007</a>), tropical cyclones formed in the North Atlantic Ocean both during or before May and during December.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NOAA_Book_202_53-0"><a href="#cite_note-NOAA_Book_202-53">[52]</a></sup> In 1887, four storms occurred outside the season, the most in a single year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NOAA_Book_82_51-1"><a href="#cite_note-NOAA_Book_82-51">[50]</a></sup> High vertical wind shear and low <a href="/wiki/Sea_surface_temperature" title="Sea surface temperature">sea surface temperatures</a> generally preclude tropical cyclone formation during the off-season.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NHCTCclimo_7-5"><a href="#cite_note-NHCTCclimo-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>Tropical cyclones have formed in all months.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NOAA_Book_27_44-3"><a href="#cite_note-NOAA_Book_27-44">[43]</a></sup> Four tropical cyclones existed during the month of January, two of which formed during late December: the second Hurricane Alice in 1954/1955, and <a href="/wiki/Tropical_Storm_Zeta" title="Tropical Storm Zeta">Tropical Storm Zeta</a> in 2005/2006. The only two hurricanes to form in January are a Category 1 hurricane in the <a href="/wiki/1938_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="1938 Atlantic hurricane season">1938 season</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Alex_(2016)" title="Hurricane Alex (2016)">Hurricane Alex</a> in the <a href="/wiki/2016_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="2016 Atlantic hurricane season">2016 season</a>. A <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/1978_January_subtropical_storm" title="1978 January subtropical storm">subtropical storm</a> in January also began the <a href="/wiki/1978_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="1978 Atlantic hurricane season">1978 Atlantic hurricane season</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HURDAT_12-17"><a href="#cite_note-HURDAT-12">[12]</a></sup> No major hurricanes have occurred in the off-season.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NOAA_Book_146_54-0"><a href="#cite_note-NOAA_Book_146-54">[53]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Extremes">Extremes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Atlantic_hurricane&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Extremes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Hurricane_Katrina_August_28_2005_NASA.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="8000" data-file-width="6200" height="284" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a4/Hurricane_Katrina_August_28_2005_NASA.jpg/220px-Hurricane_Katrina_August_28_2005_NASA.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a4/Hurricane_Katrina_August_28_2005_NASA.jpg/330px-Hurricane_Katrina_August_28_2005_NASA.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a4/Hurricane_Katrina_August_28_2005_NASA.jpg/440px-Hurricane_Katrina_August_28_2005_NASA.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Hurricane_Katrina_August_28_2005_NASA.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Katrina" title="Hurricane Katrina">Hurricane Katrina</a> was the costliest and one of the five deadliest hurricanes in the history of the United States</div>
</div>
</div>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/List_of_Atlantic_hurricane_records" title="List of Atlantic hurricane records">List of Atlantic hurricane records</a></div>
<ul>
<li>The season in which the most tropical storms formed on record was the <a href="/wiki/2005_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="2005 Atlantic hurricane season">2005 Atlantic hurricane season</a> (28). That season was also the one in which the most hurricanes formed on record (15).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HURDAT_12-18"><a href="#cite_note-HURDAT-12">[12]</a></sup></li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/2005_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="2005 Atlantic hurricane season">2005 Atlantic hurricane season</a> and <a href="/wiki/1961_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="1961 Atlantic hurricane season">1961 Atlantic hurricane season</a> had the most <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saffir%E2%80%93Simpson_hurricane_wind_scale" title="Saffir–Simpson hurricane wind scale">major hurricanes</a> on record (7).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HURDAT_12-19"><a href="#cite_note-HURDAT-12">[12]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/1950_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="1950 Atlantic hurricane season">1950 Atlantic hurricane season</a> was once thought to have 8, but a re-analysis showed that several storms were weaker than thought, and thus the record is now held by the <a href="/wiki/2005_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="2005 Atlantic hurricane season">2005</a> season.</li>
<li>The least active season on record since 1946 (when the database is considered more reliable) was the <a href="/wiki/1983_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="1983 Atlantic hurricane season">1983 Atlantic hurricane season</a>, with one tropical storm, two hurricanes, and one major hurricane. Overall, the <a href="/wiki/1914_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="1914 Atlantic hurricane season">1914 Atlantic hurricane season</a> remains the least active, with only one documented storm.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HURDAT_12-20"><a href="#cite_note-HURDAT-12">[12]</a></sup></li>
<li>The most intense hurricane (by barometric pressure) on record to form in the North Atlantic basin was <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Wilma" title="Hurricane Wilma">Hurricane Wilma</a> (<a href="/wiki/2005_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="2005 Atlantic hurricane season">2005</a>) (882 <a href="/wiki/Bar_(unit)" title="Bar (unit)">mbar</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HURDAT_12-21"><a href="#cite_note-HURDAT-12">[12]</a></sup></li>
<li>The largest hurricane (in gale diameter) on record to form in the North Atlantic was <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Sandy" title="Hurricane Sandy">Hurricane Sandy</a> (2012) with a gale diameter of 1,100 miles (1,800 km).</li>
<li>The longest-lasting hurricane was the <a href="/wiki/1899_San_Ciriaco_hurricane" title="1899 San Ciriaco hurricane">San Ciriaco's Hurricane of 1899</a>, which lasted for 27 days and 18 hours as a tropical cyclone.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[54]</a></sup></li>
<li>The longest-tracked hurricane was <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Faith" title="Hurricane Faith">Hurricane Faith</a>, which traveled for 6,850 miles (11,020 km) as a tropical cyclone.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-noaafaq2_56-0"><a href="#cite_note-noaafaq2-56">[55]</a></sup> Faith is also the northernmost moving tropical cyclone in the Atlantic basin.</li>
<li>The most <a href="/wiki/Tornado" title="Tornado">tornadoes</a> spawned by a hurricane was 127 by <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Ivan" title="Hurricane Ivan">Hurricane Ivan</a> (<a href="/wiki/2004_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="2004 Atlantic hurricane season">2004 season</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HURDAT_12-22"><a href="#cite_note-HURDAT-12">[12]</a></sup></li>
<li>The strongest landfalling <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hurricane" title="Hurricane">hurricane</a> was the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Labor_Day_Hurricane_of_1935" title="Labor Day Hurricane of 1935">Labor Day Hurricane of 1935</a> (892 hPa).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HURDAT_12-23"><a href="#cite_note-HURDAT-12">[12]</a></sup></li>
<li>The deadliest hurricane was the <a href="/wiki/Great_Hurricane_of_1780" title="Great Hurricane of 1780">Great Hurricane of 1780</a> (22,000 fatalities).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[56]</a></sup></li>
<li>The deadliest hurricane to make landfall on the continental United States was the <a href="/wiki/1900_Galveston_hurricane" title="1900 Galveston hurricane">Galveston Hurricane</a> in 1900 which may have killed up to 12,000 people.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[57]</a></sup></li>
<li>The most damaging hurricane (adjusted for inflation) was <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Katrina" title="Hurricane Katrina">Hurricane Katrina</a> of the <a href="/wiki/2005_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="2005 Atlantic hurricane season">2005 season</a> which caused $108 billion in damages (2005 <a href="/wiki/United_States_dollar" title="United States dollar">USD</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-blake_59-0"><a href="#cite_note-blake-59">[58]</a></sup></li>
<li>The quickest forming hurricane was <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Humberto_(2007)" title="Hurricane Humberto (2007)">Hurricane Humberto</a> in 2007. It was a minimal hurricane that formed and intensified faster than any other tropical cyclone on record before landfall. Developing on September 12, 2007, in the northwestern Gulf of Mexico, the cyclone rapidly strengthened and struck High Island, Texas, with winds of about 90 mph (150 km/h) early on September 13.</li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Trends">Trends</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Atlantic_hurricane&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Trends">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>While the number of storms in the Atlantic has increased since 1995, there is no obvious global trend. The annual number of tropical cyclones worldwide remains about 87 ± 10. However, the ability of climatologists to make long-term data analysis in certain basins is limited by the lack of reliable historical data in some basins, primarily in the Southern Hemisphere.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Landsea_Trends_60-0"><a href="#cite_note-Landsea_Trends-60">[59]</a></sup> In spite of that, there is some evidence that the intensity of hurricanes is increasing. In 2006, <a href="/wiki/Kerry_Emanuel" title="Kerry Emanuel">Kerry Emanuel</a> stated, "Records of hurricane activity worldwide show an upswing of both the maximum wind speed in and the duration of hurricanes. The energy released by the average hurricane (again considering all hurricanes worldwide) seems to have increased by around 70% in the past 30 years or so, corresponding to about a 15% increase in the maximum wind speed and a 60% increase in storm lifetime."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EmanuelHomepage_61-0"><a href="#cite_note-EmanuelHomepage-61">[60]</a></sup> At the time, Emanuel theorized that increased heat from global warming was driving this trend, however, some argue that Emanuel's own research in 2008 refuted this theory. Others contend that the trend does not exist at all, but instead is a figment created by faulty readings from primitive 1970s-era measurement equipment.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Vecchi and Knutson (2008) found a weakly positive, although not statistically-significant trend in the number of North Atlantic tropical cyclones for 1878–2006, but also a surprisingly strong decrease in cyclone duration over this period.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62"><a href="#cite_note-62">[61]</a></sup></p>
<p>On May 15, 2014, the journal <i><a href="/wiki/Nature_(journal)" title="Nature (journal)">Nature</a></i> published a peer-reviewed submission from October 2013 by James P. Kossin, Kerry A. Emanuel, and Gabriel A. Vecchi that suggests that a poleward migration exists for the paths of maximum intensity of tropical cyclone activity in the Atlantic.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-63"><a href="#cite_note-63">[62]</a></sup> The focus of the report is on the latitude at which recent tropical cyclones in the Atlantic are reaching maximum intensity. Their data indicates that during the past thirty years, the peak intensity of these storms has shifted poleward in both hemispheres at a rate of approximately 60 km per decade, amounting to approximately one degree of latitude per decade.</p>
<p>Atlantic storms are becoming more destructive financially, since five of the ten <a href="/wiki/List_of_costliest_Atlantic_hurricanes" title="List of costliest Atlantic hurricanes">most expensive storms in United States history</a> have occurred since 1990. According to the <a href="/wiki/World_Meteorological_Organization" title="World Meteorological Organization">World Meteorological Organization</a>, “recent increase in societal impact from tropical cyclones has largely been caused by rising concentrations of population and infrastructure in coastal regions.”<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WMO-IWTC_64-0"><a href="#cite_note-WMO-IWTC-64">[63]</a></sup> Pielke <i>et al.</i> (2008) normalized mainland U.S. hurricane damage from 1900–2005 to 2005 values and found no remaining trend of increasing absolute damage. The 1970s and 1980s were notable because of the extremely low amounts of damage compared to other decades. The decade 1996–2005 has the second most damage among the past 11 decades, with only the decade 1926–1935 surpassing its costs. The most damaging single storm is the <a href="/wiki/1926_Miami_hurricane" title="1926 Miami hurricane">1926 Miami hurricane</a>, with $157 billion of normalized damage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pielke2008_65-0"><a href="#cite_note-Pielke2008-65">[64]</a></sup></p>
<p>Often in part because of the threat of hurricanes, many coastal regions had sparse population between major ports until the advent of automobile tourism; therefore, the most severe portions of hurricanes striking the coast may have gone unmeasured in some instances. The combined effects of ship destruction and remote landfall severely limit the number of intense hurricanes in the official record before the era of hurricane reconnaissance aircraft and satellite meteorology. Although the record shows a distinct increase in the number and strength of intense hurricanes, therefore, experts regard the early data as suspect.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BOM_TC_Guide_1.3_66-0"><a href="#cite_note-BOM_TC_Guide_1.3-66">[65]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Christopher_Landsea" title="Christopher Landsea">Christopher Landsea</a> <i>et al.</i> estimated an undercount bias of zero to six tropical cyclones per year between 1851 and 1885 and zero to four per year between 1886 and 1910. These undercounts roughly take into account the typical size of tropical cyclones, the density of shipping tracks over the Atlantic basin, and the amount of populated coastline.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-67"><a href="#cite_note-67">[66]</a></sup></p>
<p>The number and strength of Atlantic hurricanes may undergo a 50–70 year cycle, also known as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atlantic_Multidecadal_Oscillation" title="Atlantic Multidecadal Oscillation">Atlantic Multidecadal Oscillation</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-68"><a href="#cite_note-68">[67]</a></sup> Nyberg <i>et al.</i> reconstructed Atlantic major hurricane activity back to the early eighteenth century and found five periods averaging 3–5 major hurricanes per year and lasting 40–60 years, and six other averaging 1.5–2.5 major hurricanes per year and lasting 10–20 years. These periods are associated with the Atlantic multidecadal oscillation. Throughout, a decadal oscillation related to solar irradiance was responsible for enhancing/dampening the number of major hurricanes by 1–2 per year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nyberg2005_69-0"><a href="#cite_note-Nyberg2005-69">[68]</a></sup></p>
<p>Although more uncommon since 1995, few above-normal hurricane seasons occurred during 1970–94.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RMS_activity_70-0"><a href="#cite_note-RMS_activity-70">[69]</a></sup> Destructive hurricanes struck frequently from 1926–60, including many major New England hurricanes. Twenty-one Atlantic tropical storms formed in <a href="/wiki/1933_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="1933 Atlantic hurricane season">1933</a>, a record only recently exceeded in <a href="/wiki/2005_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="2005 Atlantic hurricane season">2005</a>, which saw 28 storms. Tropical hurricanes occurred infrequently during the seasons of 1900–25; however, many intense storms formed during 1870–99. During the <a href="/wiki/1887_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="1887 Atlantic hurricane season">1887 season</a>, 19 tropical storms formed, of which a record 4 occurred after November 1 and 11 strengthened into hurricanes. Few hurricanes occurred in the 1840s to 1860s; however, many struck in the early 19th century, including an <a href="/wiki/1821_Norfolk_and_Long_Island_hurricane" title="1821 Norfolk and Long Island hurricane">1821 storm</a> that made a direct hit on <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York City</a>. Some historical weather experts say these storms may have been as high as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saffir-Simpson_Hurricane_Scale" title="Saffir-Simpson Hurricane Scale">Category 4</a> in strength.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Columbia_CCSR_71-0"><a href="#cite_note-Columbia_CCSR-71">[70]</a></sup></p>
<p>These active hurricane seasons predated satellite coverage of the Atlantic basin. Before the satellite era began in 1960, tropical storms or hurricanes went undetected unless a reconnaissance aircraft encountered one, a ship reported a voyage through the storm, or a storm landed in a populated area.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BOM_TC_Guide_1.3_66-1"><a href="#cite_note-BOM_TC_Guide_1.3-66">[65]</a></sup> The official record, therefore, could miss storms in which no ship experienced gale-force winds, recognized it as a tropical storm (as opposed to a high-latitude extra-tropical cyclone, a tropical wave, or a brief squall), returned to port, and reported the experience.</p>
<p>Proxy records based on <a href="/wiki/Paleotempestology" title="Paleotempestology">paleotempestological</a> research have revealed that major hurricane activity along the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_of_Mexico" title="Gulf of Mexico">Gulf of Mexico</a> coast varies on timescales of centuries to millennia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Liu1999_29-1"><a href="#cite_note-Liu1999-29">[29]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LiuFearn2000_30-1"><a href="#cite_note-LiuFearn2000-30">[30]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-72"><a href="#cite_note-72">[71]</a></sup> Few major hurricanes struck the Gulf coast during 3000–1400 BC and again during the most recent millennium. These quiescent intervals were separated by a hyperactive period during 1400 BC and 1000 AD, when the Gulf coast was struck frequently by catastrophic hurricanes and their landfall probabilities increased by 3–5 times. This millennial-scale variability has been attributed to long-term shifts in the position of the <a href="/wiki/Azores_High" title="Azores High">Azores High</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LiuFearn2000_30-2"><a href="#cite_note-LiuFearn2000-30">[30]</a></sup> which may also be linked to changes in the strength of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/North_Atlantic_Oscillation" title="North Atlantic Oscillation">North Atlantic Oscillation</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-73"><a href="#cite_note-73">[72]</a></sup></p>
<p>According to the Azores High hypothesis, an anti-phase pattern is expected to exist between the Gulf of Mexico coast and the Atlantic coast. During the quiescent periods, a more northeasterly position of the Azores High would result in more hurricanes being steered towards the Atlantic coast. During the hyperactive period, more hurricanes were steered towards the Gulf coast as the Azores High was shifted to a more southwesterly position near the Caribbean. Such a displacement of the Azores High is consistent with paleoclimatic evidence that shows an abrupt onset of a drier climate in <a href="/wiki/Haiti" title="Haiti">Haiti</a> around 3200 <a href="/wiki/Radiocarbon_dating" title="Radiocarbon dating"><sup>14</sup>C years</a> BP,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Higuera-Gundy1999_31-1"><a href="#cite_note-Higuera-Gundy1999-31">[31]</a></sup> and a change towards more humid conditions in the <a href="/wiki/Great_Plains" title="Great Plains">Great Plains</a> during the late-Holocene as more moisture was pumped up the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mississippi_Valley" title="Mississippi Valley">Mississippi Valley</a> through the Gulf coast. Preliminary data from the northern Atlantic coast seem to support the Azores High hypothesis. A 3000-year proxy record from a coastal lake in <a href="/wiki/Cape_Cod" title="Cape Cod">Cape Cod</a> suggests that hurricane activity increased significantly during the past 500–1000 years, just as the Gulf coast was amid a quiescent period of the last millennium.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Aftermath">Aftermath</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Atlantic_hurricane&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Aftermath">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cold_wake">Cold wake</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Atlantic_hurricane&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Cold wake">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Hurricanes are very powerful natural phenomena and being that they leave behind a trail of where they have been. This can be easily noticed when they go over land and cause property damage, but less noticeable is the trail they leave behind in the ocean. One noticeable effect of a recent hurricane is a distinct path of cold water. This is known as a cold wake <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-74"><a href="#cite_note-74">[73]</a></sup>. Hurricanes draw their energy from the ocean, but more specifically they intensify by drawing energy from warm water.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_75-0"><a href="#cite_note-:0-75">[74]</a></sup> Hurricanes cause localized upwelling, which mixes deep colder water with warm surface water.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_75-1"><a href="#cite_note-:0-75">[74]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-76"><a href="#cite_note-76">[75]</a></sup> The direct heat flux from the sea surface into the storm causes some decrease in SST. However, it is mainly the upwelling and upward mixing that was caused by the storm that creates this wake of cold water behind the eye of the storm.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_75-2"><a href="#cite_note-:0-75">[74]</a></sup> A cold wake can be 50 km wide and is found about 50–100 km to the right of the storm track. In the Atlantic that puts the path of the cold wake roughly 75 km north-northeast of the eye. In one studied case, it was noted that sea surface temperature could be reduced by over 2<sup>o</sup>C.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_75-3"><a href="#cite_note-:0-75">[74]</a></sup></p>
