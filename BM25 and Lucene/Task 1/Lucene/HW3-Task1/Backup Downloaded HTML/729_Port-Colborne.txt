<p><b>Port Colborne</b> (<a href="/wiki/Canada_2016_Census" title="Canada 2016 Census">2016</a> population 18,306) is a city on <a href="/wiki/Lake_Erie" title="Lake Erie">Lake Erie</a>, at the southern end of the <a href="/wiki/Welland_Canal" title="Welland Canal">Welland Canal</a>, in the <a href="/wiki/Regional_Municipality_of_Niagara" title="Regional Municipality of Niagara">Niagara Region</a> of <a href="/wiki/Southern_Ontario" title="Southern Ontario">southern</a> <a href="/wiki/Ontario" title="Ontario">Ontario</a>, <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a>. The original settlement, known as Gravelly Bay, dates from 1832 <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-5">[5]</a></sup> and was renamed after <a href="/wiki/John_Colborne,_1st_Baron_Seaton" title="John Colborne, 1st Baron Seaton">Sir John Colborne</a>, a British war hero and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lieutenant_Governor_of_Upper_Canada" title="Lieutenant Governor of Upper Canada">Lieutenant Governor of Upper Canada</a> at the time of the opening of the (new) southern terminus of the <a href="/wiki/First_Welland_Canal" title="First Welland Canal">First Welland Canal</a> in 1833.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Port_Colborne&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In pre-<a href="/wiki/New_France" title="New France">colonial</a> times, the <a href="/wiki/Neutral_Nation" title="Neutral Nation">Neutral Indians</a> lived in the area, due in part to the ready availability of <a href="/wiki/Flint" title="Flint">flint</a> and <a href="/wiki/Chert" title="Chert">chert</a> from outcroppings on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Onondaga_(geological_formation)" title="Onondaga (geological formation)">Onondaga Escarpment</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> This advantage was diminished by the introduction of firearms by European traders, and they were driven out by the <a href="/wiki/Iroquois" title="Iroquois">Iroquois</a> around 1650 as part of the <a href="/wiki/Beaver_Wars" title="Beaver Wars">Beaver Wars</a>.</p>
<p>Originally called Gravelly Bay, after the shallow, bedrock-floored bay upon which it sits, the modern City of Port Colborne traces its roots back to the <a href="/wiki/United_Empire_Loyalist" title="United Empire Loyalist">United Empire Loyalist</a> settlements that grew up in the area following the <a href="/wiki/American_Revolution" title="American Revolution">American Revolution</a>. Growth became focused around the southern terminus of the <a href="/wiki/Welland_Canal" title="Welland Canal">Welland Canal</a> after it was extended to reach <a href="/wiki/Lake_Erie" title="Lake Erie">Lake Erie</a> in 1833. As the population rose, Port Colborne was incorporated as a village in 1870, became a town in 1918, merged with the neighbouring Village of Humberstone in 1952, and was re-incorporated as a city in 1966. In 1970, Niagara Region municipal restructuring added Humberstone Township, further expanding the city.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>Sometime during the 1880s, American tourists from the <a href="/wiki/Southern_United_States" title="Southern United States">Southern states</a> began building vacation homes on the lakeshore of the Western edge of the town. Before long, an entire gated community of vacationers from the South called Port Colborne their home during the summer months.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Today, the picturesque street of Tennessee Avenue is still home to many of these original vacation homes and buildings, as well as the original stone and wrought iron resort gates. The street boasts some immaculately maintained examples of late 19th and early 20th century Southern architecture.</p>
<p>Port Colborne was one of the hardest hit communities during the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Blizzard_of_1977" title="Blizzard of 1977">Blizzard of 1977</a>. Thousands of people were stranded when the city was paralyzed during the storm, and the incident remains one of significance to the local population.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Economy">Economy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Port_Colborne&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Economy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Maritime commerce, including supplying goods to the camps for the labourers who worked on the first canal, ship repair and the provisioning trade, was, and still is, an important part of Port Colborne's economy. Like other cities in the region, Port Colborne was a heavily industrial city throughout most of the early 20th century. A <a href="/wiki/Grain_elevator" title="Grain elevator">grain elevator</a>, two modern <a href="/wiki/Gristmill" title="Gristmill">flour mills</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vale_Inco" title="Vale Inco">Vale</a> <a href="/wiki/Nickel" title="Nickel">nickel</a> <a href="/wiki/Refinery" title="Refinery">refinery</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> a <a href="/wiki/Cement" title="Cement">cement plant</a> operated by <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Port_Colborne_Canada_Cement&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Port Colborne Canada Cement (page does not exist)">Port Colborne Canada Cement</a>, and a <a href="/wiki/Blast_furnace" title="Blast furnace">blast furnace</a> operated by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Algoma_Steel" title="Algoma Steel">Algoma Steel</a> were all major employers. However, several of these operations have closed over the past thirty years,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Dates_and_numbers#Chronological_items" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Dates and numbers"><span title="The time period mentioned near this tag is ambiguous. (September 2012)">when?</span></a></i>]</sup> while those companies that remain now employ significantly fewer residents due to modernization and cutbacks.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>In more recent years,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Dates_and_numbers#Chronological_items" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Dates and numbers"><span title="The time period mentioned near this tag is ambiguous. (September 2012)">when?</span></a></i>]</sup> Port Colborne has been successful attracting new industry, such as the agro-business operations of Casco Inc.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> and Jungbunzlauer,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> which process corn into products such as sweeteners and citric acid. However, the economy has gradually shifted towards tourism and recreation, taking advantage of the scenic beauty of the lakeshore.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vale_Inco" title="Vale Inco">International Nickel Company (now Vale)</a> has long been one of the city's main employers, since the opening of a refinery in 1918. Taking advantage of inexpensive <a href="/wiki/Hydroelectricity" title="Hydroelectricity">hydroelectricity</a> from generating stations at nearby <a href="/wiki/Niagara_Falls" title="Niagara Falls">Niagara Falls</a>, the refinery produced electro-refined nickel for the war effort, and grew to employ over 2,000 workers by the 1950s. Cutbacks in operations and increasing factory automation have reduced the workforce to its present-day total of 190.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p><b>Marine Recycling Corporation</b> is a ship recycling firm located next to the <a href="/wiki/Welland_Canal" title="Welland Canal">Welland Canal</a> at <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Gravelly_Bay&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Gravelly Bay (page does not exist)">Gravelly Bay</a> and operating since the 1970s.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Environmental_concerns">Environmental concerns</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Port_Colborne&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Environmental concerns">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Emissions from Inco's base metal refinery, closed in 1984, resulted in soils contaminated with concentrations of nickel, copper and cobalt above the Ontario Ministry of the Environment's "soil remediation criteria."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ene.gov.on.ca_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-ene.gov.on.ca-17">[17]</a></sup> However, two studies, one in 1997 and another in 1999 found "[no] adverse health effects which may have resulted from environmental exposures."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ene.gov.on.ca_17-1"><a href="#cite_note-ene.gov.on.ca-17">[17]</a></sup> After a series of public meetings between the City, the Ministry of the Environment (MOE) and Inco, it was decided to perform a Community-Based Risk Assessment, a process designed to determine whether the contamination poses a threat to the current, past, or future residents of Port Colborne, and what Inco must do to clean up the contaminated areas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>Some residents launched a <a href="/wiki/Class_action" title="Class action">Class-Action Lawsuit</a> against Inco in 2001<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> seeking $750 million in damages to health, property value, and quality-of-life. Although this suit failed to be certified in 2002,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> it was subsequently modified to limit the class, and focus solely on devaluation of property<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> and was certified on appeal on November 18, 2005.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> A timeline of the case has been written from the point of view of the plaintiffs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kmtimeline_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-kmtimeline-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<p>On July 6, 2010, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ontario_Supreme_Court" title="Ontario Supreme Court">Ontario Supreme Court</a> sided with the residents and awarded more than 7,000 households in Port Colborne a total of $36 million. Households in the Rodney Street area, in the shadow of the nickel refinery, were each awarded $23,000 while those living on the east and west sides of Port Colborne were each awarded $9,000 and $2,500 respectively.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-stcatharinesstandard.ca_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-stcatharinesstandard.ca-27">[27]</a></sup> Vale appealed the ruling to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ontario_Court_of_Appeal" title="Ontario Court of Appeal">Ontario Court of Appeal</a>, who found in 2010 that the plaintiff had not provided sufficient evidence of economic harm, raising the legal burden of proof but not invalidating <a href="/wiki/Rylands_v_Fletcher" title="Rylands v Fletcher">Rylands v Fletcher</a> as precedent law.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> In April 2012 the <a href="/wiki/Supreme_Court_of_Canada" title="Supreme Court of Canada">Supreme Court of Canada</a> sided with Vale and denied the residents the awarded compensation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> Court costs in the amount of CAD$1,766,000 were awarded the defendant by Henderson, J.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kmtimeline_26-1"><a href="#cite_note-kmtimeline-26">[26]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Culture">Culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Port_Colborne&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Port Colborne hosts the annual <a href="/wiki/Canal_Days" title="Canal Days">Canal Days</a> festival in recognition of the important role played by the <a href="/wiki/Welland_Canal" title="Welland Canal">Welland Canal</a> in the history of the city. Originating as a small fair held at the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Port_Colborne_Historical_and_Marine_Museum&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Port Colborne Historical and Marine Museum (page does not exist)">Port Colborne Historical and Marine Museum</a>, it has grown to feature live music, an antique car show, fireworks, tall ships, a kite festival, and food from around the globe. The festival also highlights the presence of Lock 8, which at 420 m (1,380 ft), is one of the world's longest <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Canal_locks" title="Canal locks">canal locks</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> The lock is intended as a guard to keep the water level on the <a href="/wiki/Welland_Canal" title="Welland Canal">Welland Canal</a> constant independent of weather on the lake. Hence the ships are only raised or lowered one to four feet depending on the current water level in Lake Erie. Much of the festival centres around West St., which runs along the side of the canal, and has an excellent view of the <a href="/wiki/Clarence_St._Bridge" title="Clarence St. Bridge">Clarence St. Bridge</a>, built in 1929. one of very few remaining <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lift_bridges" title="Lift bridges">lift bridges</a> on the canal.</p>
<p>The <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Port_Colborne_Historical_and_Marine_Museum&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Port Colborne Historical and Marine Museum (page does not exist)">Port Colborne Historical and Marine Museum</a>, located near the centre of town, is a resource for local history and archival research. In addition to a collection of historic buildings and artifacts, it opened up the "Marie Semley Research Wing" to foster research into local history, named to commemorate the long-standing efforts of a local resident who devoted hours to the museum.</p>
<p>The community features theatre venues with the professional Showboat Festival Theatre and the amateur Port Colborne Operatic Society.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> The company has been presenting annual productions since its inception in 1945.</p>
<p>Port Colborne is home to the Port Colborne <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lions_Club" title="Lions Club">Lions Club</a>. Chartered in 1922, the club is one of the worlds oldest Lions Clubs, and one of Canada's oldest <a href="/wiki/Service_club" title="Service club">service clubs</a> in continuous operation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> The club is still active within the community, hosting many yearly events including an annual Lions Club Carnival in the summer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup></p>
<p>Kinnear House is a local heritage property associated with the jurist <a href="/wiki/Helen_Kinnear" title="Helen Kinnear">Helen Kinnear</a>, the first woman in Canada to be appointed judge by the federal government, or to appear as counsel before the <a href="/wiki/Supreme_Court_of_Canada" title="Supreme Court of Canada">Supreme Court</a>.</p>
<p>A curiosity in town is the "incredible shrinking mill" which is an optical illusion produced when viewing the federal grain elevator. When travelling east on Lakeshore Road, the mill appears to move farther away as one drives closer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Education">Education</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Port_Colborne&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Education">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>There are two <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/High_school" title="High school">high schools</a> in Port Colborne, <a href="/wiki/Port_Colborne_High_School" title="Port Colborne High School">Port Colborne High School</a> (commonly called Port High) and the <a href="/wiki/Lakeshore_Catholic_High_School" title="Lakeshore Catholic High School">Lakeshore Catholic High School</a> (formerly a public high school called Lockview Park Secondary School). Lockview closed in 1987.</p>
