<p>The <b>1993–94 South Pacific cyclone season</b> was a near average tropical cyclone season with five <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclones" title="Tropical cyclones">tropical cyclones</a> occurring within the South Pacific to the east of <a href="/wiki/160th_meridian_east" title="160th meridian east">160°E</a>. The season officially ran from November 1, 1993, to April 30, 1994, with the first disturbance of the season forming on December 26 and the last disturbance dissipating on April 25.</p>
<p>During the season, tropical cyclones were officially monitored by the <a href="/wiki/Regional_Specialized_Meteorological_Center" title="Regional Specialized Meteorological Center">Regional Specialized Meteorological Center</a> (RSMC) in <a href="/wiki/Fiji_Meteorological_Service" title="Fiji Meteorological Service">Nadi, Fiji</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Regional_Specialized_Meteorological_Center#Tropical_cyclone_warning_centers" title="Regional Specialized Meteorological Center">Tropical Cyclone Warning Centers</a> (TCWC) in <a href="/wiki/Bureau_of_Meteorology" title="Bureau of Meteorology">Brisbane, Australia</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Meteorological_Service_of_New_Zealand_Limited" title="Meteorological Service of New Zealand Limited">Wellington, New Zealand</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-99_Plan_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-99_Plan-1">[1]</a></sup> Throughout the season the United States Navy also monitored the basin and issued unofficial warnings, through its <a href="/wiki/Joint_Typhoon_Warning_Center" title="Joint Typhoon Warning Center">Joint Typhoon Warning Center</a> (JTWC) and Naval Western and Oceanography Center (NWOC).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JTWCATCR_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-JTWCATCR-2">[2]</a></sup> Tropical cyclones that were located between 160°E and 120°W as well as the Equator and 25°S were monitored by RSMC Nadi while any that were located to the south of 25°S between 160°E and 120°W were monitored by TCWC Wellington.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-99_Plan_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-99_Plan-1">[1]</a></sup> During the season the JTWC issued warnings on any tropical cyclone that was located between 160°E and 180° while the NPMOC issued warnings for tropical cyclones forming between the 180° and the American coast. RSMC Nadi and TCWC Wellington both used the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Australian_Tropical_Cyclone_Intensity_Scale" title="Australian Tropical Cyclone Intensity Scale">Australian Tropical Cyclone Intensity Scale</a>, and measured windspeeds over a 10-minute period during the season, while the JTWC and the NPMOC measured sustained windspeeds over a 1 -minute period.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-99_Plan_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-99_Plan-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JTWCATCR_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-JTWCATCR-2">[2]</a></sup></p>

<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Cyclone_Rewa" title="Cyclone Rewa">Cyclone Rewa</a></div>
<p>Early on December 26, the JTWC started to monitor a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tropical_disturbance" title="Tropical disturbance">tropical disturbance</a> that had developed about 575 km (355 mi) to the south-east of <a href="/wiki/Nauru" title="Nauru">Nauru</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JTWC_BT_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-JTWC_BT-3">[3]</a></sup> Over the next couple of days the disturbance gradually developed as it moved towards the south-southwest under the influence of a north-easterly flow.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DTDSDEC93_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-DTDSDEC93-4">[4]</a></sup> Early on December 28, TCWC Nadi started to monitor the disturbance as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tropical_depression" title="Tropical depression">tropical depression</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hanstrum_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hanstrum-5">[5]</a></sup> Later that day, the JTWC classified the depression as Tropical Cyclone 05P, before TCWC Nadi reported that the system had intensified into a category 1 tropical cyclone on the Australian tropical cyclone intensity scale and named it Rewa.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hanstrum_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-Hanstrum-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wellington_BT_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Wellington_BT-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JTWC_ATCR_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-JTWC_ATCR-7">[7]</a></sup> Over the next few days, the system gradually intensified under the influence of favourable upper-level winds while it moved towards the south-southwest, passing through the Solomon Islands on December 29 and affecting the southeastern islands of <a href="/wiki/Papua_New_Guinea" title="Papua New Guinea">Papua New Guinea</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DTDSDEC93_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-DTDSDEC93-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rewa_TCR_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Rewa_TCR-8">[8]</a></sup> As it moved through the Solomon Islands, Rewa moved out of the <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone_basins#South_Pacific_Ocean" title="Tropical cyclone basins">South Pacific basin</a> and into the <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone_basins#Australian_region" title="Tropical cyclone basins">Australian region</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hanstrum_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-Hanstrum-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>After affecting New Caledonia, Rewa weakened into a tropical depression and moved towards the northwest over the next few days, before re-entering the Australian basin during January 10 and started to show signs of reintensification. Over the next few days the cyclone moved towards the north north-west and started to affect Papua New Guinea for the second time before it was renamed Rewa during January 13 while it recurved and started to move towards the south-west. Over the next few days the cyclone continued to move to the south-east towards the South Pacific basin, before as it peaked in intensity on January 16 as a <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone_scales#Australia_and_Fiji" title="Tropical cyclone scales">category 5 severe tropical cyclone</a>, Rewa turned and started to move towards the south-west. Over the next few days, the cyclone gradually weakened while it moved towards the south-west and a predicted landfall near Mackay in Queensland. However, during January 18, Rewa interacted with an upper level trough and as a result turned and started to move towards the south-east along the Queensland coast. Rewa then degenerated into an <a href="/wiki/Extratropical_cyclone" title="Extratropical cyclone">extratropical cyclone</a> during January 20, with its remnants last noted bringing heavy rain to New Zealand on January 23.</p>

<p>On April 20, the JTWC started to monitor an area of low pressure that located over the Solomon Islands about 155 km (95 mi) to the north of <a href="/wiki/Honiara" title="Honiara">Honiara</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29P_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-29P-11">[11]</a></sup> During that day the disturbance moved towards the southeast and passed over several of the Solomon Islands, before emerging into the Australian basin.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29P_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-29P-11">[11]</a></sup> Over the next couple of days the disturbance gradually developed further while moving towards the southwest before re-curving and moving southeastwards.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29P_11-2"><a href="#cite_note-29P-11">[11]</a></sup> On April 24, as it moved back into the South Pacific basin, the JTWC initiated advisories on the disturbance, designating it as Tropical Cyclone 29P, with peak windspeeds equivalent to a tropical depression.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29P_11-3"><a href="#cite_note-29P-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-140_JB_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-140_JB-12">[12]</a></sup> As the system was classified, it recurved again and started to move slowly towards the northwest, and started to feel the effects of a high amount of vertical windshear.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29P_11-4"><a href="#cite_note-29P-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-140_JB_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-140_JB-12">[12]</a></sup> As a result of the windshear, the center became exposed and displaced from the deep convection before the JTWC issued their final advisory on April 25 as 29P weakened into an area of low pressure, before dissipating later that day about 600 km (375 mi) to the southwest of Honiara.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29P_11-5"><a href="#cite_note-29P-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-140_JB_12-2"><a href="#cite_note-140_JB-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
