<p><b>Hurricane Joan</b> was a powerful <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hurricane" title="Hurricane">hurricane</a> that caused death and destruction in over a dozen countries in the Caribbean and Central America. Moving on a due west course for nearly two weeks in October 1988, Hurricane Joan caused widespread flooding and over 200 deaths after moving into Central America. Widespread suffering and economic crises were exacerbated by Joan, primarily across <a href="/wiki/Nicaragua" title="Nicaragua">Nicaragua</a>, as heavy rains and high winds impacted those near the hurricane's path.</p>
<p>After crossing Central America into the Pacific, the <a href="/wiki/Cyclone" title="Cyclone">cyclone</a> was renamed <b>Tropical Storm Miriam</b>, with the system's dissipation occurring southwest of Mexico. Joan–Miriam was the final hurricane of the <a href="/wiki/1988_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="1988 Atlantic hurricane season">1988 Atlantic hurricane season</a> and the final named storm of the <a href="/wiki/1988_Pacific_hurricane_season" title="1988 Pacific hurricane season">Pacific hurricane season</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MWR_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-MWR-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NHCJoanp2_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-NHCJoanp2-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NHCJoanp3_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-NHCJoanp3-3">[3]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Meteorological_history">Meteorological history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hurricane_Joan%E2%80%93Miriam&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Meteorological history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Joan-Miriam_1988_track.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="633" data-file-width="1024" height="136" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7b/Joan-Miriam_1988_track.png/220px-Joan-Miriam_1988_track.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7b/Joan-Miriam_1988_track.png/330px-Joan-Miriam_1988_track.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7b/Joan-Miriam_1988_track.png/440px-Joan-Miriam_1988_track.png 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Joan-Miriam_1988_track.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Map plotting the track and intensity of the storm, according to the <a href="/wiki/Saffir%E2%80%93Simpson_scale" title="Saffir–Simpson scale">Saffir–Simpson scale</a></div>

<p>One of the latest <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cape_Verde-type_hurricane" title="Cape Verde-type hurricane">Cape Verde-type hurricanes</a> to form in any season,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Padgett_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Padgett-4">[4]</a></sup> Joan formed from an area of convection in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Intertropical_convergence_zone" title="Intertropical convergence zone">intertropical convergence zone</a> that moved off the coast of Africa early in October. It developed banding and was upgraded to Tropical Depression Seventeen on October 10 and later designated as Tropical Storm Joan while located at low latitude in the central Atlantic Ocean.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NHCJoanp1_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-NHCJoanp1-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Hurricane_Joan_19_oct_1988_1242Z.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="632" data-file-width="680" height="204" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/26/Hurricane_Joan_19_oct_1988_1242Z.jpg/220px-Hurricane_Joan_19_oct_1988_1242Z.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/26/Hurricane_Joan_19_oct_1988_1242Z.jpg/330px-Hurricane_Joan_19_oct_1988_1242Z.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/26/Hurricane_Joan_19_oct_1988_1242Z.jpg/440px-Hurricane_Joan_19_oct_1988_1242Z.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Hurricane_Joan_19_oct_1988_1242Z.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Hurricane Joan on October 19</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Joan gradually strengthened as it passed over the southern <a href="/wiki/Windward_Islands" title="Windward Islands">Windward Islands</a> on October 15. It continued heading west in response to a strong <a href="/wiki/Ridge_(meteorology)" title="Ridge (meteorology)">Ridge</a>, and passed over the <a href="/wiki/Guajira_Peninsula" title="Guajira Peninsula">Guajira Peninsula</a> on October 17.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NHCJoanp1_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-NHCJoanp1-5">[5]</a></sup> After entering the extreme southwestern Caribbean Sea, Joan strengthened into a hurricane. It then slowly executed a small counterclockwise loop, possibly in response to the nearby Tropical Depression Eighteen.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NHCJoanp2_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-NHCJoanp2-2">[2]</a></sup> Upwelling caused by the quasi-stationary hurricane weakened the system. As the nearby depression dissipated, Joan resumed its westward track. A strong <a href="/wiki/Anticyclone" title="Anticyclone">anticyclone</a> over the southwestern Caribbean Sea created an extremely favorable environment, and Joan underwent <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rapid_deepening" title="Rapid deepening">rapid deepening</a> at a rate of 38 millibars in the space of a day.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Padgett_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-Padgett-4">[4]</a></sup> With a minimum pressure of 932 millibars, Joan was one of the strongest October hurricanes since 1961.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Padgett_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-Padgett-4">[4]</a></sup> At the time, it was located at 12°N, the southernmost <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saffir-Simpson_Hurricane_Scale" title="Saffir-Simpson Hurricane Scale">Category 4</a> hurricane ever recorded at the time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NHCJoanp1_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-NHCJoanp1-5">[5]</a></sup> That record has since been broken by <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Ivan" title="Hurricane Ivan">Hurricane Ivan</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NHCJoanp2_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-NHCJoanp2-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NHCIvan_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-NHCIvan-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NHCJoanp7_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-NHCJoanp7-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>Joan made landfall just south of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bluefields,_Nicaragua" title="Bluefields, Nicaragua">Bluefields</a> on October 22.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NHCJoanp7_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-NHCJoanp7-7">[7]</a></sup> It passed over the northern portion of <a href="/wiki/Lake_Nicaragua" title="Lake Nicaragua">Lake Nicaragua</a>, passed over Managua, and entered the Pacific Ocean near <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Leon,_Nicaragua" title="Leon, Nicaragua">Leon</a> on October 23.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NHCJoanp2_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-NHCJoanp2-2">[2]</a></sup> Joan remained a hurricane or tropical storm during its entire passage over Central America, and was a minimal tropical storm when it reached the Pacific.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NHCJoanp2_2-4"><a href="#cite_note-NHCJoanp2-2">[2]</a></sup> As per the policy at the time, Joan was renamed Miriam.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NHCJoanp2_2-5"><a href="#cite_note-NHCJoanp2-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Tropical Storm Miriam hugged the coast of Central America and reached its peak of 986 millibars on October 24.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NHCMiriamp1_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-NHCMiriamp1-8">[8]</a></sup> Ordinarily, a pressure this low would indicate a Category 1 hurricane, but due to its disorganized convection, Miriam was not upgraded to a hurricane.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NHCMiriamp1_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-NHCMiriamp1-8">[8]</a></sup> Land interaction and <a href="/wiki/Wind_shear" title="Wind shear">wind shear</a> weakened Miriam to a tropical depression on October 26. Low-level winds carried the system out to sea, and its convection dissipated on October 28.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NHCMiriamp1_8-2"><a href="#cite_note-NHCMiriamp1-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>Miriam's remnants continued out to sea, and they regenerated on October 30. The tropical depression hung on for the next few days, until its second, and final, dissipation on November 2.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NHCMiriamp2_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-NHCMiriamp2-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Unusual_path">Unusual path</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hurricane_Joan%E2%80%93Miriam&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Unusual path">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Hurricane Joan's path through the southern Caribbean in late October was highly unusual. Most October storms in the Atlantic gravitate towards the northern portion of the Caribbean, and often recurve quickly.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NHC_Climatology_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-NHC_Climatology-10">[10]</a></sup> Joan took the southernmost path of a tropical cyclone since a June system in <a href="/wiki/1933_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="1933 Atlantic hurricane season">1933</a>, although <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hurricane_Irene-Olivia" title="Hurricane Irene-Olivia">Hurricane Irene</a> in 1971 took a path that was just north of Joan's.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NHCJoanp1_5-3"><a href="#cite_note-NHCJoanp1-5">[5]</a></sup> Joan-Miriam was also unusual in that it survived passage from the <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Ocean" title="Atlantic Ocean">Atlantic</a> to Pacific Ocean. Only seven other storms have been known to survive the passage between the Atlantic and Pacific Oceans.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NHCe15_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-NHCe15-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Preparations">Preparations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hurricane_Joan%E2%80%93Miriam&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Preparations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">

<p><a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone_warnings_and_watches" title="Tropical cyclone warnings and watches">Tropical Storm watches</a> and later warnings were issued for the Windward Islands as far north as <a href="/wiki/Martinique" title="Martinique">Martinique</a> on October 13. They were dropped over the subsequent two days as Joan passed through. Along the coast of South America, additional warnings were raised and later dropped as Joan paralleled the coast.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NHCJoanp8_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-NHCJoanp8-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>The first hurricane watches were issued for parts of <a href="/wiki/Panama" title="Panama">Panama</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Providencia_Island" title="Providencia Island">Providencia</a> starting on October 18. The watch area was expanded to include <a href="/wiki/Costa_Rica" title="Costa Rica">Costa Rica</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nicaragua" title="Nicaragua">Nicaragua</a> on October 19 as the watches were upgraded to warnings. These warnings provided several hours' worth of lead time, allowing evacuations and preparations to be made.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NHCJoanp9_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-NHCJoanp9-13">[13]</a></sup> In addition, <a href="/wiki/Flash_flood" title="Flash flood">flash flood</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mudslide" title="Mudslide">mudslide</a> warnings were issued for western Panama, Costa Rica, Honduras, and Nicaragua starting on October 21.</p>
<p>In response to Miriam, the flood and mudslide warnings issued because of Joan were not lifted until October 23 and 25. Tropical Storm warnings were issued for the Pacific coast of Nicaragua, <a href="/wiki/El_Salvador" title="El Salvador">El Salvador</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_of_Fonseca" title="Gulf of Fonseca">Gulf of Fonseca</a> on October 24. They were dropped the next day. Guatemala was placed under a warning on October 24. In addition, new flash flood and mudslide warnings were raised for Guatemala and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mexican_state" title="Mexican state">Mexican state</a> of <a href="/wiki/Chiapas" title="Chiapas">Chiapas</a> on October 24 and 25, respectively. All warnings and watches were discontinued later on October 25 as Miriam turned away from the coast.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NHCMiriamp4_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-NHCMiriamp4-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>Despite the dangers from the intense hurricane, evacuations in Bluefields were met with resistance and disbelief from residents.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NHCJoanp3_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-NHCJoanp3-3">[3]</a></sup> In Managua, 70,000 people were evacuated to safe areas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NHCJoanp2_2-6"><a href="#cite_note-NHCJoanp2-2">[2]</a></sup> In the remaining areas of Nicaragua, 300,000 people were evacuated.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fox_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-Fox-15">[15]</a></sup> In <a href="/wiki/Costa_Rica" title="Costa Rica">Costa Rica</a>, a total of 55,000 people were evacuated.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NHCJoanp4_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-NHCJoanp4-16">[16]</a></sup></p>

<p>Hurricane Joan killed at least 216 people, with 118 unaccounted for. The total cost of damage was around $2 billion (1988 USD).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MWR_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-MWR-1">[1]</a></sup> All of the deaths and most of the damage were due to the Joan portion of Hurricane Joan–Miriam.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NHCMiriamp2_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-NHCMiriamp2-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Lesser_Antilles">Lesser Antilles</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hurricane_Joan%E2%80%93Miriam&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Lesser Antilles">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>No casualties were reported in the <a href="/wiki/Windward_Islands" title="Windward Islands">Windward Islands</a>, including Grenada, where, on October 15, Joan made landfall.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NHCJoanp3_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-NHCJoanp3-3">[3]</a></sup> Passing just south, Joan brought rough seas and winds of 40 mph (65 km/h) with gusts to 55 mph (90 km/h) to the islands of <a href="/wiki/Aruba" title="Aruba">Aruba</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bonaire" title="Bonaire">Bonaire</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Cura%C3%A7ao" title="Curaçao">Curaçao</a> on October 16, damaging coastal facilities and roofs. Structural damage from the storm was estimated at $1.5 million (1988 USD). Increased moisture in Joan's wake caused persistent rainfall on the islands for several days, leading to widespread flooding.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> In <a href="/wiki/Trinidad_and_Tobago" title="Trinidad and Tobago">Trinidad and Tobago</a>, Joan was described as a "minor inconvenience".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-T.26T_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-T.26T-18">[18]</a></sup> Sustained winds up to 51 mph (82 km/h) with gusts up to 64 mph (103 km/h) were recorded on Grenada. Heavy rains amounted to 6 in (152.4 mm) in St. George's. Significant flooding was reported throughout the island, resulting in hundreds of thousands of dollars in damage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EoHDL203_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-EoHDL203-19">[19]</a></sup> A rare invasion of African <a href="/wiki/Desert_locust" title="Desert locust">desert locusts</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Windward_Islands" title="Windward Islands">Windward Islands</a> were blamed on the storm.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="South_America">South America</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hurricane_Joan%E2%80%93Miriam&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: South America">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Venezuela" title="Venezuela">Venezuela</a>, 11 people were killed due to flash-flooding caused by heavy rains. Landslides and flooding killed 25 people in <a href="/wiki/Colombia" title="Colombia">Colombia</a>. The rains and flooding left 27,000 people homeless.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NHCJoanp3_3-3"><a href="#cite_note-NHCJoanp3-3">[3]</a></sup> Throughout <a href="/wiki/Colombia" title="Colombia">Colombia</a>, damages amounted to $1 billion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-UNDHA_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-UNDHA-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Nicaragua">Nicaragua</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hurricane_Joan%E2%80%93Miriam&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Nicaragua">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Most of the destruction was in <a href="/wiki/Nicaragua" title="Nicaragua">Nicaragua</a>. Throughout the country, 148 people perished, 184 were badly hurt, and 100 were unaccounted for. Roughly 23,300 homes were destroyed, with 6,000 being damaged.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NHCJoanp2_2-7"><a href="#cite_note-NHCJoanp2-2">[2]</a></sup> Many settlements on the Atlantic coast of the country were completely destroyed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fox_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-Fox-15">[15]</a></sup> The national electric company had 620 miles (1,000 km) of downed power lines, at the cost of 2.5 million USD (1988 dollars).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Preston_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-Preston-22">[22]</a></sup> A port being constructed with Bulgarian help at <a href="/wiki/El_Bluff" title="El Bluff">El Bluff</a> was severely damaged. On a brighter note, <a href="/wiki/Public_health" title="Public health">public health</a> measures managed to keep the death toll lower than it could have been.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fox_15-2"><a href="#cite_note-Fox-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bluefields,_Nicaragua" title="Bluefields, Nicaragua">Bluefields</a> was hit with extreme impact. Almost all of the 7,500 structures in the city were demolished<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EoHDL203_19-1"><a href="#cite_note-EoHDL203-19">[19]</a></sup> or had their roofs blown off. The majority of the main buildings in the city were destroyed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NHCJoanp2_2-8"><a href="#cite_note-NHCJoanp2-2">[2]</a></sup> The hurricane also wreaked havoc on agriculture in the country. Around 15,700 head of cattle, 20,000 pigs, and 456,000 chickens were killed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NHCJoanp2_2-9"><a href="#cite_note-NHCJoanp2-2">[2]</a></sup> The hurricane caused severe disruption in most of Nicaragua's remaining <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rain_forests" title="Rain forests">rain forests</a> in the areas it hit, and also stripped trees of leaves.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Vetiver_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-Vetiver-23">[23]</a></sup> In the southeast rain forests, Joan toppled or snapped 80 percent of the trees and completely destroyed 500,000 hectares (1,200,000 acres) of canopy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> Hurricane Joan caused transportation difficulties in the country. Floodwaters destroyed 30 bridges and seriously damaged 36 others. Roads totaling 404 miles (650 km) in length were washed away.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NHCJoanp2_2-10"><a href="#cite_note-NHCJoanp2-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>In an unfortunate coincidence, Hurricane Joan hit shortly after an armed conflict in the region had started to cool off. The hurricane destroyed much of the infrastructure in Nicaragua, contributing to a <a href="/wiki/Recession" title="Recession">recession</a> that was already underway. The losses to <a href="/wiki/Cash_crop" title="Cash crop">cash crops</a> severely reduced exports to under 200 million dollars (1988 USD).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Preston_22-1"><a href="#cite_note-Preston-22">[22]</a></sup> These factors combined to aggravate a recession and deepen the economic crisis. Government spending to rebuild infrastructure negated recently introduced anti-<a href="/wiki/Inflation" title="Inflation">inflation</a> measures. Hurricane Joan was a partial cause of Nicaraguans being, on average, worse-off than they were in the 1970s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Historical_Background_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-Historical_Background-25">[25]</a></sup> In all, the storm left at least 250,000 people homeless.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EoHDL204_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-EoHDL204-26">[26]</a></sup> Total damages in the country amounted to $751.1 million.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-UNDHA_21-1"><a href="#cite_note-UNDHA-21">[21]</a></sup> Joan was the first major hurricane to strike Nicaragua since <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Edith_(1971)" title="Hurricane Edith (1971)">Edith</a> in <a href="/wiki/1971_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="1971 Atlantic hurricane season">1971</a>.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Rest_of_Central_America">Rest of Central America</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hurricane_Joan%E2%80%93Miriam&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Rest of Central America">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Costa_Rica" title="Costa Rica">Costa Rica</a>, 28 people were killed, 75 were injured, and 18 were missing. Rainfall caused 20 rivers to burst their banks, flooding 75 settlements, including the city of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Quepos,_Costa_Rica" title="Quepos, Costa Rica">Quepos, Costa Rica</a> on the Pacific coast. At Ciudad Neily, Costa Rica, the Rio Corredores broke through a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dyke_(construction)" title="Dyke (construction)">dike</a>. In total, 7,500 Costa Ricans were rendered homeless.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NHCJoanp3_3-4"><a href="#cite_note-NHCJoanp3-3">[3]</a></sup> Damages in Costa Rica amounted to $65 million.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the remaining portions of <a href="/wiki/Central_America" title="Central America">Central America</a>, heavy rainfall, locally exceeding 300 millimetres (12 in), was reported. These rains caused flooding and landslides, especially in mountainous regions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NHCJoanp2_2-11"><a href="#cite_note-NHCJoanp2-2">[2]</a></sup> Those heavy rains caused seven deaths in <a href="/wiki/Panama" title="Panama">Panama</a> and left $60 million in damages.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mexico">Mexico</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hurricane_Joan%E2%80%93Miriam&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Mexico">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Mexico" title="Mexico">Mexico</a>, heavy rainfall associated with Tropical Storm Miriam caused the cancellation of flights from the airport at <a href="/wiki/Tuxtla_Guti%C3%A9rrez" title="Tuxtla Gutiérrez">Tuxtla Gutiérrez</a>. The highest rainfall reported recorded was 10.37 inches (263 mm) at Lazaro Cardenas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> In small settlements, rivers were flooded and mudslides were reported. Casualties from the flooding are not known.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NHCMiriamp2_9-2"><a href="#cite_note-NHCMiriamp2-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Aftermath">Aftermath</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hurricane_Joan%E2%80%93Miriam&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Aftermath">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The response to requests for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Foreign_aid" title="Foreign aid">international aid</a> was moderate. American church groups, <a href="/wiki/Cuba" title="Cuba">Cuba</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Mexico" title="Mexico">Mexico</a> collectively contributed around 2,500 tons of food and medicine. The <a href="/wiki/Government_of_Canada" title="Government of Canada">Canadian</a>, <a href="/wiki/Government_of_Sweden" title="Government of Sweden">Swedish</a>, <a href="/wiki/West_Germany" title="West Germany">West German</a>, <a href="/wiki/Government_of_Spain" title="Government of Spain">Spanish</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Cabinet_of_the_Netherlands" title="Cabinet of the Netherlands">Dutch Governments</a> contributed aid amounting to 2 million dollars (1988) each. No aid was sent by the United States government. The Soviet Union contributed an unknown sum.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Preston_22-2"><a href="#cite_note-Preston-22">[22]</a></sup> The slow response to requests for aid was contributed to by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sandinista" title="Sandinista">Sandinista</a> Government's actions, which was viewed by a few anonymous diplomats from other countries who commented to the press, as undermining the peace process championed by <a href="/wiki/%C3%93scar_Arias" title="Óscar Arias">Óscar Arias</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Preston_22-3"><a href="#cite_note-Preston-22">[22]</a></sup> Those actions were the July 10 arrests of opposition activists and the closing down of independent radio stations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Preston_22-4"><a href="#cite_note-Preston-22">[22]</a></sup> In addition, the government also barred foreign humanitarian groups from receiving American aid, as well as turning back a relief shipment days before the storm.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Preston_22-5"><a href="#cite_note-Preston-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p>On October 28, 1988, the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_General_Assembly" title="United Nations General Assembly">United Nations General Assembly</a> passed without a vote Resolution 17 of its 43rd session in response to the disaster. The resolution called on countries to contribute to the relief effort.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-United_Nations_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-United_Nations-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Retirement">Retirement</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hurricane_Joan%E2%80%93Miriam&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Retirement">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/List_of_retired_Atlantic_hurricane_names" title="List of retired Atlantic hurricane names">List of retired Atlantic hurricane names</a></div>
<p>The name Joan was retired in 1989 and replaced by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hurricane_Joyce_(disambiguation)" title="Hurricane Joyce (disambiguation)">Joyce</a> on the list for the <a href="/wiki/1994_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="1994 Atlantic hurricane season">1994 season</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NHC_B3_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-NHC_B3-31">[31]</a></sup> The name will never again be used for a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/North_Atlantic_hurricane" title="North Atlantic hurricane">North Atlantic hurricane</a>. Because the impact of the Miriam portion of the tropical cyclone was minimal, the name Miriam was not retired in the Pacific and is still on the list of names for that basin,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WMO_Lists_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-WMO_Lists-32">[32]</a></sup> and was most recently used in the <a href="/wiki/2012_Pacific_hurricane_season#Hurricane_Miriam" title="2012 Pacific hurricane season">2012 season</a>.</p>
