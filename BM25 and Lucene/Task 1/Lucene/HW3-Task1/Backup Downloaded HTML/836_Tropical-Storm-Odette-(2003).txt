<p><b>Tropical Storm Odette</b> was a rare off-season tropical storm that affected the <a href="/wiki/Caribbean_Sea" title="Caribbean Sea">Caribbean Sea</a> in early December 2003. The fifteenth <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone_naming" title="Tropical cyclone naming">named storm</a> of the <a href="/wiki/2003_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="2003 Atlantic hurricane season">2003 Atlantic hurricane season</a>, Odette formed near the coast of <a href="/wiki/Panama" title="Panama">Panama</a> a few days after the official end of the <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="Atlantic hurricane season">Atlantic hurricane season</a>, and ultimately made landfall on the <a href="/wiki/Dominican_Republic" title="Dominican Republic">Dominican Republic</a> as a moderate tropical storm, before becoming extratropical on December 7, dissipating two days later.</p>
<p>The storm caused heavy damage throughout the Dominican Republic from unusually heavy rainfall in December. Preparation preceding Odette's landfall resulted in only eight deaths and 14 injuries. Total damage is unknown, though crop damage in the Dominican Republic totaled to over $8 million (2003 USD, $10.4 million 2016 USD).</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Meteorological_history">Meteorological history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tropical_Storm_Odette_(2003)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Meteorological history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Odette_2003_track.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1740" data-file-width="2700" height="142" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/07/Odette_2003_track.png/220px-Odette_2003_track.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/07/Odette_2003_track.png/330px-Odette_2003_track.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/07/Odette_2003_track.png/440px-Odette_2003_track.png 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Odette_2003_track.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Map plotting the track and intensity of the storm, according to the <a href="/wiki/Saffir%E2%80%93Simpson_scale" title="Saffir–Simpson scale">Saffir–Simpson scale</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>By November 30, the last day of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/North_Atlantic_tropical_cyclone" title="North Atlantic tropical cyclone">Atlantic hurricane</a> season, a <a href="/wiki/Surface_weather_analysis" title="Surface weather analysis">stationary front</a> extended across eastern <a href="/wiki/Cuba" title="Cuba">Cuba</a> into the southwestern <a href="/wiki/Caribbean_Sea" title="Caribbean Sea">Caribbean Sea</a>. On December 1, a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Low_pressure_area" title="Low pressure area">low pressure area</a> developed within the frontal zone just north of <a href="/wiki/Panama" title="Panama">Panama</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tcr_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-tcr-1">[1]</a></sup> and an <a href="/wiki/Anticyclone" title="Anticyclone">anticyclone</a> aloft produced good <a href="/wiki/Outflow_(meteorology)" title="Outflow (meteorology)">outflow</a> over the low-level center.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> The low remained nearly stationary for the next several days, and it gradually became separated from the stationary front.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tcr_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-tcr-1">[1]</a></sup> Convection increased across the area due to moisture from the eastern <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Ocean" title="Pacific Ocean">Pacific Ocean</a> and moderate <a href="/wiki/Divergence" title="Divergence">divergence</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Increased <a href="/wiki/Wind_shear" title="Wind shear">wind shear</a> deteriorated the system on December 2,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> though convection redeveloped as the system started a northeast drift.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> On December 3 a mid-level circulation developed about 140 miles (225 km) north of the surface center. Convection increased and became better organized as a weak <a href="/wiki/Tropical_wave" title="Tropical wave">tropical wave</a> reached the area, and it is estimated the system developed into Tropical Depression Twenty at around 1200 <a href="/wiki/Coordinated_Universal_Time" title="Coordinated Universal Time">UTC</a> on December 4 while located about 345 miles (560 km) south of <a href="/wiki/Kingston,_Jamaica" title="Kingston, Jamaica">Kingston, Jamaica</a>;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tcr_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-tcr-1">[1]</a></sup> initially, the depression was forecast to track north-northeastward and pass over western <a href="/wiki/Haiti" title="Haiti">Haiti</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-disc1_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-disc1-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:TS_Odette.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="482" data-file-width="642" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8b/TS_Odette.jpg/220px-TS_Odette.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8b/TS_Odette.jpg/330px-TS_Odette.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8b/TS_Odette.jpg/440px-TS_Odette.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:TS_Odette.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Tropical Storm Odette shortly after being named on December 4</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Upon being classified as a tropical cyclone, the depression was embedded within the southwesterly flow between a <a href="/wiki/Ridge_(meteorology)" title="Ridge (meteorology)">ridge</a> over the eastern Caribbean Sea and a mid-latitude <a href="/wiki/Trough_(meteorology)" title="Trough (meteorology)">trough</a>, which caused a steady east-northeast motion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-disc1_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-disc1-6">[6]</a></sup> The convection organized into a <a href="/wiki/Eye_(cyclone)#Structure" title="Eye (cyclone)">central dense overcast</a> with a well-defined cloud band wrapping partially around the center, and based on satellite imagery estimates the depression was upgraded to tropical storm status; late on December 4, the <a href="/wiki/National_Hurricane_Center" title="National Hurricane Center">National Hurricane Center</a> named the storm <i><a href="/wiki/Odette_(given_name)" title="Odette (given name)">Odette</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-disc2_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-disc2-7">[7]</a></sup> After being named, the storm intensified despite moderate southwesterly <a href="/wiki/Wind_shear" title="Wind shear">wind shear</a>, and an <a href="/wiki/Eye_(cyclone)" title="Eye (cyclone)">eye feature</a> became evident on microwave satellite imagery.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tcr_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-tcr-1">[1]</a></sup> Additionally, about three–fourths of a mid-level eyewall developed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-disc4_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-disc4-8">[8]</a></sup> The storm was assessed with winds of 40–50 mph (65–85 km/h), though the National Hurricane Center remarked the winds could have been stronger due to lack of structural data. The eye feature diminished as the convective structure deteriorated slightly, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hurricane_Hunters" title="Hurricane Hunters">Hurricane Hunters</a> first flew into the storm at around 1200 UTC on December 5.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tcr_1-4"><a href="#cite_note-tcr-1">[1]</a></sup> Odette tracked over an area of warm <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sea_surface_temperatures" title="Sea surface temperatures">sea surface temperatures</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-disc4_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-disc4-8">[8]</a></sup> and the overall cloud pattern gradually improved;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-disc5_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-disc5-9">[9]</a></sup> the <a href="/wiki/Geophysical_Fluid_Dynamics_Laboratory" title="Geophysical Fluid Dynamics Laboratory">Geophysical Fluid Dynamics Laboratory</a> predicted the storm to attain hurricane status.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-disc6_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-disc6-10">[10]</a></sup> Early on December 6, a <a href="/wiki/Tropical_Rainfall_Measuring_Mission" title="Tropical Rainfall Measuring Mission">TRMM</a> overpass showed an 80% closed eyewall,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-disc8_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-disc8-11">[11]</a></sup> and at 0600 UTC Odette attained peak winds of 65 mph (100 km/h) while located about 245 miles (395 km/h) southwest of <a href="/wiki/Santo_Domingo" title="Santo Domingo">Santo Domingo</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dominican_Republic" title="Dominican Republic">Dominican Republic</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tcr_1-5"><a href="#cite_note-tcr-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Upon reaching peak intensity, Odette had begun accelerating northeastward, which decreased wind shear and slightly increased its southwesterly outflow.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-disc8_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-disc8-11">[11]</a></sup> The low-level center decelerated as it approached <a href="/wiki/Hispaniola" title="Hispaniola">Hispaniola</a>, though the convection continued quickly northeastward. Failing to maintain vertical organization, Odette weakened slightly and made <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Landfall_(meteorology)" title="Landfall (meteorology)">landfall</a> on <a href="/wiki/Jaragua_National_Park" title="Jaragua National Park">Jaragua National Park</a>, in the <a href="/wiki/Pedernales_Province" title="Pedernales Province">Pedernales Province</a> of the Dominican Republic, around 2300 UTC on December 6 with winds of 60 mph (95 km/h). The circulation became disrupted as it crossed the country, and on December 7 it emerged into the Atlantic Ocean with winds of 45 mph (75 km/h).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tcr_1-6"><a href="#cite_note-tcr-1">[1]</a></sup> It accelerated to the northeast ahead of an approaching <a href="/wiki/Cold_front" title="Cold front">cold front</a>, and late on December 7 Odette transitioned into an <a href="/wiki/Extratropical_cyclone" title="Extratropical cyclone">extratropical cyclone</a> as its center became embedded within the front. The remnants continued quickly northeastward before losing its identity within the frontal zone on December 9;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tcr_1-7"><a href="#cite_note-tcr-1">[1]</a></sup> the frontal zone that absorbed Odette also absorbed <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tropical_Storm_Peter_(2003)" title="Tropical Storm Peter (2003)">Tropical Storm Peter</a> a few days later.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-petertcr_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-petertcr-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Preparations">Preparations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tropical_Storm_Odette_(2003)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Preparations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Prior to Odette's predicted arrival, the <a href="/wiki/Dominican_Republic" title="Dominican Republic">Dominican Republic</a> government issued for the evacuation of more than 10,000 people, mostly from those living near rivers. At least 2,000 shelters were set up, capable of housing up to 800,000 people. In addition, the government mobilized the army to force those unwilling to leave from their homes. Such precautions were taken due to already saturated grounds from heavy rainfall three weeks prior.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2003wmo_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-2003wmo-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>A <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone_warnings_and_watches" title="Tropical cyclone warnings and watches">tropical storm watch</a> was issued between <a href="/wiki/Santo_Domingo" title="Santo Domingo">Santo Domingo</a> and the Dominican Republic/<a href="/wiki/Haiti" title="Haiti">Haiti</a> border on December 4, 56 hours prior to landfall. This was raised to a tropical storm warning on December 5 while 32 hours before landfall. In addition, tropical storm warnings were issued for all of the Haitian coastline and <a href="/wiki/Jamaica" title="Jamaica">Jamaica</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tcr_1-8"><a href="#cite_note-tcr-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Impact">Impact</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tropical_Storm_Odette_(2003)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Impact">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>While over the southwestern Caribbean Sea, Odette dropped heavy rainfall, including prior to its formation. For several days, the storm caused rains in <a href="/wiki/Panama" title="Panama">Panama</a>, <a href="/wiki/Costa_Rica" title="Costa Rica">Costa Rica</a>, and the east coast of <a href="/wiki/Nicaragua" title="Nicaragua">Nicaragua</a>. In <a href="/wiki/Colombia" title="Colombia">Colombia</a>, the storm caused rainfall totals of up to 8 inches (200 mm) in <a href="/wiki/Puerto_Colombia" title="Puerto Colombia">Puerto Colombia</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2003wmo_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-2003wmo-13">[13]</a></sup> In <a href="/wiki/Jamaica" title="Jamaica">Jamaica</a>, the storm dropped moderate rainfall, flooding several roads in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saint_Ann_Parish,_Jamaica" title="Saint Ann Parish, Jamaica">Saint Ann</a> and <a href="/wiki/Saint_Mary_Parish,_Jamaica" title="Saint Mary Parish, Jamaica">Saint Mary Parishes</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jamflod_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-jamflod-14">[14]</a></sup> Odette caused moderate damage and 8 deaths in the <a href="/wiki/Dominican_Republic" title="Dominican Republic">Dominican Republic</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Dominican_Republic">Dominican Republic</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tropical_Storm_Odette_(2003)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Dominican Republic">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:TS_Odette_Flooding.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="174" data-file-width="253" height="151" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/2/27/TS_Odette_Flooding.JPG/220px-TS_Odette_Flooding.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/2/27/TS_Odette_Flooding.JPG 1.5x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:TS_Odette_Flooding.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Flooding in the Dominican Republic</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Winds from Tropical Storm Odette were relatively light across the <a href="/wiki/Dominican_Republic" title="Dominican Republic">Dominican Republic</a>, with a peak gust of 60 mph occurring in <a href="/wiki/Santo_Domingo" title="Santo Domingo">Santo Domingo</a>. The storm dropped heavy rainfall for several hours, amounting to a maximum of 9.07 inches (230 mm) in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Isla_Saona" title="Isla Saona">Isla Saona</a>. Several other locations reported over 4 inches as well. The rainfall caused mudslides and flash flooding,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tcr_1-9"><a href="#cite_note-tcr-1">[1]</a></sup> forcing several rivers to overflow in combination with previous rains.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fas_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-fas-15">[15]</a></sup> In addition, a tornado was reported near Santo Domingo, destroying one house and uproofing several others.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2003wmo_13-2"><a href="#cite_note-2003wmo-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>The flooding and mudslides damaged up to 60,000 homes and destroyed 34. Gusty winds caused power outages. River flooding caused two bridges to collapse, isolating several communities. Landslides buried several roads, though authorities quickly repaired them. The rainfall also flooded fields, resulting in severe crop damage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2003wmo_13-3"><a href="#cite_note-2003wmo-13">[13]</a></sup> As much as 85% of the banana crop was lost, while the coffee crop suffered losses shortly before harvest season. Crop damage totaled to around $8 million (2003 USD).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dr1_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-dr1-16">[16]</a></sup> In addition, excess flooding contaminated water supplies, leaving several areas without clean water or sanitation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-drc_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-drc-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>In all, Tropical Storm Odette caused 8 deaths and 14 injuries, mostly due to flash flooding and mudslides. In addition, two indirect deaths are associated with the storm due to heart attacks. Neighboring <a href="/wiki/Haiti" title="Haiti">Haiti</a> experienced little from the storm.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tcr_1-10"><a href="#cite_note-tcr-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Puerto_Rico_and_United_States_Virgin_Islands">Puerto Rico and United States Virgin Islands</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tropical_Storm_Odette_(2003)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Puerto Rico and United States Virgin Islands">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Odette_2003_rainfall.gif"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="788" data-file-width="1292" height="152" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ee/Odette_2003_rainfall.gif/250px-Odette_2003_rainfall.gif" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ee/Odette_2003_rainfall.gif/375px-Odette_2003_rainfall.gif 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ee/Odette_2003_rainfall.gif/500px-Odette_2003_rainfall.gif 2x" width="250"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Odette_2003_rainfall.gif" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Rainfall totals from Odette</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Though Odette passed 280 miles to the west of <a href="/wiki/Mayag%C3%BCez,_Puerto_Rico" title="Mayagüez, Puerto Rico">Mayagüez, Puerto Rico</a>, the storm caused moderate rainfall across <a href="/wiki/Puerto_Rico" title="Puerto Rico">Puerto Rico</a> and the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Virgin_Islands" title="United States Virgin Islands">United States Virgin Islands</a>. Rainfall in Puerto Rico was heaviest in the southeast, where a peak of 8.73 in (221.74 mm) was recorded in <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Jajome_Alto&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Jajome Alto (page does not exist)">Jajome Alto</a>. The storm also caused up to 2.2 in (55.9 mm) of rainfall in <a href="/wiki/Christiansted,_U.S._Virgin_Islands" title="Christiansted, U.S. Virgin Islands">Christiansted</a> on <a href="/wiki/Saint_Croix,_U.S._Virgin_Islands" title="Saint Croix, U.S. Virgin Islands">Saint Croix</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hpc_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-hpc-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>Odette's rainfall caused flooding throughout the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_Puerto_Rico_rivers" title="List of Puerto Rico rivers">island's rivers</a>. The river flooding destroyed three bridges, resulting in $20,000 in damages (2003 USD). The flooding also caused a mudslide near a cemetery in <a href="/wiki/Humacao,_Puerto_Rico" title="Humacao, Puerto Rico">Humacao</a>. The rainfall also covered numerous roads, though overall damage was minimal throughout Puerto Rico and the Virgin Islands.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-unusal0312_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-unusal0312-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Aftermath_and_records">Aftermath and records</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tropical_Storm_Odette_(2003)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Aftermath and records">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In all, 65,000 people were affected by Tropical Storm Odette. The Dominican Republic Red Cross and Red Crescent deployed 105 volunteers to the impacted areas, primarily in the area of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Monte_Cristi_(province)" title="Monte Cristi (province)">Monte Cristi</a>. The organization provided food and hygiene kits to thousands of people, as well as mosquito nets. The Red Cross also gave a 5,000 liter water tank for the citizens in Monte Cristi, an area without clean water or sanitation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-drc_17-1"><a href="#cite_note-drc-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>When Odette formed on December 4, it became the first tropical storm since the beginning of the modern <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone" title="Tropical cyclone">tropical cyclone</a> record to form in the <a href="/wiki/Caribbean_Sea" title="Caribbean Sea">Caribbean Sea</a> in the month of December;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tcr_1-11"><a href="#cite_note-tcr-1">[1]</a></sup> a documented December Caribbean hurricane occurred in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/1820-1829_Atlantic_hurricane_seasons#1822_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="1820-1829 Atlantic hurricane seasons">1822</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-chenoweth_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-chenoweth-20">[20]</a></sup> However, <a href="/wiki/1989_Atlantic_hurricane_season#Tropical_Storm_Karen" title="1989 Atlantic hurricane season">Tropical Storm Karen</a>, which formed in November 1989, persisted until December while located in the northwestern Caribbean Sea.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-karen_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-karen-21">[21]</a></sup> In addition, Odette was the first Atlantic storm to form in the month of December since <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Lili_(1984)" title="Hurricane Lili (1984)">Hurricane Lili</a> in 1984.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wxresearch_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-wxresearch-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p>The 2003 season was the first season since <a href="/wiki/1954_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="1954 Atlantic hurricane season">1954</a> to have a pre-season storm and a post-season storm, with <a href="/wiki/Tropical_Storm_Ana_(2003)" title="Tropical Storm Ana (2003)">Ana</a> in April and Odette.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-meta_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-meta-23">[23]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-alice_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-alice-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
