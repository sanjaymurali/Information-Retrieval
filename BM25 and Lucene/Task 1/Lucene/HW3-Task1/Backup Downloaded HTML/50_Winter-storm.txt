<p>A <b>winter storm</b> is an event in which varieties of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Precipitation_(meteorology)" title="Precipitation (meteorology)">precipitation</a> are formed that only occur at low <a href="/wiki/Temperature" title="Temperature">temperatures</a>, such as <a href="/wiki/Snow" title="Snow">snow</a> or <a href="/wiki/Ice_pellets" title="Ice pellets">sleet</a>, or a rainstorm where ground temperatures are low enough to allow <a href="/wiki/Ice" title="Ice">ice</a> to form (i.e. <a href="/wiki/Freezing_rain" title="Freezing rain">freezing rain</a>). In temperate continental <a href="/wiki/Climate" title="Climate">climates</a>, these storms are not necessarily restricted to the winter season, but may occur in the late autumn and early spring as well. Very rarely, they may form in summer, though it would have to be an abnormally cold summer, such as the summer of <a href="/wiki/Year_Without_a_Summer" title="Year Without a Summer">1816</a> in the Northeast <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States of America</a>.</p>

<p><b>Snowstorms</b> are storms where large amounts of snow fall. Two inches (5 cm) of snow is enough to create serious disruptions to <a href="/wiki/Traffic" title="Traffic">traffic</a> and school transport (because of the difficulty to drive and maneuver the school buses on slick roads). This is particularly true in places where snowfall is not typical but heavy accumulating snowfalls can occur. In places where snowfall is typical, such small snowfalls are rarely disruptive, because of effective snow and ice removal by municipalities, increased use of <a href="/wiki/Four-wheel_drive" title="Four-wheel drive">four-wheel drive</a> and <a href="/wiki/Snow_tire" title="Snow tire">snow tires</a>, and drivers being more used to winter conditions. Snowfalls in excess of 6 inches (15 cm) are usually universally disruptive.</p>
<p>A massive snowstorm with strong winds and other conditions meeting certain criteria is known as a <a href="/wiki/Blizzard" title="Blizzard">blizzard</a>. A large number of heavy snowstorms, some of which were blizzards, occurred in the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> during <a href="/wiki/Great_Blizzard_of_1888" title="Great Blizzard of 1888">1888</a> and <a href="/wiki/North_American_blizzard_of_1947" title="North American blizzard of 1947">1947</a> as well as the early and mid-1990s. The snowfall of 1947 exceeded 2 feet (61 cm) with drifts and snow piles from plowing that reached 12 feet (3.7 m) and for months, temperatures did not rise high enough to melt the snow. The <a href="/wiki/1993_Storm_of_the_Century" title="1993 Storm of the Century">1993 "Superstorm"</a> manifested as a blizzard in most of the affected areas.</p>
<p>Large snowstorms could be quite dangerous: a 6 in (15 cm) snowstorm will make some unplowed roads impassable, and it is possible for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Automobiles" title="Automobiles">automobiles</a> to get stuck in the snow. Snowstorms exceeding 12 in (30 cm) especially in southern or generally warm climates will cave the <a href="/wiki/Roof" title="Roof">roofs</a> of some homes and cause the loss of electricity. Standing dead trees can also be brought down by the weight of the snow, especially if it is wet or very dense. Even a few inches of dry snow can form drifts many feet high under windy conditions.</p>

<p>Snowstorms are usually considered less dangerous than ice storms. However, the snow can bring secondary dangers. <a href="/wiki/Mountain" title="Mountain">Mountain</a> snowstorms can produce <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cornice_(climbing)" title="Cornice (climbing)">cornices</a> and <a href="/wiki/Avalanche" title="Avalanche">avalanches</a>. An additional danger, following a snowy winter, is spring <a href="/wiki/Flood" title="Flood">flooding</a> if the snow melts suddenly because of a dramatic rise in air temperature. Deaths can occur from hypothermia, infections brought on by frostbite or car accidents due to slippery roads. Fires and carbon monoxide poisoning can occur after a storm causes a <a href="/wiki/Power_outage" title="Power outage">power outage</a>. Large amounts of snow can also significantly reduce visibility in the area, a phenomenon known as a <a href="/wiki/Whiteout_(weather)" title="Whiteout (weather)">whiteout</a>; this can be very dangerous to those who are in densely populated areas, since the whiteout can cause major accidents on the road or while flying. There are also several cases of <a href="/wiki/Myocardial_infarction" title="Myocardial infarction">heart attacks</a> caused by overexertion while shoveling heavy wet snow. It is difficult to predict what form this precipitation will take, and it may alternate between <a href="/wiki/Rain" title="Rain">rain</a> and <a href="/wiki/Snow" title="Snow">snow</a>. Therefore, weather forecasters just predict a "wintry mix". Usually, this type of precipitation occurs at temperatures between −2 and 2 °C (28.4 and 35.6 °F). In <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Commonwealth_Countries" title="Commonwealth Countries">Commonwealth Countries</a>, a mixture of rain and snow is usually referred to as <a class="mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Sleet" title="Sleet">sleet</a>. Snowstorms generally occur when different types of air masses in the mid-latitudes interact. These storms feed on differences in temperature and moisture. Initially, a wave is typically formed in the mid levels of the atmosphere as a result of a variety of things, be it a mountain range, injection of vorticity (energy), or several other reasons. Assuming certain conditions are in place for this wave to amplify, it will do so and begin to rotate, which effectively moves warm and moist air from one air mass to the north, and much colder and drier air behind it to the south and east. The boundaries between the air masses constitute the warm and cold fronts of the new cyclone/storm.</p>
<p>Snowstorms that produce a lot of snow require an outside source of moisture, such as the Gulf of Mexico or the Atlantic Ocean in the United States. This requires yet more conditions to be just right, namely for the flow (the general wind flow at certain levels of the atmosphere) in the low-mid levels of the atmosphere to be aligned such that moisture can readily be transported from these regions directly into the storm. Another condition that must be met for a healthy storm is the air that is converging and rising in the center of this low pressure system, the snowstorm, to have an outlet as it rises up in the atmosphere. In other words, this air must be able to readily diverge at the mid levels of the atmosphere, effectively removing it from the storm and allowing this cycle to continue.</p>

<p>Heavy showers of <a href="/wiki/Freezing_rain" title="Freezing rain">freezing rain</a> are one of the most dangerous types of winter storm. They typically occur when a layer of warm air hovers over a region, but the ambient temperature a few meters above the ground is near or below 0 °C (32 °F), and the ground temperature is sub-freezing.</p>
<p>While a 10 cm (3.9 in) snowstorm is somewhat manageable by the standards of the northern <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> and <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a>, a comparable 10 mm (0.39 in) ice storm can paralyze a region: driving becomes extremely hazardous, telephone and power lines are damaged, and crops may be ruined. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Notable ice storms include an <a href="/wiki/El_Ni%C3%B1o" title="El Niño">El Niño</a>-related <a href="/wiki/January_1998_North_American_ice_storm" title="January 1998 North American ice storm">North American ice storm of 1998</a> that affected much of eastern Canada, including <a href="/wiki/Montreal" title="Montreal">Montreal</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ottawa" title="Ottawa">Ottawa</a>, as well as upstate <a href="/wiki/New_York_(state)" title="New York (state)">New York</a> and part of <a href="/wiki/New_England" title="New England">New England</a>. Three million people lost power, some for as long as six weeks. One-third of the trees in Montreal's <a href="/wiki/Mount_Royal" title="Mount Royal">Mount Royal</a> park were damaged, as well as a large proportion of the <a href="/wiki/Sugar" title="Sugar">sugar</a>-producing <a href="/wiki/Maple" title="Maple">maple</a> trees. The amount of economic damage caused by the storm has been estimated at $3 billion <a href="/wiki/Canadian_dollar" title="Canadian dollar">Canadian</a>.</p>
<p>The Christmas Day Ice Storm of 2000 caused devastating electrical issues in parts of <a href="/wiki/Arkansas" title="Arkansas">Arkansas</a>, <a href="/wiki/Oklahoma" title="Oklahoma">Oklahoma</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Texas" title="Texas">Texas</a>. The city of <a href="/wiki/Texarkana,_Arkansas" title="Texarkana, Arkansas">Texarkana, Arkansas</a> experienced the worst damage, at one point losing the ability to use telephones, power and running water. In some areas in Arkansas, Oklahoma, Texas and eventually <a href="/wiki/Louisiana" title="Louisiana">Louisiana</a>, over an inch of ice accumulated from the freezing rain.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/North_Carolina_ice_storm_of_2002" title="North Carolina ice storm of 2002">Ice Storm of December 2002</a> in <a href="/wiki/North_Carolina" title="North Carolina">North Carolina</a> resulted in massive power loss throughout much of the state, and property damage due to falling trees. Except in the mountainous western part of the state, heavy snow and icy conditions are rare in <a href="/wiki/North_Carolina" title="North Carolina">North Carolina</a>.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/December_2005_North_American_ice_storm" title="December 2005 North American ice storm">Ice Storm of December 2005</a> was another severe winter storm producing extensive ice damage across a large portion of the Southern United States on December 14 to 16. It led to power outages and at least 7 deaths.</p>
<p>In January 2005, <a href="/wiki/Kansas" title="Kansas">Kansas</a> had been declared a major disaster zone by President <a href="/wiki/George_W._Bush" title="George W. Bush">George W. Bush</a> after an ice storm caused nearly $39 million in damages to thirty-two counties. Federal funds were provided to the counties during January 4–6, 2005 to aid the recovery process.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/January_2009_North_American_ice_storm" title="January 2009 North American ice storm">January 2009 Central Plains and Midwest ice storm</a> was a crippling and historic ice storm. Most places struck by the storm, saw 2 inches (51 mm) or more of ice accumulation, and a few inches of snow on top of it. This brought down power lines, causing some people to go without power for a few days, to a few weeks. In some cases, some didn't see power for a month or more. At the height of the storm, more than 2 million people were without power.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Graupel">Graupel</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Winter_storm&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Graupel">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Graupel" title="Graupel">Graupel</a></div>
<p>Ice crystals fall through a cloud of super-cooled droplets—minute cloud droplets that have fallen below freezing temperature but have not frozen. The ice crystal plows into the super-cooled droplets and they immediately freeze to it. This process forms <i>graupel</i>, or snow pellets, as the droplet continues to accumulate on the crystal. The pellets bounce when they hit the ground.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Ice_pellets">Ice pellets</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Winter_storm&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Ice pellets">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Ice_pellets" title="Ice pellets">Ice pellets</a></div>
<p>Out ahead of the passage of a warm front, falling snow may partially melt and refreeze into a frozen rain drop before it reaches the ground. These ice pellets are called <i>sleet</i> in most of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/US" title="US">US</a>. Because it is easily seen and does not accumulate ice, it is not as dangerous as freezing rain.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Rime">Rime</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Winter_storm&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Rime">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Hard_rime" title="Hard rime">Hard rime</a></div>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Soft_rime" title="Soft rime">Soft rime</a></div>
<p><i>Rime</i> is a milky white accumulation of super-cooled cloud or fog droplets that freeze when they strike an object that has a temperature of 32 °F (0 °C), the freezing point of water. The process is called <i>riming</i> when super-cooled cloud droplets attach to ice crystals in the formation of <a href="/wiki/Winter_storm#Graupel" title="Winter storm">graupel</a>. Rime ice can pose a hazard to an airliner when it forms on a wing as an aircraft flies through a cloud of super-cooled droplets.</p>
