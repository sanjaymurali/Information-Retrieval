<p><b>Hurricane Greta–Olivia</b> was one of ten named <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_hurricane" title="Atlantic hurricane">Atlantic hurricanes</a> to <a href="/wiki/List_of_Atlantic%E2%80%93Pacific_crossover_hurricanes" title="List of Atlantic–Pacific crossover hurricanes">cross over</a> <a href="/wiki/Central_America" title="Central America">Central America</a> into the eastern Pacific while remaining a <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone" title="Tropical cyclone">tropical cyclone</a>. The seventh <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone_naming" title="Tropical cyclone naming">named storm</a> of the <a href="/wiki/1978_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="1978 Atlantic hurricane season">1978 Atlantic hurricane season</a>, Greta formed from a <a href="/wiki/Tropical_wave" title="Tropical wave">tropical wave</a> just northwest of <a href="/wiki/Trinidad" title="Trinidad">Trinidad</a> on September 13, and despite being in a climatologically unfavorable area, gradually intensified while moving west-northwestward. On September 16, it became a hurricane south of Jamaica. Two days later, the well-defined <a href="/wiki/Eye_(cyclone)" title="Eye (cyclone)">eye</a> approached northeastern Honduras but veered to the northwest. After reaching peak winds of 130 mph (215 km/h) that day, Greta weakened while paralleling the northern Honduras coast just offshore. On September 19, it made <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Landfall_(meteorology)" title="Landfall (meteorology)">landfall</a> on <a href="/wiki/Belize" title="Belize">Belize</a> near <a href="/wiki/Dangriga" title="Dangriga">Dangriga</a> and quickly weakened into a tropical depression while crossing Guatemala and southeastern Mexico. After entering the eastern Pacific, the system re-intensified into a hurricane and was renamed Olivia, which weakened before dissipating over <a href="/wiki/Chiapas" title="Chiapas">Chiapas</a> on September 23.</p>
<p>Taking a similar path to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hurricane_Fifi-Orlene" title="Hurricane Fifi-Orlene">Hurricane Fifi</a> four years prior, Greta threatened to reproduce the devastating effects of the catastrophic storm; however, damage and loss of life was significantly less than feared. In Honduras, about 1,200 homes were damaged, about half of which in towns along the coastline. The storm damaged about 75% of the houses on <a href="/wiki/Roat%C3%A1n" title="Roatán">Roatán</a> along the offshore <a href="/wiki/Bay_Islands_Department" title="Bay Islands Department">Bay Islands</a>, and there was one death in the country. In the <a href="/wiki/Belize_Barrier_Reef" title="Belize Barrier Reef">Belize Barrier Reef</a>, Greta downed trees and produced high waves, while on the mainland, there was minimal flooding despite a high <a href="/wiki/Storm_surge" title="Storm surge">storm surge</a>. In Dangriga where it made landfall, the hurricane damaged or destroyed 125 houses and the primary hospital. In <a href="/wiki/Belize_City" title="Belize City">Belize City</a>, a tornado flipped over a truck and damaged four houses. Damage in Belize was estimated at $25 million (1978 <a href="/wiki/United_States_dollar" title="United States dollar">USD</a>), and there were four deaths.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Meteorological_history">Meteorological history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hurricane_Greta%E2%80%93Olivia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Meteorological history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Greta-Olivia_1978_track.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="633" data-file-width="1024" height="136" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/31/Greta-Olivia_1978_track.png/220px-Greta-Olivia_1978_track.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/31/Greta-Olivia_1978_track.png/330px-Greta-Olivia_1978_track.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/31/Greta-Olivia_1978_track.png/440px-Greta-Olivia_1978_track.png 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Greta-Olivia_1978_track.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Map plotting the track and intensity of the storm, according to the <a href="/wiki/Saffir%E2%80%93Simpson_scale" title="Saffir–Simpson scale">Saffir–Simpson scale</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>A <a href="/wiki/Tropical_wave" title="Tropical wave">tropical wave</a> exited the west coast of <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a> near <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dakar,_Senegal" title="Dakar, Senegal">Dakar, Senegal</a> on September 7. Moving westward across the Atlantic Ocean, the wave spawned an area of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Convection_(meteorology)" title="Convection (meteorology)">convection</a> three days later, which gradually organized. On September 13, the wave moved through the <a href="/wiki/Windward_Islands" title="Windward Islands">Windward Islands</a>, producing wind gusts of 50 mph (85 km/h) on <a href="/wiki/Barbados" title="Barbados">Barbados</a>. Later that day, it is estimated the system developed into a tropical depression about 75 mi (120 km) west-northwest of <a href="/wiki/Trinidad" title="Trinidad">Trinidad</a>, based on ship and land reports. Though located in a climatologically unfavorable area, the depression intensified and continued to develop. A <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hurricane_Hunters" title="Hurricane Hunters">Hurricane Hunters</a> flight on September 14 indicated that the depression intensified into Tropical Storm Greta to the north of the <a href="/wiki/Netherlands_Antilles" title="Netherlands Antilles">Netherlands Antilles</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tcr1_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-tcr1-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>After becoming a tropical storm, Greta intensified slowly due to a strong <a href="/wiki/Trough_(meteorology)" title="Trough (meteorology)">trough</a> to the northwest,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tcr1_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-tcr1-1">[1]</a></sup> and with the South American coastline located to the south, the southerly <a href="/wiki/Inflow_(meteorology)" title="Inflow (meteorology)">inflow</a> was disrupted.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> With a <a href="/wiki/Ridge_(meteorology)" title="Ridge (meteorology)">ridge</a> to the north along the <a href="/wiki/30th_parallel_north" title="30th parallel north">30th parallel</a>, the storm moved quickly west-northwestward across the Caribbean.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> On September 16, Greta intensified into a hurricane about 275 mi (443 km) south of Jamaica.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tcr1_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-tcr1-1">[1]</a></sup> Shortly thereafter, the trough to the northwest weakened, which had been preventing the storm's intensification.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tcr1_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-tcr1-1">[1]</a></sup> An increasingly well-defined <a href="/wiki/Eye_(cyclone)" title="Eye (cyclone)">eye</a> developed while approaching the coast of Honduras as the <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_pressure" title="Atmospheric pressure">barometric pressure</a> quickly dropped.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tcr2_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-tcr2-4">[4]</a></sup> Early on September 18, the eyewall passed just offshore <a href="/wiki/Cabo_Gracias_a_Dios" title="Cabo Gracias a Dios">Cabo Gracias a Dios</a>, the sparsely populated border between Honduras and Nicaragua<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tcr2_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-tcr2-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-clim_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-clim-5">[5]</a></sup> The NHC described the eye as having "literally ricocheted off of the protruding northeast coast of Honduras", thus sparing much of the country from the strongest winds. At 0710 <a href="/wiki/Coordinated_Universal_Time" title="Coordinated Universal Time">UTC</a> on September 18, the Hurricane hunters observed a minimum pressure of 947 mbar (28.0 inHg) just off the northern Honduras coast, which was the basis for the estimated peak intensity of 130 mph (215 km/h).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tcr2_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-tcr2-4">[4]</a></sup> This made it a <a href="/wiki/List_of_Category_4_Atlantic_hurricanes" title="List of Category 4 Atlantic hurricanes">Category 4</a> hurricane on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saffir-Simpson_Hurricane_Wind_Scale" title="Saffir-Simpson Hurricane Wind Scale">Saffir-Simpson Hurricane Wind Scale</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hurdat_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-hurdat-6">[6]</a></sup></p>

<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Olivia_Sept_22_1978_0000Z.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Satellite image of Hurricane Olivia in the eastern Pacific</div>
</div>

<p>Despite the proximity to land, Greta initially maintained a well-defined structure. The hurricane continued generally west-northwestward due to the ridge to the north, and initially was expected to enter the <a href="/wiki/Bay_of_Campeche" title="Bay of Campeche">Bay of Campeche</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> After passing through the <a href="/wiki/Bay_Islands_Department" title="Bay Islands Department">Bay Islands</a> off northern Honduras,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-clim_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-clim-5">[5]</a></sup> Greta weakened slightly while approaching Belize, and made <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Landfall_(meteorology)" title="Landfall (meteorology)">landfall</a> near <a href="/wiki/Dangriga" title="Dangriga">Dangriga</a> at 0000 UTC on September 19,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tcr2_4-3"><a href="#cite_note-tcr2-4">[4]</a></sup> with winds of about 110 mph (175 km/h).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hurdat_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-hurdat-6">[6]</a></sup> The calm of the eye was reported for three to five minutes there.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hw6_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-hw6-8">[8]</a></sup> Rapidly weakening over land, the hurricane deteriorated to tropical depression status over Guatemala within 12 hours of landfall. A large high pressure area from the Carolinas to the central Gulf of Mexico turned Greta southwestward toward the eastern Pacific Ocean.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tcr3_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-tcr3-9">[9]</a></sup> At 0000 UTC on September 20, the <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Pacific_Hurricane_Center" title="Eastern Pacific Hurricane Center">Eastern Pacific Hurricane Center</a> (EPHC) took over responsibility for issuing advisories while Greta was 30 mi (50 km) from the coast. Soon after, the depression emerged over the warm waters of the eastern Pacific and re-intensified. At 0600 UTC, the depression re-attained tropical storm status and named Olivia by the EPHC. After initially moving to the north, Olivia began executing a slow counterclockwise loop. Based on observations from nearby ships and radar, it is estimated Olivia attained hurricane status early on September 22. While tracking towards the Mexican coastline, the system weakened below hurricane threshold; between 1900 and 2000 UTC, Olivia made landfall about 60 mi (95 km) east of <a href="/wiki/Salina_Cruz" title="Salina Cruz">Salina Cruz</a>. Early on September 23, Olivia dissipated over the Mexican state of <a href="/wiki/Chiapas" title="Chiapas">Chiapas</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>Hurricane Greta–Olivia was a rare <a href="/wiki/List_of_Atlantic%E2%80%93Pacific_crossover_hurricanes" title="List of Atlantic–Pacific crossover hurricanes">crossover</a> storm from the Atlantic to the Pacific, one of ten named storms to maintain tropical cyclone status during the crossing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hurdat_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-hurdat-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Impact">Impact</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hurricane_Greta%E2%80%93Olivia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Impact">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Hurricane_Greta_1978.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="464" data-file-width="700" height="146" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1f/Hurricane_Greta_1978.jpg/220px-Hurricane_Greta_1978.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1f/Hurricane_Greta_1978.jpg/330px-Hurricane_Greta_1978.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1f/Hurricane_Greta_1978.jpg/440px-Hurricane_Greta_1978.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Hurricane_Greta_1978.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Image of Hurricane Greta north of Honduras</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Early in its duration, Greta produced heavy rainfall in the Netherlands Antilles, but the strongest winds remained north of the island.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tcr1_1-4"><a href="#cite_note-tcr1-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Late on September 17 when Greta's eye was just offshore Honduras, the country's government issued a <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone_warnings_and_watches" title="Tropical cyclone warnings and watches">hurricane warning</a> for the eastern coastline. Around the same time, the Mexican government issued a hurricane warning for the eastern <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Yucat%C3%A1n_peninsula" title="Yucatán peninsula">Yucatán peninsula</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> and on September 18 a hurricane warning was issued for the Belize and Guatemala coastlines.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> These advanced warnings helped reduce fatalities.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tcr3_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-tcr3-9">[9]</a></sup> In <a href="/wiki/Puerto_Castilla,_Honduras" title="Puerto Castilla, Honduras">Puerto Castilla, Honduras</a>, about 2,000 people were evacuated in advance of the storm.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> The Honduran government put its military, police, and Red Cross on standby in advance of the storm, due to fears of a repeat of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hurricane_Fifi-Orlene" title="Hurricane Fifi-Orlene">Hurricane Fifi</a> in 1974.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> However, unlike Fifi, which caused deadly floods in the region four years prior and took a similar track, Greta did not cause as significant river flooding in Honduras.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tcr2_4-4"><a href="#cite_note-tcr2-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-clim_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-clim-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>Across much of Greta's track in Central America, the hurricane dropped locally heavy rainfall.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tcr3_9-2"><a href="#cite_note-tcr3-9">[9]</a></sup> When Greta passed just offshore northeastern Honduras, it produced sustained winds of 80 mph (130 km/h) in <a href="/wiki/Puerto_Lempira" title="Puerto Lempira">Puerto Lempira</a>, with gusts to 115 mph (185 km/h). Winds along the northern Honduras coast were diminished due to the eyewall passing to the north.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tcr2_4-5"><a href="#cite_note-tcr2-4">[4]</a></sup> In Honduras, meteorologists estimated that upwards of 15 in (380 mm) of rain fell in mountainous regions. Many villages were isolated and communication with them was severely hampered.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> In Puerto Lempira, roughly 1,500 of the town's 7,000 residents sought refuge in five large structures during the storm.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> In twelve communities along the coastline, military officials reported that 656 homes were destroyed, of which 278 were in Punta Potuca.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> In the offshore Bay Islands, the hurricane destroyed 26 houses on <a href="/wiki/Guanaja" title="Guanaja">Guanaja</a>, where many roofs were lost and several boats were destroyed. On nearby <a href="/wiki/Roat%C3%A1n" title="Roatán">Roatán</a>, about 75% of the houses lost their roofs after experiencing wind gusts of 80 mph (130 km/h).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> There was one death in Honduras,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tcr3_9-3"><a href="#cite_note-tcr3-9">[9]</a></sup> and nationwide, the hurricane damaged about 1,200 homes, washed out roads and bridges, and wrecked coconut and rice crops.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-west_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-west-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>At Greta's final landfall in Belize, the highest sustained winds were 55 mph (89 km/h) in <a href="/wiki/Belize_City" title="Belize City">Belize City</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tcr3_9-4"><a href="#cite_note-tcr3-9">[9]</a></sup> with gusts to 115 mph (185 km/h) at Dangriga near the landfall location.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bws_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-bws-21">[21]</a></sup> On the offshore <a href="/wiki/Ambergris_Caye" title="Ambergris Caye">Ambergris Caye</a>, winds reached 60 mph (97 km/h), and there was heavy rainfall.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> On <a href="/wiki/Half_Moon_Caye" title="Half Moon Caye">Half Moon Caye</a>, the hurricane damaged the base of a lighthouse and knocked over several coconut trees.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-prod_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-prod-23">[23]</a></sup> Along the <a href="/wiki/Belize_Barrier_Reef" title="Belize Barrier Reef">Belize Barrier Reef</a>, the hurricane downed palm trees and produced high waves, with <a href="/wiki/Significant_wave_height" title="Significant wave height">significant wave heights</a> of about 33 ft (10 m) along Carrie Bow Caye.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> On the mainland, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Storm_tide" title="Storm tide">storm tides</a> in Dangriga were 6 to 7 ft (1.8 to 2.1 m) above normal, which did not cause much flooding.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tcr3_9-5"><a href="#cite_note-tcr3-9">[9]</a></sup> The strong winds destroyed 50 houses there and unroofed a further 75,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hw6_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-hw6-8">[8]</a></sup> including damage to the hospital. There were also disruptions to power and water service. About 90% of the grapefruit crop was destroyed, and 50% of the orange crop was lost.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hw7_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-hw7-25">[25]</a></sup> Tides were 2 to 4 ft (0.61 to 1.22 m) above normal in Belize City,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tcr3_9-6"><a href="#cite_note-tcr3-9">[9]</a></sup> which caused flooding in conjunction with swollen rivers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hw7_25-1"><a href="#cite_note-hw7-25">[25]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/List_of_diplomatic_missions_of_the_United_States" title="List of diplomatic missions of the United States">United States embassy</a> was flooded with about one foot of mud.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> There was little damage in the city,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hw7_25-2"><a href="#cite_note-hw7-25">[25]</a></sup> although a tornado in Belize City that damaged four houses and flipped over a truck.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> During the storm, the <a href="/wiki/Philip_S._W._Goldson_International_Airport" title="Philip S. W. Goldson International Airport">Belize International Airport</a> was closed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> Farther inland, strong winds caused heavy damage at <a href="/wiki/Guanacaste_National_Park_(Belize)" title="Guanacaste National Park (Belize)">Guanacaste National Park</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-prod_23-1"><a href="#cite_note-prod-23">[23]</a></sup> Damage throughout Belize was estimated at $25 million (1978 USD), and there were four deaths.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tcr3_9-7"><a href="#cite_note-tcr3-9">[9]</a></sup> Three of the deaths were on offshore islands in areas without radios, and the other was due to electrocution.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Aftermath">Aftermath</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hurricane_Greta%E2%80%93Olivia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Aftermath">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Following the storm damage in Honduras, the country requested help from the United States. The <a href="/wiki/Tactical_Air_Command" title="Tactical Air Command">Tactical Air Command</a> had sent two squadrons to Central America in the middle of 1978, and in response to the request from Honduras, two aircraft delivered over 100,000 lb (45,000 kg) of cots, water, and generators; the units also deployed a 13–person crew who specialized in disaster relief. The aid was distributed by the <a href="/wiki/Military_of_Honduras" title="Military of Honduras">Military of Honduras</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-west_20-1"><a href="#cite_note-west-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>In late October 1978, the <a href="/wiki/United_Methodist_Church" title="United Methodist Church">United Methodist Church</a> sent books and other supplies via aircraft to Belize, after a youth group rode out the storm there and desired to help residents.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> Despite the hurricane damage, the economy of Belize continued to grow after Greta struck, including an increase in banana production.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<p>Although the National Hurricane Center does not consider it retired,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> the <a href="/wiki/World_Meteorological_Organization" title="World Meteorological Organization">World Meteorological Organization</a> lists Greta in its <a href="/wiki/List_of_retired_Atlantic_hurricane_names" title="List of retired Atlantic hurricane names">retired hurricane name list</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup></p>
