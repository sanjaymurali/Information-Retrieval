<p>A <b>snow roller</b> is a rare <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Meteorological" title="Meteorological">meteorological</a> phenomenon in which large <a href="/wiki/Snowball" title="Snowball">snowballs</a> are formed naturally as chunks of <a href="/wiki/Snow" title="Snow">snow</a> are blown along the ground by <a href="/wiki/Wind" title="Wind">wind</a>, picking up material along the way, in much the same way that the large snowballs used in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Snowmen" title="Snowmen">snowmen</a> are made. They can be as small as a tennis ball, but they can also be bigger than a car.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Unlike snowballs made by people, snow rollers are typically <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cylindrical" title="Cylindrical">cylindrical</a> in shape, and are often hollow since the inner layers, which are the first layers to form, are weak and thin compared to the outer layers and can easily be blown away, leaving what looks like a <a href="/wiki/Doughnut" title="Doughnut">doughnut</a> or <a href="/wiki/Swiss_roll" title="Swiss roll">Swiss roll</a>.</p>
<p>The following conditions are needed for snow rollers to form:</p>
<ul>
<li>There must be a relatively thin surface layer of wet, loose snow, with a temperature near the <a href="/wiki/Melting_point" title="Melting point">melting point</a> of ice.</li>
<li>Under this thin layer of wet snow there must be a substrate to which the thin surface layer of wet snow will not stick, such as ice or powder snow.</li>
<li>The wind must be strong enough to move the snow rollers, but not strong enough to blow them apart.</li>
<li>Alternatively, gravity can move the snow rollers as when a snowball, such as those that will fall from a tree or cliff, lands on a steep hill and begins to roll down the hill.</li>
</ul>
<p>Because of this last condition, snow rollers are more common in hilly areas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> However, the precise nature of the conditions required makes them a very rare phenomenon.</p>
<p>The term <b>Snow Roller</b> also applies to a horse-drawn implement used in the mid to late 19th century (though tractor-drawn examples are still in limited use today, mainly at ski resorts) to pack snow. It was mainly used in areas with high snow accumulation for access for horses and horse-drawn sleighs or wagons equipped with skis or runners. The implement was essentially, a large, very wide, wooden wheel, weighed down with stone or concrete.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
