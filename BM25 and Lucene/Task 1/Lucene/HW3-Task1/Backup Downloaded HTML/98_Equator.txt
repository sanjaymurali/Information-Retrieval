<p>An <b>equator</b> is the intersection of the surface of a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rotating" title="Rotating">rotating</a> <a href="/wiki/Spheroid" title="Spheroid">spheroid</a> (such as a <a href="/wiki/Planet" title="Planet">planet</a>) with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Plane_(mathematics)" title="Plane (mathematics)">plane</a> perpendicular to the <a href="/wiki/Rotation" title="Rotation">axis of rotation</a> and midway between its <a href="/wiki/Geographical_pole" title="Geographical pole">poles</a>.<br/>
On <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a>, <i>the Equator</i> is an imaginary line on the surface, equidistant from the <a href="/wiki/North_Pole" title="North Pole">North</a> and <a href="/wiki/South_Pole" title="South Pole">South Poles</a>, dividing the Earth into <a href="/wiki/Northern_Hemisphere" title="Northern Hemisphere">Northern</a> and <a href="/wiki/Southern_Hemisphere" title="Southern Hemisphere">Southern Hemispheres</a>. It is about 40,075 kilometres (24,901 mi) long, of which 78.7% lies across water and 21.3% over land.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Overview">Overview</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Equator&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Overview">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tmulti tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:283px;max-width:283px">

<p>The <a href="/wiki/Latitude" title="Latitude">latitude</a> of the Earth's equator is, by definition, 0° (zero <a href="/wiki/Degree_(angle)" title="Degree (angle)">degrees</a>) of arc. The Equator is one of the five notable <a href="/wiki/Circle_of_latitude" title="Circle of latitude">circles of latitude</a> on Earth; the other four are the two <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Polar_Circle" title="Polar Circle">Polar Circles</a> (the <a href="/wiki/Arctic_Circle" title="Arctic Circle">Arctic Circle</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Antarctic_Circle" title="Antarctic Circle">Antarctic Circle</a>) and the <a href="/wiki/Tropics" title="Tropics">two Tropical Circles</a> (the <a href="/wiki/Tropic_of_Cancer" title="Tropic of Cancer">Tropic of Cancer</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Tropic_of_Capricorn" title="Tropic of Capricorn">Tropic of Capricorn</a>). The Equator is the only line of latitude which is also a <a href="/wiki/Great_circle" title="Great circle">great circle</a> — that is, one whose plane passes through the center of the globe. The plane of Earth's equator, when projected outwards to the <a href="/wiki/Celestial_sphere" title="Celestial sphere">celestial sphere</a>, defines the <a href="/wiki/Celestial_equator" title="Celestial equator">celestial equator</a>.</p>
<p>In the cycle of Earth's <a href="/wiki/Season" title="Season">seasons</a>, the plane of the equator passes through the Sun twice per <a href="/wiki/Year" title="Year">year</a>: at the March and September equinoxes. To an observer on the Earth, the Sun appears to travel North or South over the Equator (or the celestial equator) at these times. Light rays from the center of the <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">Sun</a> are <a href="/wiki/Perpendicular" title="Perpendicular">perpendicular</a> to the surface of the Earth at the point of solar noon on the Equator.</p>

<p>Locations on the Equator experience the quickest <a href="/wiki/Sunrise" title="Sunrise">sunrises</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sunset" title="Sunset">sunsets</a>, because the sun moves nearly perpendicular to the horizon for most of the year. The length of daylight (sunrise to sunset) is almost constant throughout the year; it is about 14 minutes longer than nighttime due to <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_refraction" title="Atmospheric refraction">atmospheric refraction</a> and the fact that sunrise is begun (or sunset is ended) as the upper edge, not the centre, of the Sun's disk crosses the <a href="/wiki/Horizon" title="Horizon">horizon</a>.</p>
<p>The Earth <a href="/wiki/Equatorial_bulge" title="Equatorial bulge">bulges slightly at the Equator</a>; the "average" diameter of the Earth is 12,750 kilometres (7,922 mi), but the diameter at the Equator is about 43 kilometres (27 mi) greater than at the poles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-National_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-National-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Sites near the Equator, such as the <a href="/wiki/Guiana_Space_Centre" title="Guiana Space Centre">Guiana Space Centre</a> in <a href="/wiki/Kourou" title="Kourou">Kourou</a>, <a href="/wiki/French_Guiana" title="French Guiana">French Guiana</a>, are good locations for <a href="/wiki/Spaceport" title="Spaceport">spaceports</a> as they have a faster rotational speed than other latitudes; the added velocity reduces the fuel needed to launch spacecraft. Because the Earth spins to the east, spacecraft must also launch to the east (or to the southeast or northeast) to take advantage of this Earth-boost of speed.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Equatorial_seasons_and_climate">Equatorial seasons and climate</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Equator&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Equatorial seasons and climate">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main articles: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Seasons" title="Seasons">Seasons</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tropics" title="Tropics">Tropics</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Effect_of_sun_angle_on_climate" title="Effect of sun angle on climate">Effect of sun angle on climate</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:268px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Seasons.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="170" data-file-width="266" height="170" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/12/Seasons.svg/266px-Seasons.svg.png" width="266"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption"><br/>
Diagram of the seasons, depicting the situation at the December solstice. Regardless of the time of day (i.e. the <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a>'s rotation on its axis), the <a href="/wiki/North_Pole" title="North Pole">North Pole</a> will be dark, and the <a href="/wiki/South_Pole" title="South Pole">South Pole</a> will be illuminated; see also <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arctic_winter" title="Arctic winter">arctic winter</a>. In addition to the density of <a href="/wiki/Angle_of_incidence_(optics)" title="Angle of incidence (optics)">incident</a> light, the <a href="/wiki/Dissipation" title="Dissipation">dissipation</a> of light in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Earth%27s_atmosphere" title="Earth's atmosphere">atmosphere</a> is greater when it falls at a shallow angle.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Seasons result from the yearly revolution of the Earth around the Sun and the tilt of the Earth's axis relative to the plane of revolution. During the year the northern and southern hemispheres are inclined toward or away from the sun according to Earth's position in its orbit. The hemisphere inclined toward the sun receives more sunlight and is in summer, while the other hemisphere receives less sun and is in winter (see <a href="/wiki/Solstice" title="Solstice">solstice</a>).</p>
<p>At the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Equinoxes" title="Equinoxes">equinoxes</a>, the Earth's axis is not tilted toward the sun, instead it is perpendicular to the sun meaning that the day is about 12 hours long, as is the night, across the whole of the Earth.</p>
<p>Near the Equator there is little temperature fluctuation throughout the year, though there may be dramatic differences in rainfall and humidity. The terms summer, autumn, winter and spring do not generally apply, and in many tropical regions people identify two seasons: the wet season and the dry season; although some areas remain wet or dry all year. Temperatures at sea level are usually high year-round, but some higher altitudes such as the <a href="/wiki/Andes" title="Andes">Andes</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mount_Kilimanjaro" title="Mount Kilimanjaro">Mount Kilimanjaro</a> sustain glaciers.</p>
<p>The Equator lies mostly on the three largest <a href="/wiki/Ocean" title="Ocean">oceans</a>: the <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Ocean" title="Atlantic Ocean">Atlantic Ocean</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Indian_Ocean" title="Indian Ocean">Indian Ocean</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Ocean" title="Pacific Ocean">Pacific Ocean</a>. The highest point on the Equator is at the elevation of 4,690 metres (15,387 ft), at <span class="plainlinks nourlexpansion"><a class="external text" href="//tools.wmflabs.org/geohack/geohack.php?pagename=Equator&amp;params=0_0_0_N_77_59_31_W_type:landmark_region:EC&amp;title=highest+point+on+the+Equator"><span class="geo-default"><span class="geo-dms" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location"><span class="latitude">0°0′0″N</span> <span class="longitude">77°59′31″W</span></span></span><span class="geo-multi-punct">﻿ / ﻿</span><span class="geo-nondefault"><span class="vcard"><span class="geo-dec" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location">0.00000°N 77.99194°W</span><span style="display:none">﻿ / <span class="geo">0.00000; -77.99194</span></span><span style="display:none">﻿ (<span class="fn org">highest point on the Equator</span>)</span></span></span></a></span>, found on the southern slopes of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Volc%C3%A1n_Cayambe" title="Volcán Cayambe">Volcán Cayambe</a> [summit 5,790 metres (18,996 ft)] in <a href="/wiki/Ecuador" title="Ecuador">Ecuador</a>. This is slightly above the <a href="/wiki/Snow_line" title="Snow line">snow line</a> and is the only place on the Equator where snow lies on the ground. At the Equator the snow line is around 1,000 metres (3,280 ft) <i>lower</i> than on <a href="/wiki/Mount_Everest" title="Mount Everest">Mount Everest</a> and as much as 2,000 metres (6,560 ft) lower than the highest snow line in the world, near the <a href="/wiki/Tropic_of_Capricorn" title="Tropic of Capricorn">Tropic of Capricorn</a> on <a href="/wiki/Llullaillaco" title="Llullaillaco">Llullaillaco</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Crossing-the-Equator_ceremonies">Crossing-the-Equator ceremonies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Equator&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Crossing-the-Equator ceremonies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Line-crossing_ceremony" title="Line-crossing ceremony">Line-crossing ceremony</a></div>
<p>Certain navies, such as the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Navy" title="Royal Navy">Royal Navy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Russian_Navy" title="Russian Navy">Russian Navy</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/US_Navy" title="US Navy">US Navy</a>, have a tradition of holding ceremonies aboard ships to mark sailors' first crossing of the Equator. These rites of initiation have in the past been notorious for their brutality.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Milder line-crossing ceremonies, typically featuring <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Neptune_(god)" title="Neptune (god)">King Neptune</a>, are also held for passengers' entertainment on some civilian ocean liners and cruise ships.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geodesy">Geodesy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Equator&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Geodesy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Earth_ellipsoid" title="Earth ellipsoid">Earth ellipsoid</a> and <a href="/wiki/Reference_ellipsoid" title="Reference ellipsoid">Reference ellipsoid</a></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Precise_location">Precise location</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Equator&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Precise location">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The precise location of the Equator is not truly fixed; the true equatorial plane is perpendicular to the Earth's spin axis, which <a href="/wiki/Polar_motion" title="Polar motion">drifts</a> about 9 metres (30 ft) during a year. This effect must be accounted for in detailed <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Geophysical" title="Geophysical">geophysical</a> measurements.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Exact_length">Exact length</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Equator&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Exact length">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In two widely used <a href="/wiki/Geodesy" title="Geodesy">geodetic</a> standards, the Equator is modelled as a circle whose radius is a <a href="/wiki/Integer" title="Integer">whole number</a> of metres. In 1976 the <a href="/wiki/International_Astronomical_Union" title="International Astronomical Union">IAU</a> chose a radius of 6,378.140 kilometres (3,963.192 mi), later refined by the <a href="/wiki/International_Union_of_Geodesy_and_Geophysics" title="International Union of Geodesy and Geophysics">IUGG</a> to 6,378.137 kilometres (3,963.191 mi) and adopted in <a href="/wiki/World_Geodetic_System" title="World Geodetic System">WGS-84</a>, though the yet more recent IAU-2000 has retained the old IAU-1976 value. If it were really circular the length of the Equator would be exactly 2<span class="texhtml">π</span> times the radius, namely 40,075.036 kilometres (24,901.473 mi). However, due to the effect of <a href="/wiki/Earth_tide" title="Earth tide">tidal force</a> on the Earth's surface the actual circumference at the equator is 40,075.16 kilometres (24,901.55 mi).</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Geographical_mile" title="Geographical mile">geographical mile</a> is defined as one arc minute of the Equator, so it has different values depending on which radius is assumed, e.g., 1,855.3248 metres (6,087.024 ft) for WGS84 or 1,855.3257 metres (6,087.027 ft) for IAU-2000.</p>
<p>The earth is modelled commonly as a sphere flattened 0.336% along its axis. This makes the Equator 0.16% longer than a <a href="/wiki/Meridian_(geography)" title="Meridian (geography)">meridian</a> (a great circle passing through the two poles). The IUGG standard meridian is, to the nearest millimetre, 40,007.862917 kilometres (24,859.733480 mi), one arc minute of which is 1,852.216 metres (6,076.82 ft), explaining the <a href="/wiki/International_System_of_Units" title="International System of Units">SI</a> standardization of the <a href="/wiki/Nautical_mile" title="Nautical mile">nautical mile</a> as 1,852 metres (6,076 ft), more than 3 metres (9.8 ft) less than the <a href="/wiki/Geographical_mile" title="Geographical mile">geographical mile</a>.</p>
<p>The sea-level surface of the Earth (the <a href="/wiki/Geoid" title="Geoid">geoid</a>) is irregular, so the actual length of the Equator is not so easy to determine. <i>Aviation Week and Space Technology</i> on 9 October 1961 reported that measurements using the Transit IV-A satellite had shown the equatorial "diameter" from longitude 11° West to 169° East to be 1,000 feet (300 m) greater than its "diameter" ninety degrees away.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Climate">Climate</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Equator&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Climate">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Lowlands around the Equator generally have a <a href="/wiki/Tropical_rainforest_climate" title="Tropical rainforest climate">tropical rainforest climate</a>, also known as an equatorial climate, though cold currents cause some regions to have <a href="/wiki/Tropical_monsoon_climate" title="Tropical monsoon climate">tropical monsoon climates</a> with a <a href="/wiki/Dry_season" title="Dry season">dry season</a> in the middle of the year. Average annual temperature is around 30 °C (86 °F) during the day and 23 °C (73 °F) at night. Rainfall is very high, from 2,500 to 3,500 mm (98 to 138 in) per year. Average rainy days are around 200 per year and average annual sunshine hours around 2,000.</p>
