<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Landspout</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">

<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="thumb tright">

<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:GID_Landspout.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
A landspout near <a href="/wiki/North_Platte,_Nebraska" title="North Platte, Nebraska">North Platte, Nebraska</a> on May 22, 2004. Note the characteristic smooth, tubular shape, similar to that of a <a href="/wiki/Waterspout" title="Waterspout">waterspout</a>.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>A <b>landspout</b> is a term coined by meteorologist <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Howard_B._Bluestein" title="Howard B. Bluestein">Howard B. Bluestein</a> in 1985 for a kind of <a href="/wiki/Tornado" title="Tornado">tornado</a> not associated with a <a href="/wiki/Mesocyclone" title="Mesocyclone">mesocyclone</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-landspout_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-landspout-1">[1]</a></sup> The <i>Glossary of Meteorology</i> defines a landspout as</p>
<dl>
<dd>"Colloquial expression describing tornadoes occurring with a parent <a href="/wiki/Cloud" title="Cloud">cloud</a> in its growth stage and with its vorticity originating in the boundary layer.</dd>
<dd>The parent cloud does not contain a preexisting mid-level mesocyclone. The landspout was so named because it looks like "a weak <a href="/wiki/Florida_Keys" title="Florida Keys">Florida Keys</a> <a href="/wiki/Waterspout" title="Waterspout">waterspout</a> over land."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Glossary_of_Meteorology_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Glossary_of_Meteorology-2">[2]</a></sup></dd>
</dl>
<p>Landspouts form during the growth stage of a <a href="/wiki/Cumulus_congestus_cloud" title="Cumulus congestus cloud">cumulus congestus cloud</a> by stretching <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atmospheric_boundary_layer" title="Atmospheric boundary layer">boundary layer</a> <a href="/wiki/Vorticity" title="Vorticity">vorticity</a> upward and into the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cumulus_congestus" title="Cumulus congestus">cumulus congestus</a>'s updraft. They generally are smaller and weaker than <a href="/wiki/Supercell" title="Supercell">supercell</a> tornadoes and do not form from a <a href="/wiki/Mesocyclone" title="Mesocyclone">mesocyclone</a> or pre-existing rotation in the cloud. Because of this, landspouts are rarely detected by <a href="/wiki/Weather_radar" title="Weather radar">Doppler weather radar</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Landspouts share a strong resemblance and development process to that of <a href="/wiki/Waterspout" title="Waterspout">waterspouts</a>, usually taking the form of a translucent and highly <a href="/wiki/Laminar_flow" title="Laminar flow">laminar</a> helical tube. Landspouts are considered tornadoes since a rotating column of air is in contact with both the surface and a cumuliform cloud. Not all landspouts are visible, and many are first sighted as debris swirling at the surface before eventually filling in with condensation and dust. Landspouts are most common in semi-arid climates characterized by high cloud bases and considerable low-level instability. These conditions tend to occur on the High Plains of the United States from Spring through Summer.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>A few landspouts can persist in excess of 15 minutes and have produced <a href="/wiki/Fujita_scale" title="Fujita scale">F3</a> damage;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> however, they rarely produce damage greater than EF-2 given their abbreviated duration, typical slow forward motion, and compact wind field. The potential for damage is further minimized over sparsely populated regions such as the High Plains.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
