<p><i><b>Vices &amp; Virtues</b></i> is the third studio album by American <a href="/wiki/Rock_music" title="Rock music">rock</a> band <a href="/wiki/Panic!_at_the_Disco" title="Panic! at the Disco">Panic! at the Disco</a>, released on March 22, 2011 on <a href="/wiki/Fueled_by_Ramen" title="Fueled by Ramen">Fueled by Ramen</a>. Produced by <a href="/wiki/John_Feldmann" title="John Feldmann">John Feldmann</a> and <a href="/wiki/Butch_Walker" title="Butch Walker">Butch Walker</a>, the album was recorded as a duo by vocalist and multi-instrumentalist <a href="/wiki/Brendon_Urie" title="Brendon Urie">Brendon Urie</a> and drummer <a href="/wiki/Spencer_Smith_(musician)" title="Spencer Smith (musician)">Spencer Smith</a>, following the departure of lead guitarist, backing vocalist and primary lyricist <a href="/wiki/Ryan_Ross" title="Ryan Ross">Ryan Ross</a> and bassist/backing vocalist <a href="/wiki/Jon_Walker" title="Jon Walker">Jon Walker</a> in July 2009. The album's artwork was conceptualized by touring bassist <a href="/wiki/Dallon_Weekes" title="Dallon Weekes">Dallon Weekes</a> who was inducted as a full-time member of the band near the end of recording.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>While Walker and Ross' exit left a hole in the Panic! songwriting regime, the experience became the catalyst for Urie finding his own voice and the confidence to take over as the band's principal songsmith. The lyrics touch on themes of manipulation and confusion. <i>Vices &amp; Virtues</i> took over two years to create, and the increased time in the studio brought forth new musical experimentation.</p>
<p>Upon release, the album received generally mixed to positive reviews. Critics praised the album's wide variety of musical stylings, while citing compositional simplicity and Urie's verbose lyricism as detractors. Preceded by lead single, "<a href="/wiki/The_Ballad_of_Mona_Lisa" title="The Ballad of Mona Lisa">The Ballad of Mona Lisa</a>", the album debuted at number seven on the <a href="/wiki/Billboard_200" title="Billboard 200"><i>Billboard</i> 200</a> selling 56,000 copies within its first week.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Background">Background</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vices_%26_Virtues&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Background">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Material for a follow-up to <i><a href="/wiki/Pretty._Odd." title="Pretty. Odd.">Pretty. Odd.</a></i> dates back to at least April 2008, just one month after the release of the record. Former guitarist and primary songwriter Ryan Ross told <i><a href="/wiki/NME" title="NME">NME</a></i> the band had "nine or ten new songs going right now," which picked up in the same direction of <i>Pretty. Odd.</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-earliest_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-earliest-3">[3]</a></sup> "It was pretty much the same with the last record," bassist Jon Walker said. "We haven't stopped writing songs since we stopped recording <i>Pretty. Odd.</i>, because we have a lot to write about [...] It's sounding 'pretty awesome,' which is probably what we're going to call the next record," Ross joked.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-oldest_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-oldest-4">[4]</a></sup> "We've got quite a few new ideas and songs we've been working on, and we're hoping to record some of that stuff by the end of the year."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-oldest_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-oldest-4">[4]</a></sup> The article explained that Walker said the band would be taking time off for the holidays and then re-entering the studio to record new music.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-oldest_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-oldest-4">[4]</a></sup> In June 2009, singer Brendon Urie revealed the band had been writing for "roughly half a year now" at Ross' <a href="/wiki/Topanga,_California" title="Topanga, California">Topanga Canyon, California</a> home, and continued to take the band "further down the sun-dappled, retro-pop path [they] began exploring on <i>Pretty. Odd.</i>": "We've always been a pop band. We've always been fans of pop music, whether it's <a href="/wiki/The_Beatles" title="The Beatles">The Beatles</a> – which is, like, the beginning of pop music – we've always been fans of good catchy melodies and lighthearted music." Spencer Smith, drummer, added, "We moved to California since the last record, so that's inspiration. We started surfing, too, so maybe we'll get a little <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Beach_Boys" title="Beach Boys">Beach Boys</a> vibe on this one."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-early_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-early-5">[5]</a></sup> During this time, Smith moved in with Urie, while Ross began writing his own material with Walker at his home.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-talking_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-talking-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>On July 6, 2009, Ryan Ross and Jon Walker announced via the band's official website that the two were leaving the band.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MTV_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-MTV-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nowpublic_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-nowpublic-8">[8]</a></sup> The statement, in part, read: "Ryan Ross and Jon Walker will be leaving Panic at the Disco to embark on a musical excursion of their own. Though the four of us have made music together in the past, we’ve creatively evolved in different directions which has compromised what each of us want to personally achieve. Over the years, we have remained close and honest with each other, which helped us to realize that our goals were different and that parting ways is truly what is best for each of us."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nowpublic_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-nowpublic-8">[8]</a></sup> In an interview following the split, Ross explained that he first brought the idea to Smith in late June 2009 over lunch: "Spencer and I had lunch and caught up for a while, and then the big question came up, like, 'Well, what do you want to do?' and I said, 'Well, I think it might be best if we kind of do our own thing for a while,' and he said, 'I'm glad you said that, because I was going to say the same thing,' " Ross recalled. "And there was really no argument, which is really the best way that could've worked out." Ross said the split was largely due to creative differences between him and Urie. Urie wanted the band to explore a more polished pop sound, while Ross — and, by extension, Walker — was interested in making retro-inspired rock.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-split_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-split-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>The news asserted that both tour plans with <a href="/wiki/Blink-182" title="Blink-182">Blink-182</a> in August 2009 and new album production "will continue as previously announced,"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MTV_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-MTV-7">[7]</a></sup> and the announcement ended with the teaser for a "surprise" soon to come.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nowpublic_8-2"><a href="#cite_note-nowpublic-8">[8]</a></sup> The following day, <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alternative_Press_(music_magazine)" title="Alternative Press (music magazine)">Alternative Press</a></i> broke the news that "<a href="/wiki/New_Perspective_(song)" title="New Perspective (song)">New Perspective</a>", the first song recorded without Ross and Walker, debuted the following month on radio and as a part of the soundtrack to the film <i><a href="/wiki/Jennifer%27s_Body" title="Jennifer's Body">Jennifer's Body</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-altpress1_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-altpress1-10">[10]</a></sup> "New Perspective" was recorded in spring 2009, when the band had just moved out to Topanga. Smith explained the song was inspired by the band's new experiences when there: "We're living near the ocean and going surfing every day, which we've never done before. We tried to get that out in the song, I guess."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-new_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-new-11">[11]</a></sup> The band reinserted the exclamation mark into its name a few days after the departure of Ross and Walker.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-altpress2_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-altpress2-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Recording_and_production">Recording and production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vices_%26_Virtues&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Recording and production">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The writing and recording process for <i>Vices &amp; Virtues</i> took much longer than the band intended. The band was "very confused" after the split from Ross and Walker, desiring instead to go on tour to understand better.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-altpressbig_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-altpressbig-13">[13]</a></sup> As evidenced above, "Oh Glory" and "New Perspective" were largely recorded during the summer of 2009 with producer <a href="/wiki/John_Feldmann" title="John Feldmann">John Feldmann</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-talking_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-talking-6">[6]</a></sup> "These songs are just Brendon and I having fun, because that's what we want to do," Smith told MTV News.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-talking_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-talking-6">[6]</a></sup> Shortly after the departure of Ross and Walker, Smith revealed the band was recording: "We're working on an album now that, hopefully, takes the best parts from both of our records ... because we love them both and are really proud of them."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-split_2_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-split_2-14">[14]</a></sup> While on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Blink-182_Reunion_Tour" title="Blink-182 Reunion Tour">Blink-182 Reunion Tour</a>, Smith showed Blink-182 singer/bassist <a href="/wiki/Mark_Hoppus" title="Mark Hoppus">Mark Hoppus</a> several demos for the album, in hopes that he would produce a song or two.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-talking_6-3"><a href="#cite_note-talking-6">[6]</a></sup> Hoppus confirmed in August 2009 that he was committed to producing one "really strong" track on the record, as well as possible others if allowed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hoppus_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-hoppus-15">[15]</a></sup> During this time, Urie told news outlets the band had "about 10 songs" ready to consider for the band's third album.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-reuters_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-reuters-16">[16]</a></sup> In the beginning stages, a song featuring <a href="/wiki/Rivers_Cuomo" title="Rivers Cuomo">Rivers Cuomo</a> of <a href="/wiki/Weezer" title="Weezer">Weezer</a>, whom Urie later described as "super nice," was set to appear on the new album, but the song is absent from the final track listing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-spin_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-spin-17">[17]</a></sup> The band initially planned to record the album in fall 2009, to be released the beginning of 2010.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-reuters_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-reuters-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>However, recording did not begin until April 2010. Friend and <i>Pretty. Odd.</i> producer <a href="/wiki/Rob_Mathes" title="Rob Mathes">Rob Mathes</a> was "like family" to Smith and Urie, inspiring them to be excited to record despite the duo's initial beginning. He encouraged them to love the work they do, and "... Just get out of bed in the morning, don’t loathe the things that you actually love doing, because it’s just going to keep you down," for which Urie recalled the band "owed a lot" for.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-altpressbig_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-altpressbig-13">[13]</a></sup> After working with the demos produced during the summer with Feldmann for several months afterward, the duo decided they weren't satisfactory, so the band started over with new material. The biggest difference for the band was that it was now half the previous band, with Urie and Smith writing everything.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-altpressbig_13-2"><a href="#cite_note-altpressbig-13">[13]</a></sup> The album was produced by Feldmann and <a href="/wiki/Butch_Walker" title="Butch Walker">Butch Walker</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-releasedate1_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-releasedate1-18">[18]</a></sup> Urie recalled that they were initially nervous to experiment with new producers, but instead regarded Feldmann and Walker's input positively, regarding their help as "Hey, this is your record, I want to help you with your ideas. So you bring me the ideas, and we’ll help you do that. I don’t want to write anything for you, this is your record, it has to be your voice." The band continued working with Feldmann for four months in early 2010, later going to Walker to finalize and improve the songs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-altpressbig_13-3"><a href="#cite_note-altpressbig-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>On May 7, 2010, the band posted a batch of photos during recording sessions for <i>Vices &amp; Virtues</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-altpress3_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-altpress3-19">[19]</a></sup> All guitars and bass for the album were completed by early July,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-punksite1_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-punksite1-20">[20]</a></sup> and, by the end of the month, Urie confirmed that recording of the album was complete and mixing would follow soon.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-punksite_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-punksite-21">[21]</a></sup> Near the end of the writing process, the band looked back upon the lyrics and noticed that "some of the motion was pining for something, or some of it was vanity, or some of it touched on subversion or overthrowing of somebody or manipulation." Urie likened it to the <a href="/wiki/Seven_deadly_sins" title="Seven deadly sins">seven deadly sins</a>, and, after research, settled on the title <i>Vices &amp; Virtues</i>. "In the Biblical sense, there's all that stuff and then there’s just morals that we live by every day and human behavior", Urie said. "I guess this record was really a study in human behavior—mostly for us personally what we had been going through, and noticing all of that."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-altpressbig_13-4"><a href="#cite_note-altpressbig-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Music">Music</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vices_%26_Virtues&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Music">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:PanicatBowery2011.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1600" data-file-width="1200" height="227" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7a/PanicatBowery2011.jpg/170px-PanicatBowery2011.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7a/PanicatBowery2011.jpg/255px-PanicatBowery2011.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7a/PanicatBowery2011.jpg/340px-PanicatBowery2011.jpg 2x" width="170"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:PanicatBowery2011.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The duo performing on the first show of the <a href="/wiki/Vices_%26_Virtues_Tour" title="Vices &amp; Virtues Tour"><i>Vices &amp; Virtues</i> Tour</a> in New York City, February 1, 2011.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Musically, <i>Vices &amp; Virtues</i> has been described as <a href="/wiki/Alternative_rock" title="Alternative rock">alternative rock</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Pop_rock" title="Pop rock">pop rock</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-The_Chronicle_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-The_Chronicle-23">[23]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-altpress.com_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-altpress.com-24">[24]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Pop_punk" title="Pop punk">pop punk</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-The_Chronicle_23-1"><a href="#cite_note-The_Chronicle-23">[23]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Emo_pop" title="Emo pop">emo pop</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Baroque_pop" title="Baroque pop">baroque pop</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Synth-pop" title="Synth-pop">synth-pop</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
<p>The album features a variety of different musical styles in its tracks. As a testament to the amount of time of production, each song "sounds different from the next," according to Urie. In the beginning, the band had no direction for music or lyrics. However, after returning to the studio and restarting production, the band became excited to start with a second chance. "We've been working on music for about a year and a half, so I think that partly adds to why some of the songs have some varying styles," Smith said. "Part of it was us, at the beginning, trying to figure out what we wanted to do – we had a new opportunity with just the two of us – and as we sort of got a better hold on what that was, just kind of went from there."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mtv2011_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-mtv2011-33">[33]</a></sup> The duo picked the best songs out of 30 written as time went on. Urie said the most exciting part of the recording process was "...You get all the work done, record the song, arrange it and then you can add the fun little stuff, little pieces of voices and talking and weird instruments you hadn’t used before."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-altpressbig_13-5"><a href="#cite_note-altpressbig-13">[13]</a></sup> The band's musical experimentation stretched as far as employing an <a href="/wiki/IPad" title="IPad">iPad</a> to record synthesizers on the record. The band got to work with French band The <a href="/wiki/Plastiscines" title="Plastiscines">Plastiscines</a>, and a children's choir on the track "Nearly Witches (Ever Since We Met...)"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ipad_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-ipad-34">[34]</a></sup></p>
<p>A majority of the band's previous material was written by former guitarist Ryan Ross. For <i>Vices &amp; Virtues</i>, Urie had "to step up and take the reins lyrically," which he regarded as having not much experience in prior. The biggest inspiration for lyricism was keeping busy and going out, instead of locking themselves in and writing. Urie has regarded the lyrics as very honest and straightforward, as well as very fantastical with elements of storytelling. Urie explained "We were so self-conscious about everything, hyper-aware of what we’re doing, that when we’re writing I go, "Okay, I don’t want this to be <a href="/wiki/Barry_Manilow" title="Barry Manilow">Barry Manilow</a>’s "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mandy_(Barry_Manilow_song)" title="Mandy (Barry Manilow song)">Mandy</a>", but I want it to be as romantic and cute ...’ There’s a lot of that fighting within yourself. But it ended up being great, and I’m glad that we were able to figure out what we wanted to write about." The lyrics also reflect the confusion after the departure of Ross and Walker.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-altpressbig_13-6"><a href="#cite_note-altpressbig-13">[13]</a></sup> The sessions with Feldmann at his home studio led to experimentation with new sounds. "We really loved <a href="/wiki/Paul_Simon" title="Paul Simon">Paul Simon</a> and got into using marimbas and string instruments," explains Urie. "We ended up buying some synths and messing around with them. It was two kids in a candy store… and we listened to <a href="/wiki/Arcade_Fire" title="Arcade Fire">Arcade Fire</a>'s <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Suburbs_(Arcade_Fire_album)" title="The Suburbs (Arcade Fire album)">The Suburbs</a></i> on repeat for me for the past… well, since it came out [laughs]."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-spin_17-1"><a href="#cite_note-spin-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>"<a href="/wiki/The_Ballad_of_Mona_Lisa" title="The Ballad of Mona Lisa">The Ballad of Mona Lisa</a>" has been described by <i>Alternative Press</i> as "[having] the upbeat pop energy of <i><a href="/wiki/A_Fever_You_Can%27t_Sweat_Out" title="A Fever You Can't Sweat Out">A Fever You Can't Sweat Out</a></i>, with the focus and clarity of <i>Pretty. Odd.</i>" Urie has explained that he and Smith desired to achieve a sound that is more similar to the former: "We missed a couple things from our first record in terms of sonically, with these little instruments that we hadn’t really used on our second record. There [were] a lot of organic instruments and not a lot of electronics or synthesizers. So we wanted to get back to some of that."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-altpressbig_13-7"><a href="#cite_note-altpressbig-13">[13]</a></sup> Smith agreed, and stated "I think there's stuff from the first album that we kind of got away from on the second record that people who were fans of the first one will enjoy."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mtv2011_33-1"><a href="#cite_note-mtv2011-33">[33]</a></sup> "The Calendar", originally written about an <a href="/wiki/Intimate_relationship" title="Intimate relationship">intimate relationship</a> between a male and a female, soon began to resemble the friendships in former members Ross and Walker; the song was finished as a direct response to their departure from the group. "Sarah Smiles" was written for Urie's wife. "'Sarah Smiles' is about my girlfriend, actually, as sappy as that is," he told <i>Spin</i> in 2011. "When I met her I wrote this song to try and impress her. I was infatuated with her. I played it for her and we've been dating ever since. That was a huge step for me, personally."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-spin_17-2"><a href="#cite_note-spin-17">[17]</a></sup> "Nearly Witches (Ever Since We Met...)" was rejected for inclusion on <i>Pretty. Odd.</i> due it not fitting within the concept of the album; Urie and Smith finished the song for <i>Vices &amp; Virtues</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-McMahon20_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-McMahon20-35">[35]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Promotion">Promotion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vices_%26_Virtues&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Promotion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The album's title and other early information was announced in <i>Alternative Press</i> in its December 2010 issue: it set the album for a March 2011 release, "with the first single tentatively titled 'Mona Lisa' due out in January 2011."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-altpress_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-altpress-36">[36]</a></sup> The band did a promotional <i>Vices &amp; Virtues</i> photoshoot with designer Anthony Franco on the January 8, 2011.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-photoshoot_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-photoshoot-37">[37]</a></sup> On January 18, the band revealed via a post on their website that the record would be released on March 29, 2011,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-releasedate1_18-1"><a href="#cite_note-releasedate1-18">[18]</a></sup> and on January 25, the cover art was revealed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-artwork_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-artwork-38">[38]</a></sup> A behind the scenes video of the photoshoot revealed that <a href="/wiki/Dallon_Weekes" title="Dallon Weekes">Dallon Weekes</a>, a touring only member at the time, featured on the album cover, masked and standing in the background behind Smith and Urie.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> On February 15, 2011, responding to eager public demand, the release date for <i>Vices &amp; Virtues</i> was moved up to March 22 by the band.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-marketplace_40-0"><a href="#cite_note-marketplace-40">[40]</a></sup></p>
<p>The first single "<a href="/wiki/The_Ballad_of_Mona_Lisa" title="The Ballad of Mona Lisa">The Ballad of Mona Lisa</a>" was released on February 1, 2011. The music video for the single was released February 8, 2011 and entered heavy rotation across the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/MTV_Networks" title="MTV Networks">MTV Networks</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-marketplace_40-1"><a href="#cite_note-marketplace-40">[40]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mtvnews3_41-0"><a href="#cite_note-mtvnews3-41">[41]</a></sup> The single received positive reviews, many noting the similarity in sound to <i>A Fever You Can't Sweat Out</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-usa_42-0"><a href="#cite_note-usa-42">[42]</a></sup></p>
<p>The deluxe edition of the album includes the demo "Oh Glory", which was initially released via the band's official website in July 2009 as a 30-second sample.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-si_live_43-0"><a href="#cite_note-si_live-43">[43]</a></sup> Like <i>Pretty. Odd.</i>, <i>Vices &amp; Virtues</i> received heavy popularity through online music stores; by February 2011, the record was already up to the top five overall on the "Top Albums" chart.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-marketplace_40-2"><a href="#cite_note-marketplace-40">[40]</a></sup> Promotion for <i>Vices &amp; Virtues</i> included an appearance on <i><a href="/wiki/Conan_(talk_show)" title="Conan (talk show)">Conan</a></i> a week after the record's release on March 28, and <i><a href="/wiki/Lopez_Tonight" title="Lopez Tonight">Lopez Tonight</a></i> a week after that on April 4, 2011.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-marketplace_40-3"><a href="#cite_note-marketplace-40">[40]</a></sup></p>
<p>In promotion of the record, Smith and Urie filmed a short film to accompany the album, titled <i>The Overture</i>. Directed by Shane Drake, the film was released on March 9, 2011 and contains several musical snippets from the record.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-overture_44-0"><a href="#cite_note-overture-44">[44]</a></sup> The nearly seven-minute mini-film, features Urie and Smith with a score of actors ranging from identical twins to a <a href="/wiki/Dwarfism" title="Dwarfism">little people</a> couple.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-overture1_45-0"><a href="#cite_note-overture1-45">[45]</a></sup> The film is filled with symbolism regarding the departure of Ross and Walker, and the process of moving on without them.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-overture_44-1"><a href="#cite_note-overture-44">[44]</a></sup></p>
<p>In March 2011, the band began streaming the album in its entirety (with the exception of bonus tracks) from the official Panic! at the Disco Facebook page, along with an accompanying introductory video from Urie and Smith.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-facebook_46-0"><a href="#cite_note-facebook-46">[46]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Release">Release</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vices_%26_Virtues&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Release">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><i>Vices &amp; Virtues</i> was released March 18, 2011 in the United States, Brazil, and Canada, as well as multiple other dates for other countries. The record debuted at number seven on the US <a href="/wiki/Billboard_200" title="Billboard 200"><i>Billboard</i> 200</a> chart, with first-week sales of 56,000 copies in the United States.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-marketplacevirtues_47-0"><a href="#cite_note-marketplacevirtues-47">[47]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-chrisbrown_48-0"><a href="#cite_note-chrisbrown-48">[48]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup> This is in stark contrast to the group's previous effort, <i>Pretty. Odd.</i>, which debuted in 2008 with first-week sales of 139,000.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-marketplaceodd_50-0"><a href="#cite_note-marketplaceodd-50">[50]</a></sup> The album also came in second on <a href="/wiki/Nielsen_SoundScan" title="Nielsen SoundScan"><i>Billboard</i></a>'s Alternative Albums chart and fifth on the Digital Albums ranking.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-marketplacevirtues_47-1"><a href="#cite_note-marketplacevirtues-47">[47]</a></sup> As of January 2016, it has sold 194,000 copies in the United States.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup></p>

<p><i>Vices &amp; Virtues</i> has received mixed to positive reviews. At <a href="/wiki/Metacritic" title="Metacritic">Metacritic</a>, which assigns a normalized rating out of 100 to reviews from mainstream critics, the album has received an average score of 62 out of 100, which indicates "generally favourable reviews" based on 15 reviews.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Metacritic_52-1"><a href="#cite_note-Metacritic-52">[52]</a></sup> Jarett Wieselman of the <i><a href="/wiki/New_York_Post" title="New York Post">New York Post</a></i> called the record "one of 2011's best and most captivating albums," calling "Sarah Smiles" the "stand-out" and noting the wise change in tone to high-energy with the second track, "Let's Kill Tonight".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nypost_63-0"><a href="#cite_note-nypost-63">[63]</a></sup> <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alternative_Press_(music_magazine)" title="Alternative Press (music magazine)">Alternative Press</a></i> also welcomed the new version of Panic! at the Disco. "Perhaps Smith and Urie's biggest success on <i>Vices</i> is their disinterest in simply recreating either of their previous albums, instead choosing to meld the youthful exuberance of their debut with the restraint and maturity of 2008's <i>Pretty. Odd.</i>," wrote critic Evan Lucy. "The duo sound absolutely recharged, likely a result of the lineup shuffling, but also perhaps a realization that the '70s were a nice place to visit for a couple years, but life in the 21st century is much more fun."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-apreview_54-1"><a href="#cite_note-apreview-54">[54]</a></sup> He went on to praise the "<a href="/wiki/Danny_Elfman" title="Danny Elfman">Elfman</a>-esque string arrangements, creepy undertones and twinkling pianos" on several tracks and enjoy the "scaled-back," more personal tracks, such as "The Calendar" and "Memories".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-apreview_54-2"><a href="#cite_note-apreview-54">[54]</a></sup> James Montgomery of <a href="/wiki/MTV_News" title="MTV News">MTV News</a> said of the album, "<i>Vices</i> is at its very core a <i>studio</i> album, one stuffed with ideas and instrumentation, which not only positions Panic! as perhaps this generation's <a href="/wiki/Brian_Wilson" title="Brian Wilson">Brian-Wilsons</a>-on-a-budget, but serves as a solid starting point for wherever Urie and Smith choose to go from here." He complimented the new sound of the band: "It's not exactly new, not exactly old, but it's definitely something. Call it a fresh start, a return to form, a re-imaging of the past, just don't call it <i>Pretty. Odd.</i> because that ship has sailed. Panic! are back to their old tricks, exclamation point and all ... and perhaps that was the secret ingredient all along."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mtvreview_64-0"><a href="#cite_note-mtvreview-64">[64]</a></sup></p>
<p>Negative criticism for the album mostly came regarding the inexperienced lyrcism of Urie. William Goodman of <i><a href="/wiki/Spin_(magazine)" title="Spin (magazine)">Spin</a></i> criticized Urie's lyrics of the album ("the result is verbose and generic diary-entry romance") but praised the musical experimentation, remarking, "<i>Vices &amp; Virtues</i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">'</span>s saving grace, though, is the varied instrumentation – <a href="/wiki/Marimba" title="Marimba">marimbas</a>, <a href="/wiki/Xylophone" title="Xylophone">xylophones</a>, <a href="/wiki/Accordion" title="Accordion">accordion</a>, <a href="/wiki/Synthesizer" title="Synthesizer">synths</a>, and digital atmospherics."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-spinreview_61-1"><a href="#cite_note-spinreview-61">[61]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Jody_Rosen" title="Jody Rosen">Jody Rosen</a> of <i><a href="/wiki/Rolling_Stone" title="Rolling Stone">Rolling Stone</a></i> concurred with Goodman and also focused positive opinion upon the arrangements of the album, recommending "Nearly Witches" and calling the album "emo retropop", commenting, "It's the closest emo has come to the sound of old-school pop and rock, with Beach Boys harmonies and even gypsy-style swing flavoring the usual hopped-up confessions."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rstonereview_60-1"><a href="#cite_note-rstonereview-60">[60]</a></sup> Mikael Wood of <i>Entertainment Weekly</i> agreed with these sentiments, recommending "The Ballad of Mona-Lisa" and the "harmony-drenched" "Sarah Smiles".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ewreview_56-1"><a href="#cite_note-ewreview-56">[56]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span id="Vices_&amp;_Virtues_tour"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Vices_.26_Virtues_tour"><i>Vices &amp; Virtues</i> tour</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vices_%26_Virtues&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Vices &amp; Virtues tour">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Vices_%26_Virtues_Tour" title="Vices &amp; Virtues Tour">Vices &amp; Virtues Tour</a></div>
<p>In support of the record, Panic! at the Disco announced multiple tours. Dubbed thus far as "An Intimate Evening with Panic! at the Disco", the short North American tour ran from January 23 to April 4, 2011. A full-fledged European tour took place May 2–17, 2011.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-maytour_65-0"><a href="#cite_note-maytour-65">[65]</a></sup> Panic! had another tour of North America from May 20 to June 30, 2011.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-maytour1_66-0"><a href="#cite_note-maytour1-66">[66]</a></sup> Continuing the album's theme of getting back to their roots, the tour took them back to the small stages they first played during their <i>A Fever You Can't Sweat Out</i> era. Joining them for the jaunt were to be their labelmates <a href="/wiki/Fun_(band)" title="Fun (band)">fun.</a>, with <a href="/wiki/Foxy_Shazam" title="Foxy Shazam">Foxy Shazam</a> and <a href="/wiki/Funeral_Party" title="Funeral Party">Funeral Party</a> splitting duties as the third act on the bill.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-maytour2_67-0"><a href="#cite_note-maytour2-67">[67]</a></sup></p>
<p>The tour sported the same electric, over-the-top theatricality the band was known for during the <i>Fever</i> era. "I really miss wearing costumes and makeup," Urie told <i>Spin</i>. "I love throwing a big production. I've recently been reading about Tesla coils and I'm trying to figure out how I can get one that sits on the stage and shoots sparks without hurting anybody."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-maytour1_66-1"><a href="#cite_note-maytour1-66">[66]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Uses_in_media">Uses in media</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vices_%26_Virtues&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Uses in media">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The song "Ready to Go (Get Me Out of My Mind)" was used as the end credit song for the 2011 film <i><a href="/wiki/The_Smurfs_(film)" title="The Smurfs (film)">The Smurfs</a></i>. It is also featured in three Diet Coke commercials in 2011, a commercial for Sky TV in the UK, a commercial promoting Universal Studios, and commercials for the 20th season of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Amazing_Race_20" title="The Amazing Race 20">The Amazing Race</a></i>. It has been used in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Block_(Australian_TV_series)" title="The Block (Australian TV series)">The Block</a></i> in Australia.</p>
