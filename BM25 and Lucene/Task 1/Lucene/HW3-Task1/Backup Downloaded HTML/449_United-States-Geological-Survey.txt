<p>The <b>United States Geological Survey</b> (<b>USGS</b>, formerly simply <b>Geological Survey</b>) is a scientific <a href="/wiki/Government_agency" title="Government agency">agency</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Federal_government_of_the_United_States" title="Federal government of the United States">United States government</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Scientist" title="Scientist">scientists</a> of the USGS study the <a href="/wiki/Landscape" title="Landscape">landscape</a> of the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>, its <a href="/wiki/Natural_resource" title="Natural resource">natural resources</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Natural_hazard" title="Natural hazard">natural hazards</a> that threaten it. The organization has four major <a href="/wiki/Science" title="Science">science</a> disciplines, concerning <a href="/wiki/Biology" title="Biology">biology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Geography" title="Geography">geography</a>, <a href="/wiki/Geology" title="Geology">geology</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Hydrology" title="Hydrology">hydrology</a>. The USGS is a fact-finding research organization with no regulatory responsibility.</p>
<p>The USGS is a bureau of the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_the_Interior" title="United States Department of the Interior">United States Department of the Interior</a>; it is that department's sole scientific agency. The USGS employs approximately 8,670 people<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AP-20091023_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-AP-20091023-2">[2]</a></sup> and is headquartered in <a href="/wiki/Reston,_Virginia" title="Reston, Virginia">Reston</a>, <a href="/wiki/Virginia" title="Virginia">Virginia</a>. The USGS also has major offices near <a href="/wiki/Lakewood,_Colorado" title="Lakewood, Colorado">Lakewood</a>, <a href="/wiki/Colorado" title="Colorado">Colorado</a>, at the <a href="/wiki/Denver_Federal_Center" title="Denver Federal Center">Denver Federal Center</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Menlo_Park,_California" title="Menlo Park, California">Menlo Park</a>, <a href="/wiki/California" title="California">California</a>.</p>
<p>The current motto of the USGS, in use since August 1997, is "science for a changing world."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> The agency's previous slogan, adopted on the occasion of its hundredth anniversary, was "Earth Science in the Public Service."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=United_States_Geological_Survey&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Prompted by a report from the <a href="/wiki/National_Academy_of_Sciences" title="National Academy of Sciences">National Academy of Sciences</a>, the USGS was created, by a last-minute amendment, to an act of <a href="/wiki/United_States_Congress" title="United States Congress">Congress</a> on March 3, 1879. It was charged with the "classification of the public lands, and examination of the geological structure, mineral resources, and products of the national domain". This task was driven by the need to inventory the vast lands added to the United States by the <a href="/wiki/Louisiana_Purchase" title="Louisiana Purchase">Louisiana Purchase</a> in 1803 and the <a href="/wiki/Mexican%E2%80%93American_War" title="Mexican–American War">Mexican–American War</a> in 1848.</p>
<p>The legislation also provided that the <a href="/wiki/Hayden_Geological_Survey_of_1871" title="Hayden Geological Survey of 1871">Hayden</a>, <a href="/wiki/Powell_Geographic_Expedition_of_1869" title="Powell Geographic Expedition of 1869">Powell</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Wheeler_Survey" title="Wheeler Survey">Wheeler</a> surveys be discontinued as of June 30, 1879.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Clarence_King" title="Clarence King">Clarence King</a>, the first director of USGS, assembled the new organization from disparate regional survey agencies. After a short tenure, King was succeeded in the director's chair by <a href="/wiki/John_Wesley_Powell" title="John Wesley Powell">John Wesley Powell</a>.</p>

<p>Since 2012, the USGS science focus is directed at six topical "Mission Areas",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> namely (1) Climate and Land Use Change, (2) Core Science Systems, (3) Ecosystems, (4) Energy and Minerals and Environmental Health, (5) Natural Hazards, and (6) Water. In December 2012, the USGS split the Energy and Minerals and Environmental Health Mission Area resulting in seven topical Mission Areas, with the two new areas being: <a class="external text" href="http://usgs.gov/energy_minerals" rel="nofollow">Energy and Minerals</a> and <a class="external text" href="http://usgs.gov/envirohealth" rel="nofollow">Environmental Health</a>. Administratively, it is divided into a Headquarters unit and six Regional Units.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Other specific programs include:</p>
<ul>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Earthquake_Hazards_Program" title="Earthquake Hazards Program">Earthquake Hazards Program</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> monitors <a href="/wiki/Earthquake" title="Earthquake">earthquake</a> activity worldwide. The <a href="/wiki/National_Earthquake_Information_Center" title="National Earthquake Information Center">National Earthquake Information Center</a> (NEIC) in <a href="/wiki/Golden,_Colorado" title="Golden, Colorado">Golden, Colorado</a> on the campus of the <a href="/wiki/Colorado_School_of_Mines" title="Colorado School of Mines">Colorado School of Mines</a> detects the location and magnitude of global earthquakes. The USGS also runs or supports several regional monitoring networks in the United States under the umbrella of the Advanced National Seismic System (ANSS).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> The USGS informs authorities, emergency responders, the media, and the public, both domestic and worldwide, about significant earthquakes. It also maintains long-term archives of earthquake data for scientific and engineering research. It also conducts and supports research on long-term <a href="/wiki/Seismic_hazard" title="Seismic hazard">seismic hazards</a>. USGS has released the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/California_earthquake_forecast" title="California earthquake forecast">UCERF California earthquake forecast.</a></li>
<li>As of 2005, the agency is working to create a <a href="/wiki/Volcano_warning_schemes_of_the_United_States" title="Volcano warning schemes of the United States">National Volcano Early Warning System</a> by improving the instrumentation monitoring <a href="/wiki/List_of_volcanoes_in_the_United_States" title="List of volcanoes in the United States">the 169 volcanoes in U.S. territory</a> and by establishing methods for measuring the relative threats posed at each site.</li>
<li>The USGS <a href="/wiki/National_Geomagnetism_Program" title="National Geomagnetism Program">National Geomagnetism Program</a> monitors the <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_field" title="Magnetic field">magnetic field</a> at magnetic observatories and distributes <a href="/wiki/Magnetometer" title="Magnetometer">magnetometer</a> data in real time.</li>
<li>The USGS collaborates with <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canadian</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mexico" title="Mexico">Mexican</a> government scientists, along with the <a href="/wiki/Commission_for_Environmental_Cooperation" title="Commission for Environmental Cooperation">Commission for Environmental Cooperation</a>, to produce the <a href="/wiki/North_American_Environmental_Atlas" title="North American Environmental Atlas">North American Environmental Atlas</a>, which is used to depict and track environmental issues for a continental perspective.</li>
<li>The USGS operates the streamgaging network for the United States, with over 7400 <a href="/wiki/Stream_gauge" title="Stream gauge">streamgages</a>. Real-time streamflow data<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> are available online.</li>
<li>National Climate Change and Wildlife Science Center (NCCWSC)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> implements partner-driven science to improve understanding of past and present land use change, develops relevant climate and land use forecasts, and identifies lands, resources, and communities that are most vulnerable to adverse impacts of change from the local to global scale.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></li>
<li>Since 1962, the <a href="/wiki/Astrogeology_Research_Program" title="Astrogeology Research Program">Astrogeology Research Program</a> has been involved in global, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lunar_exploration" title="Lunar exploration">lunar</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Planetary_exploration" title="Planetary exploration">planetary</a> exploration and <a href="/wiki/Cartography" title="Cartography">mapping</a>.</li>
<li>In collaboration with <a href="/wiki/Stanford_University" title="Stanford University">Stanford University</a>, the USGS also operates the USGS-Stanford Ion Microprobe Laboratory,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> a world-class<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2008)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> analytical facility for U-(Th)-Pb geochronology and trace element analyses of minerals and other earth materials.</li>
<li>USGS operates a number of water related programs, notably the National Streamflow Information Program<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> and National Water-Quality Assessment Program.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> USGS Water data is publicly available from their National Water Information System<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> database.</li>
<li>The USGS also operates the <a href="/wiki/National_Wildlife_Health_Center" title="National Wildlife Health Center">National Wildlife Health Center</a>, whose mission is "to serve the nation and its natural resources by providing sound science and technical support, and to disseminate information to promote science-based decisions affecting wildlife and ecosystem health. The NWHC provides information, technical assistance, research, education, and leadership on national and international wildlife health issues."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> It is the agency primarily responsible for surveillance of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/H5N1" title="H5N1">H5N1</a> <a href="/wiki/Avian_influenza" title="Avian influenza">avian influenza</a> outbreaks in the United States. The USGS also runs 17 biological research centers in the United States, including the <a href="/wiki/Patuxent_Wildlife_Research_Center" title="Patuxent Wildlife Research Center">Patuxent Wildlife Research Center</a>.</li>
<li>The USGS is investigating collaboration with the social networking site <a href="/wiki/Twitter" title="Twitter">Twitter</a> to allow for more rapid construction of ShakeMaps.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-twittereq_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-twittereq-20">[20]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-twittereqblog_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-twittereqblog-21">[21]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Topographic_mapping">Topographic mapping</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=United_States_Geological_Survey&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Topographic mapping">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Mount_Marcy_New_York_USGS_topo_map_1892.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3596" data-file-width="2997" height="204" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2d/Mount_Marcy_New_York_USGS_topo_map_1892.jpg/170px-Mount_Marcy_New_York_USGS_topo_map_1892.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2d/Mount_Marcy_New_York_USGS_topo_map_1892.jpg/255px-Mount_Marcy_New_York_USGS_topo_map_1892.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2d/Mount_Marcy_New_York_USGS_topo_map_1892.jpg/340px-Mount_Marcy_New_York_USGS_topo_map_1892.jpg 2x" width="170"/></a>

<p>The USGS produces several national series of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Topographic_maps" title="Topographic maps">topographic maps</a> which vary in <a href="/wiki/Scale_(map)" title="Scale (map)">scale</a> and extent, with some wide gaps in coverage, notably the complete absence of 1:50,000 scale topographic maps or their equivalent. The largest (both in terms of scale and quantity) and best-known topographic series is the 7.5-minute, 1:24,000 scale, <a href="/wiki/Quadrangle_(geography)" title="Quadrangle (geography)">quadrangle</a>, a non-metric scale virtually unique to the United States. Each of these maps covers an area bounded by two lines of <a href="/wiki/Latitude" title="Latitude">latitude</a> and two lines of <a href="/wiki/Longitude" title="Longitude">longitude</a> spaced 7.5 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Minute_of_arc" title="Minute of arc">minutes</a> apart. Nearly 57,000 individual maps in this series cover the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Continental_United_States" title="Continental United States">48 contiguous states</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hawaii" title="Hawaii">Hawaii</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_States_Territories" title="United States Territories">U.S. territories</a>, and areas of <a href="/wiki/Alaska" title="Alaska">Alaska</a> near Anchorage, Fairbanks, and Prudhoe Bay. The area covered by each map varies with the latitude of its represented location due to convergence of the meridians. At lower latitudes, near 30° north, a 7.5-minute quadrangle contains an area of about 64 square miles (166 km<sup>2</sup>). At 49° north latitude, 49 square miles (127 km<sup>2</sup>) are contained within a quadrangle of that size. As a unique non-metric map scale, the 1:24,000 scale naturally requires a separate and specialized <a href="/wiki/Romer" title="Romer">romer</a> scale for plotting map positions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-USGS_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-USGS-22">[22]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> In recent years, budget constraints have forced the USGS to rely on donations of time by civilian volunteers in an attempt to update its 7.5-minute topographic map series, and USGS stated outright in 2000 that the program was to be phased out in favor of <i><a href="/wiki/The_National_Map" title="The National Map">The National Map</a></i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> (not to be confused with the <a href="/wiki/National_Atlas_of_the_United_States" title="National Atlas of the United States">National Atlas of the United States</a> produced by the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_the_Interior" title="United States Department of the Interior">Department of the Interior</a>, one of whose bureaus is USGS).</p>
<p>An older series of maps, the 15-minute series, was once used to map the contiguous 48 states at a scale of 1:62,500, but was discontinued some time ago for maps covering the continental United States. Each map was bounded by two <a href="/wiki/Circle_of_latitude" title="Circle of latitude">parallels</a> and two <a href="/wiki/Meridian_(geography)" title="Meridian (geography)">meridians</a> spaced 15 minutes apart—the same area covered by four maps in the 7.5-minute series. The 15-minute series, at a scale of 1:63,360 (one inch representing one mile), remains the primary topographic quadrangle for the state of Alaska (and only for that particular state). Nearly 3,000 maps cover 97% of the state.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-USGS_22-1"><a href="#cite_note-USGS-22">[22]</a></sup> The United States remains virtually the only developed country in the world without a standardized civilian topographic map series in the standard 1:25,000 or 1:50,000 metric scales, making coordination difficult in border regions (the U.S. military does issue 1:50,000 scale topo maps of the continental United States, though only for use by members of its defense forces).</p>
<p>The next-smallest topographic series, in terms of scale, is the 1:100,000 series. These maps are bounded by two lines of longitude and two lines of latitude. However, in this series, the lines of latitude are spaced 30 minutes apart and the lines of longitude are spaced 60 minutes, which is the source of another name for these maps; the 30 x 60-minute quadrangle series. Each of these quadrangles covers the area contained within 32 maps in the 7.5-minute series. The 1:100,000 scale series is unusual in that it employs the <a href="/wiki/Metric_system" title="Metric system">Metric system</a> primarily. One centimeter on the map represents one kilometer of distance on the ground. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Contour_interval" title="Contour interval">Contour intervals</a>, spot elevations, and horizontal distances are also specified in meters.</p>
<p>The final regular quadrangle series produced by the USGS is the 1:250,000 scale topographic series. Each of these quadrangles in the conterminous United States measures 1 degree of latitude by 2 degrees of longitude. This series was produced by the U.S. <a href="/wiki/Army_Map_Service" title="Army Map Service">Army Map Service</a> in the 1950s, prior to the maps in the larger-scale series, and consists of 489 sheets, each covering an area ranging from 8,218 square miles (21,285 km<sup>2</sup>) at 30° north to 6,222 square miles (16,115 km<sup>2</sup>) at 49° north.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-USGS_22-2"><a href="#cite_note-USGS-22">[22]</a></sup> Hawaii is mapped at this scale in quadrangles measuring 1° by 1°.</p>
<p>USGS topographic quadrangle maps are marked with grid lines and tics around the map collar which make it possible to identify locations on the map by several methods, including the <a href="/wiki/Geographic_coordinate_system" title="Geographic coordinate system">graticule</a> measurements of longitude and latitude, the <a href="/wiki/Survey_township" title="Survey township">township</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Section_(land)" title="Section (land)">section</a> method within the <a href="/wiki/Public_Land_Survey_System" title="Public Land Survey System">Public Land Survey System</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Cartesian_coordinate_system" title="Cartesian coordinate system">cartesian coordinates</a> in both the <a href="/wiki/State_Plane_Coordinate_System" title="State Plane Coordinate System">State Plane Coordinate System</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Universal_Transverse_Mercator_coordinate_system" title="Universal Transverse Mercator coordinate system">Universal Transverse Mercator coordinate system</a>.</p>
<p>Other specialty maps have been produced by the USGS at a variety of scales. These include <a href="/wiki/County_(United_States)" title="County (United States)">county</a> maps, maps of special interest areas, such as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_States_National_Park" title="United States National Park">national parks</a>, and areas of scientific interest.</p>
<p>A number of Internet sites have made these maps available on the web for affordable commercial and professional use. Because works of the U.S. government are in the <a href="/wiki/Public_domain" title="Public domain">public domain</a>, it is also possible to find many of these maps for free at various locations on the Internet. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Georeference" title="Georeference">Georeferenced</a> map images are available from the USGS as <a href="/wiki/Digital_raster_graphic" title="Digital raster graphic">digital raster graphics</a> (DRGs) in addition to digital data sets based on USGS maps, notably <a href="/wiki/Digital_line_graph" title="Digital line graph">digital line graphs</a> (DLGs) and <a href="/wiki/Digital_elevation_model" title="Digital elevation model">digital elevation models</a> (DEMs).</p>
<p>In 2015, the USGS unveiled the topoView website, a new way to view their entire digitized collection of over 178,000 maps from 1884–2006. The site is an interactive map of the United States that allows users to search or move around the map to find the USGS collection of maps for a specific area. Users may then view the maps in great detail and download them if desired.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="The_National_Map_and_U.S._Topo">The National Map and U.S. Topo</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=United_States_Geological_Survey&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: The National Map and U.S. Topo">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 2008 the USGS abandoned traditional methods of surveying, revising, and updating topographic maps based on aerial photography and field checks.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MOO_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-MOO-26">[26]</a></sup> Today's U.S. Topo quadrangle (1:24,000) maps are mass-produced, using automated and semiautomated processes, with cartographic content supplied from the National GIS Database.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MOO_26-1"><a href="#cite_note-MOO-26">[26]</a></sup> In the two years from June 2009 to May 2011, the USGS produced nearly 40,000 maps, more than 80 maps per work day.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MOO_26-2"><a href="#cite_note-MOO-26">[26]</a></sup> Only about two hours of interactive work are spent on each map, mostly on text placement and final inspection; there are essentially no field checks or field inspections to confirm map details.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MOO_26-3"><a href="#cite_note-MOO-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<p>While much less expensive to compile and produce, the revised digital U.S. topo maps have been criticized for a lack of accuracy and detail in comparison to older generation maps based on aerial photo survey and field checks.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MOO_26-4"><a href="#cite_note-MOO-26">[26]</a></sup> As the digital databases were not designed for producing general purpose maps, data integration can be a problem when retrieved from sources with different resolutions and collection dates.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MOO_26-5"><a href="#cite_note-MOO-26">[26]</a></sup> Man-made features once recorded by direct field observation are not in any public domain national database, and are frequently omitted from the newest generation digital topo maps, including windmills, mines and mineshafts, water tanks, fence lines, survey marks, parks, recreational trails, buildings, boundaries, pipelines, telephone lines, power transmission lines, and even railroads.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MOO_26-6"><a href="#cite_note-MOO-26">[26]</a></sup> Additionally, the digital map's use of existing software may not properly integrate different feature classes or prioritize and organize text in areas of crowded features, obscuring important geographic details.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MOO_26-7"><a href="#cite_note-MOO-26">[26]</a></sup> As a result, some have noted that the U.S. Topo maps currently fall short of traditional topographic map presentation standards achieved in maps drawn from 1945 to 1992.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MOO_26-8"><a href="#cite_note-MOO-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="USGS_Hydrologic_Instrumentation_Facility">USGS Hydrologic Instrumentation Facility</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=United_States_Geological_Survey&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: USGS Hydrologic Instrumentation Facility">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Hydrologic Instrumentation Facility (HIF) has four sections within its organizational structure;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> the Field Services Section which includes the warehouse, repair shop, and Engineering Unit; the Testing Section which includes the Hydraulic Laboratory, testing chambers, and Water Quality Laboratory; the Information Technology Section which includes computer support and the Drafting Unit; and the Administrative Section.</p>
<p>The HIF was given national responsibility for the design, testing, evaluation, repair, calibration, warehousing, and distribution of hydrologic instrumentation. Distribution is accomplished by direct sales and through a rental program. The HIF supports data collection activities through centralized warehouse and laboratory facilities. The HIF warehouse provides hydrologic instruments, equipment, and supplies for USGS as well as Other Federal Agencies (OFA) and USGS Cooperators. The HIF also tests, evaluates, repairs, calibrates, and develops hydrologic equipment and instruments. The HIF Hydraulic Laboratory facilities include a towing tank, jet tank, pipe flow facility, and tilting flume. In addition, the HIF provides training and technical support for the equipment it stocks.</p>
<p>The Engineering Group seeks out new technology and designs for instrumentation that can work more efficiently, be more accurate, and or be produced at a lower cost than existing instrumentation. HIF works directly with <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Forest_Technology_Systems&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Forest Technology Systems (page does not exist)">vendors</a> to help them produce products that will meet the mission needs of the USGS. For instrument needs not currently met by a vendor, the Engineering Group designs, tests, and issues contracts to have HIF designed equipment made. Sometimes HIF will patent a new design in the hope that instrument vendors will buy the rights and mass-produce the instrument at a lower cost to everyone.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="USGS_publications">USGS publications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=United_States_Geological_Survey&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: USGS publications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>USGS researchers publish the results of their science in a variety of ways. Many researchers publish their science in peer-reviewed scientific journals as well as in one of a variety of series that includes series for preliminary results, maps data, and final results. These series include:</p>
<dl>
<dd>
<ul>
<li><b>Biological Science Report (BSR)</b>: Record significant scientific interpretations and findings, usually of lasting scientific interest, addressing a wide variety of topics relevant to Biological Resources Discipline (BRD) investigations and research. May include extensive data or theoretical analyses. Reports published by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._Biological_Survey" title="U.S. Biological Survey">U.S. Biological Survey</a> and later by the U.S. Geological Survey. The report series began in 1995 and continued through 2003.</li>
</ul>
</dd>
<dd>
<ul>
<li><b>Bulletin (B)</b>: Significant data and interpretations of lasting scientific interest but generally narrower in scope than professional papers. Results of resource studies, geologic or topographic studies, and collections of short papers on related topics.</li>
</ul>
</dd>
<dd>
<ul>
<li><b>Circular (CIR/C)</b>: A wide variety of topics covered concisely and clearly to provide a synthesis of understanding about processes, geographic areas, issues, or USGS programs. The Circular should be aimed at enhancing knowledge and understanding among general audiences, decision makers, university students, and scientists in related fields.</li>
</ul>
</dd>
<dd>
<ul>
<li><b>Circum-Pacific Map (CP)</b>: Multicolor equal-area maps at scales of 1:10,000,000 for the Northwest, Northeast, Southwest, Southeast quadrants of the Pacific and the <a href="/wiki/Arctic" title="Arctic">Arctic</a> and Antarctic regions, and of 1:17,000,000 for the whole Pacific Basin. The series consists of base, geographic, geodynamic, plate-tectonic, geologic, tectonic, mineral-resources, and energy-resources maps, as well as other miscellaneous maps.</li>
</ul>
</dd>
<dd>
<ul>
<li><b>Coal Investigations (COAL/C-) Map</b>: Origin, character, and resource potential of coal deposits shown by geologic maps, structure contours, cross sections, columnar sections, and measured coal sections, where appropriate. Text on same sheet or in an accompanying pamphlet.</li>
</ul>
</dd>
<dd>
<ul>
<li><b>Data Series (DS)</b>: The Data Series is intended for release of basic data sets, databases, and multimedia or motion graphics. This series can be used for videos, computer programs, and collections of digital photographs.</li>
</ul>
</dd>
<dd>
<ul>
<li><b>General Interest Publication (GIP)</b>: A wide variety of topics covered concisely and clearly in a variety of formats. Focus is on USGS programs, projects, and services and general scientific information of public interest. The series covers a broad range of topics in a variety of media, including pamphlets, postcards, posters, videos, teacher kits, CD/DVDs, bookmarks, and interactive and motion graphics. Previously called "General Interest Publications".</li>
</ul>
</dd>
<dd>
<ul>
<li><b>Geologic Quadrangle (GQ) Map</b>: Detailed geologic maps depicting areas of special importance to the solution of geologic problems. May portray bedrock or surficial units, or both. May include brief texts, structure sections, and columnar sections. 71/2- or 15-minute quadrangles printed in multicolor on topographic bases that meet National Map Accuracy standards.</li>
</ul>
</dd>
<dd>
<ul>
<li><b>Geophysical Investigations (GP) Map</b>: Chiefly the results of aeromagnetic and (or) gravity surveys shown by contours. Area depicted may range in size from a few square miles to an entire country. Single or multiple sheets.</li>
</ul>
</dd>
<dd>
<ul>
<li><b>Land Use and Land Cover (L) Map</b>: Various categories of land use and cover, both artificial and natural, for use by geographers, land-use planners, and others. Planimetric maps at scales of 1:250,000 or 1:100,000 on a single sheet.</li>
</ul>
</dd>
<dd>
<ul>
<li><b>Mineral Investigations Resource (MR) Map</b>: Information on mineral occurrences, mineral resources, mines and prospects, commodities, and target areas of possible resources <b>other than</b> coal, petroleum, or natural gas. Small scale (1:250,000 or smaller).</li>
</ul>
</dd>
<dd>
<ul>
<li><b>Miscellaneous Field Studies (MF) Map</b>: Rapidly prepared, low-budget maps in a broad range of presentations in terms of portrayal, completeness, interpretations, draftsmanship, scale, and area coverage. Single or multiple sheets.</li>
</ul>
</dd>
<dd>
<ul>
<li><b>Miscellaneous Investigations/ Geologic Investigations (I) Series</b>: High-quality maps and charts of varied subject matter such as bathymetry, geology, hydrogeology, landforms, land-use classification, vegetation, and others including maps of planets, the Moon, and other satellites. Various scales. Topographic or planimetric bases; regular or irregular areas. May include a text printed as an accompanying pamphlet.</li>
</ul>
</dd>
<dd>
<ul>
<li><b>Oil and Gas Investigations (OC) Chart</b>: Information about known or possible petroleum resources, presented as logs, correlation diagrams, graphs, and tables, but ordinarily not as maps. Single or multiple sheets. Text printed on same sheet or in an accompanying pamphlet.</li>
</ul>
</dd>
<dd>
<ul>
<li><b>Oil and Gas Investigations (OM) Map</b>: Apply particularly to areas of known or possible petroleum resources. Typically include cross sections, columnar sections, structure contours, correlation diagrams, and information on wells drilled for oil and gas. Single or multiple sheets. Text usually on map sheet but sometimes printed as an accompanying pamphlet.</li>
</ul>
</dd>
<dd>
<ul>
<li><b>Open-File Report (OFR/OF)</b>: Interpretive information that needs to be released immediately; maps and reports (and their supporting data) that need to be released as supporting documentation because they are referenced, discussed, or interpreted in another information product; preliminary findings (pending a final map or report); interim computer programs and user guides; bibliographies.</li>
</ul>
</dd>
<dd>
<ul>
<li><b>Professional Paper (PP)</b>: <b>Premier series</b> of the USGS. Comprehensive reports of wide and lasting interest and scientific importance, characterized by thoroughness of study and breadth of scientific or geographic coverage. The series may include collections of related papers addressing different aspects of a single scientific topic, either issued together under one cover or separately as chapters.</li>
</ul>
</dd>
<dd>
<ul>
<li><b>Water-Resources Investigations Report (WRIR/WRI)</b>: Hydrologic information, mainly of local interest, intended for quick release. Book or map format. Varied scales.</li>
</ul>
</dd>
<dd>
<ul>
<li><b>Water-Supply Paper (WSP)</b>: Reports on all aspects of hydrology, including quality, recoverability, and use of water resources; statistical reports on streamflow, floods, groundwater levels, and water quality; and collections of short papers on related topics.</li>
</ul>
</dd>
</dl>
<p>A complete listing of descriptions of USGS Series is available at the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Alaska_Division_of_Geological_%26_Geophysical_Surveys&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Alaska Division of Geological &amp; Geophysical Surveys (page does not exist)">Alaska Division of Geological &amp; Geophysical Surveys</a> (ADGGS) website.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Locating_USGS_publications">Locating USGS publications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=United_States_Geological_Survey&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Locating USGS publications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/United_States_Geological_Survey_Library" title="United States Geological Survey Library">United States Geological Survey Library</a> holds copies of current and historical USGS publications, and is the largest earth sciences library in the world. Most publications are available for inter-library loan within the United States. Under the Organic Act, which provided for the formation of the USGS, the library was given extra copies of all USGS publications when published to be used in exchange with other domestic and foreign geological agencies, making the acquisition of the USGS Library collection one of the most cost efficient libraries in the U.S. government.</p>
<p>USGS publications are available for purchase at USGS Store.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> Many USGS published reports are available to view and access on-line from the USGS Publications Warehouse,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> while many USGS publications are now available online (see <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/External_links#Publications" title="External links">Publications</a> below).</p>
<p>Many older USGS publications have been scanned and digitized by such services as <a href="/wiki/Google_Books" title="Google Books">Google Books</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hathi_Trust" title="Hathi Trust">Hathi Trust</a> and <a href="/wiki/Internet_Archive" title="Internet Archive">Internet Archive</a>. An online search will quickly reveal if a digital version is available. All USGS publications are <a href="/wiki/Public_domain" title="Public domain">public domain</a>.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Non-USGS_related_websites">Non-USGS related websites</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=United_States_Geological_Survey&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Non-USGS related websites">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul>
<li>The <a class="external text" href="http://libre.redjar.org/maps/" rel="nofollow">Libre Map Project</a> offers free, high-quality USGS DRG maps in TIFF format along with world files for use with your GIS software</li>
<li><a class="external text" href="http://msrmaps.com/" rel="nofollow">Microsoft Research Maps</a> (formerly TerraServer-USA) and <a class="external text" href="http://mapper.acme.com/" rel="nofollow">Acme</a> host USGS topographic maps (and aerial photos on <a href="/wiki/Microsoft_Research_Maps" title="Microsoft Research Maps">Microsoft Research Maps</a>);</li>
<li><a class="external text" href="http://historical.mytopo.com/" rel="nofollow">Mytopo historical maps</a> hosts historical USGS topos in the northeast U.S.</li>
<li><a class="external text" href="http://www.usgsquads.com/index.php/map-indexes/mapfinder/" rel="nofollow">Mapfinder Utility</a> download USGS Topographic maps for free in Tiff format using Google Earth.</li>
<li>The <a class="external text" href="http://mazamascience.com/Minerals/USGS/" rel="nofollow">US Minerals Databrowser</a> provides interactive access to data visualizations based on data from USGS DS140: "Historical Statistics for Mineral Commodities"</li>
<li>U.S. Geological <a class="external text" href="https://ttu-ir.tdl.org/ttu-ir/handle/2346/61588" rel="nofollow">Survey Documents at Texas Tech University 1873–2015</a></li>
