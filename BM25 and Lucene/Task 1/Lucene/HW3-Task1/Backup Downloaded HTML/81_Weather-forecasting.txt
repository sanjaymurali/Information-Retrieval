<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Weather forecasting</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

<p><b>Weather forecasting</b> is the application of science and technology to predict the conditions of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Earth%27s_atmosphere" title="Earth's atmosphere">atmosphere</a> for a given location and time. Human beings have attempted to predict the <a href="/wiki/Weather" title="Weather">weather</a> informally for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Millennia" title="Millennia">millennia</a> and formally since the <a href="/wiki/19th_century" title="19th century">19th century</a>. Weather forecasts are made by collecting quantitative <a href="/wiki/Data" title="Data">data</a> about the current state of the atmosphere at a given place and using <a href="/wiki/Meteorology" title="Meteorology">scientific understanding</a> of atmospheric processes to project how the atmosphere will change.</p>
<p>Once a human-only endeavor based mainly upon changes in <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_pressure" title="Atmospheric pressure">barometric pressure</a>, current weather conditions, and sky condition, weather forecasting now relies on <a href="/wiki/Numerical_weather_prediction" title="Numerical weather prediction">computer-based models</a> that take many atmospheric factors into account.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> Human input is still required to pick the best possible forecast model to base the forecast upon, which involves pattern recognition skills, <a href="/wiki/Teleconnection" title="Teleconnection">teleconnections</a>, knowledge of model performance, and knowledge of model biases. The inaccuracy of forecasting is due to the <a href="/wiki/Chaos_theory" title="Chaos theory">chaotic</a> nature of the atmosphere, the massive computational power required to solve the equations that describe the atmosphere, the error involved in measuring the initial conditions, and an incomplete understanding of atmospheric processes. Hence, forecasts become less accurate as the difference between current time and the time for which the forecast is being made (the <i>range</i> of the forecast) increases. The use of ensembles and model consensus help narrow the error and pick the most likely outcome.</p>
<p>There are a variety of end uses to weather forecasts. Weather warnings are important forecasts because they are used to protect life and property. Forecasts based on <a href="/wiki/Temperature" title="Temperature">temperature</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Precipitation_(meteorology)" title="Precipitation (meteorology)">precipitation</a> are important to <a href="/wiki/Agriculture" title="Agriculture">agriculture</a>, and therefore to traders within commodity markets. Temperature forecasts are used by utility companies to estimate demand over coming days. On an everyday basis, people use weather forecasts to determine what to wear on a given day. Since outdoor activities are severely curtailed by heavy rain, snow and <a href="/wiki/Wind_chill" title="Wind chill">wind chill</a>, forecasts can be used to plan activities around these events, and to plan ahead and survive them. In 2014, the US spent $5.1 billion on weather forecasting.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Weather_forecasting&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ancient_forecasting">Ancient forecasting</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Weather_forecasting&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Ancient forecasting">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>For millennia people have tried to forecast the weather. In 650 BC, the <a href="/wiki/Babylonia" title="Babylonia">Babylonians</a> predicted the weather from cloud patterns as well as <a href="/wiki/Astrology" title="Astrology">astrology</a>. In about 350 BC, <a href="/wiki/Aristotle" title="Aristotle">Aristotle</a> described weather patterns in <i><a href="/wiki/Meteorology_(Aristotle)" title="Meteorology (Aristotle)">Meteorologica</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Later, <a href="/wiki/Theophrastus" title="Theophrastus">Theophrastus</a> compiled a book on weather forecasting, called the <i>Book of Signs</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">Chinese</a> weather prediction lore extends at least as far back as 300 BC,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> which was also around the same time ancient <a href="/wiki/Indian_astronomy" title="Indian astronomy">Indian astronomers</a> developed weather-prediction methods.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> In New Testament times, Christ himself referred to deciphering and understanding local weather patterns, by saying, "When evening comes, you say, 'It will be fair weather, for the sky is red', and in the morning, 'Today it will be stormy, for the sky is red and overcast.' You know how to interpret the appearance of the sky, but you cannot interpret the signs of the times."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 904 AD, <a href="/wiki/Ibn_Wahshiyya" title="Ibn Wahshiyya">Ibn Wahshiyya</a>'s <i>Nabatean Agriculture</i> discussed the weather forecasting of atmospheric changes and signs from the planetary astral alterations; signs of rain based on observation of the <a href="/wiki/Lunar_phase" title="Lunar phase">lunar phases</a>; and weather forecasts based on the movement of winds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>Ancient weather forecasting methods usually relied on observed patterns of events, also termed pattern recognition. For example, it might be observed that if the sunset was particularly red, the following day often brought fair weather. This experience accumulated over the generations to produce <a href="/wiki/Weather_lore" title="Weather lore">weather lore</a>. However, not all<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Avoid_weasel_words" title="Wikipedia:Avoid weasel words"><span title="The material near this tag possibly uses too vague attribution or weasel words. (April 2015)">which?</span></a></i>]</sup> of these predictions prove reliable, and many of them have since been found not to stand up to rigorous statistical testing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Modern_methods">Modern methods</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Weather_forecasting&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Modern methods">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:StateLibQld_1_186783_Royal_Charter_(ship).jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="633" data-file-width="1000" height="190" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4e/StateLibQld_1_186783_Royal_Charter_%28ship%29.jpg/300px-StateLibQld_1_186783_Royal_Charter_%28ship%29.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4e/StateLibQld_1_186783_Royal_Charter_%28ship%29.jpg/450px-StateLibQld_1_186783_Royal_Charter_%28ship%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4e/StateLibQld_1_186783_Royal_Charter_%28ship%29.jpg/600px-StateLibQld_1_186783_Royal_Charter_%28ship%29.jpg 2x" width="300"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:StateLibQld_1_186783_Royal_Charter_(ship).jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<center>The <i>Royal Charter</i> sank in an 1859 storm, stimulating the establishment of modern weather forecasting.</center>
</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>It was not until the invention of the <a href="/wiki/Electrical_telegraph" title="Electrical telegraph">electric telegraph</a> in 1835 that the modern age of weather forecasting began.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Before that, the fastest that distant weather reports could travel was around 100 miles per day (160 km/d), but was more typically 40–75 miles per day (60–120 km/day) (whether by land or by sea).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-USPS_History_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-USPS_History-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Royal_Mail_coaches_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-Royal_Mail_coaches-12">[12]</a></sup> By the late 1840s, the telegraph allowed reports of weather conditions from a wide area to be received almost instantaneously,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> allowing forecasts to be made from knowledge of weather conditions further <a href="/wiki/Windward_and_leeward" title="Windward and leeward">upwind</a>.</p>
<p>The two men credited with the birth of forecasting as a science were officer of the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Navy" title="Royal Navy">Royal Navy</a> <a href="/wiki/Francis_Beaufort" title="Francis Beaufort">Francis Beaufort</a> and his <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Prot%C3%A9g%C3%A9" title="Protégé">protégé</a> <a href="/wiki/Robert_FitzRoy" title="Robert FitzRoy">Robert FitzRoy</a>. Both were influential men in <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_of_Great_Britain_and_Ireland" title="United Kingdom of Great Britain and Ireland">British</a> naval and governmental circles, and though ridiculed in the press at the time, their work gained scientific credence, was accepted by the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Navy" title="Royal Navy">Royal Navy</a>, and formed the basis for all of today's weather forecasting knowledge.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BBC_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-BBC-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>Beaufort developed the <a href="/wiki/Beaufort_scale" title="Beaufort scale">Wind Force Scale</a> and Weather Notation coding, which he was to use in his journals for the remainder of his life. He also promoted the development of reliable tide tables around British shores, and with his friend <a href="/wiki/William_Whewell" title="William Whewell">William Whewell</a>, expanded weather record-keeping at 200 British <a href="/wiki/Coast_guard" title="Coast guard">Coast guard</a> stations.</p>
<p>Robert FitzRoy was appointed in 1854 as chief of a new department within the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Board_of_Trade" title="Board of Trade">Board of Trade</a> to deal with the collection of weather data at sea as a service to <a href="/wiki/Sailor" title="Sailor">mariners</a>. This was the forerunner of the modern <a href="/wiki/Met_Office" title="Met Office">Meteorological Office</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BBC_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-BBC-15">[15]</a></sup> All ship captains were tasked with collating data on the weather and computing it, with the use of tested instruments that were loaned for this purpose.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mellersh.2C_H._E._L._1968_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-Mellersh.2C_H._E._L._1968-16">[16]</a></sup></p>

<p>A storm in 1859 that caused the loss of the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Charter_(ship)" title="Royal Charter (ship)"><i>Royal Charter</i></a> inspired FitzRoy to develop charts to allow predictions to be made, which he called <i>"forecasting the weather"</i>, thus coining the term "weather forecast".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mellersh.2C_H._E._L._1968_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-Mellersh.2C_H._E._L._1968-16">[16]</a></sup> Fifteen land stations were established to use the new <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Telegraph" title="Telegraph">telegraph</a> to transmit to him daily reports of weather at set times leading to the first gale warning service. His warning service for shipping was initiated in February 1861, with the use of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electric_telegraph" title="Electric telegraph">telegraph communications</a>. The first daily weather forecasts were published in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Times" title="The Times">The Times</a></i> in 1861.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BBC_15-2"><a href="#cite_note-BBC-15">[15]</a></sup> In the following year a system was introduced of hoisting storm warning cones at the principal ports when a gale was expected.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kington_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kington-17">[17]</a></sup> The <i>"Weather Book"</i> which FitzRoy published in 1863 was far in advance of the scientific opinion of the time.</p>
<p>As the electric telegraph network expanded, allowing for the more rapid dissemination of warnings, a national observational network was developed which could then be used to provide synoptic analyses. Instruments to continuously record variations in meteorological parameters using <a href="/wiki/Photography#Science_and_forensics" title="Photography">photography</a> were supplied to the observing stations from <a href="/wiki/King%27s_Observatory" title="King's Observatory">Kew Observatory</a> – these cameras had been invented by <a href="/wiki/Francis_Ronalds" title="Francis Ronalds">Francis Ronalds</a> in 1845 and his <a href="/wiki/Barograph" title="Barograph">barograph</a> had earlier been used by FitzRoy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>To convey accurate information, it soon became necessary to have a standard vocabulary describing clouds; this was achieved by means of a series of classifications first achieved by <a href="/wiki/Luke_Howard" title="Luke Howard">Luke Howard</a> in 1802, and standardized in the <i><a href="/wiki/International_Cloud_Atlas" title="International Cloud Atlas">International Cloud Atlas</a></i> of 1896.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Numerical_prediction">Numerical prediction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Weather_forecasting&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Numerical prediction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/History_of_numerical_weather_prediction" title="History of numerical weather prediction">History of numerical weather prediction</a></div>
<p>It was not until the 20th century that advances in the understanding of atmospheric physics led to the foundation of modern <a href="/wiki/Numerical_weather_prediction" title="Numerical weather prediction">numerical weather prediction</a>. In 1922, English scientist <a href="/wiki/Lewis_Fry_Richardson" title="Lewis Fry Richardson">Lewis Fry Richardson</a> published "Weather Prediction By Numerical Process",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> after finding notes and derivations he worked on as an ambulance driver in World War I. He described therein how small terms in the prognostic fluid dynamics equations governing atmospheric flow could be neglected, and a finite differencing scheme in time and space could be devised, to allow numerical prediction solutions to be found.</p>
<p>Richardson envisioned a large auditorium of thousands of people performing the calculations and passing them to others. However, the sheer number of calculations required was too large to be completed without the use of computers, and the size of the grid and time steps led to unrealistic results in deepening systems. It was later found, through numerical analysis, that this was due to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Numerical_instability" title="Numerical instability">numerical instability</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> The first computerised weather forecast was performed by a team composed of American meteorologists <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jule_Charney" title="Jule Charney">Jule Charney</a>, Philip Thompson, Larry Gates, and Norwegian meteorologist <a href="/wiki/Ragnar_Fj%C3%B8rtoft" title="Ragnar Fjørtoft">Ragnar Fjørtoft</a>, applied mathematician <a href="/wiki/John_von_Neumann" title="John von Neumann">John von Neumann</a>, and <a href="/wiki/ENIAC" title="ENIAC">ENIAC</a> programmer <a href="/wiki/Klara_Dan_von_Neumann" title="Klara Dan von Neumann">Klara Dan von Neumann</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> Practical use of numerical weather prediction began in 1955,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> spurred by the development of programmable electronic <a href="/wiki/Computer" title="Computer">computers</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Broadcasts">Broadcasts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Weather_forecasting&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Broadcasts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>The first ever daily weather forecasts were published in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Times" title="The Times">The Times</a></i> on 1 August 1861, and the first <a href="/wiki/Weather_map" title="Weather map">weather maps</a> were produced later in the same year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> In 1911, the <a href="/wiki/Met_Office" title="Met Office">Met Office</a> began issuing the first marine weather forecasts via radio transmission. These included gale and storm warnings for areas around Great Britain.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> In the United States, the first public radio forecasts were made in 1925 by Edward B. "E.B." Rideout, on <a href="/wiki/WEZE" title="WEZE">WEEI</a>, the Edison Electric Illuminating station in Boston.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cyc_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-cyc-28">[28]</a></sup> Rideout came from the <a href="/wiki/National_Weather_Service" title="National Weather Service">U.S. Weather Bureau</a>, as did <a href="/wiki/WBZ_(AM)" title="WBZ (AM)">WBZ</a> weather forecaster G. Harold Noyes in 1931.</p>
<p>The world's first <a href="/wiki/Live_television" title="Live television">televised</a> weather forecasts, including the use of weather maps, were experimentally broadcast by the <a href="/wiki/BBC" title="BBC">BBC</a> in 1936. This was brought into practice in 1949 after <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>. <a href="/wiki/George_Cowling" title="George Cowling">George Cowling</a> gave the first weather forecast while being televised in front of the map in 1954.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HUNT_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-HUNT-30">[30]</a></sup> In America, experimental television forecasts were made by James C Fidler in Cincinnati in either 1940 or 1947 on the <a href="/wiki/DuMont_Television_Network" title="DuMont Television Network">DuMont Television Network</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cyc_28-1"><a href="#cite_note-cyc-28">[28]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> In the late 1970s and early 80s, <a href="/wiki/John_Coleman_(news_weathercaster)" title="John Coleman (news weathercaster)">John Coleman</a>, the first weatherman on ABC-TV's <a href="/wiki/Good_Morning_America" title="Good Morning America">Good Morning America</a>, pioneered the use of on-screen <a href="/wiki/Weather_satellite" title="Weather satellite">weather satellite</a> information and <a href="/wiki/Computer_graphics" title="Computer graphics">computer graphics</a> for television forecasts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CJR_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-CJR-32">[32]</a></sup> Coleman was a co-founder of <a href="/wiki/The_Weather_Channel" title="The Weather Channel">The Weather Channel</a> (TWC) in 1982. TWC is now a 24-hour cable network. Some weather channels have started broadcasting on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Live_broadcasting" title="Live broadcasting">live broadcasting programs</a> such as <a href="/wiki/YouTube" title="YouTube">YouTube</a> and <a href="/wiki/Periscope_(app)" title="Periscope (app)">Periscope</a> to reach more viewers. One Meteorologist, Jeff Matthews, transformed the medium from forecasting live during television broadcasts to forecasting live on Periscope under the name of "<a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=CNY_Weathertainment&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="CNY Weathertainment (page does not exist)">Weathertainment</a>", becoming the first meteorologist to deliver daily forecasts on a life-streaming application...and thus, making weather forecasting, interactive, for the public.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="How_models_create_forecasts">How models create forecasts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Weather_forecasting&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: How models create forecasts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Numerical_weather_prediction" title="Numerical weather prediction">Numerical weather prediction</a></div>
<p>The basic idea of numerical weather prediction is to sample the state of the fluid at a given time and use the equations of <a href="/wiki/Fluid_dynamics" title="Fluid dynamics">fluid dynamics</a> and <a href="/wiki/Thermodynamics" title="Thermodynamics">thermodynamics</a> to estimate the state of the fluid at some time in the future. The main inputs from country-based weather services are surface observations from automated <a href="/wiki/Weather_station" title="Weather station">weather stations</a> at ground level over land and from weather buoys at sea. The <a href="/wiki/World_Meteorological_Organization" title="World Meteorological Organization">World Meteorological Organization</a> acts to standardize the instrumentation, observing practices and timing of these observations worldwide. Stations either report hourly in <a href="/wiki/METAR" title="METAR">METAR</a> reports,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> or every six hours in <a href="/wiki/SYNOP" title="SYNOP">SYNOP</a> reports.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> Sites launch <a href="/wiki/Radiosonde" title="Radiosonde">radiosondes</a>, which rise through the depth of the <a href="/wiki/Troposphere" title="Troposphere">troposphere</a> and well into the <a href="/wiki/Stratosphere" title="Stratosphere">stratosphere</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> Data from <a href="/wiki/Weather_satellite" title="Weather satellite">weather satellites</a> are used in areas where traditional data sources are not available.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> Compared with similar data from radiosondes, the satellite data has the advantage of global coverage, however at a lower accuracy and resolution.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Weather_radar" title="Weather radar">Meteorological radar</a> provide information on precipitation location and intensity, which can be used to estimate precipitation accumulations over time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> Additionally, if a <a href="/wiki/Pulse-Doppler_radar" title="Pulse-Doppler radar">pulse Doppler</a> <a href="/wiki/Weather_radar" title="Weather radar">weather radar</a> is used then wind speed and direction can be determined.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tright">

Modern weather predictions aid in timely evacuations and potentially save lives and prevent property damage</div>

<p>Commerce provides <a href="/wiki/Pilot_report" title="Pilot report">pilot reports</a> along aircraft routes,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> and ship reports along shipping routes. Research flights using <a href="/wiki/Weather_reconnaissance" title="Weather reconnaissance">reconnaissance aircraft</a> fly in and around weather systems of interest such as <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone" title="Tropical cyclone">tropical cyclones</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hurricane_Hunters_43-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hurricane_Hunters-43">[43]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SunHerald_44-0"><a href="#cite_note-SunHerald-44">[44]</a></sup> Reconnaissance aircraft are also flown over the open oceans during the cold season into systems which cause significant uncertainty in forecast guidance, or are expected to be of high impact 3–7 days into the future over the downstream continent.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup></p>
<p>Models are <i>initialized</i> using this observed data. The irregularly spaced observations are processed by <a href="/wiki/Data_assimilation" title="Data assimilation">data assimilation</a> and objective analysis methods, which perform quality control and obtain values at locations usable by the model's mathematical algorithms (usually an evenly spaced grid). The data are then used in the model as the starting point for a forecast.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup> Commonly, the set of equations used to predict the known as the physics and dynamics of the atmosphere are called <a href="/wiki/Primitive_equations" title="Primitive equations">primitive equations</a>. These equations are initialized from the analysis data and rates of change are determined. The rates of change predict the state of the atmosphere a short time into the future. The equations are then applied to this new atmospheric state to find new rates of change, and these new rates of change predict the atmosphere at a yet further time into the future. This <i>time stepping</i> procedure is continually repeated until the solution reaches the desired forecast time. The length of the time step is related to the distance between the points on the computational grid.</p>
<p>The length of the time step chosen within the model is related to the distance between the points on the computational grid, and is chosen to maintain <a href="/wiki/Numerical_stability" title="Numerical stability">numerical stability</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup> Time steps for global models are on the order of tens of minutes,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup> while time steps for regional models are between one and four minutes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup> The global models are run at varying times into the future. The <a href="/wiki/Met_Office" title="Met Office">Met Office</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Unified_Model" title="Unified Model">Unified Model</a> is run six days into the future,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-models_50-0"><a href="#cite_note-models-50">[50]</a></sup> the <a href="/wiki/European_Centre_for_Medium-Range_Weather_Forecasts" title="European Centre for Medium-Range Weather Forecasts">European Centre for Medium-Range Weather Forecasts</a> model is run out to 10 days into the future,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup> while the <a href="/wiki/Global_Forecast_System" title="Global Forecast System">Global Forecast System</a> model run by the <a href="/wiki/Environmental_Modeling_Center" title="Environmental Modeling Center">Environmental Modeling Center</a> is run 16 days into the future.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup> The visual output produced by a model solution is known as a <a href="/wiki/Prognostic_chart" title="Prognostic chart">prognostic chart</a>, or <i>prog</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup> The raw output is often modified before being presented as the forecast. This can be in the form of statistical techniques to remove known <a href="/wiki/Bias" title="Bias">biases</a> in the model, or of adjustment to take into account consensus among other numerical weather forecasts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup> MOS or model output statistics is a technique used to interpret numerical model output and produce site-specific guidance. This guidance is presented in coded numerical form, and can be obtained for nearly all National Weather Service reporting stations in the United States. As proposed by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Edward_Lorenz" title="Edward Lorenz">Edward Lorenz</a> in 1963, long range forecasts, those made at a range of two weeks or more, are impossible to definitively predict the state of the atmosphere, owing to the <a href="/wiki/Chaos_theory" title="Chaos theory">chaotic nature</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Fluid_dynamics" title="Fluid dynamics">fluid dynamics</a> equations involved. In numerical models, extremely small errors in initial values double roughly every five days for variables such as temperature and wind velocity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup></p>
<p>Essentially, a model is a computer program that produces <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Meteorological" title="Meteorological">meteorological</a> information for future times at given locations and altitudes. Within any modern model is a set of equations, known as the primitive equations, used to predict the future state of the atmosphere.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup> These equations—along with the <a href="/wiki/Ideal_gas_law" title="Ideal gas law">ideal gas law</a>—are used to evolve the <a href="/wiki/Density" title="Density">density</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pressure" title="Pressure">pressure</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Potential_temperature" title="Potential temperature">potential temperature</a> <a href="/wiki/Scalar_field" title="Scalar field">scalar fields</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Velocity" title="Velocity">velocity</a> <a href="/wiki/Vector_field" title="Vector field">vector field</a> of the atmosphere through time. Additional transport equations for pollutants and other <a href="/wiki/Aerosol" title="Aerosol">aerosols</a> are included in some primitive-equation mesoscale models as well.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup> The equations used are <a href="/wiki/Nonlinear_system" title="Nonlinear system">nonlinear</a> partial differential equations which are impossible to solve exactly through analytical methods,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-finite_58-0"><a href="#cite_note-finite-58">[58]</a></sup> with the exception of a few idealized cases.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup> Therefore, numerical methods obtain approximate solutions. Different models use different solution methods: some global models use <a href="/wiki/Spectral_method" title="Spectral method">spectral methods</a> for the horizontal dimensions and <a href="/wiki/Finite_difference_method" title="Finite difference method">finite difference methods</a> for the vertical dimension, while regional models and other global models usually use finite-difference methods in all three dimensions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-finite_58-1"><a href="#cite_note-finite-58">[58]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Techniques">Techniques</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Weather_forecasting&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Techniques">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Persistence">Persistence</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Weather_forecasting&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Persistence">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The simplest method of forecasting the weather, persistence, relies upon today's conditions to forecast the conditions tomorrow. This can be a valid way of forecasting the weather when it is in a steady state, such as during the summer season in the tropics. This method of forecasting strongly depends upon the presence of a stagnant weather pattern. Therefore, when in a fluctuating weather pattern, this method of forecasting becomes inaccurate. It can be useful in both short range forecasts and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Long_range_weather_forecasts&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Long range weather forecasts (page does not exist)">long range forecasts</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Use_of_a_barometer">Use of a barometer</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Weather_forecasting&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Use of a barometer">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Measurements of barometric pressure and the pressure tendency (the change of pressure over time) have been used in forecasting since the late 19th century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup> The larger the change in pressure, especially if more than 3.5 <a href="/wiki/Pascal_(unit)" title="Pascal (unit)">hPa</a> (2.6 <a href="/wiki/Millimeter_of_mercury" title="Millimeter of mercury">mmHg</a>), the larger the change in weather can be expected. If the pressure drop is rapid, a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Low_pressure_area" title="Low pressure area">low pressure system</a> is approaching, and there is a greater chance of rain. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/High_pressure_area" title="High pressure area">Rapid pressure rises</a> are associated with improving weather conditions, such as clearing skies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Looking_at_the_sky">Looking at the sky</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Weather_forecasting&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Looking at the sky">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Marestail.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="134" data-file-width="200" height="134" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/c/ca/Marestail.jpg" width="200"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">Marestail shows moisture at high altitude, signalling the later arrival of wet weather.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Along with pressure tendency, the condition of the sky is one of the more important parameters used to forecast weather in mountainous areas. Thickening of cloud cover or the invasion of a higher cloud deck is indicative of rain in the near future. At night, high thin <a href="/wiki/Cirrostratus_cloud" title="Cirrostratus cloud">cirrostratus clouds</a> can lead to <a href="/wiki/Halo_(optical_phenomenon)" title="Halo (optical phenomenon)">halos</a> around the <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">moon</a>, which indicates an approach of a <a href="/wiki/Warm_front" title="Warm front">warm front</a> and its associated <a href="/wiki/Rain" title="Rain">rain</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-63"><a href="#cite_note-63">[63]</a></sup> Morning <a href="/wiki/Fog" title="Fog">fog</a> portends fair conditions, as rainy conditions are preceded by wind or clouds which prevent fog formation. The approach of a line of <a href="/wiki/Thunderstorm" title="Thunderstorm">thunderstorms</a> could indicate the approach of a <a href="/wiki/Cold_front" title="Cold front">cold front</a>. Cloud-free skies are indicative of fair weather for the near future.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-64"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup> A <a href="/wiki/Bar_(tropical_cyclone)" title="Bar (tropical cyclone)">bar</a> can indicate a coming tropical cyclone. The use of sky cover in weather prediction has led to various <a href="/wiki/Weather_lore" title="Weather lore">weather lore</a> over the centuries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-65"><a href="#cite_note-65">[65]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Nowcasting">Nowcasting</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Weather_forecasting&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Nowcasting">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Nowcasting_(meteorology)" title="Nowcasting (meteorology)">Nowcasting (meteorology)</a></div>
<p>The forecasting of the weather within the next six hours is often referred to as <b>nowcasting</b>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-66"><a href="#cite_note-66">[66]</a></sup> In this time range it is possible to forecast smaller features such as individual showers and thunderstorms with reasonable accuracy, as well as other features too small to be resolved by a computer model. A human given the latest radar, satellite and observational data will be able to make a better analysis of the small scale features present and so will be able to make a more accurate forecast for the following few hours.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-67"><a href="#cite_note-67">[67]</a></sup> However, there are now <a href="/wiki/Expert_system" title="Expert system">expert systems</a> using those data and mesoscale numerical model to make better extrapolation, including evolution of those features in time.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Use_of_forecast_models">Use of forecast models</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Weather_forecasting&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Use of forecast models">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:NAM_500_MB.PNG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="480" data-file-width="640" height="188" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6b/NAM_500_MB.PNG/250px-NAM_500_MB.PNG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6b/NAM_500_MB.PNG/375px-NAM_500_MB.PNG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6b/NAM_500_MB.PNG/500px-NAM_500_MB.PNG 2x" width="250"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:NAM_500_MB.PNG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
An example of 500 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Millibar" title="Millibar">mbar</a> <a href="/wiki/Geopotential_height" title="Geopotential height">geopotential height</a> prediction from a numerical weather prediction model</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>In the past, the human forecaster was responsible for generating the entire weather forecast based upon available observations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-68"><a href="#cite_note-68">[68]</a></sup> Today, human input is generally confined to choosing a model based on various parameters, such as model biases and performance.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Klaus_69-0"><a href="#cite_note-Klaus-69">[69]</a></sup> Using a consensus of forecast models, as well as ensemble members of the various models, can help reduce forecast error.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TBK_70-0"><a href="#cite_note-TBK-70">[70]</a></sup> However, regardless how small the average error becomes with any individual system, large errors within any particular piece of guidance are still possible on any given model run.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-71"><a href="#cite_note-71">[71]</a></sup> Humans are required to interpret the model data into weather forecasts that are understandable to the end user. Humans can use knowledge of local effects which may be too small in size to be resolved by the model to add information to the forecast. While increasing accuracy of forecast models implies that humans may no longer be needed in the forecast process at some point in the future, there is currently still a need for human intervention.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-72"><a href="#cite_note-72">[72]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Analog_technique">Analog technique</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Weather_forecasting&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Analog technique">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The analog technique is a complex way of making a forecast, requiring the forecaster to remember a previous weather event which is expected to be mimicked by an upcoming event. What makes it a difficult technique to use is that there is rarely a perfect analog for an event in the future.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-73"><a href="#cite_note-73">[73]</a></sup> Some call this type of forecasting pattern recognition. It remains a useful method of observing rainfall over data voids such as oceans,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-74"><a href="#cite_note-74">[74]</a></sup> as well as the forecasting of precipitation amounts and distribution in the future. A similar technique is used in medium range forecasting, which is known as teleconnections, when systems in other locations are used to help pin down the location of another system within the surrounding regime.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-75"><a href="#cite_note-75">[75]</a></sup> An example of teleconnections are by using <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/El_Ni%C3%B1o-Southern_Oscillation" title="El Niño-Southern Oscillation">El Niño-Southern Oscillation</a> (ENSO) related phenomena.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-76"><a href="#cite_note-76">[76]</a></sup></p>

<p>Mathematical models are often developed to map the <a href="/wiki/Forecast_skill" title="Forecast skill">forecast skill</a> of a weather prediction methodology.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Communicating_forecasts_to_the_public">Communicating forecasts to the public</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Weather_forecasting&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Communicating forecasts to the public">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:292px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Newspaper_weather_forecast_-_today_and_tomorrow.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="109" data-file-width="290" height="109" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a9/Newspaper_weather_forecast_-_today_and_tomorrow.svg/290px-Newspaper_weather_forecast_-_today_and_tomorrow.svg.png" width="290"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">An example of a two-day weather forecast in the <a href="/wiki/Visual_language" title="Visual language">visual style</a> that an American newspaper might use. Temperatures are given in Fahrenheit.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Most end users of forecasts are members of the general public. Thunderstorms can create strong winds and dangerous <a href="/wiki/Lightning" title="Lightning">lightning</a> strikes that can lead to deaths, power outages,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-77"><a href="#cite_note-77">[77]</a></sup> and widespread hail damage. Heavy snow or rain can bring transportation and commerce to a stand-still,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-78"><a href="#cite_note-78">[78]</a></sup> as well as cause flooding in low-lying areas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-79"><a href="#cite_note-79">[79]</a></sup> Excessive <a href="/wiki/Heat_wave" title="Heat wave">heat</a> or <a href="/wiki/Cold_wave" title="Cold wave">cold waves</a> can sicken or kill those with inadequate utilities, and <a href="/wiki/Drought" title="Drought">droughts</a> can impact water usage and destroy vegetation.</p>
<p>Several countries employ government agencies to provide forecasts and watches/warnings/advisories to the public in order to protect life and property and maintain commercial interests. Knowledge of what the end user needs from a weather forecast must be taken into account to present the information in a useful and understandable way. Examples include the <a href="/wiki/National_Oceanic_and_Atmospheric_Administration" title="National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration">National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration</a>'s <a href="/wiki/National_Weather_Service" title="National Weather Service">National Weather Service</a> (NWS)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-80"><a href="#cite_note-80">[80]</a></sup> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Environment_Canada" title="Environment Canada">Environment Canada</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Meteorological_Service_of_Canada" title="Meteorological Service of Canada">Meteorological Service</a> (MSC).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-81"><a href="#cite_note-81">[81]</a></sup> Traditionally, newspaper, television, and radio have been the primary outlets for presenting weather forecast information to the public. Increasingly, the internet is being used due to the vast amount of specific information that can be found.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-82"><a href="#cite_note-82">[82]</a></sup> In all cases, these outlets update their forecasts on a regular basis.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Severe_weather_alerts_and_advisories">Severe weather alerts and advisories</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Weather_forecasting&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Severe weather alerts and advisories">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A major part of modern weather forecasting is the severe weather alerts and advisories which the national weather services issue in the case that severe or hazardous weather is expected. This is done to protect life and property.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-83"><a href="#cite_note-83">[83]</a></sup> Some of the most commonly known of severe weather advisories are the <a href="/wiki/Severe_thunderstorm_warning" title="Severe thunderstorm warning">severe thunderstorm</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tornado_warning" title="Tornado warning">tornado warning</a>, as well as the <a href="/wiki/Severe_thunderstorm_watch" title="Severe thunderstorm watch">severe thunderstorm</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tornado_watch" title="Tornado watch">tornado watch</a>. Other forms of these advisories include winter weather, high wind, <a href="/wiki/Flood_warning" title="Flood warning">flood</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone" title="Tropical cyclone">tropical cyclone</a>, and fog.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-84"><a href="#cite_note-84">[84]</a></sup> Severe weather advisories and alerts are broadcast through the media, including radio, using emergency systems as the <a href="/wiki/Emergency_Alert_System" title="Emergency Alert System">Emergency Alert System</a> which break into regular programming.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-85"><a href="#cite_note-85">[85]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Low_temperature_forecast">Low temperature forecast</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Weather_forecasting&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Low temperature forecast">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The low temperature forecast for the current day is calculated using the lowest temperature found between 7pm that evening through 7am the following morning.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-86"><a href="#cite_note-86">[86]</a></sup> So, in short, today's forecasted low is most likely tomorrow's low temperature.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Specialist_forecasting">Specialist forecasting</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Weather_forecasting&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Specialist forecasting">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>There are a number of sectors with their own specific needs for weather forecasts and specialist services are provided to these users.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Air_traffic">Air traffic</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Weather_forecasting&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Air traffic">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Plume_from_eruption_of_Chaiten_volcano,_Chile.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2400" data-file-width="3200" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/42/Plume_from_eruption_of_Chaiten_volcano%2C_Chile.jpg/220px-Plume_from_eruption_of_Chaiten_volcano%2C_Chile.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/42/Plume_from_eruption_of_Chaiten_volcano%2C_Chile.jpg/330px-Plume_from_eruption_of_Chaiten_volcano%2C_Chile.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/42/Plume_from_eruption_of_Chaiten_volcano%2C_Chile.jpg/440px-Plume_from_eruption_of_Chaiten_volcano%2C_Chile.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Plume_from_eruption_of_Chaiten_volcano,_Chile.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Ash cloud from the 2008 eruption of <a href="/wiki/Chait%C3%A9n_(volcano)" title="Chaitén (volcano)">Chaitén volcano</a> stretching across <a href="/wiki/Patagonia" title="Patagonia">Patagonia</a> from the Pacific to the Atlantic Ocean</div>
</div>
</div>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Terminal_Aerodrome_Forecast" title="Terminal Aerodrome Forecast">Terminal Aerodrome Forecast</a></div>
<p>Because the aviation industry is especially sensitive to the weather, accurate weather forecasting is essential. Fog or exceptionally low <a href="/wiki/Ceiling_(cloud)" title="Ceiling (cloud)">ceilings</a> can prevent many aircraft from landing and taking off.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-87"><a href="#cite_note-87">[87]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Turbulence" title="Turbulence">Turbulence</a> and <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_icing" title="Atmospheric icing">icing</a> are also significant in-flight hazards.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-88"><a href="#cite_note-88">[88]</a></sup> Thunderstorms are a problem for all aircraft because of severe turbulence due to their <a href="/wiki/Vertical_draft" title="Vertical draft">updrafts</a> and <a href="/wiki/Outflow_boundary" title="Outflow boundary">outflow boundaries</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-89"><a href="#cite_note-89">[89]</a></sup> icing due to the heavy precipitation, as well as large <a href="/wiki/Hail" title="Hail">hail</a>, strong winds, and lightning, all of which can cause severe damage to an aircraft in flight.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-90"><a href="#cite_note-90">[90]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Volcanic_ash" title="Volcanic ash">Volcanic ash</a> is also a significant problem for aviation, as aircraft can lose engine power within ash clouds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-91"><a href="#cite_note-91">[91]</a></sup> On a day-to-day basis airliners are routed to take advantage of the <a href="/wiki/Jet_stream" title="Jet stream">jet stream</a> tailwind to improve fuel efficiency.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-92"><a href="#cite_note-92">[92]</a></sup> Aircrews are briefed prior to <a href="/wiki/Takeoff" title="Takeoff">takeoff</a> on the conditions to expect en route and at their destination.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-93"><a href="#cite_note-93">[93]</a></sup> Additionally, airports often change which <a href="/wiki/Runway" title="Runway">runway</a> is being used to take advantage of a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Headwind" title="Headwind">headwind</a>. This reduces the distance required for takeoff, and eliminates potential <a href="/wiki/Crosswind" title="Crosswind">crosswinds</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-94"><a href="#cite_note-94">[94]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Marine">Marine</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Weather_forecasting&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Marine">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Marine_weather_forecasting" title="Marine weather forecasting">Marine weather forecasting</a></div>
<p>Commercial and recreational use of waterways can be limited significantly by wind direction and speed, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ocean_surface_wave" title="Ocean surface wave">wave</a> periodicity and heights, tides, and precipitation. These factors can each influence the safety of marine transit. Consequently, a variety of codes have been established to efficiently transmit detailed marine weather forecasts to vessel pilots via radio, for example the <a href="/wiki/MAFOR" title="MAFOR">MAFOR</a> (marine forecast).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-95"><a href="#cite_note-95">[95]</a></sup> Typical weather forecasts can be received at sea through the use of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/RTTY" title="RTTY">RTTY</a>, <a href="/wiki/Navtex" title="Navtex">Navtex</a> and <a href="/wiki/Radiofax" title="Radiofax">Radiofax</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Agriculture">Agriculture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Weather_forecasting&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Agriculture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Farmer" title="Farmer">Farmers</a> rely on weather forecasts to decide what work to do on any particular day. For example, drying <a href="/wiki/Hay" title="Hay">hay</a> is only feasible in dry weather. Prolonged periods of dryness can ruin <a href="/wiki/Cotton" title="Cotton">cotton</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wheat" title="Wheat">wheat</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-96"><a href="#cite_note-96">[96]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Maize" title="Maize">corn</a> crops. While corn crops can be ruined by drought, their dried remains can be used as a cattle feed substitute in the form of <a href="/wiki/Silage" title="Silage">silage</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-97"><a href="#cite_note-97">[97]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Frost" title="Frost">Frosts</a> and freezes play havoc with crops both during the spring and fall. For example, <a href="/wiki/Peach" title="Peach">peach</a> trees in full bloom can have their potential peach crop decimated by a spring freeze.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-98"><a href="#cite_note-98">[98]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Orange_(fruit)" title="Orange (fruit)">Orange</a> groves can suffer significant damage during frosts and freezes, regardless of their timing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-99"><a href="#cite_note-99">[99]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Forestry">Forestry</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Weather_forecasting&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Forestry">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Weather forecasting of wind, precipitations and humidity is essential for preventing and controlling <a href="/wiki/Wildfire" title="Wildfire">wildfires</a>. Different indices, like the <i><a href="/wiki/Forest_fire_weather_index" title="Forest fire weather index">Forest fire weather index</a></i> and the <i><a href="/wiki/Haines_Index" title="Haines Index">Haines Index</a></i>, have been developed to predict the areas more at risk to experience fire from natural or human causes. Conditions for the development of harmful insects can be predicted by forecasting the evolution of weather, too.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Utility_companies">Utility companies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Weather_forecasting&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Utility companies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Air_handling_unit.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="738" data-file-width="1109" height="146" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/00/Air_handling_unit.JPG/220px-Air_handling_unit.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/00/Air_handling_unit.JPG/330px-Air_handling_unit.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/00/Air_handling_unit.JPG/440px-Air_handling_unit.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Air_handling_unit.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
An <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Air_handling_unit" title="Air handling unit">air handling unit</a> is used for the heating and cooling of air in a central location (click on image for legend).</div>
</div>
</div>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Degree_day" title="Degree day">Degree day</a></div>
<p>Electricity and gas companies rely on weather forecasts to anticipate demand which can be strongly affected by the weather. They use the quantity termed the degree day to determine how strong of a use there will be for heating (<a href="/wiki/Heating_degree_day" title="Heating degree day">heating degree day</a>) or cooling (cooling degree day). These quantities are based on a daily average temperature of 65 °F (18 °C). Cooler temperatures force heating degree days (one per degree Fahrenheit), while warmer temperatures force cooling degree days.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-100"><a href="#cite_note-100">[100]</a></sup> In winter, severe cold weather can cause a surge in demand as people turn up their heating.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-101"><a href="#cite_note-101">[101]</a></sup> Similarly, in summer a surge in demand can be linked with the increased use of <a href="/wiki/Air_conditioning" title="Air conditioning">air conditioning</a> systems in hot weather.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-102"><a href="#cite_note-102">[102]</a></sup> By anticipating a surge in demand, utility companies can purchase additional supplies of power or natural gas before the price increases, or in some circumstances, supplies are restricted through the use of <a href="/wiki/Brownout_(electricity)" title="Brownout (electricity)">brownouts</a> and <a href="/wiki/Power_outage" title="Power outage">blackouts</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-103"><a href="#cite_note-103">[103]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_commercial_companies">Other commercial companies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Weather_forecasting&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Other commercial companies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Increasingly, private companies pay for weather forecasts tailored to their needs so that they can increase their profits or avoid large losses.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-104"><a href="#cite_note-104">[104]</a></sup> For example, supermarket chains may change the stocks on their shelves in anticipation of different consumer spending habits in different weather conditions. Weather forecasts can be used to invest in the commodity market, such as futures in oranges, corn, soybeans, and oil.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-105"><a href="#cite_note-105">[105]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Military_applications">Military applications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Weather_forecasting&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: Military applications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="United_Kingdom_Armed_Forces">United Kingdom Armed Forces</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Weather_forecasting&amp;action=edit&amp;section=26" title="Edit section: United Kingdom Armed Forces">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p><b>Royal Navy</b></p>
<p>The UK <a href="/wiki/Royal_Navy" title="Royal Navy">Royal Navy</a>, working with the UK <a href="/wiki/Met_Office" title="Met Office">Met Office</a>, has its own specialist branch of weather observers and forecasters, as part of the Hydrographic and Meteorological (HM) specialisation, who monitor and forecast operational conditions across the globe, to provide accurate and timely weather and oceanographic information to submarines, ships and <a href="/wiki/Fleet_Air_Arm" title="Fleet Air Arm">Fleet Air Arm</a> aircraft.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Royal_Air_Force">Royal Air Force</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Weather_forecasting&amp;action=edit&amp;section=27" title="Edit section: Royal Air Force">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>A mobile unit in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/RAF" title="RAF">RAF</a>, working with the UK <a href="/wiki/Met_Office" title="Met Office">Met Office</a>, forecasts the weather for regions in which British, allied servicemen and women are deployed. A group based at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Camp_Bastion" title="Camp Bastion">Camp Bastion</a> provides forecasts for the British armed forces in <a href="/wiki/Afghanistan" title="Afghanistan">Afghanistan</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-106"><a href="#cite_note-106">[106]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="United_States_Armed_Forces">United States Armed Forces</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Weather_forecasting&amp;action=edit&amp;section=28" title="Edit section: United States Armed Forces">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="US_Navy">US Navy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Weather_forecasting&amp;action=edit&amp;section=29" title="Edit section: US Navy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:102px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Npmoc.gif"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="101" data-file-width="100" height="101" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/3/30/Npmoc.gif" width="100"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Npmoc.gif" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Emblem of JTWC Joint Typhoon Warning Center</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Similar to the private sector, military weather forecasters present weather conditions to the war fighter community. Military weather forecasters provide pre-flight and in-flight weather briefs to pilots and provide real time resource protection services for military installations. Naval forecasters cover the waters and ship weather forecasts. The <a href="/wiki/United_States_Navy" title="United States Navy">United States Navy</a> provides a special service to both themselves and the rest of the federal government by issuing forecasts for tropical cyclones across the Pacific and Indian Oceans through their <a href="/wiki/Joint_Typhoon_Warning_Center" title="Joint Typhoon Warning Center">Joint Typhoon Warning Center</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-107"><a href="#cite_note-107">[107]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="US_Air_Force">US Air Force</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Weather_forecasting&amp;action=edit&amp;section=30" title="Edit section: US Air Force">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Within the United States, Air Force Weather provides weather forecasting for the Air Force and the Army. <a href="/wiki/United_States_Air_Force" title="United States Air Force">Air Force</a> forecasters cover air operations in both wartime and peacetime operations and provide <a href="/wiki/United_States_Army" title="United States Army">Army</a> support;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-108"><a href="#cite_note-108">[108]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/United_States_Coast_Guard" title="United States Coast Guard">United States Coast Guard</a> marine science technicians provide ship forecasts for ice breakers and other various operations within their realm;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-109"><a href="#cite_note-109">[109]</a></sup> and Marine forecasters provide support for ground- and air-based <a href="/wiki/United_States_Marine_Corps" title="United States Marine Corps">United States Marine Corps</a> operations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-110"><a href="#cite_note-110">[110]</a></sup> All four military branches take their initial enlisted meteorology technical training at <a href="/wiki/Keesler_Air_Force_Base" title="Keesler Air Force Base">Keesler Air Force Base</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-111"><a href="#cite_note-111">[111]</a></sup> Military and civilian forecasters actively cooperate in analyzing, creating and critiquing weather forecast products.</p>
