<p><b>Fog</b> consists of visible cloud <a href="/wiki/Water" title="Water">water</a> droplets or ice crystals suspended in the air at or near the <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a>'s surface.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DefinitionFog_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-DefinitionFog-1">[1]</a></sup> Fog can be considered a type of low-lying <a href="/wiki/Cloud" title="Cloud">cloud</a> and is heavily influenced by nearby bodies of water, <a href="/wiki/Topography" title="Topography">topography</a>, and wind conditions. In turn, fog has affected many human activities, such as shipping, travel, and warfare.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Definition">Definition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fog&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Definition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The term "fog" is typically distinguished from the more generic term "cloud" in that fog is low-lying, and the moisture in the fog is often generated locally (such as from a nearby body of water, like a lake or the ocean, or from nearby moist ground or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Marshes" title="Marshes">marshes</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DistinguishCloud_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-DistinguishCloud-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>By definition, fog reduces <a href="/wiki/Visibility" title="Visibility">visibility</a> to less than 1 kilometre (0.62 mi), whereas <a href="/wiki/Mist" title="Mist">mist</a> causes lesser impairment of visibility.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>For aviation purposes in the UK, a visibility of less than 5 kilometres (3.1 mi) but greater than 999 metres (3,278 ft) is considered to be mist if the <a href="/wiki/Relative_humidity" title="Relative humidity">relative humidity</a> is 70% or greater; below 70%, <a href="/wiki/Haze" title="Haze">haze</a> is reported.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources#What_information_to_include" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources"><span title="A more detailed citation is required. (February 2010)">full citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Formation">Formation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fog&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Formation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>Fog forms when the difference between air temperature and <a href="/wiki/Dew_point" title="Dew point">dew point</a> is less than 2.5 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/%C2%B0C" title="°C">°C</a> or 4 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/%C2%B0F" title="°F">°F</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>Fog begins to form when <a href="/wiki/Water_vapor" title="Water vapor">water vapor</a> <a href="/wiki/Condensation" title="Condensation">condenses</a> into tiny liquid water droplets suspended in the air. Six examples of ways that water vapor is added to the air are by wind convergence into areas of upward motion;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-convection_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-convection-6">[6]</a></sup> precipitation or <a href="/wiki/Virga" title="Virga">virga</a> falling from above;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> daytime heating evaporating water from the surface of oceans, water bodies, or wet land;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Transpiration" title="Transpiration">transpiration</a> from plants;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> cool or dry air moving over warmer water;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> and lifting air over mountains.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MT_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-MT-11">[11]</a></sup> Water vapor normally begins to condense on <a href="/wiki/Cloud_condensation_nuclei" title="Cloud condensation nuclei">condensation nuclei</a> such as dust, ice, and salt in order to form clouds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DR_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-DR-13">[13]</a></sup> Fog, like its elevated cousin <a href="/wiki/Stratus_cloud" title="Stratus cloud">stratus</a>, is a stable cloud deck which tends to form when a cool, stable air mass is trapped underneath a warm air mass.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>Fog normally occurs at a <a href="/wiki/Relative_humidity" title="Relative humidity">relative humidity</a> near 100%.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gliess_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-gliess-15">[15]</a></sup> This occurs from either added moisture in the air, or falling ambient air temperature.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gliess_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-gliess-15">[15]</a></sup> However, fog can form at lower humidities, and can sometimes fail to form with relative humidity at 100%. At 100% relative humidity, the air cannot hold additional moisture, thus, the air will become <a href="/wiki/Supersaturation" title="Supersaturation">supersaturated</a> if additional moisture is added.</p>
<p>Fog can form suddenly and can dissipate just as rapidly. The sudden formation of fog is known as "flash fog".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>Fog commonly produces <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Precipitation_(meteorology)" title="Precipitation (meteorology)">precipitation</a> in the form of <a href="/wiki/Drizzle" title="Drizzle">drizzle</a> or very light snow. Drizzle occurs when the humidity of fog attains 100% and the minute cloud droplets begin to coalesce into larger droplets.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> This can occur when the fog layer is lifted and cooled sufficiently, or when it is forcibly compressed from above by descending air. Drizzle becomes freezing drizzle when the temperature at the surface drops below the freezing point.</p>
<p>The thickness of a fog layer is largely determined by the altitude of the inversion boundary, which in coastal or oceanic locales is also the top of the <a href="/wiki/Marine_layer" title="Marine layer">marine layer</a>, above which the air mass is warmer and drier. The inversion boundary varies its altitude primarily in response to the weight of the air above it, which is measured in terms of atmospheric pressure. The marine layer, and any fogbank it may contain, will be "squashed" when the pressure is high, and conversely, may expand upwards when the pressure above it is lowering.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Types">Types</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fog&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Types">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Fog can form in a number of ways, depending on how the cooling that caused the <a href="/wiki/Water_vapor#Condensation" title="Water vapor">condensation</a> occurred.</p>
<p><b>Radiation fog</b> is formed by the cooling of land after sunset by <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Infrared_thermal_radiation&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Infrared thermal radiation (page does not exist)">infrared thermal radiation</a> in calm conditions with a clear sky. The cooling ground then cools adjacent air <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=By_conduction&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="By conduction (page does not exist)">by conduction</a>, causing the air temperature to fall below the dew point, forming fog. In perfect calm, the fog layer can be less than a meter thick, but <a href="/wiki/Turbulence" title="Turbulence">turbulence</a> can promote a thicker layer. Radiation fogs occur at night, and usually doesn't last long after sunrise, but it can persist all day in the winter months especially in areas bounded by high ground. Radiation fog is most common in autumn and early winter. Examples of this phenomenon include the <a href="/wiki/Tule_fog" title="Tule fog">Tule fog</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p><b>Ground fog</b> is fog that obscures less than 60% of the sky and does not extend to the base of any overhead clouds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> However, the term is usually a synonym for radiation fog which is very shallow; in some cases the depth of the fog is on the order of tens of centimeters over certain kinds of terrain with the absence of wind.</p>

<p><b>Advection fog</b> occurs when moist air passes over a cool surface by <a href="/wiki/Advection" title="Advection">advection</a> (wind) and is cooled.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> It is common as a <a href="/wiki/Warm_front" title="Warm front">warm front</a> passes over an area with significant snow-pack. It is most common at sea when moist air encounters cooler waters, including areas of cold water <a href="/wiki/Upwelling" title="Upwelling">upwelling</a>, such as along the California coast (<i>see</i> <a href="/wiki/San_Francisco_fog" title="San Francisco fog">San Francisco fog</a>). A strong enough temperature difference over water or bare ground can also cause advection fog.</p>
<p>Although strong winds often mix the air and can disperse, fragment, or prevent many kinds of fog, markedly warmer and humid air blowing over a snowpack can continue to generate advection fog at elevated velocities up to 50 mph/80 km/h or more -- this fog will be in a turbulent, rapidly moving, and comparatively shallow layer, observed as inches/a few cm in depth over flat farm fields, flat urban terrain and the like, and/or form more complex forms where the terrain is different such as rotating areas in the lee of hills or large buildings and so on.</p>
<p>The advection of fog along the California coastline is propelled onto land by one of several processes. A cold front can push the marine layer coast-ward, an occurrence most typical in the spring or late fall. During the summer months, a low pressure trough produced by intense heating inland creates a strong pressure gradient, drawing in the dense marine layer. Also during the summer, strong high pressure aloft over the desert southwest, usually in connection with the summer <a href="/wiki/Monsoon" title="Monsoon">monsoon</a>, produces a south to southeasterly flow which can drive the offshore marine layer up the coastline; a phenomenon known as a "southerly surge", typically following a coastal heat spell. However, if the monsoonal flow is sufficiently turbulent, it might instead break up the marine layer and any fog it may contain. Moderate turbulence will typically transform a fog bank, lifting it and breaking it up into shallow convective clouds called <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stratocumulus" title="Stratocumulus">stratocumulus</a>.</p>
<p><b>Evaporation fog</b> or <b>steam fog</b> forms over bodies of water overlain by much colder air; this situation can also lead to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Steam_devils" title="Steam devils">steam devils</a> forming. Lake effect fog is of this type, sometimes in combination with other causes like radiation fog. It tends to differ from most advective fog formed over land in that it is, like <a href="/wiki/Lake-effect_snow" title="Lake-effect snow">lake-effect snow</a>, a convective phenomenon, resulting in fog which can be quite a bit denser, deeper, and looks fluffy from above. Most other fog is stratiform; steam devils, which look like their <a href="/wiki/Dust_devil" title="Dust devil">dust counterparts</a>, are often seen in this situation.</p>
<p><b>Frontal fog</b> forms near a front when raindrops, falling from relatively warm air above a frontal surface, evaporate into cooler air close to the Earth's surface and cause it to become saturated.</p>
<p><b>Ice fog</b> forming in very low temperatures can be the result of other mechanisms mentioned here, as well as the exhalation of moist warm air by herds of animals. It can be associated with the <a href="/wiki/Diamond_dust" title="Diamond dust">diamond dust</a> form of precipitation, in which very small crystals of ice form and slowly fall. This often occurs during blue sky conditions which can cause many types of halos and other results of refraction of sunlight by the airborne crystals.</p>
<p><b>Freezing fog</b>, which deposits <a href="/wiki/Hard_rime" title="Hard rime">rime</a>, is composed of droplets of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Supercooled_water" title="Supercooled water">supercooled water</a> which freezes to surfaces on contact.</p>
<p><b>Precipitation fog</b> (or <b>frontal fog</b>) forms as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Precipitation_(meteorology)" title="Precipitation (meteorology)">precipitation</a> falls into drier air below the cloud, the liquid droplets <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Evaporate" title="Evaporate">evaporate</a> into water vapor. The water vapor cools and at the dewpoint it condenses and fog forms.</p>
<p><b>Hail fog</b> sometimes occurs in the vicinity of significant <a href="/wiki/Hail" title="Hail">hail</a> accumulations due to decreased temperature and increased moisture leading to saturation in a very shallow layer near the surface. It most often occurs when there is a warm, humid layer atop the hail and when wind is light. This ground fog tends to be localized but can be extremely dense and abrupt. It may form shortly after the hail falls; when the hail has had time to cool the air and as it <a href="/wiki/Phase_transition" title="Phase transition">absorbs heat</a> when <a href="/wiki/Melting" title="Melting">melting</a> and <a href="/wiki/Evaporation" title="Evaporation">evaporating</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Marshall_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-Marshall-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p><b>Upslope fog</b> forms when moist air is going up the slope of a mountain or hill which condenses into fog on account of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Adiabatic_cooling" title="Adiabatic cooling">adiabatic cooling</a>, and to a lesser extent the drop in pressure with altitude.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Freezing_conditions">Freezing conditions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fog&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Freezing conditions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><b><span id="Freezing_fog">Freezing fog</span></b> occurs when liquid fog droplets freeze to surfaces, forming white <a href="/wiki/Soft_rime" title="Soft rime">soft</a> or <a href="/wiki/Hard_rime" title="Hard rime">hard rime</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bbcw_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-bbcw-22">[22]</a></sup> This is very common on mountain tops which are exposed to low clouds. It is equivalent to <a href="/wiki/Freezing_rain" title="Freezing rain">freezing rain</a>, and essentially the same as the ice that forms inside a freezer which is not of the "frostless" or "frost-free" type. The term "freezing fog" may also refer to fog where water vapor is super-cooled, filling the air with small ice crystals similar to very light snow. It seems to make the fog "tangible", as if one could "grab a handful".</p>
<p>In the western United States, freezing fog may be referred to as <b>pogonip</b>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-merriam-webster_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-merriam-webster-24">[24]</a></sup> It occurs commonly during cold winter spells, usually in deep mountain valleys. The word pogonip is derived from the <a href="/wiki/Shoshone" title="Shoshone">Shoshone</a> word <i>paγi̵nappi̵h</i>, which means "cloud".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-merriam-webster_24-1"><a href="#cite_note-merriam-webster-24">[24]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dictionary.com_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-dictionary.com-25">[25]</a></sup> In <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Old_Farmer%27s_Almanac" title="The Old Farmer's Almanac">The Old Farmer's Almanac</a>,</i> in the calendar for December, the phrase "Beware the Pogonip" regularly appears. In his anthology <i>Smoke Bellew</i>, <a href="/wiki/Jack_London" title="Jack London">Jack London</a> described a pogonip which surrounded the main characters, killing one of them.</p>
<p>The phenomenon is also extremely common in the inland areas of the Pacific Northwest, with temperatures in the 10 to 30 °F (−12 to −1 °C) range. The Columbia Plateau experiences this phenomenon most years due to temperature inversions, sometimes lasting for as long as three weeks. The fog typically begins forming around the area of the Columbia River and expands, sometimes covering the land to distances as far away as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/LaPine,_Oregon" title="LaPine, Oregon">LaPine, Oregon</a>, almost 150 miles (240 km) due south of the river and into south central Washington.</p>
<p><b>Frozen fog</b> (also known as <a href="/wiki/Ice_fog" title="Ice fog">ice fog</a>) is any kind of fog where the droplets have <a href="/wiki/Freezing" title="Freezing">frozen</a> into extremely tiny <a href="/wiki/Crystal" title="Crystal">crystals</a> of <a href="/wiki/Ice" title="Ice">ice</a> in midair. Generally this requires temperatures at or below −35 °C (−31 °F), making it common only in and near the <a href="/wiki/Arctic" title="Arctic">Arctic</a> and <a href="/wiki/Antarctic" title="Antarctic">Antarctic</a> regions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> It is most often seen in urban areas where it is created by the freezing of water vapor present in automobile exhaust and combustion products from heating and power generation. Urban ice fog can become extremely dense and will persist day and night until the temperature rises. Extremely small amounts of ice fog falling from the sky form a type of precipitation called <a href="/wiki/Diamond_dust" title="Diamond dust">ice crystals</a>, often reported in <a href="/wiki/Barrow,_Alaska" title="Barrow, Alaska">Barrow</a>, Alaska. Ice fog often leads to the visual phenomenon of <a href="/wiki/Light_pillar" title="Light pillar">light pillars</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Topographical_influences">Topographical influences</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fog&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Topographical influences">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tmulti tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:428px;max-width:428px">
<div class="tsingle" style="float:left;margin:1px;width:212px;max-width:212px">

<div class="thumbcaption" style="clear:left;text-align:left;background-color:transparent">Fog over the <i>Pedra do Sino</i> (Bell Rock; left) and <i>Dedo de Deus</i> (God's Finger; right) peaks in the <a href="/wiki/Serra_dos_%C3%93rg%C3%A3os_National_Park" title="Serra dos Órgãos National Park">Serra dos Órgãos National Park</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rio_de_Janeiro_(state)" title="Rio de Janeiro (state)">Rio de Janeiro state</a>, <a href="/wiki/Brazil" title="Brazil">Brazil</a></div>

<p><b>Up-slope fog</b> or <b>hill fog</b> forms when winds blow air up a <a href="/wiki/Slope" title="Slope">slope</a> (called <a href="/wiki/Orographic_lift" title="Orographic lift">orographic lift</a>), <a href="/wiki/Adiabatic_process" title="Adiabatic process">adiabatically cooling</a> it as it rises, and causing the moisture in it to condense. This often causes freezing fog on mountaintops, where the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cloud_ceiling" title="Cloud ceiling">cloud ceiling</a> would not otherwise be low enough.</p>
<p><b>Valley fog</b> forms in mountain <a href="/wiki/Valley" title="Valley">valleys</a>, often during winter. It is essentially a radiation fog confined by local <a href="/wiki/Topography" title="Topography">topography</a>, and can last for several days in calm conditions. In California's <a href="/wiki/Central_Valley_(California)" title="Central Valley (California)">Central Valley</a>, valley fog is often referred to as <a href="/wiki/Tule_fog" title="Tule fog">Tule fog</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Sea_and_coastal_fog">Sea and coastal fog</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fog&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Sea and coastal fog">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Sea fog (also known as <i><a href="/wiki/Haar_(fog)" title="Haar (fog)">haar</a></i> or <i>fret</i>) is heavily influenced by the presence of <a href="/wiki/Sea_spray" title="Sea spray">sea spray</a> and microscopic airborne <a href="/wiki/Salt" title="Salt">salt</a> crystals. Clouds of all types require minute <a href="/wiki/Hygroscopy" title="Hygroscopy">hygroscopic</a> particles upon which water vapor can condense. Over the ocean surface, the most common particles are salt from salt <a href="/wiki/Spray_(liquid_drop)" title="Spray (liquid drop)">spray</a> produced by breaking waves. Except in areas of storminess, the most common areas of breaking waves are located near coastlines, hence the greatest densities of airborne salt particles are there.</p>
<p>Condensation on salt particles has been observed to occur at humidities as low as 70%, thus fog can occur even in relatively dry air in suitable locations such as the California coast. Typically, such lower humidity fog is preceded by a transparent mistiness along the coastline as condensation competes with evaporation, a phenomenon that is typically noticeable by beachgoers in the afternoon. Another recently discovered source of condensation nuclei for coastal fog is <a href="/wiki/Kelp" title="Kelp">kelp</a> seaweed. Researchers have found that under stress (intense sunlight, strong evaporation, etc.), kelp releases particles of <a href="/wiki/Iodine" title="Iodine">iodine</a> which in turn become nuclei for condensation of water vapor, causing fog that diffuses direct sunlight.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<p><b><a href="/wiki/Sea_smoke" title="Sea smoke">Sea smoke</a></b>, also called <b><a href="/wiki/Steam" title="Steam">steam</a> fog</b> or <b>evaporation fog</b>, is the most localized form and is created by cold air passing over warmer water or moist land.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bbcw_22-1"><a href="#cite_note-bbcw-22">[22]</a></sup> It often causes freezing fog, or sometimes <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hoar_frost" title="Hoar frost">hoar frost</a>.</p>
<p><b>Arctic sea smoke</b> is similar to <a href="/wiki/Sea_smoke" title="Sea smoke">sea smoke</a>, but occurs when the air is very cold. Instead of condensing into water droplets, columns of freezing, rising, and condensing water vapor is formed. The water vapor produces the <i>sea smoke fog</i>, and is usually misty and smoke-like.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<p><b><a href="/wiki/Gar%C3%BAa" title="Garúa">Garúa</a> fog</b> near the coast of <a href="/wiki/Chile" title="Chile">Chile</a> and <a href="/wiki/Peru" title="Peru">Peru</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> occurs when typical fog produced by the sea travels inland, but suddenly meets an area of hot air. This causes the water particles of fog to shrink by evaporation, producing a "transparent mist". Garua fog is nearly invisible, yet it still forces drivers to use <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Windshield_wipers" title="Windshield wipers">windshield wipers</a> because of deposition of liquid water on hard surfaces.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Visibility_effects">Visibility effects</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fog&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Visibility effects">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>Depending on the concentration of the droplets, visibility in fog can range from the appearance of haze, to almost zero visibility. Many lives are lost each year worldwide from accidents involving fog conditions on the highways, including <a href="/wiki/Multiple-vehicle_collision" title="Multiple-vehicle collision">multiple-vehicle collisions</a>.</p>
<p>The aviation travel industry is affected by the severity of fog conditions. Even though modern <a href="/wiki/Autoland" title="Autoland">auto-landing</a> computers can put an aircraft down without the aid of a pilot, personnel manning an airport <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Control_tower" title="Control tower">control tower</a> must be able to see if aircraft are sitting on the runway awaiting takeoff. Safe operations are difficult in thick fog, and civilian airports may forbid takeoffs and landings until conditions improve.</p>
<p>A solution for landing returning military aircraft developed in World War II was called <a href="/wiki/Fog_Investigation_and_Dispersal_Operation" title="Fog Investigation and Dispersal Operation">Fog Investigation and Dispersal Operation</a> (FIDO). It involved burning enormous amounts of fuel alongside runways to evaporate fog, allowing returning fighter and bomber pilots sufficient visual cues to safely land their aircraft. The high energy demands of this method discourage its use for routine operations.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Shadows">Shadows</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fog&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Shadows">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Fog_shadow_tv_tower.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1000" data-file-width="779" height="218" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e3/Fog_shadow_tv_tower.jpg/170px-Fog_shadow_tv_tower.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e3/Fog_shadow_tv_tower.jpg/255px-Fog_shadow_tv_tower.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e3/Fog_shadow_tv_tower.jpg/340px-Fog_shadow_tv_tower.jpg 2x" width="170"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Fog_shadow_tv_tower.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/Sutro_Tower" title="Sutro Tower">Sutro Tower</a> casts a 3-dimensional fog shadow</div>

<p>Shadows are cast through fog in three dimensions. The fog is dense enough to be illuminated by light that passes through gaps in a structure or tree, but thin enough to let a large quantity of that light pass through to illuminate points further on. As a result, object shadows appear as "beams" oriented in a direction parallel to the light source. These voluminous shadows are created the same way as <a href="/wiki/Crepuscular_rays" title="Crepuscular rays">crepuscular rays</a>, which are the shadows of clouds. In fog, it is solid objects that cast shadows.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Sound_propagation_and_acoustic_effects">Sound propagation and acoustic effects</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fog&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Sound propagation and acoustic effects">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Acoustic_location" title="Acoustic location">Acoustic location</a>, <a href="/wiki/Acoustic_shadow" title="Acoustic shadow">Acoustic shadow</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Foghorn" title="Foghorn">Foghorn</a></div>
<p>Sound typically travels fastest and farthest through solids, then liquids, then gases such as the atmosphere. Sound is affected during fog conditions due to the small distances between water droplets, and air temperature differences.</p>
<p><b>Molecular effect:</b> Though fog is essentially liquid water, the many droplets are separated by small air gaps. High-pitched sounds have a high frequency, which in turn means they have a short wavelength. To transmit a high frequency wave, air must move back and forth very quickly. Short-wavelength high-pitched sound waves are reflected and refracted by many separated water droplets, partially cancelling and dissipating their energy (a process called "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Damping" title="Damping">damping</a>"). In contrast, low pitched notes, with a low frequency and a long wavelength, move the air less rapidly and less often, and lose less energy to interactions with small water droplets. Low-pitched notes are less affected by fog and travel further, which is why <a href="/wiki/Foghorn" title="Foghorn">foghorns</a> use a low-pitched tone.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<p><b>Temperature effect:</b> A fog can be caused by a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Temperature_inversion" title="Temperature inversion">temperature inversion</a> where cold air is pooled at the surface which helped to create the fog, while warmer air sits above it. The inverted boundary between cold air and warm air reflects sound waves back toward the ground, allowing sound that would normally radiate out escaping into the upper atmosphere to instead bounce back and travel near the surface. A temperature inversion increases the distance that lower frequency sounds can travel, by reflecting the sound between the ground and the inversion layer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Record_extremes">Record extremes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fog&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Record extremes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The foggiest place in the world<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> is <a href="/wiki/Hamilton,_New_Zealand" title="Hamilton, New Zealand">Hamilton, New Zealand</a>, followed closely by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Grand_Banks" title="Grand Banks">Grand Banks</a> off the coast of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Newfoundland" title="Newfoundland">Newfoundland</a> (the meeting place of the cold <a href="/wiki/Labrador_Current" title="Labrador Current">Labrador Current</a> from the north and the much warmer <a href="/wiki/Gulf_Stream" title="Gulf Stream">Gulf Stream</a> from the south). Some of the foggiest land areas in the world include <a href="/wiki/Argentia" title="Argentia">Argentia</a> (Newfoundland) and <a href="/wiki/Point_Reyes" title="Point Reyes">Point Reyes</a> (California), each with over 200 foggy days per year.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Even in generally warmer southern Europe, thick fog and localized fog are often found in lowlands and valleys, such as the lower part of the <a href="/wiki/Po_Valley" title="Po Valley">Po Valley</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Arno" title="Arno">Arno</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tiber" title="Tiber">Tiber</a> valleys in Italy; <a href="/wiki/Ebro" title="Ebro">Ebro</a> Valley in northeastern Spain; as well as on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Swiss_plateau" title="Swiss plateau">Swiss plateau</a>, especially in the <a href="/wiki/Seeland_(Switzerland)" title="Seeland (Switzerland)">Seeland</a> area, in late autumn and winter.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Other notably foggy areas include coastal <a href="/wiki/Chile" title="Chile">Chile</a> (in the south); coastal <a href="/wiki/Namibia" title="Namibia">Namibia</a>; <a href="/wiki/Nord,_Greenland" title="Nord, Greenland">Nord, Greenland</a>; and the <a href="/wiki/Severnaya_Zemlya" title="Severnaya Zemlya">Severnaya Zemlya</a> islands.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="As_a_water_source">As a water source</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fog&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: As a water source">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Sequoia_sempervirens" title="Sequoia sempervirens">Redwood</a> forests in California receive approximately 30–40% of their moisture from coastal fog by way of <a href="/wiki/Fog_drip" title="Fog drip">fog drip</a>. Change in climate patterns could result in relative drought in these areas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> Some animals, including insects, depend on wet fog as a principal source of water, particularly in otherwise desert climes, as along many African coastal areas. Some coastal communities use <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fog_net" title="Fog net">fog nets</a> to extract moisture from the atmosphere where groundwater pumping and rainwater collection are insufficient.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Artificial_fog">Artificial fog</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fog&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Artificial fog">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><b>Artificial fog</b> is man-made fog that is usually created by vaporizing a water- and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Glycol" title="Glycol">glycol</a>-based or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Glycerine" title="Glycerine">glycerine</a>-based fluid. The fluid is injected into a heated metal block, and evaporates quickly. The resulting pressure forces the vapor out of a vent. Upon coming into contact with cool outside air, the vapor condenses in microscopic droplets and appears as fog.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> Such <a href="/wiki/Fog_machine" title="Fog machine">fog machines</a> are primarily used for <a href="/wiki/Theatrical_smoke_and_fog" title="Theatrical smoke and fog">entertainment applications</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Historical_references">Historical references</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fog&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Historical references">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Pea_soup_fog" title="Pea soup fog">Pea soup fog</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fog_of_war" title="Fog of war">Fog of war</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Theatrical_smoke_and_fog" title="Theatrical smoke and fog">Theatrical smoke and fog</a></div>
<p>The presence of fog has often played a key role in historical events, such as strategic battles. One example is the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Long_Island" title="Battle of Long Island">Battle of Long Island</a> (August 27, 1776), when American general <a href="/wiki/George_Washington" title="George Washington">George Washington</a> and his command were able to evade imminent capture by the British Army, using fog to conceal their escape. Another example is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/D-Day" title="D-Day">D-Day</a> (June 6, 1944) during <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>, when the Allies landed on the beaches of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Normandy,_France" title="Normandy, France">Normandy, France</a> during fog conditions. Both positive and negative results were reported from both sides during that battle, due to impaired visibility.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup></p>
