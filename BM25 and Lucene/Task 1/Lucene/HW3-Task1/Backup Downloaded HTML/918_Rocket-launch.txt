<p>A <b>rocket launch</b> is the takeoff phase of the flight of a <a href="/wiki/Rocket" title="Rocket">rocket</a>. Launches for <a href="/wiki/Orbital_spaceflight" title="Orbital spaceflight">orbital spaceflights</a>, or launches into <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Interplanetary_space" title="Interplanetary space">interplanetary space</a>, are usually from a fixed location on the ground, but may also be from a floating platform or from an airplane.</p>
<p>Launches of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Suborbital_flight" title="Suborbital flight">suborbital flights</a> (including <a href="/wiki/Missile" title="Missile">missile</a> launches), can also be from:</p>
<ul>
<li>a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Missile_silo" title="Missile silo">missile silo</a></li>
<li>a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mobile_launcher_vehicle" title="Mobile launcher vehicle">mobile launcher vehicle</a></li>
<li>a <a href="/wiki/Submarine" title="Submarine">submarine</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Air_launch" title="Air launch">air launch</a>:
<ul>
<li>from a plane (e.g. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Scaled_Composites_SpaceShipOne" title="Scaled Composites SpaceShipOne">Scaled Composites SpaceShipOne</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pegasus_Rocket" title="Pegasus Rocket">Pegasus Rocket</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/X-15" title="X-15">X-15</a>)</li>
<li>from a <a href="/wiki/Balloon" title="Balloon">balloon</a> (<a href="/wiki/Rockoon" title="Rockoon">Rockoon</a>, <a href="/wiki/Da_Vinci_Project" title="Da Vinci Project">da Vinci Project</a> (under development))</li>
</ul>
</li>
<li>a surface ship (<a href="/wiki/Aegis_Ballistic_Missile_Defense_System" title="Aegis Ballistic Missile Defense System">Aegis Ballistic Missile Defense System</a>) or ocean based platform (<a href="/wiki/Sea_Launch" title="Sea Launch">Sea Launch</a>)</li>
<li>an inclined rail (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Rocket_sled_launch" title="Rocket sled launch">rocket sled launch</a>)</li>
</ul>
<p>Launches not into space can also be from:</p>
<ul>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shoulder-launched_missile_weapon" title="Shoulder-launched missile weapon">the shoulder</a></li>
</ul>
<p>A <a href="/wiki/Skyrocket" title="Skyrocket">skyrocket</a> is launched from some stand.</p>
<p>"Rocket launch technologies" generally refers to the entire set of systems needed to successfully launch a vehicle, not just the vehicle itself, but also the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Firing_control_system&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Firing control system (page does not exist)">firing control systems</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ground_control_station" title="Ground control station">ground control station</a>, <a href="/wiki/Launch_pad" title="Launch pad">launch pad</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tracking_station" title="Tracking station">tracking stations</a> needed for a successful launch and/or recovery.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Launch_vehicles">Launch vehicles</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rocket_launch&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Launch vehicles">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Launch_vehicle" title="Launch vehicle">launch vehicle</a></div>
<p>If a rocket is launched to deliver a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Payload_(air_and_space_craft)" title="Payload (air and space craft)">payload</a> from a planetary surface into space it is called a <a href="/wiki/Launch_vehicle" title="Launch vehicle">launch vehicle</a>.</p>
<p>There are several broad categories that launch vehicles fall under, including:</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Expendable_launch_system" title="Expendable launch system">Expendable launch system</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Reusable_launch_system" title="Reusable launch system">Reusable launch system</a></li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Single_stage_to_orbit" title="Single stage to orbit">Single stage to orbit</a></li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Two_stage_to_orbit" title="Two stage to orbit">Two stage to orbit</a></li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Three_stage_to_orbit" title="Three stage to orbit">Three stage to orbit</a></li>
</ul>
<p>For the launch vehicles currently in use for <a href="/wiki/Human_spaceflight" title="Human spaceflight">human spaceflight</a>, see that article. There were over 70 orbital launches in 2010.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Orbital_launches">Orbital launches</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rocket_launch&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Orbital launches">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:ISRO_PSLV_dogleg_maneuver.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="599" data-file-width="852" height="155" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/80/ISRO_PSLV_dogleg_maneuver.jpg/220px-ISRO_PSLV_dogleg_maneuver.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/80/ISRO_PSLV_dogleg_maneuver.jpg/330px-ISRO_PSLV_dogleg_maneuver.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/80/ISRO_PSLV_dogleg_maneuver.jpg/440px-ISRO_PSLV_dogleg_maneuver.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:ISRO_PSLV_dogleg_maneuver.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
ISRO PSLV C20 SARAL mission dogleg maneuver</div>

<p>Orbital launch vehicles commonly take off vertically, and then begin to progressively lean over, usually following a <a href="/wiki/Gravity_turn" title="Gravity turn">gravity turn</a> trajectory.</p>
<p>Once above the majority of the atmosphere, the vehicle then angles the rocket jet, pointing it largely horizontally but somewhat downwards, which permits the vehicle to gain and then maintain altitude while increasing horizontal speed. As the speed grows, the vehicle will become more and more horizontal until at orbital speed, the engine will cut off.</p>
<p>All current vehicles <i>stage</i>, that is, jettison hardware on the way to orbit. Although <a href="/wiki/Single-stage-to-orbit" title="Single-stage-to-orbit">vehicles have been proposed</a> which would be able to reach orbit without staging, none have ever been constructed, and the <a href="/wiki/Tsiolkovsky_rocket_equation" title="Tsiolkovsky rocket equation">exponentially increasing fuel requirements</a> of such a vehicle would make its useful payload tiny or nonexistent. Most current and historical launch vehicles "expend" their jettisoned hardware, typically by allowing it to crash into the ocean, but some have recovered and reused jettisoned hardware, either by parachute or by propulsive landing.</p>
<p>When launching a spacecraft to orbit, a "dogleg" is a guided, powered turn during ascent phase that causes a rocket's flight path to deviate from a "straight" path. A dogleg is necessary if the desired launch azimuth, to reach a desired orbital inclination, would take the <a href="/wiki/Ground_track" title="Ground track">ground track</a> over land (or over a populated area, e.g. Russia usually does launch over land, but over unpopulated areas), or if the rocket is trying to reach an orbital plane that does not reach the <a href="/wiki/Latitude" title="Latitude">latitude</a> of the launch site. Doglegs are undesirable due to extra onboard fuel required, causing heavier load, and a reduction of vehicle performance.</p>
