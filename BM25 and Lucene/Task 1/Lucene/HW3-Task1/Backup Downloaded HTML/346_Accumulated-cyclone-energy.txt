<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Accumulated cyclone energy</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">
					Jump to:					<a href="#mw-head">navigation</a>, 					<a href="#p-search">search</a>
</div>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><p><b>Accumulated cyclone energy</b> (<b>ACE</b>) is a measure used by the <a href="/wiki/National_Oceanic_and_Atmospheric_Administration" title="National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration">National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration</a> (NOAA) to express the activity of individual <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclones" title="Tropical cyclones">tropical cyclones</a> and entire tropical cyclone seasons, particularly the North <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atlantic" title="Atlantic">Atlantic</a> <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="Atlantic hurricane season">hurricane season</a>. It uses an approximation of the wind energy used by a tropical system over its lifetime and is calculated every six hours. The ACE of a season is the sum of the ACEs for each storm and takes into account the number, strength, and duration of all the <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone" title="Tropical cyclone">tropical storms</a> in the season. The highest ACE calculated for a single storm is 82, for <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Ioke" title="Hurricane Ioke">Hurricane/Typhoon Ioke</a> in 2006.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Calculation">Calculation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Accumulated_cyclone_energy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Calculation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The ACE of a season is calculated by summing the squares of the estimated maximum sustained velocity of every active tropical storm (wind speed 35 knots [65 km/h] or higher), at six-hour intervals. Since the calculation is sensitive to the starting point of the six-hour intervals, the convention is to use 00:00, 06:00, 12:00, and 18:00 <a href="/wiki/Coordinated_Universal_Time" title="Coordinated Universal Time">UTC</a>. If any storms of a season happen to cross years, the storm's ACE counts for the previous year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> The numbers are usually divided by 10,000 to make them more manageable. One unit of ACE equals <span class="nowrap">10<sup>4</sup> <a href="/wiki/Knot_(unit)" title="Knot (unit)">kn</a><sup>2</sup>,</span> and for use as an index the unit is assumed. Thus:</p>

<p>where <i>v</i><sub>max</sub> is estimated sustained wind speed in <a href="/wiki/Knot_(unit)" title="Knot (unit)">knots</a>.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Kinetic_energy" title="Kinetic energy">Kinetic energy</a> is proportional to the square of velocity, and by adding together the energy per some interval of time, the accumulated energy is found. As the duration of a storm increases, more values are summed and the ACE also increases such that longer-duration storms may accumulate a larger ACE than more-powerful storms of lesser duration. Although ACE is a value proportional to the energy of the system, it is not a direct calculation of energy (the mass of the moved air and therefore the size of the storm would show up in a real energy calculation).</p>
<p>A related quantity is hurricane destruction potential (HDP), which is ACE but only calculated for the time where the system is a hurricane.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BellEtAl2000_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-BellEtAl2000-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Competing_measurement_metrics">Competing measurement metrics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Accumulated_cyclone_energy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Competing measurement metrics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>ACE focuses upon the most intense winds concentrated in a storm's center. Whereas, the actual destructive power of a storm depends upon the total area impacted with damaging winds, waves, and storm surge. The metric Integrated Kinetic Energy (IKE), developed in 2007, more accurately reflects the destructive potential. It examines the kinetic energy that forces a storm surge and creates high waves as well as the horizontal wind loads over the full storm. This metric yields a time specific value for a storm and doesn't account for the destruction caused by a storm over its entire life. Therefore, in 2013 the researchers at the University of Florida created a metric called Track Integrated Kinetic Energy (TIKE). TIKE expands the concept by accumulating IKE over the lifespan of an individual tropical cyclone and a total value for all named tropical cyclones in the season. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Categories">Categories</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Accumulated_cyclone_energy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Categories">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>A season's ACE is used by NOAA and others to categorize the hurricane season into 3 groups by its activity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-background_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-background-5">[5]</a></sup> Measured over the period 1951–2000 for the Atlantic basin, the <a href="/wiki/Median" title="Median">median</a> annual index was 87.5 and the <a href="/wiki/Arithmetic_mean" title="Arithmetic mean">mean</a> annual index was 93.2. The NOAA categorization system divides seasons into:</p>
<ul>
<li><i>Above-normal season</i>: An ACE value above 111 (120% of the 1981–2010 median), provided at least two of the following three parameters are also exceeded: number of tropical storms: 12, hurricanes: 6, and major hurricanes: 2.</li>
<li><i>Near-normal season</i>: neither above-normal nor below normal</li>
<li><i>Below-normal season</i>: An ACE value below 66 (71.4% of the 1981–2010 median), or none of the following three parameters are exceeded: number of tropical storms: 9, hurricanes: 4, and major hurricanes: 1.</li>
</ul>
<p>According to the NOAA categorization system for the Atlantic, the most recent above-normal season is the <a href="/wiki/2017_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="2017 Atlantic hurricane season">2017 season</a>, the most recent near-normal season is the <a href="/wiki/2014_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="2014 Atlantic hurricane season">2014 season</a>, and the most recent below normal season is the <a href="/wiki/2015_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="2015 Atlantic hurricane season">2015 season</a>.</p>
<p>The term <i>hyperactive</i> is used by Goldenberg <i>et al.</i> (2001)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> based on a different weighting algorithm<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> (reference link is broken/need new citatation), which places more weight on major hurricanes, but typically equating to an ACE of about 153 (175% of the 1951–2000 median).</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Individual_storms">Individual storms</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Accumulated_cyclone_energy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Individual storms">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The highest ever ACE estimated for a single storm in the Atlantic is 73.6, for the <a href="/wiki/1899_San_Ciriaco_hurricane" title="1899 San Ciriaco hurricane">San Ciriaco hurricane</a> in <a href="/wiki/1899_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="1899 Atlantic hurricane season">1899</a>. This single storm had an ACE higher than many whole Atlantic storm seasons. Other Atlantic storms with high ACEs include <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Ivan" title="Hurricane Ivan">Hurricane Ivan</a> in <a href="/wiki/2004_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="2004 Atlantic hurricane season">2004</a>, with an ACE of 70.4, <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Irma" title="Hurricane Irma">Hurricane Irma</a> in <a href="/wiki/2017_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="2017 Atlantic hurricane season">2017</a> with an ACE of 66.6, the <a href="/wiki/1893_Atlantic_hurricane_season#Hurricane_Nine" title="1893 Atlantic hurricane season">Great Charleston Hurricane of 1893</a>, with an ACE of 63.5, <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Isabel" title="Hurricane Isabel">Hurricane Isabel</a> in <a href="/wiki/2003_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="2003 Atlantic hurricane season">2003</a>, with an ACE of 63.3, and the <a href="/wiki/1932_Cuba_hurricane" title="1932 Cuba hurricane">1932 Cuba hurricane</a>, with an ACE of 59.8.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HURDAT_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-HURDAT-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>The highest ACE of a tropical storm was <a href="/wiki/Tropical_Storm_Alice_(1953)" title="Tropical Storm Alice (1953)">Tropical Storm Alice</a> of <a href="/wiki/1953_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="1953 Atlantic hurricane season">1953</a>, which attained an ACE of 9.6. The highest ACE of a Category 1 hurricane was <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hurricane_Nadine_(2012)" title="Hurricane Nadine (2012)">Hurricane Nadine</a> of <a href="/wiki/2012_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="2012 Atlantic hurricane season">2012</a>, which attained an ACE of 26.3. The lowest ACE of a tropical storm was <a href="/wiki/2000_Atlantic_hurricane_season#Tropical_Storm_Chris" title="2000 Atlantic hurricane season">Tropical Storm Chris</a> of <a href="/wiki/2000_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="2000 Atlantic hurricane season">2000</a>, which was a tropical storm for only six hours and had an ACE of 0.1. The lowest ACE of any hurricane was the <a href="/wiki/1959_Escuminac_disaster" title="1959 Escuminac disaster">Escuminac hurricane</a> of <a href="/wiki/1959_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="1959 Atlantic hurricane season">1959</a>, which was only active for one day and had an ACE of just 1.2. The lowest ACE of a major hurricane (Category 3 or higher), was <a href="/wiki/1960_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="1960 Atlantic hurricane season">1960</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Ethel_(1960)" title="Hurricane Ethel (1960)">Hurricane Ethel</a> and <a href="/wiki/1969_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="1969 Atlantic hurricane season">1969</a>'s <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hurricane_Gerda_(1969)" title="Hurricane Gerda (1969)">Hurricane Gerda</a>, each with an ACE of 5.3.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Individual_ACE_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Individual_ACE-9">[9]</a></sup> <span class="citation-needed-content" style="padding-left:0.1em; padding-right:0.1em; color:#595959; border:1px solid #DDD;">The only years since <a href="/wiki/1950_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="1950 Atlantic hurricane season">1950</a> to feature two storms with an ACE index of over 40 points have been <a href="/wiki/1966_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="1966 Atlantic hurricane season">1966</a>, <a href="/wiki/2003_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="2003 Atlantic hurricane season">2003</a>, and <a href="/wiki/2004_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="2004 Atlantic hurricane season">2004</a>, and the only year to feature three storms is <a href="/wiki/2017_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="2017 Atlantic hurricane season">2017</a>.</span><sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>

<h3><span id="Atlantic_hurricane_seasons,_1950–2017"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Atlantic_hurricane_seasons.2C_1950.E2.80.932017">Atlantic hurricane seasons, 1950–2017</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Accumulated_cyclone_energy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Atlantic hurricane seasons, 1950–2017">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>ACE data for the Atlantic hurricane season is less reliable prior to the modern satellite era, but NOAA has analyzed the best available information dating back to 1851. The <a href="/wiki/1933_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="1933 Atlantic hurricane season">1933 Atlantic hurricane season</a> is considered the highest ACE on record with a total of 259<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup>. For the current season or the season that just ended, the ACE is preliminary based on National Hurricane Center bulletins, which may later be revised.</p>

<p>Accumulated Cyclone Energy is also used in the eastern and central Pacific Ocean. Data on ACE is considered reliable starting with the <a href="/wiki/1971_Pacific_hurricane_season" title="1971 Pacific hurricane season">1971 season</a>. The season with the highest ACE since 1971 is the <a href="/wiki/1992_Pacific_hurricane_season" title="1992 Pacific hurricane season">1992 season</a>. The <a href="/wiki/1977_Pacific_hurricane_season" title="1977 Pacific hurricane season">1977 season</a> has the lowest ACE. The most recent above-normal season is the <a href="/wiki/2016_Pacific_hurricane_season" title="2016 Pacific hurricane season">2016 season</a>, the most recent near-normal season is the <a href="/wiki/2012_Pacific_hurricane_season" title="2012 Pacific hurricane season">2012 season</a>, and the most recent below normal season is the <a href="/wiki/2013_Pacific_hurricane_season" title="2013 Pacific hurricane season">2013 season</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> The 35 year median 1971–2005 is 115 x 10<sup>4</sup> kn<sup>2</sup> (100 in the EPAC zone east of 140°W, 13 in the CPAC zone); the mean is 130 (112 + 18).</p>
<p>The (unofficial) categorization of seasons for this table is based <i><a href="/wiki/Mutatis_mutandis" title="Mutatis mutandis">mutatis mutandis</a></i> on that used in the Atlantic basin:</p>
<ul>
<li><i>Above-normal season</i>: An ACE value above 135 (117% of the median), provided at least two of the following three parameters exceed the long-term average: number of tropical storms (16), hurricanes (9), and major hurricanes (4).</li>
<li><i>Near-normal season</i>: neither above-normal nor below normal</li>
<li><i>Below-normal season</i>: An ACE value below 86 (75% of the median)</li>
</ul>
