<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">History of Atlantic hurricane warnings</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"><span class="mw-redirectedfrom">  (Redirected from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_Atlantic_tropical_cyclone_warnings&amp;redirect=no" title="History of Atlantic tropical cyclone warnings">History of Atlantic tropical cyclone warnings</a>)</span></div>

<p>The <b>history of Atlantic tropical cyclone warnings</b> details the progress of <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone_warnings_and_watches" title="Tropical cyclone warnings and watches">tropical cyclone warnings</a> in the north Atlantic Ocean. The first service was set up in the 1870s from <a href="/wiki/Cuba" title="Cuba">Cuba</a> with the work of Father Benito Viñes. After his death, hurricane warning services were assumed by the <a href="/wiki/Signal_Corps_(United_States_Army)" title="Signal Corps (United States Army)">United States Signal Corp</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_States_Weather_Bureau" title="United States Weather Bureau">United States Weather Bureau</a> over the next few decades, first based in <a href="/wiki/Jamaica" title="Jamaica">Jamaica</a> and Cuba before shifting to <a href="/wiki/Washington,_D.C." title="Washington, D.C.">Washington, D.C.</a>. The central office in Washington, which would evolve into the National Meteorological Center and the <a href="/wiki/Weather_Prediction_Center" title="Weather Prediction Center">Weather Prediction Center</a>, assumed the responsibilities by the early 20th century. This responsibility passed to regional hurricane offices in 1935, and the concept of the <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="Atlantic hurricane season">Atlantic hurricane season</a> was established in order to keep a vigilant lookout for tropical cyclones during certain times of the year. Hurricane advisories issued every six hours by the regional hurricane offices began at this time.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/National_Hurricane_Center" title="National Hurricane Center">National Hurricane Center</a> became a tropical cyclone warning center in 1956, and assumed many of the functions it has today by 1965. The <a href="/wiki/National_Hurricane_Research_Project" title="National Hurricane Research Project">National Hurricane Research Project</a>, begun in the 1950s, used aircraft to study tropical cyclones and carry out experiments on mature hurricanes through its <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stormfury" title="Stormfury">Stormfury</a> project. Forecasts within the hurricane advisories were issued one day into the future in 1954 before being extended to two days into the future in 1961, three days into the future in 1964, and five days into the future in 2001. From the 1960s through the 1980s, work from the various regional hurricane offices was consolidated into the National Hurricane center. Its name was changed to the Tropical Prediction Center in 1995, before reassuming its National Hurricane Center name in 2010. Tropical cyclone forecasting is done nowadays using statistical methods based on tropical cyclone climatology, as well as methods of <a href="/wiki/Numerical_weather_prediction" title="Numerical weather prediction">numerical weather prediction</a> where computers use mathematical equations of motion to determine their movement.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_years">Early years</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_Atlantic_hurricane_warnings&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early years">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Labor_Day_hurricane_1935-09-04_weather_map.gif"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="757" data-file-width="888" height="213" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/10/Labor_Day_hurricane_1935-09-04_weather_map.gif/250px-Labor_Day_hurricane_1935-09-04_weather_map.gif" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/10/Labor_Day_hurricane_1935-09-04_weather_map.gif/375px-Labor_Day_hurricane_1935-09-04_weather_map.gif 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/10/Labor_Day_hurricane_1935-09-04_weather_map.gif/500px-Labor_Day_hurricane_1935-09-04_weather_map.gif 2x" width="250"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Labor_Day_hurricane_1935-09-04_weather_map.gif" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Weather Bureau surface weather map of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/1935_Labor_Day_Hurricane" title="1935 Labor Day Hurricane">1935 Labor Day Hurricane</a> moving up the west coast of Florida</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The first hurricane warning service was set up in the early 1870s from Cuba with the work of Father Benito Viñes, who served as director of the Meteorological Observatory of the Royal College of Belén. He established a network of observation sites and developed the first method to forecast tropical cyclone movement, with the oldest known forecast made for a <a href="/wiki/1873_Atlantic_hurricane_season#Hurricane_Two" title="1873 Atlantic hurricane season">hurricane in late August 1873</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sheets_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Sheets-1">[1]</a></sup> He would give track details days in advance, based upon clouds which progress well in advance of hurricanes. His hurricane warning service in Cuba continued until his death on July 23, 1893.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> Within the United States, the public was dissatisfied with the Signal Corp forecasts after the <a href="/wiki/1875_Atlantic_hurricane_season#Hurricane_Three" title="1875 Atlantic hurricane season">Hurricane of 1875</a>. The immediate response by the Signal Corp was the creation of the hurricane warning flag, a pair of red flags ten by eight feet each in size, inset with black rectangles. Beginning on October 1, 1875, hurricane warning flags were hoisted in areas where hurricane warnings were in effect, and illuminated at night.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/United_States_Congress" title="United States Congress">United States Congress</a> passed a bill to authorize the establishment and operations of <a href="/wiki/Weather_station" title="Weather station">weather stations</a> across the <a href="/wiki/West_Indies" title="West Indies">West Indies</a> and Caribbean Sea on July 7, 1889. The ultimate outcome was the advent of the Weather Bureau in 1890, through the passing of the Organic Act which assigned the new organization to the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_Agriculture" title="United States Department of Agriculture">Department of Agriculture</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wb59_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-wb59-4">[4]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/1896_Cedar_Keys_hurricane" title="1896 Cedar Keys hurricane">September 1896 hurricane</a> led to an expansion of the Weather Bureau hurricane network through the Caribbean Sea.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sheets_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Sheets-1">[1]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Spanish%E2%80%93American_War" title="Spanish–American War">Spanish–American War</a> led to the United States establishing a hurricane warning office in <a href="/wiki/Kingston,_Jamaica" title="Kingston, Jamaica">Kingston, Jamaica</a> in 1898, before shifting to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Havana,_Cuba" title="Havana, Cuba">Havana, Cuba</a> after the war's end in 1899.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wb59_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-wb59-4">[4]</a></sup> After the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/1900_Galveston_Hurricane" title="1900 Galveston Hurricane">1900 Galveston Hurricane</a>, a hurricane warning office was established at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/New_Orleans,_Louisiana" title="New Orleans, Louisiana">New Orleans, Louisiana</a> to deal with hurricane warnings in the Gulf of Mexico.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Simpson_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Simpson-5">[5]</a></sup> The Hurricane Warning Service moved to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Washington,_D._C." title="Washington, D. C.">Washington, D. C.</a> in 1902. The use of <a href="/wiki/Radio" title="Radio">radio</a> by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shipping" title="Shipping">shipping</a>, which began in 1905, added significantly more information for those tracking hurricanes. The first report from a hurricane was received in 1909, with the total of radio reports rising to 21,000 per hurricane season in 1935.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wb59_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-wb59-4">[4]</a></sup> Despite the issuance of hurricane watches and warnings, forecasting the path of tropical cyclones did not occur until 1920.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WJ_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-WJ-6">[6]</a></sup></p>

<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Hurricane_warning_(USA).jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Hurricane warning flag used in the United States</div>

<p>A hurricane warning program was established in 1935 and established regional offices in <a href="/wiki/Jacksonville,_Florida" title="Jacksonville, Florida">Jacksonville, Florida</a>, <a href="/wiki/Washington,_D.C." title="Washington, D.C.">Washington, D.C.</a>, <a href="/wiki/San_Juan,_Puerto_Rico" title="San Juan, Puerto Rico">San Juan, Puerto Rico</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/New_Orleans,_Louisiana" title="New Orleans, Louisiana">New Orleans, Louisiana</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sheets_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-Sheets-1">[1]</a></sup> Hurricane warning offices issued advisories at six hourly intervals for tropical cyclones,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> issuing warnings for <a href="/wiki/Wind#Wind_force_scale" title="Wind">storm and hurricane-force winds</a>. The idea of aircraft reconnaissance of hurricanes was put forth by Captain W. L. Farnsworth of the Galveston Commercial Association in the early 1930s. Supported by the United States Weather Bureau, the "storm patrol bill" passed both the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Senate" title="United States Senate">United States Senate</a> and <a href="/wiki/United_States_House_of_Representatives" title="United States House of Representatives">United States House of Representatives</a> on June 15, 1936.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> The Jacksonville hurricane warning office moved to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Miami,_Florida" title="Miami, Florida">Miami, Florida</a> in 1943, and a hurricane warning office was opened in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Boston,_Massachusetts" title="Boston, Massachusetts">Boston, Massachusetts</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wb59_4-3"><a href="#cite_note-wb59-4">[4]</a></sup> After <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>, the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Navy" title="United States Navy">United States Navy</a> and <a href="/wiki/United_States_Air_Force" title="United States Air Force">United States Air Force</a> had separate warning centers for the military. In the case of tropical cyclones, the civilian and military warning agencies maintained close coordination.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Simpson_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-Simpson-5">[5]</a></sup> Tropical cyclone naming began for Atlantic tropical cyclones using the <a href="/wiki/Spelling_alphabet" title="Spelling alphabet">phonetic alphabet</a> by 1947,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> switching to female names in 1953.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Starting in 1950, the Miami Hurricane Warning Office began to prepare the annual hurricane season summary articles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> Tropical cyclone track forecasts for one day in the future began in 1954.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-verify_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-verify-12">[12]</a></sup> After the <a href="/wiki/1954_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="1954 Atlantic hurricane season">1954 Atlantic hurricane season</a>, efforts were made to enhance the hurricane reporting network along the coast by setting up a Cooperative Hurricane Reporting Network (CHURN) by supplying anemometers and barometers to members of the public which left no length of United States coastline along the Atlantic or Gulf coasts greater than 25 miles (40 km) uncovered.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Hurricane_season_concept">Hurricane season concept</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_Atlantic_hurricane_warnings&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Hurricane season concept">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">

<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Hurricane_Donna.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Radar image of <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Donna" title="Hurricane Donna">Hurricane Donna</a> (1960)</div>
</div>

<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="Atlantic hurricane season">Atlantic hurricane season</a></div>
<p>The basic concept of a hurricane season began during 1935,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> when dedicated <a href="/wiki/Two-wire_circuit" title="Two-wire circuit">wire circuits</a> known as hurricane circuits began to be set up along the Gulf and Atlantic coasts,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AHS1959_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-AHS1959-15">[15]</a></sup> a process completed by 1955.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> It was originally the time frame when the <a href="/wiki/Tropics" title="Tropics">tropics</a> were monitored routinely for tropical cyclone activity, and was originally defined as from June 15 through October 31.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1960AHS_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-1960AHS-17">[17]</a></sup> Over the years, the beginning date was shifted back to June 1, while the end date was shifted to November 15,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AHS1959_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-AHS1959-15">[15]</a></sup> before settling at November 30 by 1965.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> This was when <a href="/wiki/53d_Weather_Reconnaissance_Squadron" title="53d Weather Reconnaissance Squadron">hurricane reconnaissance planes</a> were sent out to fly across the Atlantic and Gulf of Mexico on a routine basis to look for potential tropical cyclones, in the years prior to the continuous <a href="/wiki/Weather_satellite" title="Weather satellite">weather satellite</a> era.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1960AHS_17-1"><a href="#cite_note-1960AHS-17">[17]</a></sup> After regular satellite surveillance began, hurricane hunter aircraft flew only into areas which were spotted first by satellite imagery.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> Starting in 1958, a cooperative hurricane reporting network composed of over 100 unpaid observers would send observations in real-time during the hurricane season to their local Weather Bureau offices. The spacing of the network was roughly 40 kilometres (25 mi) between sites, and the locations would report wind speed, wind direction, rainfall, and pressure.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="National_Hurricane_Research_Project">National Hurricane Research Project</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_Atlantic_hurricane_warnings&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: National Hurricane Research Project">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Simpson_%26_Gentry_at_ROB.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1207" data-file-width="960" height="277" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/06/Simpson_%26_Gentry_at_ROB.jpg/220px-Simpson_%26_Gentry_at_ROB.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/06/Simpson_%26_Gentry_at_ROB.jpg/330px-Simpson_%26_Gentry_at_ROB.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/06/Simpson_%26_Gentry_at_ROB.jpg/440px-Simpson_%26_Gentry_at_ROB.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Simpson_%26_Gentry_at_ROB.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
NHRP opening day</div>
</div>
</div>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/National_Hurricane_Research_Project" title="National Hurricane Research Project">National Hurricane Research Project</a></div>
<p>The National Hurricane Research Project (NHRP) was initiated in 1955 by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_States_Weather_Bureau" title="United States Weather Bureau">United States Weather Bureau</a> in response to the devastating 1954 hurricane season, which significantly impacted the Mid-Atlantic states and New England. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bob_Simpson_(meteorologist)" title="Bob Simpson (meteorologist)">Robert Simpson</a>, a Weather Bureau meteorologist who had participated in Air Force hurricane reconnaissance flights as an observer, was appointed as the first director of NHRP and organized the Research Operations Base at Morrison Air Force Base (now <a href="/wiki/Palm_Beach_International_Airport" title="Palm Beach International Airport">Palm Beach International Airport</a>) in <a href="/wiki/West_Palm_Beach,_Florida" title="West Palm Beach, Florida">West Palm Beach, Florida</a> in 1956. During the first three years of the Project, scientists used three specially instrumented Air Force <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hurricane_Hunters" title="Hurricane Hunters">Hurricane Hunters</a> aircraft with crews on bailment from the 55th Weather Reconnaissance Squadron, collecting data which delineated the structure and energy budget of hurricanes for the first time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HRDhistory_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-HRDhistory-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1959, the Project was moved to Miami and collocated with the Miami hurricane forecast office. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Department_of_Commerce" title="Department of Commerce">Department of Commerce</a> leased two DC-6 aircraft and received a B-57 jet from the Air Force so that NHRP could continue to carry out airborne experiments on hurricanes. The combination of research project, forecast center, and aircraft facility was dubbed the "<a href="/wiki/National_Hurricane_Center" title="National Hurricane Center">National Hurricane Center</a>" (NHC). During the 1960s, while NHRP continued to carry out research flights, the Project also began to create computer models of hurricane circulation, formulated a statistical track program (NHC-64), wrote a manual on hurricane forecasting, and evaluated the accuracy of track forecasts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HRDhistory_22-1"><a href="#cite_note-HRDhistory-22">[22]</a></sup> Starting with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hurricane_Esther_(1961)" title="Hurricane Esther (1961)">Hurricane Esther (1961)</a>, NHRP was heavily involved with <a href="/wiki/Project_Stormfury" title="Project Stormfury">Project Stormfury</a>, the U. S. Government's experiment in hurricane modification. Late in 1964, the Project was renamed the <a href="/wiki/National_Hurricane_Research_Laboratory" title="National Hurricane Research Laboratory">National Hurricane Research Laboratory</a> in recognition of it becoming a permanent institution within the Weather Bureau. This presaged the creation of the Environmental Research Laboratories the next year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-stormfury_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-stormfury-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="National_Hurricane_Center">National Hurricane Center</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_Atlantic_hurricane_warnings&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: National Hurricane Center">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Nhctour.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="226" data-file-width="448" height="111" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/11/Nhctour.png/220px-Nhctour.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/11/Nhctour.png/330px-Nhctour.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/11/Nhctour.png/440px-Nhctour.png 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Nhctour.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Front view of the National Hurricane Center</div>
</div>
</div>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/National_Hurricane_Center" title="National Hurricane Center">National Hurricane Center</a></div>
<p>On July 1, 1956, a National Hurricane Information Center had become established in Miami, Florida which became a warehouse for all hurricane-related information from one office.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> The Miami Hurricane Warning Office (HWO) was moved from Lindsey Hopkins Hotel to the Aviation Building 4 miles (6.4 km) to the northwest on July 1, 1958.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> Forecasts for tropical cyclone movement were extended to two days in advance (at one day intervals) in 1961.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-verify_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-verify-12">[12]</a></sup> The Miami HWO moved to the campus of the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Miami" title="University of Miami">University of Miami</a> in 1964,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-stormfury_23-1"><a href="#cite_note-stormfury-23">[23]</a></sup> which is when tropical cyclone forecasts were extended to three days into the future, at one day intervals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-verify_12-2"><a href="#cite_note-verify-12">[12]</a></sup> The Miami HWO tropical cyclone reports were done regularly and took on their modern format in 1964.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> In 1970, NHC began analyzing tropical cyclone initial positions in real-time and added a 12‑hour forecast.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-verify_12-3"><a href="#cite_note-verify-12">[12]</a></sup> Beginning in 1973, a portion of the National Meteorological Center (later renamed the Weather Prediction Center)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HPChistory_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-HPChistory-27">[27]</a></sup> gained advisory responsibility for dissipating inland tropical depressions. The <a href="/wiki/World_Meteorological_Organization" title="World Meteorological Organization">World Meteorological Organization</a> assumed control of the Atlantic hurricane naming list in 1977.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1978, the NHC's offices moved off campus to the IRE Financial Building.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> The hurricane warning offices remained active past 1983.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WJ_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-WJ-6">[6]</a></sup> Organizationally, the NHC left the aegis of the <a href="/wiki/National_Weather_Service" title="National Weather Service">National Weather Service</a> State Forecast Office in Miami in 1984,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> leaving the Southern Region and becoming its own national center, one of three in existence at that time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sheets_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-Sheets-1">[1]</a></sup> In 1988, NHC added a 36-hour point to their forecast.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-verify_12-4"><a href="#cite_note-verify-12">[12]</a></sup> During the National Weather Service reorganization in the 1990s, the NHC was renamed the Tropical Prediction Center on October 1, 1995,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TPC_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-TPC-31">[31]</a></sup> and moved into a building on the campus of <a href="/wiki/Florida_International_University" title="Florida International University">Florida International University</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NHC_bunker_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-NHC_bunker-32">[32]</a></sup> The Hurricane Specialists were grouped as a separate NHC unit under the Tropical Prediction Center,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TPC_31-1"><a href="#cite_note-TPC-31">[31]</a></sup> separating themselves from the Tropical Analysis and Forecast Branch. By 2001, tropical cyclone forecasts were extended from three to five days in advance, including forecast points for 96 and 120 hours.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-verify_12-5"><a href="#cite_note-verify-12">[12]</a></sup> On October 1, 2010, the Tropical Prediction Center was renamed the NHC,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> and the group formerly known as the NHC became known as the Hurricane Specialists Unit (HSU).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Tropical_cyclone_forecasting">Tropical cyclone forecasting</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_Atlantic_hurricane_warnings&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Tropical cyclone forecasting">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Ernesto2006modelspread.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="914" data-file-width="1282" height="178" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fd/Ernesto2006modelspread.png/250px-Ernesto2006modelspread.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fd/Ernesto2006modelspread.png/375px-Ernesto2006modelspread.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fd/Ernesto2006modelspread.png/500px-Ernesto2006modelspread.png 2x" width="250"/></a>

<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Numerical_weather_prediction" title="Numerical weather prediction">Numerical weather prediction</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone_forecast_model" title="Tropical cyclone forecast model">Tropical cyclone forecast model</a></div>
<p>Tropical cyclone forecasting relies on data provided by numerical weather models. Three main classes of <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone_forecast_model" title="Tropical cyclone forecast model">tropical cyclone guidance models</a> exist: Statistical models are based on an analysis of storm behavior using climatology, and correlate a storm's position and date to produce a forecast that is not based on the physics of the atmosphere at the time. Dynamical models are numerical models that solve the governing equations of fluid flow in the atmosphere; they are based on the same principles as other limited-area numerical weather prediction models but may include special computational techniques such as refined spatial domains that move along with the cyclone. Models that use elements of both approaches are called statistical-dynamical models.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1978, the first <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone_forecast_model" title="Tropical cyclone forecast model">hurricane-tracking model</a> based on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atmospheric_dynamics#Dynamic_meteorology" title="Atmospheric dynamics">atmospheric dynamics</a>—the movable fine-mesh (MFM) model—began operating.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Shuman_W.26F_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-Shuman_W.26F-36">[36]</a></sup> Within the field of <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone_track_forecasting" title="Tropical cyclone track forecasting">tropical cyclone track forecasting</a>, despite the ever-improving dynamical model guidance which occurred with increased computational power, it was not until the 1980s when numerical weather prediction showed <a href="/wiki/Forecast_skill" title="Forecast skill">skill</a>, and until the 1990s when it consistently outperformed <a href="/wiki/Statistical_model" title="Statistical model">statistical</a> or simple dynamical models.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> Predictions of the intensity of a tropical cyclone based on numerical weather prediction continue to be a challenge, since statistical methods continue to show higher skill over dynamical guidance.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span id="Advisory,_watch,_and_warning_evolution"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Advisory.2C_watch.2C_and_warning_evolution">Advisory, watch, and warning evolution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_Atlantic_hurricane_warnings&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Advisory, watch, and warning evolution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Stormwarning.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="292" data-file-width="292" height="220" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/99/Stormwarning.png/220px-Stormwarning.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/9/99/Stormwarning.png 1.5x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Stormwarning.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Tropical storm warning flag used in the United States</div>
</div>
</div>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone_warnings_and_watches#North_Atlantic_systems" title="Tropical cyclone warnings and watches">Tropical cyclone warnings and watches § North Atlantic systems</a></div>
<p>The timing and naming of tropical cyclone advisories, watches, and warnings has changed over time. In 1958, tropical cyclone advisories were issued every six hours starting at 0400 <a href="/wiki/Coordinated_Universal_Time" title="Coordinated Universal Time">UTC</a> each day. During 1967, hurricane watches were used to designate areas where hurricane conditions were possible in the next 24 hours, while hurricane warnings indicated areas where the hurricane center should cross the coast. Small craft, gale, and storm warnings were issued for hurricanes not expected to make <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Landfall_(meteorology)" title="Landfall (meteorology)">landfall</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> By 1987, the definition of tropical cyclone watches had changed to areas where gale or hurricane-force winds were possible within 36 hours, with warnings issued when gale or hurricane-force winds were expected within 24 hours. In 1987, gale watches/warnings were renamed tropical storm watches/warnings.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> In 1991, advisory timing shifted back to every six hours starting at 0330 UTC every day.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> In 1992, advisory timing changed to every six hours starting at 0300 UTC each day.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> The length of time used for watches and warnings changed again in 2010, with watches using a 48‑hour time frame and warnings using a 36-hour time frame.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup></p>
<p>Within the United States, a separate set of inland tropical cyclone warnings was issued by National Weather Service forecast offices, based upon the latest NHC advisory, during the decade of the 2000s. These warnings used the word "Wind" inserted before the "Watch" or "Warning", which was dropped for 2005. Previously, standard High Wind Warnings and Watches were issued (which denote ≥39 MPH winds or ≥58 mph gusts). When they were inland, watches and warnings were posted for tropical storm or hurricane-force winds expected during the next 24 and 12 hours, respectively.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Current_operations">Current operations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_Atlantic_hurricane_warnings&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Current operations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/National_Hurricane_Center" title="National Hurricane Center">National Hurricane Center</a> and <a href="/wiki/Weather_Prediction_Center" title="Weather Prediction Center">Weather Prediction Center</a></div>
<p>During the hurricane season, the National Hurricane Center routinely issues their Tropical Weather Outlook product, which identifies areas of concern within the tropics which could develop into tropical cyclones. If systems occur outside the defined hurricane season, special Tropical Weather Outlooks will be issued.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup> While a tropical cyclone is active, six hourly advisories will be issued, which become more frequent once a tropical cyclone watch or warning are issued. Tropical cyclone watches and warnings are coordinated within the countries and dependencies involved, with the National Hurricane Center coordinating with National Weather Service Forecast Offices concerning storms threatening the United States and its dependencies. Routine coordination occurs at 1700 UTC each day between the Weather Prediction Center and National Hurricane Center to identify systems for the pressure maps three to seven days into the future within the tropics, and points for existing tropical cyclones six to seven days into the future.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup></p>
