<p>The U.S. state of <a href="/wiki/North_Carolina" title="North Carolina">North Carolina</a> is divided into 100 <a href="/wiki/County_(United_States)" title="County (United States)">counties</a>. North Carolina ranks 29th in size by area, but has the seventh-highest number of counties in the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">country</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Following the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Restoration" title="The Restoration">restoration of the monarchy</a> in 1660, <a href="/wiki/Charles_II_of_England" title="Charles II of England">King Charles II</a> rewarded eight persons on March 24, 1663, for their faithful support of his efforts to regain the throne of England. He gave the eight grantees, called <a href="/wiki/Lords_Proprietor" title="Lords Proprietor">Lords Proprietor</a>, the land called Carolina, in honor of <a href="/wiki/Charles_I_of_England" title="Charles I of England">King Charles I</a>, his father. The <a href="/wiki/Province_of_Carolina" title="Province of Carolina">Province of Carolina</a>, from 1663 to 1729, was a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/North_American" title="North American">North American</a> <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_England" title="Kingdom of England">English</a> (1663-1707), then <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Great_Britain" title="Kingdom of Great Britain">British</a> (from 1707 union with Scotland) colony. In 1729, the <a href="/wiki/Province_of_North_Carolina" title="Province of North Carolina">Province of North Carolina</a> became a separate entity from the <a href="/wiki/Province_of_South_Carolina" title="Province of South Carolina">Province of South Carolina</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>The establishment of North Carolina counties stretches over 240 years, beginning in 1668 with the creation of <a href="/wiki/Albemarle_County,_North_Carolina" title="Albemarle County, North Carolina">Albemarle County</a> and ending with the 1911 creation of <a href="/wiki/Avery_County,_North_Carolina" title="Avery County, North Carolina">Avery</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hoke_County,_North_Carolina" title="Hoke County, North Carolina">Hoke</a> counties. Five counties have been divided or abolished altogether, the last being <a href="/wiki/Dobbs_County,_North_Carolina" title="Dobbs County, North Carolina">Dobbs County</a> in 1791.</p>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Federal_Information_Processing_Standard" title="Federal Information Processing Standard">Federal Information Processing Standard (FIPS)</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EPA_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-EPA-3">[3]</a></sup> which is used by the United States government to uniquely identify states and counties, is provided with each entry. North Carolina's FIPS code is 37, which when combined with the county code is written as 37<var style="padding-right: 1px;">XXX</var>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Dates_and_numbers#Chronological_items" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Dates and numbers"><span title="FIPS codes for states and counties were withdrawn in 2008. (February 2017)">needs update</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
