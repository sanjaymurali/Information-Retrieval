<p>The <b>Lockheed WP-3D Orion</b> is a highly modified <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/P-3_Orion" title="P-3 Orion">P-3 Orion</a> used by the <a href="/wiki/Aircraft_Operations_Center" title="Aircraft Operations Center">Aircraft Operations Center</a> division of the <a href="/wiki/National_Oceanic_and_Atmospheric_Administration" title="National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration">National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration (NOAA)</a>. Only two of these aircraft exist, each incorporating numerous features for the role of collecting weather information. During <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="Atlantic hurricane season">hurricane season</a>, the WP-3Ds are deployed for duty as <a href="/wiki/NOAA_Hurricane_Hunters" title="NOAA Hurricane Hunters">hurricane hunters</a>. The aircraft also support research on other topics, such as Arctic ice coverage, air chemistry studies, and ocean water temperature and current analysis.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-:0-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>The WP-3Ds are equipped with three weather <a href="/wiki/Radar" title="Radar">radars</a>, <a href="/wiki/C_band_(IEEE)" title="C band (IEEE)">C band</a> radars in the nose and on the lower fuselage, and an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/X-band_radar" title="X-band radar">X-band radar</a> in the aircraft's tail. They are also equipped with the ability to deploy <a href="/wiki/Dropsonde" title="Dropsonde">dropsondes</a> into storm systems, and have onboard temperature sensors, and other meteorological equipment. While the aircraft are not specially strengthened for flying into hurricanes, their decks were reinforced to withstand the additional equipment load.</p>
<p>It has two <a href="/wiki/Barber%27s_pole" title="Barber's pole">barber-pole</a> samplers (named for their red-and-white stripes) that protrude from the aircraft’s front, a tail <a href="/wiki/Weather_radar" title="Weather radar">Doppler weather radar</a>, and other unique-looking instruments hanging from the wing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>NOAA currently operates two WP-3Ds nicknamed <i><a href="/wiki/Miss_Piggy" title="Miss Piggy">Miss Piggy</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Kermit_the_Frog" title="Kermit the Frog">Kermit</a></i>, and their logos featuring the characters created by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jim_Henson_Productions" title="Jim Henson Productions">Jim Henson Productions</a>. NOAA's other hurricane hunting aircraft, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gulfstream_IV-SP" title="Gulfstream IV-SP">Gulfstream IV-SP</a>, is named <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gonzo_(muppet)" title="Gonzo (muppet)">Gonzo</a></i>; they complement the fleet of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/WC-130" title="WC-130">WC-130</a> aircraft operated by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._Air_Force" title="U.S. Air Force">U.S. Air Force</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/53rd_Weather_Reconnaissance_Squadron" title="53rd Weather Reconnaissance Squadron">53rd Weather Reconnaissance Squadron</a>. As of 2014, the two Orions had each flown more than 10,000 hours and flown into more than 80 hurricanes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-:0-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>During 2015 to 2017 the aircraft will receive major overhauls, costing a total of $35 million. This work is being performed by the US Navy, Fleet Readiness Center SE in Jacksonville, Florida. The work includes new wings and engines and upgraded radars and avionics. NOAA anticipates that these changes will allow the aircraft to fly for another 15-20 years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-:0-1">[1]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span id="Specifications_(P-3_Orion)"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Specifications_.28P-3_Orion.29">Specifications (P-3 Orion)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lockheed_WP-3D_Orion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Specifications (P-3 Orion)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><span style="font-size: 120%;"><b>General characteristics</b></span></p>
<ul>
<li><b>Crew:</b> 11</li>
<li><b>Length:</b> 116 ft 10 in (35.6 m)</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Wingspan" title="Wingspan">Wingspan</a>:</b> 99 ft 8 in (30.4 m)</li>
<li><b>Height:</b> 33 ft 8.5 in (10.3 m)</li>
<li><b>Wing area:</b> 1300 ft² (120.8 m²)</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Manufacturer%27s_empty_weight" title="Manufacturer's empty weight">Empty weight</a>:</b> 61,500 lb (27,900 kg)</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Maximum_takeoff_weight" title="Maximum takeoff weight">Max. takeoff weight</a>:</b> 139,760 lb (63,400 kg)</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Aircraft_engine" title="Aircraft engine">Powerplant</a>:</b> 4 × <a href="/wiki/Allison_T56" title="Allison T56">Allison T56-A-14</a> turboprop, 4,600 shp (3,700 kW) each</li>
</ul>
<p><span style="font-size: 120%;"><b>Performance</b></span></p>
<ul>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/V_speeds#Regulatory_V-speeds" title="V speeds">Maximum speed</a>:</b> 466 mph (745 km/h)</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Range_(aeronautics)" title="Range (aeronautics)">Range</a>:</b> 2,700 miles combat / 5,600 miles ferry (4,400 km / 9,000 km)</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Ceiling_(aeronautics)" title="Ceiling (aeronautics)">Service ceiling</a>:</b> 28,300 ft (8,600 m)</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Rate_of_climb" title="Rate of climb">Rate of climb</a>:</b> 3,140 ft/min (957 m/min)</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Wing_loading" title="Wing loading">Wing loading</a>:</b> 107 lb/ft² (530 kg/m²)</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Power-to-weight_ratio" title="Power-to-weight ratio">Power/mass</a>:</b> 0.3 hp/lb (0.06 kW/kg)</li>
</ul>
