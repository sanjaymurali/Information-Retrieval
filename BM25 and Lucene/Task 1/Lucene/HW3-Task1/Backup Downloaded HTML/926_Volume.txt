<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Volume</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">

A <a href="/wiki/Measuring_cup" title="Measuring cup">measuring cup</a> can be used to measure volumes of <a href="/wiki/Liquid" title="Liquid">liquids</a>. This cup measures volume in units of <a href="/wiki/Cup_(unit)" title="Cup (unit)">cups</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fluid_ounce" title="Fluid ounce">fluid ounces</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Millilitre" title="Millilitre">millilitres</a>.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p><b>Volume</b> is the <a href="/wiki/Quantity" title="Quantity">quantity</a> of <a href="/wiki/Three-dimensional_space" title="Three-dimensional space">three-dimensional space</a> enclosed by a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Closed_surface" title="Closed surface">closed surface</a>, for example, the space that a substance (<a href="/wiki/Solid" title="Solid">solid</a>, <a href="/wiki/Liquid" title="Liquid">liquid</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gas" title="Gas">gas</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Plasma_(physics)" title="Plasma (physics)">plasma</a>) or shape occupies or contains.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> Volume is often quantified numerically using the <a href="/wiki/SI_derived_unit" title="SI derived unit">SI derived unit</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Cubic_metre" title="Cubic metre">cubic metre</a>. The volume of a container is generally understood to be the capacity of the container; i. e., the amount of fluid (gas or liquid) that the container could hold, rather than the amount of space the container itself displaces.</p>
<p>Three dimensional mathematical shapes are also assigned volumes. Volumes of some simple shapes, such as regular, straight-edged, and circular shapes can be easily calculated using <a href="/wiki/Arithmetic" title="Arithmetic">arithmetic</a> <a href="/wiki/Formula" title="Formula">formulas</a>. Volumes of a complicated shape can be calculated by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Integral_calculus" title="Integral calculus">integral calculus</a> if a formula exists for the shape's boundary. Where a variance in shape and volume occurs, such as those that exist between different human beings, these can be calculated using three-dimensional techniques such as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Body_Volume_Index" title="Body Volume Index">Body Volume Index</a>. One-dimensional figures (such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Line_(mathematics)" title="Line (mathematics)">lines</a>) and two-dimensional shapes (such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Square_(geometry)" title="Square (geometry)">squares</a>) are assigned zero volume in the three-dimensional space.</p>
<p>The volume of a solid (whether regularly or irregularly shaped) can be determined by <a href="/wiki/Displacement_(fluid)" title="Displacement (fluid)">fluid displacement</a>. Displacement of liquid can also be used to determine the volume of a gas. The combined volume of two substances is usually greater than the volume of one of the substances. However, sometimes one substance dissolves in the other and the combined volume is not <a href="/wiki/Additive_map" title="Additive map">additive</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>In <i><a href="/wiki/Differential_geometry" title="Differential geometry">differential geometry</a></i>, volume is expressed by means of the <a href="/wiki/Volume_form" title="Volume form">volume form</a>, and is an important global <a href="/wiki/Riemannian_geometry" title="Riemannian geometry">Riemannian</a> <a href="/wiki/Invariant_(mathematics)" title="Invariant (mathematics)">invariant</a>. In <i><a href="/wiki/Thermodynamics" title="Thermodynamics">thermodynamics</a></i>, volume is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gas_volume" title="Gas volume">fundamental parameter</a>, and is a <a href="/wiki/Conjugate_variables_(thermodynamics)" title="Conjugate variables (thermodynamics)">conjugate variable</a> to <a href="/wiki/Pressure" title="Pressure">pressure</a>.</p>
<p></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Units">Units</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Volume&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Units">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>Any unit of <a href="/wiki/Length" title="Length">length</a> gives a corresponding unit of volume: the volume of a <a href="/wiki/Cube" title="Cube">cube</a> whose sides have the given length. For example, a <a href="/wiki/Cubic_centimetre" title="Cubic centimetre">cubic centimetre</a> (cm<sup>3</sup>) is the volume of a cube whose sides are one <a href="/wiki/Centimetre" title="Centimetre">centimetre</a> (1 cm) in length.</p>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/International_System_of_Units" title="International System of Units">International System of Units</a> (SI), the standard unit of volume is the cubic metre (m<sup>3</sup>). The <a href="/wiki/Metric_system" title="Metric system">metric system</a> also includes the <a href="/wiki/Litre" title="Litre">litre</a> (L) as a unit of volume, where one litre is the volume of a 10-centimetre cube. Thus</p>
<dl>
<dd>1 litre = (10 cm)<sup>3</sup> = 1000 cubic centimetres = 0.001 cubic metres,</dd>
</dl>
<p>so</p>
<dl>
<dd>1 cubic metre = 1000 litres.</dd>
</dl>
<p>Small amounts of liquid are often measured in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Millilitre" title="Millilitre">millilitres</a>, where</p>
<dl>
<dd>1 millilitre = 0.001 litres = 1 cubic centimetre.</dd>
</dl>
<p>Various other traditional units of volume are also in use, including the <a href="/wiki/Cubic_inch" title="Cubic inch">cubic inch</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Cubic_foot" title="Cubic foot">cubic foot</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Cubic_mile" title="Cubic mile">cubic mile</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Teaspoon" title="Teaspoon">teaspoon</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Tablespoon" title="Tablespoon">tablespoon</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Fluid_ounce" title="Fluid ounce">fluid ounce</a>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fluid_dram" title="Fluid dram">fluid dram</a>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gill_(volume)" title="Gill (volume)">gill</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Pint" title="Pint">pint</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Quart" title="Quart">quart</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Gallon" title="Gallon">gallon</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Minim_(unit)" title="Minim (unit)">minim</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Barrel_(unit)" title="Barrel (unit)">barrel</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Cord_(unit)" title="Cord (unit)">cord</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Peck" title="Peck">peck</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Bushel" title="Bushel">bushel</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Hogshead" title="Hogshead">hogshead</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Related_terms">Related terms</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Volume&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Related terms">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><i>Capacity</i> is defined by the <a href="/wiki/Oxford_English_Dictionary" title="Oxford English Dictionary">Oxford English Dictionary</a> as "the measure applied to the content of a vessel, and to liquids, grain, or the like, which take the shape of that which holds them".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> (The word <i>capacity</i> has other unrelated meanings, as in e.g. <a href="/wiki/Capacity_management" title="Capacity management">capacity management</a>.) Capacity is not identical in meaning to volume, though closely related; the capacity of a container is always the volume in its interior. Units of capacity are the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/SI" title="SI">SI</a> litre and its derived units, and Imperial units such as <a href="/wiki/Gill_(unit)" title="Gill (unit)">gill</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pint" title="Pint">pint</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gallon" title="Gallon">gallon</a>, and others. Units of volume are the cubes of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Units_of_length" title="Units of length">units of length</a>. In SI the units of volume and capacity are closely related: one litre is exactly 1 cubic decimetre, the capacity of a cube with a 10 cm side. In other systems the conversion is not trivial; the capacity of a vehicle's fuel tank is rarely stated in cubic feet, for example, but in gallons (a gallon fills a volume of 0.1605 cu ft).</p>
<p>The <i><a href="/wiki/Density" title="Density">density</a></i> of an object is defined as the ratio of the mass to the volume.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> The inverse of density is <i><a href="/wiki/Specific_volume" title="Specific volume">specific volume</a></i> which is defined as volume divided by mass. Specific volume is a concept important in <a href="/wiki/Thermodynamics" title="Thermodynamics">thermodynamics</a> where the <a href="/wiki/Volume_(thermodynamics)" title="Volume (thermodynamics)">volume of a working fluid</a> is often an important parameter of a system being studied.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Volumetric_flow_rate" title="Volumetric flow rate">volumetric flow rate</a> in <a href="/wiki/Fluid_dynamics" title="Fluid dynamics">fluid dynamics</a> is the volume of fluid which passes through a given surface per unit time (for example cubic meters per second [m<sup>3</sup> s<sup>−1</sup>]).</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Volume_in_differential_geometry">Volume in differential geometry</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Volume&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Volume in differential geometry">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Volume_form" title="Volume form">Volume form</a></div>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Differential_geometry" title="Differential geometry">differential geometry</a>, a branch of <a href="/wiki/Mathematics" title="Mathematics">mathematics</a>, a <b>volume form</b> on a <a href="/wiki/Differentiable_manifold" title="Differentiable manifold">differentiable manifold</a> is a <a href="/wiki/Differential_form" title="Differential form">differential form</a> of top degree (i.e., whose degree is equal to the dimension of the manifold) that is nowhere equal to zero. A manifold has a volume form if and only if it is orientable. An orientable manifold has infinitely many volume forms, since multiplying a volume form by a non-vanishing function yields another volume form. On non-orientable manifolds, one may instead define the weaker notion of a <a href="/wiki/Density_on_a_manifold" title="Density on a manifold">density</a>. Integrating the volume form gives the volume of the manifold according to that form.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Volume_in_thermodynamics">Volume in thermodynamics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Volume&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Volume in thermodynamics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Volume_(thermodynamics)" title="Volume (thermodynamics)">Volume (thermodynamics)</a></div>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Thermodynamics" title="Thermodynamics">thermodynamics</a>, the <b>volume</b> of a <a href="/wiki/Thermodynamic_system" title="Thermodynamic system">system</a> is an important <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Extensive_parameter" title="Extensive parameter">extensive parameter</a> for describing its <a href="/wiki/Thermodynamic_state" title="Thermodynamic state">thermodynamic state</a>. The <b>specific volume</b>, an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Intensive_property" title="Intensive property">intensive property</a>, is the system's volume per unit of mass. Volume is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Function_of_state" title="Function of state">function of state</a> and is interdependent with other thermodynamic properties such as <a href="/wiki/Pressure" title="Pressure">pressure</a> and <a href="/wiki/Thermodynamic_temperature" title="Thermodynamic temperature">temperature</a>. For example, volume is related to the <a href="/wiki/Pressure" title="Pressure">pressure</a> and <a href="/wiki/Thermodynamic_temperature" title="Thermodynamic temperature">temperature</a> of an <a href="/wiki/Ideal_gas" title="Ideal gas">ideal gas</a> by the <a href="/wiki/Ideal_gas_law" title="Ideal gas law">ideal gas law</a>.</p>
