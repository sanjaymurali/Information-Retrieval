<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Low-pressure area</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

<p>A <b>low-pressure area</b>, <b>low</b>, or <b>depression</b> is a region on the topographic map where the <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_pressure" title="Atmospheric pressure">atmospheric pressure</a> is lower than that of surrounding locations. Low-pressure systems form under areas of wind divergence that occur in the upper levels of the <a href="/wiki/Troposphere" title="Troposphere">troposphere</a>. The formation process of a low-pressure area is known as <a href="/wiki/Cyclogenesis" title="Cyclogenesis">cyclogenesis</a>. Within the field of <a href="/wiki/Meteorology#Dynamic_meteorology" title="Meteorology">meteorology</a>, atmospheric divergence aloft occurs in two areas. The first area is on the east side of upper troughs, which form half of a <a href="/wiki/Rossby_wave" title="Rossby wave">Rossby wave</a> within the <a href="/wiki/Westerlies" title="Westerlies">Westerlies</a> (a <a href="/wiki/Trough_(meteorology)" title="Trough (meteorology)">trough</a> with large <a href="/wiki/Wavelength" title="Wavelength">wavelength</a> that extends through the troposphere). A second area of wind divergence aloft occurs ahead of <a href="/wiki/Shortwave_(meteorology)" title="Shortwave (meteorology)">embedded shortwave troughs</a>, which are of smaller wavelength. Diverging winds aloft ahead of these troughs cause <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atmospheric" title="Atmospheric">atmospheric</a> lift within the troposphere below, which lowers surface pressures as upward motion partially counteracts the force of gravity.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Thermal_low" title="Thermal low">Thermal lows</a> form due to localized heating caused by greater sunshine over deserts and other land masses. Since localized areas of warm air are less dense than their surroundings, this warmer air rises, which lowers atmospheric pressure near that portion of the <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a>'s surface. Large-scale thermal lows over <a href="/wiki/Continent" title="Continent">continents</a> help drive <a href="/wiki/Monsoon" title="Monsoon">monsoon</a> circulations. Low-pressure areas can also form due to organized thunderstorm activity over warm water. When this occurs over the tropics in concert with the <a href="/wiki/Intertropical_Convergence_Zone" title="Intertropical Convergence Zone">Intertropical Convergence Zone</a>, it is known as a <a href="/wiki/Monsoon_trough" title="Monsoon trough">monsoon trough</a>. Monsoon troughs reach their northerly extent in August and their southerly extent in February. When a convective low acquires a well-hot circulation in the tropics it is termed a <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone" title="Tropical cyclone">tropical cyclone</a>. Tropical cyclones can form during any month of the year globally, but can occur in either the northern or southern hemisphere during November.</p>
<p>Atmospheric lift will also generally produce <a href="/wiki/Cloud_cover" title="Cloud cover">cloud cover</a> through <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Adiabatic_lapse_rate" title="Adiabatic lapse rate">adiabatic</a> cooling once the air becomes saturated as it rises, although the low-pressure area typically brings cloudy skies, which act to minimize <a href="/wiki/Diurnal_temperature_variation" title="Diurnal temperature variation">diurnal temperature extremes</a>. Since clouds reflect <a href="/wiki/Sunlight" title="Sunlight">sunlight</a>, incoming <a href="/wiki/Shortwave_radiation" title="Shortwave radiation">shortwave</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solar_radiation" title="Solar radiation">solar radiation</a> decreases, which causes lower <a href="/wiki/Temperature" title="Temperature">temperatures</a> during the day. At night the absorptive effect of clouds on <a href="/wiki/Outgoing_longwave_radiation" title="Outgoing longwave radiation">outgoing longwave radiation</a>, such as heat energy from the surface, allows for warmer diurnal low temperatures in all seasons. The stronger the area of low pressure, the stronger the <a href="/wiki/Wind" title="Wind">winds</a> experienced in its vicinity. Globally, low-pressure systems are most frequently located over the <a href="/wiki/Tibetan_Plateau" title="Tibetan Plateau">Tibetan Plateau</a> and in the <a href="/wiki/Windward_and_leeward" title="Windward and leeward">lee</a> of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rocky_mountains" title="Rocky mountains">Rocky mountains</a>. In Europe (particularly in the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a>), recurring low-pressure weather systems are typically known as "depressions".</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Formation">Formation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Low-pressure_area&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Formation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Cyclogenesis" title="Cyclogenesis">Cyclogenesis</a></div>
<p>Cyclogenesis is the development and strengthening of <a href="/wiki/Cyclone" title="Cyclone">cyclonic</a> circulations, or <b>low-pressure areas</b>, within the <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere" title="Atmosphere">atmosphere</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> Cyclogenesis is the opposite of <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/cyclolysis" title="wikt:cyclolysis">cyclolysis</a>, and has an anticyclonic (high-pressure system) equivalent which deals with the formation of <a href="/wiki/High-pressure_area" title="High-pressure area">high-pressure areas</a>—<a href="/wiki/Anticyclogenesis" title="Anticyclogenesis">anticyclogenesis</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CyclogenesisDef_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-CyclogenesisDef-2">[2]</a></sup> Cyclogenesis is an umbrella term for several different processes, all of which result in the development of some sort of <a href="/wiki/Cyclone" title="Cyclone">cyclone</a>. Meteorologists use the term "cyclone" where circular pressure systems flow in the direction of the Earth's rotation,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AMSCcDef_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-AMSCcDef-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AMSCycDef_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-AMSCycDef-4">[4]</a></sup> which normally coincides with areas of low pressure.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BBCCycDef_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-BBCCycDef-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-UCARCycDef_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-UCARCycDef-6">[6]</a></sup> The largest low-pressure systems are cold-core polar cyclones and extratropical cyclones which lie on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Synoptic_scale" title="Synoptic scale">synoptic scale</a>. Warm-core cyclones such as tropical cyclones, <a href="/wiki/Mesocyclone" title="Mesocyclone">mesocyclones</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Polar_low" title="Polar low">polar lows</a> lie within the smaller <a href="/wiki/Mesoscale_meteorology" title="Mesoscale meteorology">mesoscale</a>. Subtropical cyclones are of intermediate size.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AnlFcastHelp_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-AnlFcastHelp-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Cyclogenesis can occur at various scales, from the microscale to the synoptic scale. Larger-scale troughs, also called Rossby waves, are synoptic in scale.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Shortwave troughs embedded within the flow around larger scale troughs are smaller in scale, or mesoscale in nature.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Both Rossby waves and shortwaves embedded within the flow around Rossby waves migrate equatorward of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Polar_cyclone" title="Polar cyclone">polar cyclones</a> located in both the Northern and Southern hemispheres.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> All share one important aspect, that of upward vertical motion within the troposphere. Such upward motions decrease the mass of local atmospheric columns of air, which lowers surface pressure.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>Extratropical cyclones form as waves along <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Weather_fronts" title="Weather fronts">weather fronts</a> due to a passing by shortwave aloft or upper level jet streak<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (January 2017)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup> before occluding later in their life cycle as cold-core cyclones.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NorCycMod_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-NorCycMod-16">[16]</a></sup> Polar lows are small-scale, short-lived atmospheric low-pressure systems that occur over the ocean areas poleward of the main <a href="/wiki/Polar_front" title="Polar front">polar front</a> in both the Northern and Southern Hemispheres. They are part of the larger class of <a href="/wiki/Mesoscale_meteorology" title="Mesoscale meteorology">mesoscale</a> weather-systems. Polar lows can be difficult to detect using conventional weather reports and are a hazard to high-latitude operations, such as shipping and gas- and oil-platforms. They are vigorous systems that have near-surface winds of at least 17 metres per second (38 mph).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>

<p><a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone" title="Tropical cyclone">Tropical cyclones</a> form due to latent heat driven by significant thunderstorm activity, and are warm-core with well-defined circulations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AOML_FAQ_A7_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-AOML_FAQ_A7-18">[18]</a></sup> Certain criteria need to be met for their formation. In most situations, <a href="/wiki/Sea_surface_temperature" title="Sea surface temperature">water temperatures</a> of at least 26.5 °C (79.7 °F) are needed down to a depth of at least 50 m (160 ft);<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AOML_FAQ_A15_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-AOML_FAQ_A15-19">[19]</a></sup> waters of this temperature cause the overlying atmosphere to be unstable enough to sustain convection and thunderstorms.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AOML_FAQ_A16_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-AOML_FAQ_A16-20">[20]</a></sup> Another factor is rapid cooling with height, which allows the release of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heat_of_condensation" title="Heat of condensation">heat of condensation</a> that powers a tropical cyclone.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AOML_FAQ_A15_19-1"><a href="#cite_note-AOML_FAQ_A15-19">[19]</a></sup> High humidity is needed, especially in the lower-to-mid <a href="/wiki/Troposphere" title="Troposphere">troposphere</a>; when there is a great deal of moisture in the atmosphere, conditions are more favorable for disturbances to develop.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AOML_FAQ_A15_19-2"><a href="#cite_note-AOML_FAQ_A15-19">[19]</a></sup> Low amounts of <a href="/wiki/Wind_shear" title="Wind shear">wind shear</a> are needed, as high shear is disruptive to the storm's circulation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AOML_FAQ_A15_19-3"><a href="#cite_note-AOML_FAQ_A15-19">[19]</a></sup> Lastly, a formative tropical cyclone needs a pre-existing system of disturbed weather, although without a circulation no cyclonic development will take place.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AOML_FAQ_A15_19-4"><a href="#cite_note-AOML_FAQ_A15-19">[19]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Mesocyclone" title="Mesocyclone">Mesocyclones</a> form as warm core cyclones over land, and can lead to tornado formation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MesocyloneDef_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-MesocyloneDef-21">[21]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Waterspout" title="Waterspout">Waterspouts</a> can also form from mesocyclones, but more often develop from environments of high instability and low vertical <a href="/wiki/Wind_shear" title="Wind shear">wind shear</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Desert" title="Desert">deserts</a>, lack of ground and plant moisture that would normally provide <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Evaporative_cooling" title="Evaporative cooling">evaporative cooling</a> can lead to intense, rapid solar heating of the lower layers of air. The hot air is less dense than surrounding cooler air. This, combined with the rising of the hot air, results in a low-pressure area called a thermal low.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-thermal_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-thermal-23">[23]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Monsoon" title="Monsoon">Monsoon</a> circulations are caused by thermal lows which form over large areas of land and their strength is driven by how land heats more quickly than the surrounding nearby ocean. This generates a steady wind blowing toward the land, bringing the moist near-surface air over the oceans with it.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cause_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-cause-24">[24]</a></sup> Similar <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Precipitation_(meteorology)" title="Precipitation (meteorology)">rainfall</a> is caused by the moist ocean-air being lifted upwards by mountains,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MT_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-MT-25">[25]</a></sup> surface heating,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> convergence at the surface,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> divergence aloft, or from storm-produced outflows at the surface.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> However the lifting occurs, the air cools due to expansion in lower pressure, which in turn produces <a href="/wiki/Condensation" title="Condensation">condensation</a>. In winter, the land cools off quickly, but the ocean keeps the heat longer due to its higher specific heat. The hot air over the ocean rises, creating a low-pressure area and a breeze from land to ocean while a large area of drying high pressure is formed over the land, increased by wintertime cooling.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cause_24-1"><a href="#cite_note-cause-24">[24]</a></sup> Monsoons resemble <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sea_breezes" title="Sea breezes">sea and land breezes</a>, terms usually referring to the localized, diurnal (daily) cycle of circulation near coastlines everywhere, but they are much larger in scale - also stronger and seasonal.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Climatology">Climatology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Low-pressure_area&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Climatology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mid-latitudes_and_subtropics">Mid-latitudes and subtropics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Low-pressure_area&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Mid-latitudes and subtropics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Quikscatcyclone.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="290" data-file-width="450" height="161" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/08/Quikscatcyclone.jpg/250px-Quikscatcyclone.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/08/Quikscatcyclone.jpg/375px-Quikscatcyclone.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/0/08/Quikscatcyclone.jpg 2x" width="250"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Quikscatcyclone.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/QuikSCAT" title="QuikSCAT">QuikSCAT</a> image of typical extratropical cyclones over the ocean. Note the maximum winds are on the poleward side of the <a href="/wiki/Occluded_front" title="Occluded front">occluded front</a>.</div>
</div>
</div>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Arctic_oscillation" title="Arctic oscillation">Arctic oscillation</a>, <a href="/wiki/Extratropical_cyclone" title="Extratropical cyclone">Extratropical cyclone</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Thermal_low" title="Thermal low">Thermal low</a></div>
<p>Large polar cyclones help determine the steering of systems moving through the mid-latitudes, south of the Arctic and north of the <a href="/wiki/Antarctic" title="Antarctic">Antarctic</a>. An index which is used to gauge the magnitude of this effect in the Northern Hemisphere is the <a href="/wiki/Arctic_oscillation" title="Arctic oscillation">Arctic oscillation</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Extratropical_cyclone" title="Extratropical cyclone">Extratropical cyclones</a> tend to form east of climatological trough positions aloft near the east coast of continents, or west side of oceans.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-extra_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-extra-31">[31]</a></sup> A study of extratropical cyclones in the <a href="/wiki/Southern_Hemisphere" title="Southern Hemisphere">Southern Hemisphere</a> shows that between the <a href="/wiki/30th_parallel_south" title="30th parallel south">30th</a> and <a href="/wiki/70th_parallel_south" title="70th parallel south">70th parallels</a>, there are an average of 37 cyclones in existence during any 6-hour period.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-VarSouthCyc_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-VarSouthCyc-32">[32]</a></sup> A separate study in the <a href="/wiki/Northern_Hemisphere" title="Northern Hemisphere">Northern Hemisphere</a> suggests that approximately 234 significant extratropical cyclones form each winter.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WinterStormsNorth_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-WinterStormsNorth-33">[33]</a></sup> In Europe, particularly the United Kingdom, recurring extratropical low-pressure weather systems are typically known as depressions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> These tend to bring wet weather throughout the year. <i>Thermal lows</i> also occur over continental areas across the subtropics during the summer such as the <a href="/wiki/Sonoran_Desert" title="Sonoran Desert">Sonoran Desert</a>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mexican_plateau" title="Mexican plateau">Mexican plateau</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sahara" title="Sahara">Sahara</a>, <a href="/wiki/South_America" title="South America">South America</a>, and Southeast Asia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-thermal_23-1"><a href="#cite_note-thermal-23">[23]</a></sup> The lows are most commonly located over the Tibetan plateau and in the lee of the Rocky mountains.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-extra_31-1"><a href="#cite_note-extra-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Monsoon_trough">Monsoon trough</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Low-pressure_area&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Monsoon trough">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Februarystreamlinesnavy.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="473" data-file-width="900" height="131" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/54/Februarystreamlinesnavy.jpg/250px-Februarystreamlinesnavy.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/54/Februarystreamlinesnavy.jpg/375px-Februarystreamlinesnavy.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/54/Februarystreamlinesnavy.jpg/500px-Februarystreamlinesnavy.jpg 2x" width="250"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Februarystreamlinesnavy.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
February position of the ITCZ and monsoon trough in the Pacific Ocean, depicted by area of convergent streamlines offshore Australia and in the equatorial eastern Pacific</div>
</div>
</div>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Monsoon_trough" title="Monsoon trough">Monsoon trough</a></div>
<p>Elongated areas of low pressure form at the <a href="/wiki/Monsoon_trough" title="Monsoon trough">monsoon trough</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Intertropical_convergence_zone" title="Intertropical convergence zone">intertropical convergence zone</a> as part of the <a href="/wiki/Hadley_cell" title="Hadley cell">Hadley cell</a> circulation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> Monsoon troughing in the western Pacific reaches its zenith in latitude during the late summer when the wintertime surface ridge in the opposite hemisphere is the strongest. It can reach as far as the <a href="/wiki/40th_parallel_north" title="40th parallel north">40th parallel</a> in East Asia during August and <a href="/wiki/20th_parallel_south" title="20th parallel south">20th parallel</a> in Australia during February. Its poleward progression is accelerated by the onset of the summer monsoon which is characterized by the development of lower air pressure over the warmest part of the various continents.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NCFMRF_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-NCFMRF-36">[36]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> The large-scale thermal lows over <a href="/wiki/Continent" title="Continent">continents</a> help create pressure gradients which drive <a href="/wiki/Monsoon" title="Monsoon">monsoon</a> circulations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> In the southern hemisphere, the monsoon trough associated with the Australian monsoon reaches its most southerly latitude in February,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NAVY2_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-NAVY2-39">[39]</a></sup> oriented along a west-northwest/east-southeast axis. Many of the world's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rainforests" title="Rainforests">rainforests</a> are associated with these climatological low-pressure systems.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Tropical_cyclone">Tropical cyclone</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Low-pressure_area&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Tropical cyclone">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Winston_2016-02-12_1200Z.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="600" data-file-width="600" height="220" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e5/Winston_2016-02-12_1200Z.png/220px-Winston_2016-02-12_1200Z.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e5/Winston_2016-02-12_1200Z.png/330px-Winston_2016-02-12_1200Z.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e5/Winston_2016-02-12_1200Z.png/440px-Winston_2016-02-12_1200Z.png 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Winston_2016-02-12_1200Z.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Infrared image of a powerful southern hemisphere cyclone, <a href="/wiki/Cyclone_Winston" title="Cyclone Winston">Winston</a>, near its initial peak intensity</div>
</div>
</div>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone" title="Tropical cyclone">Tropical cyclone</a></div>
<p>Tropical cyclones generally need to form more than 555 km (345 mi) or poleward of the <a href="/wiki/5th_parallel_north" title="5th parallel north">5th parallel north</a> and <a href="/wiki/5th_parallel_south" title="5th parallel south">5th parallel south</a>, allowing the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coriolis_effect" title="Coriolis effect">Coriolis effect</a> to deflect winds blowing towards the low-pressure center and creating a circulation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AOML_FAQ_A15_19-5"><a href="#cite_note-AOML_FAQ_A15-19">[19]</a></sup> Worldwide, tropical cyclone activity peaks in late summer, when the difference between temperatures aloft and sea surface temperatures is the greatest. However, each particular basin has its own seasonal patterns. On a worldwide scale, May is the least active month while September is the most active month. November is the only month that activity in all the tropical cyclone basins is possible.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AOML_FAQ_G1_41-0"><a href="#cite_note-AOML_FAQ_G1-41">[41]</a></sup> Nearly one-third of the world's tropical cyclones form within the western Pacific Ocean, making it the most active <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone_basins" title="Tropical cyclone basins">tropical cyclone basin</a> on <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-enso_42-0"><a href="#cite_note-enso-42">[42]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Associated_weather">Associated weather</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Low-pressure_area&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Associated weather">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Coriolis_effect10.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="611" data-file-width="642" height="209" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fe/Coriolis_effect10.svg/220px-Coriolis_effect10.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fe/Coriolis_effect10.svg/330px-Coriolis_effect10.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fe/Coriolis_effect10.svg/440px-Coriolis_effect10.svg.png 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Coriolis_effect10.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Schematic representation of flow around a low-pressure area in the Northern hemisphere. The pressure-gradient force is represented by blue arrows, the Coriolis acceleration (always perpendicular to the velocity) by red arrows</div>
</div>
</div>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Geostrophic_wind" title="Geostrophic wind">Geostrophic wind</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Precipitation_(meteorology)" title="Precipitation (meteorology)">Precipitation (meteorology)</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Wind" title="Wind">Wind</a> is initially accelerated from areas of high pressure to areas of low pressure.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> This is due to <a href="/wiki/Density" title="Density">density</a> (or temperature and moisture) differences between two <a href="/wiki/Air_mass" title="Air mass">air masses</a>. Since stronger high-pressure systems contain cooler or drier air, the air mass is denser and flows towards areas that are warm or moist, which are in the vicinity of low-pressure areas in advance of their associated <a href="/wiki/Cold_front" title="Cold front">cold fronts</a>. The stronger the pressure difference, or <a href="/wiki/Pressure_gradient" title="Pressure gradient">pressure gradient</a>, between a high-pressure system and a low-pressure system, the stronger the wind.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jet_44-0"><a href="#cite_note-jet-44">[44]</a></sup> Thus, stronger areas of low pressure are associated with stronger winds.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Coriolis_force" title="Coriolis force">Coriolis force</a> caused by the <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a>'s rotation is what gives winds around low-pressure areas (such as in <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_hurricane" title="Atlantic hurricane">hurricanes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cyclone" title="Cyclone">cyclones</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Typhoon" title="Typhoon">typhoons</a>) their counter-clockwise (anticlockwise) circulation in the northern hemisphere (as the wind moves inward and is deflected right from the center of high pressure) and clockwise circulation in the southern hemisphere (as the wind moves inward and is deflected left from the center of high pressure).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup> A cyclone differs from a hurricane or typhoon only on the basis of location.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HCT_46-0"><a href="#cite_note-HCT-46">[46]</a></sup> A <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_hurricane" title="Atlantic hurricane">hurricane</a> is a <a href="/wiki/Storm" title="Storm">storm</a> that occurs in the <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Ocean" title="Atlantic Ocean">Atlantic Ocean</a> and northeastern <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Ocean" title="Pacific Ocean">Pacific Ocean</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Typhoon" title="Typhoon">typhoon</a> occurs in the northwestern Pacific Ocean, and a <a href="/wiki/Cyclone" title="Cyclone">cyclone</a> occurs in the south Pacific or <a href="/wiki/Indian_Ocean" title="Indian Ocean">Indian Ocean</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HCT_46-1"><a href="#cite_note-HCT-46">[46]</a></sup> Friction with land slows down the wind flowing into low-pressure systems and causes wind to flow more inward, or flowing more <a href="/wiki/Geostrophic_wind" title="Geostrophic wind">ageostrophically</a>, toward their centers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jet_44-1"><a href="#cite_note-jet-44">[44]</a></sup> A low-pressure area is commonly associated with inclement weather,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup> while a <a href="/wiki/High-pressure_area" title="High-pressure area">high-pressure area</a> is associated with light winds and fair skies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Tornado" title="Tornado">Tornados</a> are often too small, and of too short duration, to be influenced by the Coriolis force, but may be so-influenced when arising from a low-pressure system.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup></p>
