<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Tomb of the Unknowns</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">
					Jump to:					<a href="#mw-head">navigation</a>, 					<a href="#p-search">search</a>
</div>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><p>The <b>Tomb of the Unknowns</b> is a monument dedicated to U.S. service members who have died without their remains being identified. It is also known as the <b><a href="/wiki/Tomb_of_the_Unknown_Soldier" title="Tomb of the Unknown Soldier">Tomb of the Unknown Soldier</a></b>, but it has never been officially named. It is located in <a href="/wiki/Arlington_National_Cemetery" title="Arlington National Cemetery">Arlington National Cemetery</a> in <a href="/wiki/Virginia" title="Virginia">Virginia</a>, United States of America. The World War I "Unknown" is a recipient of the <a href="/wiki/Medal_of_Honor" title="Medal of Honor">Medal of Honor</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Victoria_Cross" title="Victoria Cross">Victoria Cross</a>, and several other foreign nations' highest service awards. The U.S. Unknowns who were interred are also recipients of the Medal of Honor, presented by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._Presidents" title="U.S. Presidents">U.S. Presidents</a> who presided over their funerals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Tomb_of_1921">Tomb of 1921</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tomb_of_the_Unknowns&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Tomb of 1921">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:TombOfTheUnknownSoldier11-11-1922.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="413" data-file-width="1000" height="91" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ec/TombOfTheUnknownSoldier11-11-1922.jpg/220px-TombOfTheUnknownSoldier11-11-1922.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ec/TombOfTheUnknownSoldier11-11-1922.jpg/330px-TombOfTheUnknownSoldier11-11-1922.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ec/TombOfTheUnknownSoldier11-11-1922.jpg/440px-TombOfTheUnknownSoldier11-11-1922.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:TombOfTheUnknownSoldier11-11-1922.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Tomb as of November 11, 1922. The Tomb of 1931 would occupy this same location.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>On March 4, 1921, the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Congress" title="United States Congress">United States Congress</a> approved the burial of an unidentified American serviceman from World War I in the plaza of the new <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Memorial_Amphitheater" title="Memorial Amphitheater">Memorial Amphitheater</a>. On November 11, 1921, the unknown soldier brought back from France was interred below a three-level marble tomb. The bottom two levels are six marble sections each and the top at least nine blocks with a rectangular opening in the center of each level through which the unknown remains were placed through the tomb and into the ground below. A stone, rather than marble, slab covers the rectangular opening.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Quartermaster1963_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Quartermaster1963-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-World_War_I_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-World_War_I-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Tomb_of_1931">Tomb of 1931</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tomb_of_the_Unknowns&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Tomb of 1931">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Since 1921 the intent was to place a superstructure on top of the Tomb, but it was not until July 3, 1926, that Congress authorized the completion of the Tomb and the expenditure of $50,000 (with a completed cost of $48,000). A design competition was held and won by architect <a href="/wiki/Lorimer_Rich" title="Lorimer Rich">Lorimer Rich</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[nb 1]</a></sup> and sculptor <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Hudson_Jones" title="Thomas Hudson Jones">Thomas Hudson Jones</a>. An appropriation from Congress for the work was secured and on December 21, 1929, a contract for completion of the Tomb itself was entered into. The Tomb would consist of seven pieces of marble in four levels (cap, die, base and sub-base) of which the die is the largest block with the sculpting on all four sides.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Quartermaster1963_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-Quartermaster1963-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TGHistory_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-TGHistory-8">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>Quarrying the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Yule_marble" title="Yule marble">Yule marble</a> (3.9 miles south of <a href="/wiki/Marble,_Colorado" title="Marble, Colorado">Marble, Colorado</a> by the Vermont Marble Company) was a one-year process beginning in 1930. The cap was quarried on the first attempt but the base required three tries. The large middle block also required three tries. In late January 1931, the 56 ton middle block was lifted out of the quarry. The quarrying involved 75 men. When the block was separated from the mountain inside the quarry it weighed 124 tons. A wire saw was then brought into the quarry to cut the block down to 56 tons.</p>
<p>On February 3, the block reached the marble mill site (in the town of Marble) after a four-day trip from the quarry. Here it was crated, then shipped to Vermont on February 8. The block was sawn to final size in West Rutland, Vermont and fabricated by craftsmen in <a href="/wiki/Proctor,_Vermont" title="Proctor, Vermont">Proctor, Vermont</a> before it was shipped by train to Arlington National Cemetery, Virginia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CityStone_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-CityStone-9">[8]</a></sup> By September, all 7 blocks were on the grounds of the Tomb site, at Arlington.</p>
<p>Assembly began in September 1931. An imperfection was found in the base requiring three more quarryings. By the end of December 1931, the assembly was completed. Finishing work followed with the carvings on the die block by the <a href="/wiki/Piccirilli_Brothers" title="Piccirilli Brothers">Piccirilli Brothers</a> under the direction of the sculptor Thomas Jones.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Quartermaster1963_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-Quartermaster1963-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MemoryStone_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-MemoryStone-10">[9]</a></sup> (The brothers also carved the Lincoln statue for the Lincoln Memorial). The Tomb was completed without formal ceremony on April 9, 1932.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Tomb<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[nb 2]</a></sup> was placed at the head of the grave of the World War I Unknown. West of this grave are the crypts of Unknowns from World War II (south) and <a href="/wiki/Korean_War" title="Korean War">Korea</a> (north). Between the two lies a crypt that once contained an Unknown from <a href="/wiki/Vietnam_War" title="Vietnam War">Vietnam</a> (middle). His remains were positively identified in 1998 through DNA testing as First Lieutenant <a href="/wiki/Michael_Blassie" title="Michael Blassie">Michael Blassie</a>, United States Air Force and were removed. Those three graves are marked with white marble slabs flush with the plaza.</p>
<p>The Tomb has a flat-faced form and is relieved at the corners and along the sides by neo-classical <a href="/wiki/Pilaster" title="Pilaster">pilasters</a> set into the surface with objects and inscription carved into the sides. The 1931 symbolism<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MemoryStone_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-MemoryStone-10">[9]</a></sup> of the objects on the north, south and east sides changed over time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Quartermaster1963_4-3"><a href="#cite_note-Quartermaster1963-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TGHistory_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-TGHistory-8">[7]</a></sup></p>
<dl>
<dd><b>North and South panel</b> with 3 wreaths on each side represent (in 1931) "a world of memories" but later the six major battles engaged in by American forces in France; Ardennes, Belleau Wood, Château-Thierry, Meusse-Argonne, Oisiu-Eiseu, and Somme. Each wreath has 38 leaves and 12 berries.</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dd><b>East panel</b> that faces Washington, D.C., are three Greek figures representing Peace, Victory, and "American Manhood" but later "Valor" instead of "American Manhood"</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dd><b>Western panel</b> is inscribed the words (centered on the panel):</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dd>HERE RESTS IN</dd>
<dd>HONORED GLORY</dd>
<dd>AN AMERICAN</dd>
<dd>SOLDIER</dd>
<dd>KNOWN BUT TO GOD</dd>
</dl>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Unknown_of_World_War_I">The Unknown of World War I</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tomb_of_the_Unknowns&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: The Unknown of World War I">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Unknown_Soldier_at_the_Washington_Navy_Yard.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="798" data-file-width="867" height="202" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/79/Unknown_Soldier_at_the_Washington_Navy_Yard.jpg/220px-Unknown_Soldier_at_the_Washington_Navy_Yard.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/79/Unknown_Soldier_at_the_Washington_Navy_Yard.jpg/330px-Unknown_Soldier_at_the_Washington_Navy_Yard.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/79/Unknown_Soldier_at_the_Washington_Navy_Yard.jpg/440px-Unknown_Soldier_at_the_Washington_Navy_Yard.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Unknown_Soldier_at_the_Washington_Navy_Yard.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The World War I Unknown arriving at the <a href="/wiki/Washington_Navy_Yard" title="Washington Navy Yard">Washington Navy Yard</a>, 1921 (<a href="/wiki/Hand-colouring_of_photographs" title="Hand-colouring of photographs">colorized</a>)</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>On <a href="/wiki/Memorial_Day" title="Memorial Day">Memorial Day</a>, 1921, four unknown servicemen were exhumed from four World War I American cemeteries in France. <a href="/wiki/United_States_Army" title="United States Army">U.S. Army</a> Sgt. <a href="/wiki/Edward_F._Younger" title="Edward F. Younger">Edward F. Younger</a>, who was wounded in combat, highly decorated for valor and received the <a href="/wiki/Distinguished_Service_Cross_(United_States)" title="Distinguished Service Cross (United States)">Distinguished Service Cross</a> in "The Great War" selected the Unknown of World War I from four identical caskets at the city hall in <a href="/wiki/Ch%C3%A2lons-en-Champagne" title="Châlons-en-Champagne">Châlons-en-Champagne</a>, France, on October 24, 1921.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-World_War_I_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-World_War_I-6">[6]</a></sup> Younger selected the World War I Unknown by placing a spray of white roses on one of the caskets. He chose the third casket from the left. The chosen Unknown was transported to the United States aboard the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/USS_Olympia_(CL-15)" title="USS Olympia (CL-15)">USS <i>Olympia</i></a>. Those remaining were interred in the <a href="/wiki/Meuse-Argonne_American_Cemetery_and_Memorial" title="Meuse-Argonne American Cemetery and Memorial">Meuse Argonne Cemetery</a>, France.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-World_War_I_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-World_War_I-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>The World War I Unknown <a href="/wiki/Lying_in_state#United_States" title="Lying in state">lay in state</a> in the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Capitol_rotunda" title="United States Capitol rotunda">Capitol Rotunda</a> from his arrival in the United States until <a href="/wiki/Armistice_Day" title="Armistice Day">Armistice Day</a>, 1921. On November 11, 1921, President <a href="/wiki/Warren_G._Harding" title="Warren G. Harding">Warren G. Harding</a> officiated at the interment ceremonies at the Memorial Amphitheater at Arlington National Cemetery. During the ceremony, the World War I Unknown was awarded the <a href="/wiki/Victoria_Cross" title="Victoria Cross">Victoria Cross</a> by <a href="/wiki/Admiral_of_the_Fleet_(Royal_Navy)" title="Admiral of the Fleet (Royal Navy)">Admiral of the Fleet</a> <a href="/wiki/David_Beatty,_1st_Earl_Beatty" title="David Beatty, 1st Earl Beatty">Lord Beatty</a>, on behalf of King <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/George_V_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="George V of the United Kingdom">George V of the United Kingdom</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[12]</a></sup> (The United Kingdom Victoria Cross was placed with the soldier. Earlier, on March 4, 1921, the British Unknown Warrior was conferred the U.S. Medal of Honor by <a href="/wiki/General_of_the_Armies" title="General of the Armies">General of the Armies</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/John_Pershing" title="John Pershing">John Pershing</a>.) In 1928, the Unknown Soldier was presented the <a href="/wiki/Silver_Buffalo_Award" title="Silver Buffalo Award">Silver Buffalo Award</a> for distinguished service to America's youth by the <a href="/wiki/Boy_Scouts_of_America" title="Boy Scouts of America">Boy Scouts of America</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[13]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Unknowns_of_World_War_II_and_Korea">The Unknowns of World War II and Korea</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tomb_of_the_Unknowns&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: The Unknowns of World War II and Korea">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:UnknownKorea.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="592" data-file-width="740" height="176" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fe/UnknownKorea.jpg/220px-UnknownKorea.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fe/UnknownKorea.jpg/330px-UnknownKorea.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fe/UnknownKorea.jpg/440px-UnknownKorea.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:UnknownKorea.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The Korean War Unknown joins the two candidates from World War II aboard the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/USS_Canberra_(CA-70)" title="USS Canberra (CA-70)">USS <i>Canberra</i></a></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>On August 3, 1956, President <a href="/wiki/Dwight_D._Eisenhower" title="Dwight D. Eisenhower">Dwight D. Eisenhower</a> signed a bill to select and pay the tribute to the Unknowns of World War II and the Korean War. The selection ceremonies and the interment of these Unknowns took place in 1958. The World War II Unknown was selected from remains exhumed from cemeteries in Europe, Africa, <a href="/wiki/Hawaii" title="Hawaii">Hawaii</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Philippines" title="Philippines">Philippines</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-QuarterMaster1964_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-QuarterMaster1964-17">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>Two Unknowns from World War II, one from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/European_Theater_of_Operations" title="European Theater of Operations">European Theater</a> and one from the <a href="/wiki/Asiatic-Pacific_Theater" title="Asiatic-Pacific Theater">Pacific Theater</a>, were placed in identical caskets and taken aboard the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/USS_Canberra_(CA-70)" title="USS Canberra (CA-70)">USS <i>Canberra</i></a>, a guided-missile cruiser resting off the Virginia Capes. Navy <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hospital_Corpsman" title="Hospital Corpsman">Hospital Corpsman</a> 1st Class <a href="/wiki/William_R._Charette" title="William R. Charette">William R. Charette</a>, then the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._Navy" title="U.S. Navy">U.S. Navy</a>'s only active-duty Medal of Honor recipient who was an enlisted man, selected the World War II Unknown. The remaining casket received a solemn <a href="/wiki/Burial_at_sea" title="Burial at sea">burial at sea</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-QuarterMaster1964_17-1"><a href="#cite_note-QuarterMaster1964-17">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>Four unknown Americans who died in the Korean War were disinterred from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/National_Cemetery_of_the_Pacific" title="National Cemetery of the Pacific">National Cemetery of the Pacific</a> in Hawaii. Army <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Master_Sergeant#United_States" title="Master Sergeant">Master Sergeant</a> Ned Lyle made the final selection.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-QuarterMaster1964_17-2"><a href="#cite_note-QuarterMaster1964-17">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>Both caskets arrived in Washington on May 28, 1958, where they lay in the Capitol Rotunda until the morning of May 30, when they were carried on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Caisson_(military)" title="Caisson (military)">caissons</a> to Arlington National Cemetery. President Eisenhower awarded each the Medal of Honor, and the Unknowns of World War II and the Korean War were interred in the plaza beside their World War I comrade.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-QuarterMaster1964_17-3"><a href="#cite_note-QuarterMaster1964-17">[14]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Unknown_of_Vietnam">The Unknown of Vietnam</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tomb_of_the_Unknowns&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: The Unknown of Vietnam">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The designation of the Vietnam Unknown has proven to be difficult. With improvements in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/DNA_testing" title="DNA testing">DNA testing</a> it is possible, though unlikely, that the recovered remains for every unknown soldier killed in the Vietnam War will be identified.</p>
<p>The Vietnam Unknown service member was designated by Medal of Honor recipient <a href="/wiki/United_States_Marine_Corps" title="United States Marine Corps">U.S. Marine Corps</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sergeant_Major" title="Sergeant Major">Sgt. Maj.</a> <a href="/wiki/Allan_J._Kellogg" title="Allan J. Kellogg">Allan Jay Kellogg, Jr.</a>, during a ceremony at <a href="/wiki/Pearl_Harbor" title="Pearl Harbor">Pearl Harbor</a>, Hawaii, May 17, 1984.</p>
<p>Each branch of the Armed Services took part in the transportation to Honor the unknown. The Marines from Marine Barracks Hawaii consisted of an Honor Guard of 9 enlisted men and Lt. Denis Muller. The Vietnam Unknown was transported aboard the fast frigate <a href="/wiki/USS_Brewton_(FF-1086)" title="USS Brewton (FF-1086)">USS <i>Brewton</i></a>, where the Marines stood guard over the casket during the seven day ocean voyage to <a href="/wiki/Naval_Air_Station_Alameda" title="Naval Air Station Alameda">Naval Air Station Alameda</a>, California where the debarkation ceremony turned the unknown over to the USAF on May 24. The next day, the remains of the Vietnam Unknown were then flown from <a href="/wiki/Travis_Air_Force_Base" title="Travis Air Force Base">Travis Air Force Base</a>, California, arriving at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Andrews_Air_Force_Base" title="Andrews Air Force Base">Andrews Air Force Base</a>, Maryland. Once there the remains were turned over to the US Army, where the remains were taken to Fort McNair for placement upon the horse-drawn wagon which later carried the Unknown to the Capital Rotunda for display before interment. While on display for public viewing, all branches of the US Armed Forces stood in Honor, Guarding the casket of the Unknown for 2 weeks.</p>

<p>Many Vietnam veterans and President <a href="/wiki/Ronald_Reagan" title="Ronald Reagan">Ronald Reagan</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nancy_Reagan" title="Nancy Reagan">Nancy Reagan</a> visited the Vietnam Unknown in the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Capitol" title="United States Capitol">U.S. Capitol</a>. An Army caisson carried the Vietnam Unknown from the Capitol to the Memorial Amphitheater at Arlington National Cemetery on Memorial Day, May 28, 1984.</p>
<p>President Reagan presided over the funeral, and presented the Medal of Honor to the Vietnam Unknown, and also acted as next of kin by accepting the interment flag at the end of the ceremony. The interment flags of all Unknowns at the Tomb of the Unknowns are on view in the Memorial Display Room.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Identification_of_the_Unknown">Identification of the Unknown</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tomb_of_the_Unknowns&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Identification of the Unknown">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1994, Ted Sampley, a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/POW/MIA" title="POW/MIA">POW/MIA</a> activist, determined that the remains of the Vietnam Unknown were likely those of Air Force 1st Lt. <a href="/wiki/Michael_Blassie" title="Michael Blassie">Michael Joseph Blassie</a>, who was shot down near <a href="/wiki/An_L%E1%BB%99c" title="An Lộc">An Lộc</a>, Vietnam, in 1972. Sampley published an article in his newsletter and contacted Blassie's family, who attempted to pursue the case with the Air Force's casualty office without result. In January 1998 CBS News broadcast a report based on Sampley's investigation which brought political pressure to support the identification of the remains.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[15]</a></sup> The body was exhumed on May 14, 1998. Based on <a href="/wiki/Mitochondrial_DNA" title="Mitochondrial DNA">mitochondrial DNA</a> testing, Department of Defense scientists confirmed the remains were those of Blassie. The identification was announced on June 30, 1998, and on July 10, Blassie's remains arrived home to his family in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/St._Louis,_Missouri" title="St. Louis, Missouri">St. Louis, Missouri</a>; he was reinterred at <a href="/wiki/Jefferson_Barracks_National_Cemetery" title="Jefferson Barracks National Cemetery">Jefferson Barracks National Cemetery</a> on July 11.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[nb 4]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Redesignation_of_the_crypt">Redesignation of the crypt</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tomb_of_the_Unknowns&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Redesignation of the crypt">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The slab over the crypt that once held the remains of the Vietnam Unknown has since been replaced. The original inscription of "Vietnam" and the dates of the conflict has been changed to "Honoring and Keeping Faith with America's Missing Servicemen" as a reminder of the commitment of the Armed Forces to the fullest possible accounting of missing service members.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Tomb_guards">Tomb guards</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tomb_of_the_Unknowns&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Tomb guards">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The tomb guards are soldiers of the United States Army. The first military guards were troopers from the 3rd Cavalry, "Brave Rifles", who were posted nearby on <a href="/wiki/Fort_Myer" title="Fort Myer">Fort Myer</a>. Since April 6, 1948, (known then as "Army Day"), when the regiment was reactivated, it has been guarded by soldiers from 3rd Infantry Regiment, "The Old Guard". The Old Guard is also posted to <a href="/wiki/Fort_Myer" title="Fort Myer">Fort Myer</a>, Virginia, adjacent to Arlington National Cemetery. It is considered one of the highest honors to serve as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/3rd_US_Infantry_Regiment_(The_Old_Guard)" title="3rd US Infantry Regiment (The Old Guard)">Sentinel</a> at the Tomb of the Unknowns. Fewer than 20 percent of all volunteers are accepted for training and of those only a fraction pass training to become full-fledged Tomb Guards. This attrition rate has made the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tomb_of_the_Unknown_Soldier_Guard_Identification_Badge" title="Tomb of the Unknown Soldier Guard Identification Badge">Tomb of the Unknown Soldier Guard Identification Badge</a> the second least-awarded qualification badge of the United States military (the first being the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Astronaut_Badge" title="Astronaut Badge">Astronaut Badge</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>The soldier "walking the mat" does not wear rank insignia, so as not to outrank the Unknowns, whatever their ranks may have been. Non-commissioned officers (usually the Relief Commander and Assistant Relief Commanders), do wear insignia of their rank when changing the guard only. They have a separate uniform (without rank) that is worn when they actually guard the Unknowns or are "posted."</p>
<p>The duties of the sentinels are not purely ceremonial. The sentinels will confront people who cross the barriers at the tomb or who they perceive to be disrespectful or excessively loud.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Weapons">Weapons</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tomb_of_the_Unknowns&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Weapons">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Over the years there have been several different types of weapons used by the Tomb Guards. The changes in weapons reflect the changes in the Army, including <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/M1903_Springfield_rifle" title="M1903 Springfield rifle">M1903 Springfield rifle</a>, <a href="/wiki/M1_Garand" title="M1 Garand">M1 Garand</a> and <a href="/wiki/M14_rifle" title="M14 rifle">M14</a> rifles, <a href="/wiki/M1911_pistol" title="M1911 pistol">M1911</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/M9_pistol" title="M9 pistol">M9</a> pistols. Tomb Guards currently carry M14 rifles, which are unloaded and affixed to ceremonial rifle stocks. These rifles are cleaned daily and kept ready for use at all times.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[18]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Walking_the_mat">Walking the mat</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tomb_of_the_Unknowns&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Walking the mat">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>There is a meticulous routine that the guard follows when watching over the graves.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[19]</a></sup> The Tomb Guard:</p>
<ol>
<li>Marches 21 steps south down the black mat laid across the Tomb.</li>
<li>Turns and faces east, toward the Tomb, for 21 seconds.</li>
<li>Turns and faces north, changes weapon to outside shoulder, and waits 21 seconds.</li>
<li>Marches 21 steps down the mat.</li>
<li>Turns and faces east for 21 seconds.</li>
<li>Turns and faces south, changes weapon to outside shoulder, and waits 21 seconds.</li>
<li>Repeats the routine until the soldier is relieved of duty at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Guard_Mounting" title="Guard Mounting">Changing of the Guard</a>.</li>
</ol>
<p>After each turn, the Guard executes a sharp "shoulder-arms" movement to place the weapon on the shoulder closest to the visitors to signify that the Guard stands between the Tomb and any possible threat.</p>
<p>Out of respect for the interred, the sentinels command silence at the tombs. If the guard walking the mat must vocally confront a disturbance from spectators, or a threat, the routine is interrupted, and remains so until the disturbance is under control. The sentinel will exit the mat, place the weapon in port arms position, and confront the disturbance. Once under control, the sentinel then walks on the pavement to the other side of the mat, turns to shoulder arms, and continues the routine.</p>
<p>Twenty-one was chosen because it symbolizes the highest military honor that can be bestowed—the <a href="/wiki/21-gun_salute" title="21-gun salute">21-gun salute</a>.</p>
<p>The mat is usually replaced twice per year: before <a href="/wiki/Memorial_Day" title="Memorial Day">Memorial Day</a> and before <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Veterans_Day_(United_States)" title="Veterans Day (United States)">Veterans Day</a>. This is required because of the wear on the rubber mat by the special shoes worn by Tomb Guards. The sentinels have metal plates built into the soles and inner parts of their shoes to allow for a more rugged sole and to give the signature click of the heel during maneuvers. The sentinels wear sunglasses because of the bright reflection from the marble surrounding the Tomb and the Memorial Amphitheater.</p>
<p>On the ground not covered by the mat, a rust pattern in the tile can be seen that corresponds to the precise steps taken during the changing of the guard. The metal from the guards' boots causes the brown rust markings on the stone. On the mat itself, footprints caused by standing guard are also visible.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Changing_of_the_Guard">Changing of the Guard</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tomb_of_the_Unknowns&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Changing of the Guard">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Tomb_of_the_Unknown_Soldiers.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2388" data-file-width="3712" height="142" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/32/Tomb_of_the_Unknown_Soldiers.jpg/220px-Tomb_of_the_Unknown_Soldiers.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/32/Tomb_of_the_Unknown_Soldiers.jpg/330px-Tomb_of_the_Unknown_Soldiers.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/32/Tomb_of_the_Unknown_Soldiers.jpg/440px-Tomb_of_the_Unknown_Soldiers.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Tomb_of_the_Unknown_Soldiers.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Changing of the Guard, 2005.</div>

<p>While Arlington National Cemetery is open, during the day in summer months from April 1 to September 30, the guard is changed every half hour. During the winter months, from October 1 to March 31, the guard is changed every hour. After the cemetery closes to the public (7 p.m. to 8 a.m. April through September, and 5 p.m. to 8 a.m. October through March), the guard is changed every 2 hours. The ceremony can be witnessed by the public whenever <a href="/wiki/Arlington_National_Cemetery" title="Arlington National Cemetery">Arlington National Cemetery</a> is open.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FAQ_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-FAQ-24">[20]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p>The guard change is very symbolic, but also conducted in accordance with Army regulations. The relief commander or assistant relief commander, along with the oncoming guard, are both required for a guard change to take place. The relief commander orders the guard being relieved to "pass on your orders" to the oncoming guard. The guard being relieved will say to the oncoming guard, "Post and orders remain as directed." The oncoming guard's response is always, "Orders acknowledged." During changes when the public is witnessing the ceremony, the commander will inform the public that the ceremony is about to take place and that those in attendance should remain "silent and standing" throughout the entire event.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Dedication">Dedication</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tomb_of_the_Unknowns&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Dedication">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A civilian guard was first posted at the Tomb on November 17, 1925, to prevent, among other things, families from picnicking on the flat marble slab with views of the city. A military guard was first posted on March 25, 1926. The first 24-hour guard was posted on midnight, July 2, 1937. The Tomb of the Unknowns has been guarded continuously, 24 hours a day, 7 days a week, since that time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[22]</a></sup> Inclement weather,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[23]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[24]</a></sup> terrorist attacks,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[25]</a></sup> et cetera, do not cause the watch to cease.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[26]</a></sup></p>
<p>Since 1948, the Tomb Guards, a special platoon within the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/3rd_United_States_Infantry_Regiment" title="3rd United States Infantry Regiment">3rd U.S. Infantry Regiment</a> (The Old Guard) work on a team rotation of 24 hours on, 24 hours off, for five days, taking the following four days off. A guard takes an average of six hours to prepare his uniform – heavy wool, regardless of the time of year – for the next day's work. In addition to preparing the uniform, guards also conduct physical training, Tomb Guard training, participate in field exercises, cut their hair before the next work day, and at times are involved in regimental functions as well. Tomb Guards are required to memorize 17 pages of information about Arlington National Cemetery and the Tomb of the Unknown Soldier, including the locations of nearly 300 graves and who is buried in each one.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FAQ_24-1"><a href="#cite_note-FAQ-24">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>A special Army decoration, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tomb_of_the_Unknown_Soldier_Guard_Identification_Badge" title="Tomb of the Unknown Soldier Guard Identification Badge">Tomb of the Unknown Soldier Guard Identification Badge</a> is authorized for wear after passing a detailed test of 100 questions (from a pool of more than 300), a uniform test with two gigs (errors) or fewer (measured to the 1/64"), and a test on the guard changing sequence. After serving honorably for a period of nine months, and having passed the sequence of tests, a Tomb Guard is permanently awarded the Badge. Since the first award on February 7, 1958, fewer than 650 soldiers have completed training and been awarded this Badge, including four women. A small number of Tomb Guard Identification Badges have also been retroactively awarded to soldiers who served as Guards before 1959. Those numbers make the Badge the second rarest award currently issued in the United States Army; only the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Astronaut_Badge#United_States_Army" title="Astronaut Badge">Army Astronaut Badge</a> is rarer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[nb 5]</a></sup></p>
<p>The badge was designed in 1956 and first issued to members of the Honor Guard at the Tomb of the Unknowns on February 7, 1958. The badge was first issued only as a temporary wear item, meaning the soldiers could only wear the badge during their tenure as members of the Honor Guard. Upon leaving the duty, the badge was returned and reissued to incoming soldiers. In 1963, a regulation was enacted that allowed the badge to be worn as a permanent part of the military uniform, even after the soldier's completion of duty at the Tomb of the Unknowns.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Damage_and_repair_to_the_Tomb_Monument">Damage and repair to the Tomb Monument</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tomb_of_the_Unknowns&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Damage and repair to the Tomb Monument">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Tomb_of_the_Unknowns_crack.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2128" data-file-width="2832" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/05/Tomb_of_the_Unknowns_crack.jpg/220px-Tomb_of_the_Unknowns_crack.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/05/Tomb_of_the_Unknowns_crack.jpg/330px-Tomb_of_the_Unknowns_crack.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/05/Tomb_of_the_Unknowns_crack.jpg/440px-Tomb_of_the_Unknowns_crack.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Tomb_of_the_Unknowns_crack.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The crack can be seen underneath the words "An American" and above the word "Soldier."</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Cracking and weathering are causing concerns for the long-term preservation of the Tomb Monument. A November 1963 report first recorded horizontal cracking of the monument's marble die block. Though this was the first time that the damage was documented, the report made it clear that the cracks had become visible some time before that date.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Damage_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-Damage-32">[27]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1963–1964, there were two cracks—referred to as "primary" and "secondary"—extending approximately 34 feet around the die block. By 1974, they had extended to 40 feet. They grew another 4.6 feet over the next 15 years. Inspection has determined that the cracks have increased horizontally since 1990. Analysis also indicates that the cracks are not surficial but extend partially through the block and will eventually extend all the way through.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Damage_32-1"><a href="#cite_note-Damage-32">[27]</a></sup></p>
<p>The 1990 report documented deterioration of the marble's surface. As much as 2.85 mm of the marble surface has been lost through weathering. The study projected that before 2010, the Tomb Monument will have been weathered enough to have a negative effect on the experience of the visitors and concludes the only solutions are to enclose or replace the monument.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Damage_32-2"><a href="#cite_note-Damage-32">[27]</a></sup></p>
<p>Several options have been considered to deal with the damage. Officials at Arlington National Cemetery determined that proper repair can return the Tomb Monument to an acceptable appearance. However, because the cracks will continue to lengthen and widen, continuous grouting, regrouting, touch-up, monitoring, and maintenance would be required. Therefore, a report commissioned by Arlington National Cemetery and published in June 2006 confirmed the Cemetery's conclusion that "replacement of the three pieces of the Tomb Monument is the preferred alternative". A final decision was scheduled to be made on September 30, 2007.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Damage_32-3"><a href="#cite_note-Damage-32">[27]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/National_Trust_for_Historic_Preservation" title="National Trust for Historic Preservation">National Trust for Historic Preservation</a> objects to the plan to replace the authentic Tomb Monument. The Trust expressed concern that Arlington National Cemetery seeks to replace the existing monument with marble from the original quarry, which experts agree is likely eventually to crack.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[28]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Trust has observed that the Cemetery's own 1990 report recommended that the monument be repaired and that the Cemetery, in fact, commissioned Oehrlein Architects to repair the stone. In 2007, Mary Oehrlein informed Congressional staff members that: "The existing monument can easily be repaired, as was done 17 years ago, using conventional conservation methods to re-grout the cracks. Once repaired, the fault lines would be virtually invisible from the public viewing areas."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[29]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2008_report_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-2008_report-35">[30]</a></sup></p>
<p>On September 26, 2007, U.S. Senator <a href="/wiki/Daniel_Akaka" title="Daniel Akaka">Daniel Akaka</a> announced that an amendment crafted together with Senator <a href="/wiki/Jim_Webb" title="Jim Webb">Jim Webb</a> will be added to the <a href="/wiki/National_Defense_Authorization_Act" title="National Defense Authorization Act">National Defense Authorization Act</a> for Fiscal Year 2008 (H.R. 1585) that would require a report on the plans of the Secretary of the Army and the Secretary of Veterans Affairs to replace the monument at the Tomb of the Unknowns. The secretaries would be required to advise Congress on the current efforts to maintain and preserve the monument. Additionally, they would have to provide an assessment on the feasibility and advisability of repairing rather than replacing the Tomb Monument. Finally, if the secretaries choose replacement, they would have to report those plans and detail how they intend to dispose of the current monument. Once the report is provided, the secretaries are prevented from taking action to replace the monument for at least 180 days. The Akaka-Webb amendment was included in the bill by unanimous consent of the Senate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[31]</a></sup> An amendment to the Fiscal Year 2008 Defense Authorization Bill authorized a review of the monument's condition. The bill also authorized repair, but not replacement, of the monument.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[32]</a></sup> Final passage of the <a href="/wiki/National_Defense_Authorization_Act" title="National Defense Authorization Act">National Defense Authorization Act</a> for 2008 (H.R. 4986) was signed by President Bush on January 28, 2008.</p>

October 21, 2011: Completed repair of the cracks in the Tomb.</div>

<p>In 2003 John Haines, a retired car dealer, offered to donate a large slab of marble to the Arlington National Cemetery to replace the existing marble. Haines paid $31,000 for the marble slab.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Donation_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-Donation-38">[33]</a></sup> The marble was not removed from the quarry, however, because imperfections were found and the block was rejected.</p>
<p>In June 2009 Arlington National Cemetery and the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Army_Corps_of_Engineers" title="United States Army Corps of Engineers">U.S. Army Corps of Engineers</a> announced that the monument was to be repaired, not replaced.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[34]</a></sup> In 2010, the cracks were filled but the repairs lasted only a few months.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Engineers_40-0"><a href="#cite_note-Engineers-40">[35]</a></sup> As of June 2011, the cemetery was struggling to repair the cracks in the monument, one of which measured 28.4 feet (8.7 m) long, with another at 16.2 feet (4.9 m).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[36]</a></sup> In September 2011, the cracks were filled again<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Engineers_40-1"><a href="#cite_note-Engineers-40">[35]</a></sup> and on October 21, 2011, inspection by the Corps of Engineers and other experts pronounced the repairs a success.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[37]</a></sup></p>
