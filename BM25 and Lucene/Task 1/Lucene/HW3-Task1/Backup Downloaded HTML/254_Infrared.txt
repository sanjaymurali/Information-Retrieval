<p><b>Infrared radiation</b>, or simply <b>infrared</b> or <b>IR</b>, is <a href="/wiki/Electromagnetic_radiation" title="Electromagnetic radiation">electromagnetic radiation</a> (EMR) with longer <a href="/wiki/Wavelength" title="Wavelength">wavelengths</a> than those of <a href="/wiki/Light" title="Light">visible light</a>, and is therefore invisible, although it is sometimes loosely called <b>infrared light</b>. It extends from the nominal <a href="/wiki/Red" title="Red">red</a> edge of the <a href="/wiki/Visible_spectrum" title="Visible spectrum">visible spectrum</a> at 700 <a href="/wiki/Nanometre" title="Nanometre">nanometers</a> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Frequency_spectrum" title="Frequency spectrum">frequency</a> 430 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Terahertz_(unit)" title="Terahertz (unit)">THz</a>), to 1 mm (300 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/GHz" title="GHz">GHz</a>)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> (although people can see infrared up to at least 1050 nm in experiments<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sliney1976_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Sliney1976-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LynchLivingston2001_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-LynchLivingston2001-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dash2009_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Dash2009-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Saidman1933_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Saidman1933-5">[5]</a></sup>). Most of the <a href="/wiki/Thermal_radiation" title="Thermal radiation">thermal radiation</a> emitted by objects near room temperature is infrared. Like all EMR, IR carries <a href="/wiki/Radiant_energy" title="Radiant energy">radiant energy</a>, and behaves <a href="/wiki/Wave%E2%80%93particle_duality" title="Wave–particle duality">both</a> like a wave and like its <a href="/wiki/Quantum" title="Quantum">quantum</a> particle, the <a href="/wiki/Photon" title="Photon">photon</a>.</p>
<p>Infrared was discovered in 1800 by astronomer Sir <a href="/wiki/William_Herschel" title="William Herschel">William Herschel</a>, who discovered a type of invisible radiation in the spectrum lower in energy than red light, by means of its effect on a thermometer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Slightly more than half of the total energy from the Sun was eventually found to arrive on Earth in the form of infrared. The balance between absorbed and emitted infrared radiation has a critical effect on Earth's <a href="/wiki/Climate" title="Climate">climate</a>.</p>
<p>Infrared radiation is emitted or absorbed by <a href="/wiki/Molecule" title="Molecule">molecules</a> when they change their <a href="/wiki/Infrared_spectroscopy" title="Infrared spectroscopy">rotational-vibrational</a> movements. It excites <a href="/wiki/Vibration" title="Vibration">vibrational</a> modes in a <a href="/wiki/Molecule" title="Molecule">molecule</a> through a change in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Molecular_dipole_moment" title="Molecular dipole moment">dipole moment</a>, making it a useful frequency range for study of these energy states for molecules of the proper symmetry. <a href="/wiki/Infrared_spectroscopy" title="Infrared spectroscopy">Infrared spectroscopy</a> examines absorption and transmission of <a href="/wiki/Photon" title="Photon">photons</a> in the infrared range.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>Infrared radiation is used in industrial, scientific, and medical applications. Night-vision devices using active near-infrared illumination allow people or animals to be observed without the observer being detected. <a href="/wiki/Infrared_astronomy" title="Infrared astronomy">Infrared astronomy</a> uses sensor-equipped <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Telescopes" title="Telescopes">telescopes</a> to penetrate dusty regions of space such as <a href="/wiki/Molecular_cloud" title="Molecular cloud">molecular clouds</a>, detect objects such as <a href="/wiki/Planet" title="Planet">planets</a>, and to view highly <a href="/wiki/Redshift" title="Redshift">red-shifted</a> objects from the early days of the <a href="/wiki/Universe" title="Universe">universe</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ir_astronomy_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-ir_astronomy-8">[8]</a></sup> Infrared thermal-imaging cameras are used to detect heat loss in insulated systems, to observe changing blood flow in the skin, and to detect overheating of electrical apparatus.</p>
<p>Thermal-infrared imaging is used extensively for military and civilian purposes. Military applications include <a href="/wiki/Target_acquisition" title="Target acquisition">target acquisition</a>, surveillance, <a href="/wiki/Night_vision" title="Night vision">night vision</a>, homing, and tracking. Humans at normal body temperature radiate chiefly at wavelengths around 10 μm (micrometers). Non-military uses include <a href="/wiki/Thermal_efficiency" title="Thermal efficiency">thermal efficiency</a> analysis, environmental monitoring, industrial facility inspections, remote temperature sensing, short-ranged <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wireless_communication" title="Wireless communication">wireless communication</a>, <a href="/wiki/Spectroscopy" title="Spectroscopy">spectroscopy</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Weather_forecasting" title="Weather forecasting">weather forecasting</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Definition_and_relationship_to_the_electromagnetic_spectrum">Definition and relationship to the electromagnetic spectrum</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Infrared&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Definition and relationship to the electromagnetic spectrum">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Infrared radiation extends from the nominal <a href="/wiki/Red" title="Red">red</a> edge of the <a href="/wiki/Visible_spectrum" title="Visible spectrum">visible spectrum</a> at 700 <a href="/wiki/Nanometre" title="Nanometre">nanometers</a> (nm) to 1 mm. This range of wavelengths corresponds to a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Frequency_spectrum" title="Frequency spectrum">frequency</a> range of approximately 430 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Terahertz_(unit)" title="Terahertz (unit)">THz</a> down to 300 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/GHz" title="GHz">GHz</a>. Below infrared is the microwave portion of the <a href="/wiki/Electromagnetic_spectrum" title="Electromagnetic spectrum">electromagnetic spectrum</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Natural_infrared">Natural infrared</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Infrared&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Natural infrared">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Sunlight, at an effective temperature of 5,780 kelvins, is composed of near thermal-spectrum radiation that is slightly more than half infrared. At zenith, sunlight provides an <a href="/wiki/Irradiance" title="Irradiance">irradiance</a> of just over 1 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kilowatt" title="Kilowatt">kilowatt</a> per square meter at sea level. Of this energy, 527 watts is infrared radiation, 445 watts is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Visible_light" title="Visible light">visible light</a>, and 32 watts is <a href="/wiki/Ultraviolet" title="Ultraviolet">ultraviolet</a> radiation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Nearly all the infrared radiation in sunlight is near infrared, shorter than 4 micrometers.</p>
<p>On the surface of Earth, at far lower temperatures than the surface of the Sun, almost all thermal radiation consists of infrared in mid-infrared region, much longer than in sunlight. Of these natural thermal radiation processes only lightning and natural fires are hot enough to produce much visible energy, and fires produce far more infrared than visible-light energy.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Regions_within_the_infrared">Regions within the infrared</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Infrared&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Regions within the infrared">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In general, objects emit infrared radiation across a spectrum of wavelengths, but sometimes only a limited region of the spectrum is of interest because sensors usually collect radiation only within a specific bandwidth. Thermal infrared radiation also has a maximum emission wavelength, which is inversely proportional to the absolute temperature of object, in accordance with <a href="/wiki/Wien%27s_displacement_law" title="Wien's displacement law">Wien's displacement law</a>.</p>
<p>Therefore, the infrared band is often subdivided into smaller sections.</p>

<p>A third scheme divides up the band based on the response of various detectors:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Miller_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-Miller-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li>Near-infrared: from 0.7 to 1.0 µm (from the approximate end of the response of the <a href="/wiki/Human_eye" title="Human eye">human eye</a> to that of silicon).</li>
<li>Short-wave infrared: 1.0 to 3 µm (from the cut-off of silicon to that of the MWIR atmospheric window). InGaAs covers to about 1.8 µm; the less sensitive lead salts cover this region.</li>
<li>Mid-wave infrared: 3 to 5 µm (defined by the atmospheric window and covered by <a href="/wiki/Indium_antimonide" title="Indium antimonide">Indium antimonide</a> [InSb] and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/HgCdTe" title="HgCdTe">HgCdTe</a> and partially by <a href="/wiki/Lead_selenide" title="Lead selenide">lead selenide</a> [PbSe]).</li>
<li>Long-wave infrared: 8 to 12, or 7 to 14 µm (this is the atmospheric window covered by HgCdTe and <a href="/wiki/Microbolometer" title="Microbolometer">microbolometers</a>).</li>
<li>Very-long wave infrared (VLWIR) (12 to about 30 µm, covered by doped silicon).</li>
</ul>
<p>Near-infrared is the region closest in wavelength to the radiation detectable by the <a href="/wiki/Human_eye" title="Human eye">human eye</a>. mid- and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Far-infrared" title="Far-infrared">far-infrared</a> are progressively further from the <a href="/wiki/Visible_spectrum" title="Visible spectrum">visible spectrum</a>. Other definitions follow different physical mechanisms (emission peaks, vs. bands, water absorption) and the newest follow technical reasons (the common <a href="/wiki/Silicon" title="Silicon">silicon</a> detectors are sensitive to about 1,050 nm, while <a href="/wiki/Indium_gallium_arsenide" title="Indium gallium arsenide">InGaAs</a>'s sensitivity starts around 950 nm and ends between 1,700 and 2,600 nm, depending on the specific configuration). No international standards for these specifications are currently available.</p>
<p>The onset of infrared is defined (according to different standards) at various values typically between 700 nm and 800 nm, but the boundary between visible and infrared light is not precisely defined. The human eye is markedly less sensitive to light above 700 nm wavelength, so longer wavelengths make insignificant contributions to scenes illuminated by common light sources. However, particularly intense near-IR light (e.g., from IR <a href="/wiki/Laser" title="Laser">lasers</a>, IR LED sources, or from bright daylight with the visible light removed by colored gels) can be detected up to approximately 780 nm, and will be perceived as red light. Intense light sources providing wavelengths as long as 1050 nm can be seen as a dull red glow, causing some difficulty in near-IR illumination of scenes in the dark (usually this practical problem is solved by indirect illumination). Leaves are particularly bright in the near IR, and if all visible light leaks from around an IR-filter are blocked, and the eye is given a moment to adjust to the extremely dim image coming through a visually opaque IR-passing photographic filter, it is possible to see the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wood_effect" title="Wood effect">Wood effect</a> that consists of IR-glowing foliage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Heat">Heat</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Infrared&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Heat">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Thermal_radiation" title="Thermal radiation">Thermal radiation</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">

<p>Infrared radiation is popularly known as "heat radiation"<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>, but light and electromagnetic waves of any frequency will heat surfaces that absorb them. Infrared light from the Sun accounts for 49%<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> of the heating of Earth, with the rest being caused by visible light that is absorbed then re-radiated at longer wavelengths. Visible light or <a href="/wiki/Ultraviolet" title="Ultraviolet">ultraviolet</a>-emitting <a href="/wiki/Laser" title="Laser">lasers</a> can char paper and incandescently hot objects emit visible radiation. Objects at room <a href="/wiki/Temperature" title="Temperature">temperature</a> will <a href="/wiki/Spontaneous_emission" title="Spontaneous emission">emit</a> <a href="/wiki/Thermal_radiation" title="Thermal radiation">radiation</a> concentrated mostly in the 8 to 25 µm band, but this is not distinct from the emission of visible light by incandescent objects and ultraviolet by even hotter objects (see <a href="/wiki/Black_body" title="Black body">black body</a> and <a href="/wiki/Wien%27s_displacement_law" title="Wien's displacement law">Wien's displacement law</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Heat" title="Heat">Heat</a> is energy in transit that flows due to temperature difference. Unlike heat transmitted by <a href="/wiki/Thermal_conduction" title="Thermal conduction">thermal conduction</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thermal_convection" title="Thermal convection">thermal convection</a>, thermal radiation can propagate through a <a href="/wiki/Vacuum" title="Vacuum">vacuum</a>. Thermal radiation is characterized by a particular spectrum of many wavelengths that is associated with emission from an object, due to the vibration of its molecules at a given temperature. Thermal radiation can be emitted from objects at any wavelength, and at very high temperatures such radiations are associated with spectra far above the infrared, extending into visible, ultraviolet, and even X-ray regions (e.g. the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solar_corona" title="Solar corona">solar corona</a>). Thus, the popular association of infrared radiation with thermal radiation is only a coincidence based on typical (comparatively low) temperatures often found near the surface of planet Earth.</p>
<p>The concept of <a href="/wiki/Emissivity" title="Emissivity">emissivity</a> is important in understanding the infrared emissions of objects. This is a property of a surface that describes how its thermal emissions deviate from the ideal of a <a href="/wiki/Black_body" title="Black body">black body</a>. To further explain, two objects at the same physical temperature will not show the same infrared image if they have differing emissivity. For example, for any pre-set emissivity value, objects with higher emissivity will appear hotter, and those with a lower emissivity will appear cooler. For that reason, incorrect selection of emissivity will give inaccurate results when using infrared cameras and pyrometers.</p>

<p>Infrared is used in night vision equipment when there is insufficient <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Visible_light" title="Visible light">visible light</a> to see.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-how_nightvision_works_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-how_nightvision_works-22">[22]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Night_vision_devices" title="Night vision devices">Night vision devices</a> operate through a process involving the conversion of ambient light photons into electrons that are then amplified by a chemical and electrical process and then converted back into visible light.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-how_nightvision_works_22-1"><a href="#cite_note-how_nightvision_works-22">[22]</a></sup> Infrared light sources can be used to augment the available ambient light for conversion by night vision devices, increasing in-the-dark visibility without actually using a visible light source.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-how_nightvision_works_22-2"><a href="#cite_note-how_nightvision_works-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p>The use of infrared light and night vision devices should not be confused with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thermal_imaging" title="Thermal imaging">thermal imaging</a>, which creates images based on differences in surface temperature by detecting infrared radiation (<a href="/wiki/Heat" title="Heat">heat</a>) that emanates from objects and their surrounding environment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Thermography">Thermography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Infrared&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Thermography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Thermography" title="Thermography">Thermography</a></div>
<p>Infrared radiation can be used to remotely determine the temperature of objects (if the emissivity is known). This is termed thermography, or in the case of very hot objects in the NIR or visible it is termed <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pyrometry" title="Pyrometry">pyrometry</a>. Thermography (thermal imaging) is mainly used in military and industrial applications but the technology is reaching the public market in the form of infrared cameras on cars due to the massively reduced production costs.</p>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thermographic_cameras" title="Thermographic cameras">Thermographic cameras</a> detect radiation in the infrared range of the electromagnetic spectrum (roughly 900–14,000 nanometers or 0.9–14 μm) and produce images of that radiation. Since infrared radiation is emitted by all objects based on their temperatures, according to the <a href="/wiki/Black_body" title="Black body">black body</a> radiation law, thermography makes it possible to "see" one's environment with or without visible illumination. The amount of radiation emitted by an object increases with temperature, therefore thermography allows one to see variations in temperature (hence the name).</p>

<p>A hyperspectral image is a "picture" containing continuous <a href="/wiki/Infrared_spectroscopy" title="Infrared spectroscopy">spectrum</a> through a wide spectral range at each pixel. Hyperspectral imaging is gaining importance in the field of applied spectroscopy particularly with NIR, SWIR, MWIR, and LWIR spectral regions. Typical applications include biological, mineralogical, defence, and industrial measurements.</p>
<p>Thermal infrared hyperspectral imaging can be similarly performed using a <a href="/wiki/Thermographic_camera" title="Thermographic camera">Thermographic camera</a>, with the fundamental difference that each pixel contains a full LWIR spectrum. Consequently, chemical identification of the object can be performed without a need for an external light source such as the sun or the moon. Such cameras are typically applied for geological measurements, outdoor surveillance and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/UAV" title="UAV">UAV</a> applications.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Frost.26Sullivan_Specim_Owl_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-Frost.26Sullivan_Specim_Owl-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_imaging">Other imaging</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Infrared&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Other imaging">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Infrared_photography" title="Infrared photography">infrared photography</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Infrared_filter" title="Infrared filter">infrared filters</a> are used to capture the near-infrared spectrum. <a href="/wiki/Digital_camera" title="Digital camera">Digital cameras</a> often use infrared <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Filter_(optics)" title="Filter (optics)">blockers</a>. Cheaper digital cameras and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Camera_phones" title="Camera phones">camera phones</a> have less effective filters and can "see" intense near-infrared, appearing as a bright purple-white color. This is especially pronounced when taking pictures of subjects near IR-bright areas (such as near a lamp), where the resulting infrared interference can wash out the image. There is also a technique called '<a href="/wiki/Terahertz_radiation" title="Terahertz radiation">T-ray</a>' imaging, which is imaging using <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Far-infrared" title="Far-infrared">far-infrared</a> or <a href="/wiki/Terahertz_radiation" title="Terahertz radiation">terahertz radiation</a>. Lack of bright sources can make terahertz photography more challenging than most other infrared imaging techniques. Recently T-ray imaging has been of considerable interest due to a number of new developments such as <a href="/wiki/Terahertz_time-domain_spectroscopy" title="Terahertz time-domain spectroscopy">terahertz time-domain spectroscopy</a>.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Tracking">Tracking</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Infrared&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Tracking">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Infrared_homing" title="Infrared homing">Infrared homing</a></div>
<p>Infrared tracking, also known as infrared homing, refers to a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Passive_homing" title="Passive homing">passive missile guidance system</a>, which uses the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Light_emission" title="Light emission">emission</a> from a target of <a href="/wiki/Electromagnetic_radiation" title="Electromagnetic radiation">electromagnetic radiation</a> in the infrared part of the <a href="/wiki/Electromagnetic_spectrum" title="Electromagnetic spectrum">spectrum</a> to track it. Missiles that use infrared seeking are often referred to as "heat-seekers", since infrared (IR) is just below the visible spectrum of light in frequency and is radiated strongly by hot bodies. Many objects such as people, vehicle engines, and aircraft generate and retain heat, and as such, are especially visible in the infrared wavelengths of light compared to objects in the background.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Heating">Heating</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Infrared&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Heating">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Infrared_heating" title="Infrared heating">Infrared heating</a></div>

<p>Infrared radiation can be used as a deliberate heating source. For example, it is used in infrared saunas to heat the occupants. It may also be used in other heating applications, such as to remove ice from the wings of aircraft (de-icing).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> Infrared can be used in cooking and heating food as it predominantly heats the opaque, absorbent objects, rather than the air around them.</p>
<p>Infrared heating is also becoming more popular in industrial manufacturing processes, e.g. curing of coatings, forming of plastics, annealing, plastic welding, and print drying. In these applications, infrared heaters replace convection ovens and contact heating.</p>
<p>Efficiency is achieved by matching the wavelength of the infrared heater to the absorption characteristics of the material.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cooling">Cooling</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Infrared&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Cooling">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Radiative_cooling" title="Radiative cooling">Radiative cooling</a></div>
<p>A variety of technologies or proposed technologies take advantage of infrared emissions to cool buildings or other systems. The LWIR (8–15 µm) region is especially useful since some radiation at these wavelengths can escape into space through the atmosphere.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Communications">Communications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Infrared&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Communications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>IR data transmission is also employed in short-range communication among computer peripherals and <a href="/wiki/Personal_digital_assistant" title="Personal digital assistant">personal digital assistants</a>. These devices usually conform to standards published by <a href="/wiki/Infrared_Data_Association" title="Infrared Data Association">IrDA</a>, the Infrared Data Association. Remote controls and IrDA devices use infrared <a href="/wiki/Light-emitting_diode" title="Light-emitting diode">light-emitting diodes</a> (LEDs) to emit infrared radiation that is focused by a plastic <a href="/wiki/Lens_(optics)" title="Lens (optics)">lens</a> into a narrow beam. The beam is <a href="/wiki/Modulation" title="Modulation">modulated</a>, i.e. switched on and off, to prevent interference from other sources of infrared (like sunlight or artificial lighting). The receiver uses a <a href="/wiki/Silicon" title="Silicon">silicon</a> <a href="/wiki/Photodiode" title="Photodiode">photodiode</a> to convert the infrared radiation to an <a href="/wiki/Electric_current" title="Electric current">electric current</a>. It responds only to the rapidly pulsing signal created by the transmitter, and filters out slowly changing infrared radiation from ambient light. Infrared communications are useful for indoor use in areas of high population density. IR does not penetrate walls and so does not interfere with other devices in adjoining rooms. Infrared is the most common way for <a href="/wiki/Remote_control" title="Remote control">remote controls</a> to command appliances. Infrared remote control protocols like <a href="/wiki/RC-5" title="RC-5">RC-5</a>, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Sony_Infrared_Remote_Control&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Sony Infrared Remote Control (page does not exist)">SIRC</a>, are used to communicate with infrared.</p>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Free_space_optical_communication" title="Free space optical communication">Free space optical communication</a> using infrared <a href="/wiki/Laser" title="Laser">lasers</a> can be a relatively inexpensive way to install a communications link in an urban area operating at up to 4 gigabit/s, compared to the cost of burying fiber optic cable, except for the radiation damage. "Since the eye cannot detect IR, blinking or closing the eyes to help prevent or reduce damage may not happen."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<p>Infrared lasers are used to provide the light for <a href="/wiki/Optical_fiber" title="Optical fiber">optical fiber</a> communications systems. Infrared light with a wavelength around 1,330 nm (least <a href="/wiki/Dispersion_(optics)" title="Dispersion (optics)">dispersion</a>) or 1,550 nm (best transmission) are the best choices for standard <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Silica" title="Silica">silica</a> fibers.</p>
<p>IR data transmission of encoded audio versions of printed signs is being researched as an aid for visually impaired people through the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/RIAS_(Remote_Infrared_Audible_Signage)" title="RIAS (Remote Infrared Audible Signage)">RIAS (Remote Infrared Audible Signage)</a> project. Transmitting IR data from one device to another is sometimes referred to as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Beaming" title="Beaming">beaming</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Spectroscopy">Spectroscopy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Infrared&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Spectroscopy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Infrared_spectroscopy" title="Infrared spectroscopy">Infrared vibrational spectroscopy</a> (see also <a href="/wiki/Near-infrared_spectroscopy" title="Near-infrared spectroscopy">near-infrared spectroscopy</a>) is a technique that can be used to identify molecules by analysis of their constituent bonds. Each chemical bond in a molecule vibrates at a frequency characteristic of that bond. A group of atoms in a molecule (e.g., CH<sub>2</sub>) may have multiple modes of oscillation caused by the stretching and bending motions of the group as a whole. If an oscillation leads to a change in <a href="/wiki/Dipole" title="Dipole">dipole</a> in the molecule then it will absorb a <a href="/wiki/Photon" title="Photon">photon</a> that has the same frequency. The vibrational frequencies of most molecules correspond to the frequencies of infrared light. Typically, the technique is used to study <a href="/wiki/Organic_compound" title="Organic compound">organic compounds</a> using light radiation from 4000–400 cm<sup>−1</sup>, the mid-infrared. A spectrum of all the frequencies of absorption in a sample is recorded. This can be used to gain information about the sample composition in terms of chemical groups present and also its purity (for example, a wet sample will show a broad O-H absorption around 3200 cm<sup>−1</sup>).</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Thin_film_metrology">Thin film metrology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Infrared&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Thin film metrology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the semiconductor industry, infrared light can be used to characterize materials such as thin films and periodic trench structures. By measuring the reflectance of light from the surface of a semiconductor wafer, the index of refraction (n) and the extinction Coefficient (k) can be determined via the <a href="/wiki/Refractive_index_and_extinction_coefficient_of_thin_film_materials" title="Refractive index and extinction coefficient of thin film materials">Forouhi-Bloomer dispersion equations</a>. The reflectance from the infrared light can also be used to determine the critical dimension, depth, and sidewall angle of high aspect ratio trench structures.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Meteorology">Meteorology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Infrared&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Meteorology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft">

<p><a href="/wiki/Weather_satellite" title="Weather satellite">Weather satellites</a> equipped with scanning radiometers produce thermal or infrared images, which can then enable a trained analyst to determine cloud heights and types, to calculate land and surface water temperatures, and to locate ocean surface features. The scanning is typically in the range 10.3–12.5 µm (IR4 and IR5 channels).</p>
<p>High, cold ice clouds such as <a href="/wiki/Cirrus_cloud" title="Cirrus cloud">Cirrus</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cumulonimbus" title="Cumulonimbus">Cumulonimbus</a> show up bright white, lower warmer clouds such as <a href="/wiki/Stratus_cloud" title="Stratus cloud">Stratus</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stratocumulus" title="Stratocumulus">Stratocumulus</a> show up as grey with intermediate clouds shaded accordingly. Hot land surfaces will show up as dark-grey or black. One disadvantage of infrared imagery is that low cloud such as stratus or <a href="/wiki/Fog" title="Fog">fog</a> can be a similar temperature to the surrounding land or sea surface and does not show up. However, using the difference in brightness of the IR4 channel (10.3–11.5 µm) and the near-infrared channel (1.58–1.64 µm), low cloud can be distinguished, producing a <i>fog</i> satellite picture. The main advantage of infrared is that images can be produced at night, allowing a continuous sequence of weather to be studied.</p>
<p>These infrared pictures can depict ocean eddies or vortices and map currents such as the Gulf Stream, which are valuable to the shipping industry. Fishermen and farmers are interested in knowing land and water temperatures to protect their crops against frost or increase their catch from the sea. Even <a href="/wiki/El_Ni%C3%B1o" title="El Niño">El Niño</a> phenomena can be spotted. Using color-digitized techniques, the gray-shaded thermal images can be converted to color for easier identification of desired information.</p>
<p>The main water vapour channel at 6.40 to 7.08 µm can be imaged by some weather satellites and shows the amount of moisture in the atmosphere.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Climatology">Climatology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Infrared&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Climatology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the field of climatology, atmospheric infrared radiation is monitored to detect trends in the energy exchange between the earth and the atmosphere. These trends provide information on long-term changes in Earth's climate. It is one of the primary parameters studied in research into <a href="/wiki/Global_warming" title="Global warming">global warming</a>, together with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solar_radiation" title="Solar radiation">solar radiation</a>.</p>

<p>A <a href="/wiki/Pyrgeometer" title="Pyrgeometer">pyrgeometer</a> is utilized in this field of research to perform continuous outdoor measurements. This is a broadband infrared radiometer with sensitivity for infrared radiation between approximately 4.5 µm and 50 µm.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Astronomy">Astronomy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Infrared&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Astronomy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Infrared_astronomy" title="Infrared astronomy">Infrared astronomy</a> and <a href="/wiki/Far-infrared_astronomy" title="Far-infrared astronomy">far-infrared astronomy</a></div>

<p>Astronomers observe objects in the infrared portion of the electromagnetic spectrum using optical components, including mirrors, lenses and solid state digital detectors. For this reason it is classified as part of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Optical_astronomy" title="Optical astronomy">optical astronomy</a>. To form an image, the components of an infrared telescope need to be carefully shielded from heat sources, and the detectors are chilled using liquid <a href="/wiki/Helium" title="Helium">helium</a>.</p>
<p>The sensitivity of Earth-based infrared telescopes is significantly limited by water vapor in the atmosphere, which absorbs a portion of the infrared radiation arriving from space outside of selected <a href="/wiki/Infrared_window" title="Infrared window">atmospheric windows</a>. This limitation can be partially alleviated by placing the telescope observatory at a high altitude, or by carrying the telescope aloft with a balloon or an aircraft. Space telescopes do not suffer from this handicap, and so outer space is considered the ideal location for infrared astronomy.</p>
<p>The infrared portion of the spectrum has several useful benefits for astronomers. Cold, dark <a href="/wiki/Molecular_cloud" title="Molecular cloud">molecular clouds</a> of gas and dust in our galaxy will glow with radiated heat as they are irradiated by imbedded stars. Infrared can also be used to detect <a href="/wiki/Protostar" title="Protostar">protostars</a> before they begin to emit visible light. Stars emit a smaller portion of their energy in the infrared spectrum, so nearby cool objects such as <a href="/wiki/Planet" title="Planet">planets</a> can be more readily detected. (In the visible light spectrum, the glare from the star will drown out the reflected light from a planet.)</p>
<p>Infrared light is also useful for observing the cores of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Active_galaxy" title="Active galaxy">active galaxies</a>, which are often cloaked in gas and dust. Distant galaxies with a high <a href="/wiki/Redshift" title="Redshift">redshift</a> will have the peak portion of their spectrum shifted toward longer wavelengths, so they are more readily observed in the infrared.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ir_astronomy_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-ir_astronomy-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Infrared_cleaning">Infrared cleaning</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Infrared&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: Infrared cleaning">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Infrared_cleaning" title="Infrared cleaning">Infrared cleaning</a> is a technique used by some <a href="/wiki/Motion_picture_film_scanner" title="Motion picture film scanner">motion picture film scanners</a>, <a href="/wiki/Film_scanner" title="Film scanner">film scanners</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Flatbed_scanner" title="Flatbed scanner">flatbed scanners</a> to reduce or remove the effect of dust and scratches upon the finished <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Image_scanning" title="Image scanning">scan</a>. It works by collecting an additional infrared channel from the scan at the same position and resolution as the three visible color channels (red, green, and blue). The infrared channel, in combination with the other channels, is used to detect the location of scratches and dust. Once located, those defects can be corrected by scaling or replaced by <a href="/wiki/Inpainting" title="Inpainting">inpainting</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup></p>

<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Infrared_reflectography" title="Infrared reflectography">Infrared reflectography</a><span class="noprint" style="font-size:85%; font-style: normal;"> (<a class="extiw" href="https://fr.wikipedia.org/wiki/r%C3%A9flectographie_infrarouge" title="fr:réflectographie infrarouge">fr</a>; <a class="extiw" href="https://it.wikipedia.org/wiki/Riflettografia" title="it:Riflettografia">it</a>; <a class="extiw" href="https://es.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reflectograf%C3%ADa_infrarroja" title="es:Reflectografía infrarroja">es</a>)</span>, as called by art conservators,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> can be applied to paintings to reveal underlying layers in a completely non-destructive manner, in particular the <a href="/wiki/Underdrawing" title="Underdrawing">underdrawing</a> or outline drawn by the artist as a guide. This often reveals the artist's use of <a href="/wiki/Carbon_black" title="Carbon black">carbon black</a>, which shows up well in reflectograms, as long as it has not also been used in the ground underlying the whole painting. Art conservators are looking to see whether the visible layers of paint differ from the underdrawing or layers in between – such alterations are called <a href="/wiki/Pentimento" title="Pentimento">pentimenti</a> when made by the original artist. This is very useful information in deciding whether a painting is the <a href="/wiki/Prime_version" title="Prime version">prime version</a> by the original artist or a copy, and whether it has been altered by over-enthusiastic restoration work. In general, the more pentimenti the more likely a painting is to be the prime version. It also gives useful insights into working practices.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<p>Among many other changes in the <a href="/wiki/Arnolfini_Portrait" title="Arnolfini Portrait">Arnolfini Portrait</a> of 1434 (left), the man's face was originally higher by about the height of his eye; the woman's was higher, and her eyes looked more to the front. Each of his feet was underdrawn in one position, painted in another, and then overpainted in a third. These alterations are seen in infrared reflectograms.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
<p>Recent progress in the design of infrared sensitive cameras made it possible to discover and depict not only underpaintings and pentimenti but entire paintings which were later overpainted by the artist.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> Notable examples are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Picasso" title="Picasso">Picasso</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Woman_Ironing" title="Woman Ironing">"Woman ironing</a>" and "<a class="external text" href="http://thecreatorsproject.vice.com/blog/how-art-experts-discovered-a-hidden-portrait-beaneath-a-picasso-masterpiece" rel="nofollow">Blue room</a>", where in both cases, a portrait of a man has been made visible under the painting as it is known today.</p>
<p>Similar uses of infrared are made by conservators and scientists on various types of objects, especially very old written documents such as the <a href="/wiki/Dead_Sea_Scrolls" title="Dead Sea Scrolls">Dead Sea Scrolls</a>, the Roman works in the <a href="/wiki/Villa_of_the_Papyri" title="Villa of the Papyri">Villa of the Papyri</a>, and the Silk Road texts found in the <a href="/wiki/Mogao_Caves" title="Mogao Caves">Dunhuang Caves</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> Carbon black used in ink can show up extremely well.</p>

<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Crotalinae" title="Crotalinae">pit viper</a> has a pair of infrared sensory pits on its head. There is uncertainty regarding the exact thermal sensitivity of this biological infrared detection system.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup></p>
<p>Other organisms that have thermoreceptive organs are pythons (family <a href="/wiki/Pythonidae" title="Pythonidae">Pythonidae</a>), some boas (family <a href="/wiki/Boidae" title="Boidae">Boidae</a>), the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Common_Vampire_Bat" title="Common Vampire Bat">Common Vampire Bat</a> (<i>Desmodus rotundus</i>), a variety of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jewel_beetle" title="Jewel beetle">jewel beetles</a> (<i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Melanophila_acuminata&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Melanophila acuminata (page does not exist)">Melanophila acuminata</a></i>),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Evans_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-Evans-37">[37]</a></sup> darkly pigmented butterflies (<i><a href="/wiki/Pachliopta_aristolochiae" title="Pachliopta aristolochiae">Pachliopta aristolochiae</a></i> and <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Troides_rhadamantus_plateni&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Troides rhadamantus plateni (page does not exist)">Troides rhadamantus plateni</a></i>), and possibly blood-sucking bugs (<i><a href="/wiki/Triatoma_infestans" title="Triatoma infestans">Triatoma infestans</a></i>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup></p>
<p>Although near-infrared vision (780–1000 nm) has long been deemed impossible due to noise in visual pigments,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Meuthen_et_al._39-0"><a href="#cite_note-Meuthen_et_al.-39">[39]</a></sup> sensation of near-infrared light was reported in the common carp and in three cichlid species.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Meuthen_et_al._39-1"><a href="#cite_note-Meuthen_et_al.-39">[39]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Shcherbakov_et_al._43-0"><a href="#cite_note-Shcherbakov_et_al.-43">[43]</a></sup> Fish use NIR to capture prey<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Meuthen_et_al._39-2"><a href="#cite_note-Meuthen_et_al.-39">[39]</a></sup> and for phototactic swimming orientation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Shcherbakov_et_al._43-1"><a href="#cite_note-Shcherbakov_et_al.-43">[43]</a></sup> NIR sensation in fish may be relevant under poor lighting conditions during twilight<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Meuthen_et_al._39-3"><a href="#cite_note-Meuthen_et_al.-39">[39]</a></sup> and in turbid surface waters.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Shcherbakov_et_al._43-2"><a href="#cite_note-Shcherbakov_et_al.-43">[43]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Photobiomodulation">Photobiomodulation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Infrared&amp;action=edit&amp;section=28" title="Edit section: Photobiomodulation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Near-infrared light, or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Photobiomodulation" title="Photobiomodulation">photobiomodulation</a>, is used for treatment of chemotherapy-induced oral ulceration as well as wound healing. There is some work relating to anti-herpes virus treatment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> Research projects include work on central nervous system healing effects via cytochrome c oxidase upregulation and other possible mechanisms.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Health_hazard">Health hazard</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Infrared&amp;action=edit&amp;section=29" title="Edit section: Health hazard">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Strong infrared radiation in certain industry high-heat settings may be hazardous to the eyes, resulting in damage or blindness to the user. Since the radiation is invisible, special IR-proof goggles must be worn in such places.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup></p>

<p>The discovery of infrared radiation is ascribed to <a href="/wiki/William_Herschel" title="William Herschel">William Herschel</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Astronomer" title="Astronomer">astronomer</a>, in the early 19th century. Herschel published his results in 1800 before the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Royal_Society_of_London" title="Royal Society of London">Royal Society of London</a>. Herschel used a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Triangular_prism_(optics)" title="Triangular prism (optics)">prism</a> to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Refract" title="Refract">refract</a> light from the <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">sun</a> and detected the infrared, beyond the <a href="/wiki/Red" title="Red">red</a> part of the spectrum, through an increase in the temperature recorded on a <a href="/wiki/Thermometer" title="Thermometer">thermometer</a>. He was surprised at the result and called them "Calorific Rays". The term 'Infrared' did not appear until late in the 19th century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup></p>

<ul>
<li>1737: <a href="/wiki/%C3%89milie_du_Ch%C3%A2telet" title="Émilie du Châtelet">Émilie du Châtelet</a> predicted what is today known as infrared radiation in <i>Dissertation sur la nature et la propagation du feu</i>.</li>
<li>1835: <a href="/wiki/Macedonio_Melloni" title="Macedonio Melloni">Macedonio Melloni</a> made the first <a href="/wiki/Thermopile" title="Thermopile">thermopile</a> IR detector.</li>
<li>1840: <a href="/wiki/John_Herschel" title="John Herschel">John Herschel</a> produces the first thermal image <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thermogram" title="Thermogram">thermogram</a>.</li>
<li>1860: <a href="/wiki/Gustav_Kirchhoff" title="Gustav Kirchhoff">Gustav Kirchhoff</a> formulated the <a href="/wiki/Kirchhoff%27s_law_of_thermal_radiation" title="Kirchhoff's law of thermal radiation">blackbody theorem</a> <span class="mwe-math-element"><span class="mwe-math-mathml-inline mwe-math-mathml-a11y" style="display: none;"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">

<li>1873: <a href="/wiki/Willoughby_Smith" title="Willoughby Smith">Willoughby Smith</a> discovered the photoconductivity of <a href="/wiki/Selenium" title="Selenium">selenium</a>.</li>
<li>1878: <a href="/wiki/Samuel_Pierpont_Langley" title="Samuel Pierpont Langley">Samuel Pierpont Langley</a> invents the first <a href="/wiki/Bolometer" title="Bolometer">bolometer</a>, a device which is able to measure small temperature fluctuations, and thus the power of far infrared sources.</li>
<li>1879: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stefan-Boltzmann_law" title="Stefan-Boltzmann law">Stefan-Boltzmann law</a> formulated empirically that the power radiated by a blackbody is proportional to T<sup>4</sup>.</li>
<li>1880s &amp; 1890s: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/John_Strutt,_3rd_Baron_Rayleigh" title="John Strutt, 3rd Baron Rayleigh">Lord Rayleigh</a> and <a href="/wiki/Wilhelm_Wien" title="Wilhelm Wien">Wilhelm Wien</a> solved part of the blackbody equation, but both solutions diverged in parts of the electromagnetic spectrum. This problem was called the "<a href="/wiki/Ultraviolet_catastrophe" title="Ultraviolet catastrophe">Ultraviolet catastrophe</a> and Infrared Catastrophe".</li>
<li>1901: <a href="/wiki/Max_Planck" title="Max Planck">Max Planck</a> published the <a href="/wiki/Planck%27s_law" title="Planck's law">blackbody equation</a> and theorem. He solved the problem by quantizing the allowable energy transitions.</li>
<li>1905: <a href="/wiki/Albert_Einstein" title="Albert Einstein">Albert Einstein</a> developed the theory of the <a href="/wiki/Photoelectric_effect" title="Photoelectric effect">photoelectric effect</a>.</li>
<li>1917: <a href="/wiki/Theodore_Case" title="Theodore Case">Theodore Case</a> developed the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thallous_sulfide" title="Thallous sulfide">thallous sulfide</a> detector; British scientist built the first <a href="/wiki/Infra-red_search_and_track" title="Infra-red search and track">infra-red search and track</a> (IRST) device able to detect aircraft at a range of one mile (1.6 km).</li>
<li>1935: Lead salts – early missile guidance in <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>.</li>
<li>1938: <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Teau_Ta&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Teau Ta (page does not exist)">Teau Ta</a> – predicted that the pyroelectric effect could be used to detect infrared radiation.</li>
<li>1945: The <a href="/wiki/Zielger%C3%A4t_1229" title="Zielgerät 1229">Zielgerät 1229</a> "Vampir" infrared weapon system was introduced as the first portable infrared device for military applications.</li>
<li>1952: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/H._Welker" title="H. Welker">H. Welker</a> grew synthetic InSb crystals.</li>
<li>1950s: <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Paul_Kruse_(engineer)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Paul Kruse (engineer) (page does not exist)">Paul Kruse</a> (at Honeywell) and Texas Instruments recorded infrared images.</li>
<li>1950s and 1960s: Nomenclature and radiometric units defined by <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Fred_Nicodemenus&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Fred Nicodemenus (page does not exist)">Fred Nicodemenus</a>, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=G.J._Zissis&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="G.J. Zissis (page does not exist)">G.J. Zissis</a> and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=R._Clark&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="R. Clark (page does not exist)">R. Clark</a>; <a href="/wiki/Robert_Clark_Jones" title="Robert Clark Jones">Robert Clark Jones</a> defined <i>D</i>*.</li>
<li>1958: <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=W.D._Lawson&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="W.D. Lawson (page does not exist)">W.D. Lawson</a> (<a href="/wiki/Royal_Radar_Establishment" title="Royal Radar Establishment">Royal Radar Establishment</a> in Malvern) discovered IR detection properties of HgCdTe.</li>
<li>1958: <a href="/wiki/AIM-4_Falcon" title="AIM-4 Falcon">Falcon</a> and <a href="/wiki/AIM-9_Sidewinder" title="AIM-9 Sidewinder">Sidewinder</a> missiles were developed using infrared technology.</li>
<li>1961: <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=J._Cooper&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="J. Cooper (page does not exist)">J. Cooper</a> demonstrated pyroelectric detection.</li>
<li>1964: W.G. Evans discovered infrared thermoreceptors in a pyrophile beetle.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Evans_37-1"><a href="#cite_note-Evans-37">[37]</a></sup></li>
<li>1965: First IR Handbook; first commercial imagers (<a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Barnes,_Agema&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Barnes, Agema (page does not exist)">Barnes, Agema</a> {now part of <a href="/wiki/FLIR_Systems" title="FLIR Systems">FLIR Systems</a> Inc.}; <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Richard_Hudson_(physicist)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Richard Hudson (physicist) (page does not exist)">Richard Hudson</a>'s landmark text; F4 TRAM FLIR by <a href="/wiki/Hughes_Aircraft_Company" title="Hughes Aircraft Company">Hughes</a>; phenomenology pioneered by <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Fred_Simmons_(scientist)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Fred Simmons (scientist) (page does not exist)">Fred Simmons</a> and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=A.T._Stair&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="A.T. Stair (page does not exist)">A.T. Stair</a>; U.S. Army's night vision lab formed (now <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Night_Vision_and_Electronic_Sensors_Directorate" title="Night Vision and Electronic Sensors Directorate">Night Vision and Electronic Sensors Directorate</a> (NVESD), and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Rachets&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Rachets (page does not exist)">Rachets</a> develops detection, recognition and identification modeling there.</li>
<li>1970: <a href="/wiki/Willard_Boyle" title="Willard Boyle">Willard Boyle</a> and <a href="/wiki/George_E._Smith" title="George E. Smith">George E. Smith</a> proposed CCD at <a href="/wiki/Bell_Labs" title="Bell Labs">Bell Labs</a> for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Picture_phone" title="Picture phone">picture phone</a>.</li>
<li>1972: <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Common_module_program&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Common module program (page does not exist)">Common module program</a> started by NVESD.</li>
<li>1978: Infrared imaging astronomy came of age, observatories planned, <a href="/wiki/NASA_Infrared_Telescope_Facility" title="NASA Infrared Telescope Facility">IRTF</a> on Mauna Kea opened; 32 by 32 and 64 by 64 arrays produced using <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/InSb" title="InSb">InSb</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/HgCdTe" title="HgCdTe">HgCdTe</a> and other materials.</li>
<li>2013: On February 14 researchers developed a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Neural_implant" title="Neural implant">neural implant</a> that gives <a href="/wiki/Rat" title="Rat">rats</a> the ability to sense infrared light which for the first time provides <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Living_creatures" title="Living creatures">living creatures</a> with new abilities, instead of simply replacing or augmenting existing abilities.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
