<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Hurricane-proof building</h1>

<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tornadoes" title="Tornadoes">Tornadoes</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cyclones" title="Cyclones">cyclones</a>, and other storms with strong winds damage or destroy many buildings. However, with proper design and construction, the damage to buildings by these forces can be greatly reduced. A variety of methods can help a building survive strong winds and storm surge. </p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Storm_surge_considerations">Storm surge considerations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hurricane-proof_building&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Storm surge considerations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Waves along coastal areas can destroy a building. Buildings should preferably be built on high ground in order to avoid waves. If waves can reach the building site, the building ought to be elevated on steel, concrete, or wooden pilings or anchored to solid rock.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Wind_loading_considerations">Wind loading considerations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hurricane-proof_building&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Wind loading considerations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_foundation">The foundation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hurricane-proof_building&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: The foundation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Pensacola_Beach,_Florida_after_Hurricane_Dennis_in_2005.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="600" data-file-width="800" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/58/Pensacola_Beach%2C_Florida_after_Hurricane_Dennis_in_2005.jpg/220px-Pensacola_Beach%2C_Florida_after_Hurricane_Dennis_in_2005.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/58/Pensacola_Beach%2C_Florida_after_Hurricane_Dennis_in_2005.jpg/330px-Pensacola_Beach%2C_Florida_after_Hurricane_Dennis_in_2005.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/58/Pensacola_Beach%2C_Florida_after_Hurricane_Dennis_in_2005.jpg/440px-Pensacola_Beach%2C_Florida_after_Hurricane_Dennis_in_2005.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">

A <a href="/wiki/Monolithic_dome" title="Monolithic dome">monolithic dome</a> in <a href="/wiki/Pensacola_Beach,_Florida#Novelty_houses" title="Pensacola Beach, Florida">Pensacola Beach, Florida</a>, after <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Dennis" title="Hurricane Dennis">Hurricane Dennis</a> in 2005</div>

<p>Wind acting on the roof surfaces of a building can cause negative pressures that create a lifting force sufficient to lift the roof off the building. Once this occurs, the building is weakened considerably and the rest of the building will likely fail as well. To minimize this vulnerability, the upper structure ought to be anchored through the walls to the foundation.</p>
<p>Several methods can be used to anchor the roof. Typically, roof trusses are "toenailed" into the top of the walls, which provide insufficient force to resist high winds. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hurricane_tie" title="Hurricane tie">Hurricane ties</a> nail into the wall and wrap over the trusses provide higher force resistance.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Mobile_home_tie_down_to_the_foundation">Mobile home tie down to the foundation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hurricane-proof_building&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Mobile home tie down to the foundation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Interlocking metal pan roof systems installed on mobile homes can fail under the pressure differential (lift) created by the high velocity winds passing over the surface plane of the roof. This is compounded by the wind entering the building allowing the building interior to pressurize lifting the underside of the roof panels, resulting in destruction of the building. One example of pan roof systems can be found in this document from <a class="external text" href="http://www.structall.com/pdf/guides/RoofOverGuide.pdf" rel="nofollow">Structall Building Systems</a>.</p>
<p>To mitigate this pressure differential, pre-installed aluminum tabular channels can permanently be fastened perpendicularly across the top of the interlocking ribs of the metal roof system without disturbing the flow of rainwater at the eaves, mid-span, and ridge locations of the building.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Earth-sheltering">Earth-sheltering</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hurricane-proof_building&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Earth-sheltering">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Earth_sheltering" title="Earth sheltering">Earth-sheltered</a> construction is generally more resistant to strong winds and tornadoes than standard construction. Cellars and other earth sheltered components of other buildings, can provide safe refuge during tornadoes.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Dome_homes">Dome homes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hurricane-proof_building&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Dome homes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Geodesic_dome" title="Geodesic dome">Geodesic dome</a> and <a href="/wiki/Monolithic_dome" title="Monolithic dome">Monolithic dome</a></div>
<p>The physical geometry of a building affects its aerodynamic properties and how well it can withstand a storm. Geodesic dome roofs or buildings have low drag coefficients and can withstand higher wind forces than a square building of the same area.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> Even stronger buildings result from <a href="/wiki/Monolithic_dome" title="Monolithic dome">monolithic dome</a> construction.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Building_components">Building components</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hurricane-proof_building&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Building components">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Building openings such as garage doors and windows are often weak points susceptible to failure by wind pressure and blowing debris. Once failure occurs, wind pressure builds up inside the building resulting in the roof lifting off the building. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hurricane_shutters" title="Hurricane shutters">Hurricane shutters</a> can provide protection.</p>
<p>Doors can be blown into the house by wind causing potential <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Structural_failure" title="Structural failure">structural failure</a> (see <a class="external free" href="http://www.floridadisaster.org/hrg/content/openings/openings_index.asp#Hinged_Exterior_Doors" rel="nofollow">http://www.floridadisaster.org/hrg/content/openings/openings_index.asp#Hinged_Exterior_Doors</a>).</p>
<p>Windows can be constructed with plastic panes, shatterproof glass, or glass with protective membranes. The panes are often more firmly attached than normal window panes, including using screws or bolts through the edges of larger panes. Tapcons are used to fasten windows with the concrete structure surrounding.</p>
<p>Wood has a relatively high degree of flexibility which can be beneficial under certain building stresses.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Reinforced_concrete" title="Reinforced concrete">Reinforced concrete</a> is a strong, dense material that, if used in a building that is designed properly, can withstand the destructive power of very high winds, and high-speed debris.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Regulation">Regulation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hurricane-proof_building&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Regulation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>After <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Andrew" title="Hurricane Andrew">Hurricane Andrew</a> in 1992 caused $16 billion in insured damage, the state of <a href="/wiki/Florida" title="Florida">Florida</a> established new building standards and enforcement. The state increased performance criteria for wind-load provisions and adopted new wind provisions from the <a href="/wiki/American_Society_of_Civil_Engineers" title="American Society of Civil Engineers">American Society of Civil Engineers</a>. One important addition included with the new code was the requirement of <a href="/wiki/Toughened_glass" title="Toughened glass">missile-impact resisting glass</a>, which can withstand high velocity impact from wind-borne debris during a hurricane. Many houses built in South Florida since Hurricane Andrew are <a href="/wiki/Concrete_masonry_unit" title="Concrete masonry unit">cinder block masonry</a> construction reinforced with concrete pillars, <a href="/wiki/Truss" title="Truss">hurricane-strapped roof trusses</a>, and codes requirements for <a href="/wiki/Adhesive" title="Adhesive">adhesives</a> and types of roofing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Florida also designated high velocity hurricane zones (i.e. High Velocity Hurricane Zone) with special requirements defined for <a href="/wiki/Miami-Dade_County,_Florida" title="Miami-Dade County, Florida">Miami-Dade</a> and <a href="/wiki/Broward_County,_Florida" title="Broward County, Florida">Broward</a> Counties.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>Hong Kong requires many structures to withstand winds from typhoons.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
