<p>The <b>1887 Atlantic hurricane season</b> was the most active <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="Atlantic hurricane season">Atlantic hurricane season</a> on record at the time. However, it has been surpassed by the <a href="/wiki/1933_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="1933 Atlantic hurricane season">1933</a> and <a href="/wiki/2005_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="2005 Atlantic hurricane season">2005</a>, now tying with the <a href="/wiki/1995_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="1995 Atlantic hurricane season">1995</a>, <a href="/wiki/2010_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="2010 Atlantic hurricane season">2010</a>, <a href="/wiki/2011_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="2011 Atlantic hurricane season">2011</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/2012_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="2012 Atlantic hurricane season">2012</a> seasons for having the third most number of storms. The season ran through the summer and almost all of the fall in 1887, and was surpassed in total number of <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone" title="Tropical cyclone">tropical cyclones</a> only by the seasons of <a href="/wiki/1933_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="1933 Atlantic hurricane season">1933</a> and the record-breaking <a href="/wiki/2005_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="2005 Atlantic hurricane season">2005</a>. The 1887 season saw tropical activity as early as May, and as late as December. Tropical cyclones that did not approach populated areas or shipping lanes, especially if they were relatively weak and of short duration, may have remained undetected. Because technologies such as <a href="/wiki/Satellite_imagery" title="Satellite imagery">satellite monitoring</a> were not available until the 1960s, historical data on tropical cyclones from this period may not be comprehensive. An undercount bias of zero to six tropical cyclones per year between 1851 and 1885 and zero to four per year between 1886 and 1910 has been estimated.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> Of the known 1887 cyclones, Tropical Storm One and Tropical Storm Three were first documented in 1996 by Jose Fernandez-Partagas and Henry Diaz. They also proposed large alterations to the known tracks of several of the other 1887 storms.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1996a_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-1996a-2">[2]</a></sup> Later re-analysis led to the known duration of Hurricane Six, and also that of Hurricane Fifteen, being increased.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-meta_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-meta-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Ten of the season's nineteen known storms attained hurricane status. However, only two of these storms became <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Major_hurricane" title="Major hurricane">major hurricanes</a>, with sustained winds of over 111 mph (179 km/h); the strongest reached peak winds of 125 mph (205 km/h), with a minimum <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Barometric_pressure" title="Barometric pressure">barometric pressure</a> of 946 mbar (27.9 inHg) off the <a href="/wiki/East_Coast_of_the_United_States" title="East Coast of the United States">East Coast of the United States</a> in late August. Only a few of the storms during the 1887 season did not impact land, but there was a low number of deaths.</p>

<p>The first storm of the season formed south of <a href="/wiki/Bermuda" title="Bermuda">Bermuda</a> on May 15, outside of the season boundaries and moved to the northwest, staying out to sea. After peaking at 70 mph (110 km/h) late the next day, it turned northward, and became extratropical on May 18 over <a href="/wiki/Newfoundland_(island)" title="Newfoundland (island)">Newfoundland</a>.</p>

<p>Another May storm formed south of <a href="/wiki/Jamaica" title="Jamaica">Jamaica</a> on May 17, way outside of the season and moved generally northward. It crossed <a href="/wiki/Cuba" title="Cuba">Cuba</a> on May 19 as a tropical storm, and moved out to sea. Two peaked at 60 mph (95 km/h) twice, once on May 18 and May 20. Two dissipated on May May 21 in the <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Ocean" title="Atlantic Ocean">Atlantic Ocean</a>. When this storm and the first storm were active simultaneously from May 17 to May 18, it became the earliest for two storms to be active at one time.</p>

<p>On July 20 a tropical storm formed 150 miles southeast of <a href="/wiki/Barbados" title="Barbados">Barbados</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hurdat_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-hurdat-4">[4]</a></sup> The next day it passed south of the island as a Category 1 hurricane and caused several vessels to be wrecked or to be run aground there. The hurricane continued westward into the <a href="/wiki/Caribbean_Sea" title="Caribbean Sea">Caribbean Sea</a>, becoming a strong Category 2 hurricane with winds of 100 mph (160 km/h) on July 22. Although the hurricane passed far to the south of Cuba, it caused several vessels to sink at <a href="/wiki/Bataban%C3%B3,_Cuba" title="Batabanó, Cuba">Batabanó</a> and brought heavy rain and flooding to the islands interior. Continuing westward, the hurricane passed over the eastern tip of the <a href="/wiki/Yucat%C3%A1n_Peninsula" title="Yucatán Peninsula">Yucatán Peninsula</a> before first turning northward and then northeasterly. It made landfall on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Florida_panhandle" title="Florida panhandle">Florida panhandle</a> on the morning of July 27 as a Category 1 hurricane, with a wind speed of 85 mph (140 km/h). The storm continued northward, as a tropical storm, before dissipating late on July 28 near <a href="/wiki/Augusta,_Georgia" title="Augusta, Georgia">Augusta, Georgia</a>. The storm and its remnants brought heavy rain to the Southeast, up to 8 inches in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cedar_Keys" title="Cedar Keys">Cedar Keys</a> and a maximum of 16.5 inches (420 mm) at <a href="/wiki/Union_Point,_Georgia" title="Union Point, Georgia">Union Point, Georgia</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> The cyclone caused extensive damage to the cotton crop throughout <a href="/wiki/Georgia_(U.S._state)" title="Georgia (U.S. state)">Georgia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Alabama" title="Alabama">Alabama</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1996a_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-1996a-2">[2]</a></sup></p>

<p>A depression strengthened into Tropical Storm Twelve on October 8 and peaked at 70 mph (110 km/h) winds later that day. After passing over <a href="/wiki/Bermuda" title="Bermuda">Bermuda</a>, Twelve dissipated the next day. Twelve was the shortest lived storm in the 1887 season lasting only two days (October 8 &amp; 9th).</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Hurricane_Thirteen">Hurricane Thirteen</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=1887_Atlantic_hurricane_season&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Hurricane Thirteen">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<table class="infobox" style="width:22em;width: 24em;">

<td>85 mph (140 km/h) <small>(1-min)</small>  981 <a href="/wiki/Bar_(unit)" title="Bar (unit)">mbar</a> (<a href="/wiki/Pascal_(unit)" title="Pascal (unit)">hPa</a>)</td>

<p>A tropical storm was first seen on October 9 to the northeast of the <a href="/wiki/Lesser_Antilles" title="Lesser Antilles">Lesser Antilles</a>. It moved westward to cross <a href="/wiki/Puerto_Rico" title="Puerto Rico">Puerto Rico</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hispaniola" title="Hispaniola">Hispaniola</a>, before strengthening into a hurricane while south of Cuba on October 12. The hurricane maintained a peak wind speed of 85 mph (137 km/h) for four days, from October 15 to October 19, whilst crossing the Gulf of Mexico. It struck the <a href="/wiki/Louisiana" title="Louisiana">Louisiana</a> coast near <a href="/wiki/New_Orleans" title="New Orleans">New Orleans</a> on October 19 as a tropical storm. Considerable damage and some flooding were seen in New Orleans, trees were blown down in <a href="/wiki/Algiers,_New_Orleans" title="Algiers, New Orleans">Algiers</a> and there were significant amounts of crop damage in <a href="/wiki/Abbeville,_Louisiana" title="Abbeville, Louisiana">Abbeville</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Iberville_Parish" title="Iberville Parish">Iberville Parish</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rola_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-rola-8">[8]</a></sup> The storm swept across Georgia and the Carolinas before becoming dissipating at sea early on October 20.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NCar_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-NCar-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>Around October 14, a storm moved over <a href="/wiki/Belize" title="Belize">Belize</a>, causing moderate crop damage and disruption in the southern part of the country. Because the 13th storm of the season existed at the same time to its northeast, this system was not included, though further research is under way to determine if it was a new storm or a variation of the 13th storm of the season.</p>

<p>On October 10, a depression strengthened into Tropical Storm Fourteen and moved across the ocean. Fourteen peaked with 85 mph (140 km/h) winds as a Category 1 hurricane on October 11. On October 12, Fourteen weakened and was declared extratropical.</p>

<p>A tropical storm was discovered on late afternoon of October 15 and was named Tropical Storm Fifteen on that day with 80 mph (130 km/h) winds. From October 15 to 16th, Fifteen peaked at 105 mph (169 km/h) winds making it a Category 2 hurricane. Fifteen stayed out to sea and dissipated on October 19.</p>

<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/1887_Halloween_tropical_storm" title="1887 Halloween tropical storm">1887 Halloween tropical storm</a></div>
<p>A tropical storm formed on October 27 and took a long, erratic track across the <a href="/wiki/Caribbean_Sea" title="Caribbean Sea">Caribbean Sea</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Ocean" title="Atlantic Ocean">Atlantic Ocean</a>. Sixteen made landfall and moved across <a href="/wiki/Florida" title="Florida">Florida</a> as a tropical storm on October 30. Sixteen paralleled the Carolina coastline and peaked at an 70 mph (110 km/h) storm with a tropical pressure of 993 mbar on October 31. Sixteen was declared extratropical a day later and hit an extratropical peak of 80 mph (130 km/h) and a 990 mbar extratropical pressure. The storm was dissipated on November 6. Sixteen caused a ship to capsize, killing 2 sailors, and resulted in the loss of four other ships.</p>

<p>On November 27, a tropical storm formed north of <a href="/wiki/Puerto_Rico" title="Puerto Rico">Puerto Rico</a>. It moved to the west-northwest, and looped to the northeast. While looping, it peaked at 80 mph (130 km/h), but rapid weakening began while the storm was moving to the northeast. The storm was last seen on December 4 in the open Atlantic Ocean.</p>

<p>The 18th storm was first seen to the northeast of the <a href="/wiki/Lesser_Antilles" title="Lesser Antilles">Lesser Antilles</a> on December 4. It moved northwestward at first, then turned to the northeast, strengthening into a hurricane on December 7 before rapid weakening began and Eighteen was declared an extratropical storm on December 9.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Tropical_Storm_Nineteen">Tropical Storm Nineteen</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=1887_Atlantic_hurricane_season&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Tropical Storm Nineteen">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>The 19th and final tropical storm formed on December 7 and moved westward through the <a href="/wiki/Caribbean_Sea" title="Caribbean Sea">Caribbean Sea</a>. Nineteen peaked in intensity on December 10 with 60 mph (95 km/h) winds. Nineteen passed by <a href="/wiki/Saint_Vincent_and_the_Grenadines" title="Saint Vincent and the Grenadines">St. Vincent</a> and continued westward until dissipating near the coast of <a href="/wiki/Costa_Rica" title="Costa Rica">Costa Rica</a> on December 12. When Tropical Storm Nineteen formed on December 7, it made 1887 the year with the most off-season storms (five).</p>
<p>At the time, Nineteen was the only tropical storm to pass over Costa Rica on record. In <a href="/wiki/2016_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="2016 Atlantic hurricane season">2016</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Otto" title="Hurricane Otto">Hurricane Otto</a> passed over Costa Rica as a minimal hurricane. However, prior to doing this Otto made landfall in extreme southern <a href="/wiki/Nicaragua" title="Nicaragua">Nicaragua</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Otto_2016_TCR_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Otto_2016_TCR-10">[10]</a></sup> Therefore, Nineteen remains the only tropical storm recorded to make landfall in Costa Rica.</p>
