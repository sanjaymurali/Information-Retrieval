<p>In <a href="/wiki/Thermodynamics" title="Thermodynamics">thermodynamics</a>, a <b>heat engine</b> is a system that converts heat or <a href="/wiki/Thermal_energy" title="Thermal energy">thermal energy</a>—and <a href="/wiki/Chemical_energy" title="Chemical energy">chemical energy</a>—to <a href="/wiki/Mechanical_energy" title="Mechanical energy">mechanical energy</a>, which can then be used to do <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mechanical_work" title="Mechanical work">mechanical work</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> It does this by bringing a working substance from a higher state temperature to a lower state temperature. A heat "source" generates thermal energy that brings the working substance to the high temperature state. The working substance generates work in the "<a href="/wiki/Thermodynamic_system" title="Thermodynamic system">working body</a>" of the engine while <a href="/wiki/Heat_transfer" title="Heat transfer">transferring heat</a> to the colder "<a href="/wiki/Thermal_reservoir" title="Thermal reservoir">sink</a>" until it reaches a low temperature state. During this process some of the thermal energy is converted into <a href="/wiki/Energy" title="Energy">work</a> by exploiting the properties of the working substance. The working substance can be any system with a non-zero <a href="/wiki/Heat_capacity" title="Heat capacity">heat capacity</a>, but it usually is a gas or liquid. During this process, a lot of heat is lost to the surroundings, i.e. it cannot be used.</p>
<p>In general an <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Engine_(mechanical)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Engine (mechanical) (page does not exist)">engine</a> converts energy to mechanical <a href="/wiki/Work_(physics)" title="Work (physics)">work</a>. Heat engines distinguish themselves from other types of engines by the fact that their efficiency is fundamentally limited by <a href="/wiki/Carnot%27s_theorem_(thermodynamics)" title="Carnot's theorem (thermodynamics)">Carnot's theorem</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Although this efficiency limitation can be a drawback, an advantage of heat engines is that most forms of energy can be easily converted to heat by processes like <a href="/wiki/Exothermic_reaction" title="Exothermic reaction">exothermic reactions</a> (such as combustion), <a href="/wiki/Absorption_(electromagnetic_radiation)" title="Absorption (electromagnetic radiation)">absorption</a> of light or energetic particles, <a href="/wiki/Friction" title="Friction">friction</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dissipation" title="Dissipation">dissipation</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electrical_resistance" title="Electrical resistance">resistance</a>. Since the heat source that supplies thermal energy to the engine can thus be powered by virtually any kind of energy, heat engines are very versatile and have a wide range of applicability.</p>
<p>Heat engines are often confused with the cycles they attempt to implement. Typically, the term "engine" is used for a physical device and "cycle" for the model.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Overview">Overview</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Heat_engine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Overview">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Heat_engine.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="600" data-file-width="600" height="300" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a2/Heat_engine.png/300px-Heat_engine.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a2/Heat_engine.png/450px-Heat_engine.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/a/a2/Heat_engine.png 2x" width="300"/></a>

<p>In <a href="/wiki/Thermodynamics" title="Thermodynamics">thermodynamics</a>, heat engines are often modeled using a standard engineering model such as the <a href="/wiki/Otto_cycle" title="Otto cycle">Otto cycle</a>. The theoretical model can be refined and augmented with actual data from an operating engine, using tools such as an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Indicator_diagram" title="Indicator diagram">indicator diagram</a>. Since very few actual implementations of heat engines exactly match their underlying thermodynamic cycles, one could say that a thermodynamic cycle is an ideal case of a mechanical engine. In any case, fully understanding an engine and its efficiency requires gaining a good understanding of the (possibly simplified or idealized) theoretical model, the practical nuances of an actual mechanical engine, and the discrepancies between the two.</p>
<p>In general terms, the larger the difference in temperature between the hot source and the cold sink, the larger is the potential <a href="/wiki/Thermal_efficiency" title="Thermal efficiency">thermal efficiency</a> of the cycle. On Earth, the cold side of any heat engine is limited to being close to the ambient temperature of the environment, or not much lower than 300 <a href="/wiki/Kelvin" title="Kelvin">kelvins</a>, so most efforts to improve the thermodynamic efficiencies of various heat engines focus on increasing the temperature of the source, within material limits. The maximum theoretical efficiency of a heat engine (which no engine ever attains) is equal to the temperature difference between the hot and cold ends divided by the temperature at the hot end, all expressed as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Absolute_temperature" title="Absolute temperature">absolute temperatures</a> (in <a href="/wiki/Kelvin" title="Kelvin">kelvins</a>).</p>
<p>The efficiency of various heat engines proposed or used today has a large range:</p>
<ul>
<li>3%<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> (97 percent waste heat using low quality heat) for the <a href="/wiki/Ocean_thermal_energy_conversion" title="Ocean thermal energy conversion">OTEC</a> ocean power proposal.</li>
<li>25% for most automotive gasoline engines <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></li>
<li>49% for a <a href="/wiki/Supercritical_steam_generator" title="Supercritical steam generator">supercritical</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coal-fired_power_station" title="Coal-fired power station">coal-fired power station</a> such as the <a href="/wiki/Aved%C3%B8re_Power_Station" title="Avedøre Power Station">Avedøre Power Station</a>, and many others</li>
<li>60% for a steam-cooled <a href="/wiki/Combined_cycle" title="Combined cycle">combined cycle</a> <a href="/wiki/Gas_turbine" title="Gas turbine">gas turbine</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<p><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p><br/>
All these processes gain their efficiency (or lack thereof) from the temperature drop across them. Significant energy may be used for auxiliary equipment, such as pumps, which effectively reduces efficiency.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Power">Power</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Heat_engine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Power">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Heat engines can be characterized by their <a href="/wiki/Power_density" title="Power density">specific power</a>, which is typically given in kilowatts per litre of <a href="/wiki/Engine_displacement" title="Engine displacement">engine displacement</a> (in the U.S. also <a href="/wiki/Horsepower" title="Horsepower">horsepower</a> per cubic inch). The result offers an approximation of the peak power output of an engine. This is not to be confused with <a href="/wiki/Fuel_efficiency" title="Fuel efficiency">fuel efficiency</a>, since high efficiency often requires a lean fuel-air ratio, and thus lower power density. A modern high-performance car engine makes in excess of 75 kW/l (1.65 hp/in<sup>3</sup>).</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Everyday_examples">Everyday examples</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Heat_engine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Everyday examples">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Examples of everyday heat engines include the <a href="/wiki/Steam_engine" title="Steam engine">steam engine</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Internal_combustion_engine" title="Internal combustion engine">internal combustion engine</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Stirling_engine" title="Stirling engine">stirling engine</a> is also heat engine, as well as the <a href="/wiki/Drinking_bird" title="Drinking bird">drinking bird</a> toy. All of these heat engines are powered by the expansion of heated gases. The general surroundings are the heat sink, which provides relatively cool gases that, when heated, expand rapidly to drive the mechanical motion of the engine.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Examples_of_heat_engines">Examples of heat engines</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Heat_engine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Examples of heat engines">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>It is important to note that although some cycles have a typical combustion location (internal or external), they often can be implemented with the other. For example, <a href="/wiki/John_Ericsson" title="John Ericsson">John Ericsson</a> developed an external heated engine running on a cycle very much like the earlier <a href="/wiki/Diesel_cycle" title="Diesel cycle">Diesel cycle</a>. In addition, externally heated engines can often be implemented in open or closed cycles.</p>
<h3><span id="Earth's_heat_engine"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Earth.27s_heat_engine">Earth's heat engine</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Heat_engine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Earth's heat engine">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Earth's atmosphere and hydrosphere—Earth’s heat engine—are coupled processes that constantly even out solar heating imbalances through evaporation of surface water, convection, rainfall, winds, and ocean circulation, when distributing heat around the globe.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lindsey_2009_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Lindsey_2009-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Hadley system provides an example of a heat engine. The Hadley circulation is identified with rising of warm and moist air in the equatorial region with descent of colder air in the subtropics corresponding to a thermally driven direct circulation, with consequent net production of kinetic energy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Phase-change_cycles">Phase-change cycles</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Heat_engine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Phase-change cycles">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In these cycles and engines, the working fluids are gases and liquids. The engine converts the working fluid from a gas to a liquid, from liquid to gas, or both, generating work from the fluid expansion or compression.</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Rankine_cycle" title="Rankine cycle">Rankine cycle</a> (classical <a href="/wiki/Steam_engine" title="Steam engine">steam engine</a>)</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Regenerative_cycle" title="Regenerative cycle">Regenerative cycle</a> (<a href="/wiki/Steam_engine" title="Steam engine">steam engine</a> more efficient than <a href="/wiki/Rankine_cycle" title="Rankine cycle">Rankine cycle</a>)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Organic_Rankine_cycle" title="Organic Rankine cycle">Organic Rankine cycle</a> (Coolant changing phase in temperature ranges of ice and hot liquid water)</li>
<li>Vapor to liquid cycle (<a href="/wiki/Drinking_bird" title="Drinking bird">Drinking bird</a>, <a href="/wiki/Injector" title="Injector">Injector</a>, <a href="/wiki/Minto_wheel" title="Minto wheel">Minto wheel</a>)</li>
<li>Liquid to solid cycle (<a href="/wiki/Frost_heaving" title="Frost heaving">Frost heaving</a> — water changing from ice to liquid and back again can lift rock up to 60 cm.)</li>
<li>Solid to gas cycle (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dry_ice_cannon" title="Dry ice cannon">Dry ice cannon</a> — Dry ice sublimes to gas.)</li>
</ul>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cycles_used_for_refrigeration">Cycles used for refrigeration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Heat_engine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Cycles used for refrigeration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Refrigeration" title="Refrigeration">refrigeration</a></div>
<p>A domestic <a href="/wiki/Refrigerator" title="Refrigerator">refrigerator</a> is an example of a <a href="/wiki/Heat_pump" title="Heat pump">heat pump</a>: a heat engine in reverse. Work is used to create a heat differential. Many cycles can run in reverse to move heat from the cold side to the hot side, making the cold side cooler and the hot side hotter. Internal combustion engine versions of these cycles are, by their nature, not reversible.</p>
<p>Refrigeration cycles include:</p>

<li><a href="/wiki/Vapor-compression_refrigeration" title="Vapor-compression refrigeration">Vapor-compression refrigeration</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Stirling_engine#Stirling_cryocoolers" title="Stirling engine">Stirling cryocoolers</a></li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gas-absorption_refrigerator" title="Gas-absorption refrigerator">Gas-absorption refrigerator</a></li>

<li><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Vuilleumier_refrigeration&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Vuilleumier refrigeration (page does not exist)">Vuilleumier refrigeration</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Magnetic_refrigeration" title="Magnetic refrigeration">Magnetic refrigeration</a></li>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Evaporative_heat_engines">Evaporative heat engines</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Heat_engine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Evaporative heat engines">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Barton_evaporation_engine" title="Barton evaporation engine">Barton evaporation engine</a> is a heat engine based on a cycle producing power and cooled moist air from the evaporation of water into hot dry air.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mesoscopic_heat_engines">Mesoscopic heat engines</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Heat_engine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Mesoscopic heat engines">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Mesoscopic heat engines are nanoscale devices that may serve the goal of processing heat fluxes and perform useful work at small scales. Potential applications include e.g. electric cooling devices. In such mesoscopic heat engines, work per cycle of operation fluctuates due to thermal noise. There is exact equality that relates average of exponents of work performed by any heat engine and the heat transfer from the hotter heat bath.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sinitsyn-11jpa_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-sinitsyn-11jpa-11">[11]</a></sup> This relation transforms the Carnot's inequality into exact equality.</p>

<p>The reasoning behind this being the <b>maximal</b> efficiency goes as follows. It is first assumed that if a more efficient heat engine than a Carnot engine is possible, then it could be driven in reverse as a heat pump. Mathematical analysis can be used to show that this assumed combination would result in a net decrease in <a href="/wiki/Entropy" title="Entropy">entropy</a>. Since, by the <a href="/wiki/Second_law_of_thermodynamics" title="Second law of thermodynamics">second law of thermodynamics</a>, this is statistically improbable to the point of exclusion, the Carnot efficiency is a theoretical upper bound on the reliable efficiency of <i>any</i> process.</p>
<p>Empirically, no heat engine has ever been shown to run at a greater efficiency than a Carnot cycle heat engine.</p>

<p>The most Carnot efficiency as a criterion of heat engine performance is the fact that by its nature, any maximally efficient Carnot cycle must operate at an infinitesimal temperature gradient. This is because <i>any</i> transfer of heat between two bodies at differing temperatures is irreversible, and therefore the Carnot efficiency expression only applies in the infinitesimal limit. The major problem with that is that the object of most heat engines is to output some sort of power, and infinitesimal power is usually not what is being sought.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Heat_engine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Timeline_of_heat_engine_technology" title="Timeline of heat engine technology">Timeline of heat engine technology</a></div>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/History_of_the_internal_combustion_engine" title="History of the internal combustion engine">History of the internal combustion engine</a> and <a href="/wiki/History_of_thermodynamics" title="History of thermodynamics">History of thermodynamics</a></div>
<p>Heat engines have been known since antiquity but were only made into useful devices at the time of the industrial revolution in the 18th century. They continue to be developed today.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Heat_engine_enhancements">Heat engine enhancements</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Heat_engine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Heat engine enhancements">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Engineers have studied the various heat engine cycles extensively in effort to improve the amount of usable work they could extract from a given power source. The Carnot cycle limit cannot be reached with any gas-based cycle, but engineers have worked out at least two ways to possibly go around that limit, and one way to get better efficiency without bending any rules.</p>
<ol>
<li>Increase the temperature difference in the heat engine. The simplest way to do this is to increase the hot side temperature, which is the approach used in modern combined-cycle <a href="/wiki/Gas_turbine" title="Gas turbine">gas turbines</a>. Unfortunately, physical limits (such as the melting point of the materials used to build the engine) and environmental concerns regarding <a href="/wiki/NOx" title="NOx">NO<sub>x</sub></a> production restrict the maximum temperature on workable heat engines. Modern gas turbines run at temperatures as high as possible within the range of temperatures necessary to maintain acceptable NO<sub>x</sub> output<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>. Another way of increasing efficiency is to lower the output temperature. One new method of doing so is to use mixed chemical working fluids, and then exploit the changing behavior of the mixtures. One of the most famous is the so-called <a href="/wiki/Kalina_cycle" title="Kalina cycle">Kalina cycle</a>, which uses a 70/30 mix of <a href="/wiki/Ammonia" title="Ammonia">ammonia</a> and water as its working fluid. This mixture allows the cycle to generate useful power at considerably lower temperatures than most other processes.</li>
<li>Exploit the physical properties of the working fluid. The most common such exploitation is the use of water above the so-called critical point, or so-called supercritical steam. The behavior of fluids above their critical point changes radically, and with materials such as water and <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> it is possible to exploit those changes in behavior to extract greater thermodynamic efficiency from the heat engine, even if it is using a fairly conventional Brayton or Rankine cycle. A newer and very promising material for such applications is <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">CO<sub>2</sub></a>. <a href="/wiki/Sulfur_dioxide" title="Sulfur dioxide">SO<sub>2</sub></a> and <a href="/wiki/Xenon" title="Xenon">xenon</a> have also been considered for such applications, although SO<sub>2</sub> is toxic.</li>
<li>Exploit the chemical properties of the working fluid. A fairly new and novel exploit is to use exotic working fluids with advantageous chemical properties. One such is <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen_dioxide" title="Nitrogen dioxide">nitrogen dioxide</a> (NO<sub>2</sub>), a toxic component of smog, which has a natural <a href="/wiki/Dimer_(chemistry)" title="Dimer (chemistry)">dimer</a> as di-nitrogen tetraoxide (N<sub>2</sub>O<sub>4</sub>). At low temperature, the N<sub>2</sub>O<sub>4</sub> is compressed and then heated. The increasing temperature causes each N<sub>2</sub>O<sub>4</sub> to break apart into two NO<sub>2</sub> molecules. This lowers the molecular weight of the working fluid, which drastically increases the efficiency of the cycle. Once the NO<sub>2</sub> has expanded through the turbine, it is cooled by the <a href="/wiki/Heat_sink" title="Heat sink">heat sink</a>, which makes it recombine into N<sub>2</sub>O<sub>4</sub>. This is then fed back by the compressor for another cycle. Such species as <a href="/wiki/Aluminium_bromide" title="Aluminium bromide">aluminium bromide</a> (Al<sub>2</sub>Br<sub>6</sub>), NOCl, and Ga<sub>2</sub>I<sub>6</sub> have all been investigated for such uses. To date, their drawbacks have not warranted their use, despite the efficiency gains that can be realized.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></li>
</ol>
