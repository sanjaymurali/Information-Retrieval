<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Advection</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">
					Jump to:					<a href="#mw-head">navigation</a>, 					<a href="#p-search">search</a>
</div>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><p>In <a href="/wiki/Physics" title="Physics">physics</a>, <a href="/wiki/Engineering" title="Engineering">engineering</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Earth_science" title="Earth science">earth sciences</a>, <b>advection</b> is the <a href="/wiki/Transport" title="Transport">transport</a> of a substance by bulk motion. The properties of that substance are carried with it. Generally the majority of the advected substance is a fluid. The properties that are carried with the advected substance are <a href="/wiki/Conservation_of_energy" title="Conservation of energy">conserved</a> properties such as <a href="/wiki/Energy" title="Energy">energy</a>. An example of advection is the transport of <a href="/wiki/Pollutant" title="Pollutant">pollutants</a> or <a href="/wiki/Silt" title="Silt">silt</a> in a <a href="/wiki/River" title="River">river</a> by bulk water flow downstream. Another commonly advected quantity is energy or <a href="/wiki/Enthalpy" title="Enthalpy">enthalpy</a>. Here the fluid may be any material that contains thermal energy, such as <a href="/wiki/Water" title="Water">water</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Air" title="Air">air</a>. In general, any substance or conserved, <a href="/wiki/Intensive_and_extensive_properties" title="Intensive and extensive properties">extensive</a> quantity can be advected by a <a href="/wiki/Fluid" title="Fluid">fluid</a> that can hold or contain the quantity or substance.</p>
<p>During advection, a fluid transports some conserved quantity or material via bulk motion. The fluid's motion is described <a href="/wiki/Mathematics" title="Mathematics">mathematically</a> as a <a href="/wiki/Vector_field" title="Vector field">vector field</a>, and the transported material is described by a <a href="/wiki/Scalar_field" title="Scalar field">scalar field</a> showing its distribution over space. Advection requires currents in the fluid, and so cannot happen in rigid solids. It does not include transport of substances by <a href="/wiki/Molecular_diffusion" title="Molecular diffusion">molecular diffusion</a>.</p>
<p>Advection is sometimes confused with the more encompassing process of <a href="/wiki/Convection" title="Convection">convection</a> which is the combination of advective transport and diffusive transport.</p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Meteorology" title="Meteorology">meteorology</a> and <a href="/wiki/Physical_oceanography" title="Physical oceanography">physical oceanography</a>, advection often refers to the transport of some property of the atmosphere or <a href="/wiki/Ocean" title="Ocean">ocean</a>, such as <a href="/wiki/Heat" title="Heat">heat</a>, humidity (see <a href="/wiki/Water_vapor" title="Water vapor">moisture</a>) or <a href="/wiki/Salinity" title="Salinity">salinity</a>. Advection is important for the formation of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Orographic" title="Orographic">orographic</a> clouds and the precipitation of water from clouds, as part of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydrological_cycle" title="Hydrological cycle">hydrological cycle</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Distinction_between_advection_and_convection">Distinction between advection and convection</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Advection&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Distinction between advection and convection">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The term <i>advection</i> sometimes serves as a synonym for <i><a href="/wiki/Convection" title="Convection">convection</a></i>, but technically, <i>convection</i> covers the sum of transport both by <a href="/wiki/Diffusion" title="Diffusion">diffusion</a> and by advection. Advective transport describes the movement of some quantity via the bulk flow of a fluid (as in a river or pipeline).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Meteorology">Meteorology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Advection&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Meteorology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Meteorology" title="Meteorology">meteorology</a> and <a href="/wiki/Physical_oceanography" title="Physical oceanography">physical oceanography</a>, advection often refers to the horizontal transport of some property of the atmosphere or <a href="/wiki/Ocean" title="Ocean">ocean</a>, such as <a href="/wiki/Heat" title="Heat">heat</a>, humidity or salinity, and convection generally refers to vertical transport (vertical advection). Advection is important for the formation of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Orographic_cloud" title="Orographic cloud">orographic clouds</a> (terrain-forced convection) and the precipitation of water from clouds, as part of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydrological_cycle" title="Hydrological cycle">hydrological cycle</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_quantities">Other quantities</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Advection&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Other quantities">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The advection equation also applies if the quantity being advected is represented by a <a href="/wiki/Probability_density_function" title="Probability density function">probability density function</a> at each point, although accounting for diffusion is more difficult.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Mathematics_of_advection">Mathematics of advection</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Advection&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Mathematics of advection">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <b>advection equation</b> is the <a href="/wiki/Partial_differential_equation" title="Partial differential equation">partial differential equation</a> that governs the motion of a conserved <a href="/wiki/Scalar_field" title="Scalar field">scalar field</a> as it is advected by a known <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Velocity_field" title="Velocity field">velocity vector field</a>. It is derived using the scalar field's <a href="/wiki/Conservation_law" title="Conservation law">conservation law</a>, together with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gauss%27s_theorem" title="Gauss's theorem">Gauss's theorem</a>, and taking the <a href="/wiki/Infinitesimal" title="Infinitesimal">infinitesimal</a> limit.</p>
<p>One easily visualized example of advection is the transport of ink dumped into a river. As the river flows, ink will move downstream in a "pulse" via advection, as the water's movement itself transports the ink. If added to a lake without significant bulk water flow, the ink would simply disperse outwards from its source in a <a href="/wiki/Diffusion" title="Diffusion">diffusive</a> manner, which is not advection. Note that as it moves downstream, the "pulse" of ink will also spread via diffusion. The sum of these processes is called <a href="/wiki/Convection" title="Convection">convection</a>.</p>
