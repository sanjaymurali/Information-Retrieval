<p><b>Freezing rain</b> is the name given to <a href="/wiki/Rain" title="Rain">rain</a> that falls when surface temperatures are below <a href="/wiki/Melting_point" title="Melting point">freezing</a>. Unlike sleet, <a href="/wiki/Rain_and_snow_mixed" title="Rain and snow mixed">a mixture of rain and snow</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ice_pellets" title="Ice pellets">ice pellets</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Hail" title="Hail">hail</a>, freezing rain is made entirely of liquid droplets. The raindrops become <a href="/wiki/Supercooling" title="Supercooling">supercooled</a> while passing through a sub-freezing layer of air hundreds of meters above the ground, and then <a href="/wiki/Freezing" title="Freezing">freeze</a> upon impact with any surface they encounter, including the ground, trees, electrical wires, and automobiles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> The resulting ice, called <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Glaze_ice" title="Glaze ice">glaze</a>, can accumulate to a thickness of several centimeters and cover all exposed surfaces. The <a href="/wiki/METAR" title="METAR">METAR</a> code for freezing rain is <b>FZRA</b>.</p>
<p>A storm that produces a significant thickness of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Glaze_ice" title="Glaze ice">glaze ice</a> from freezing rain is often referred to as an <a href="/wiki/Ice_storm" title="Ice storm">ice storm</a>. Although these storms are not particularly violent, freezing rain is notorious for causing travel problems on roadways, breaking tree limbs, and downing <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Power_line" title="Power line">power lines</a> from the weight of accumulating ice. Downed power lines cause power outages in affected areas while accumulated ice can also pose significant overhead hazards. It is also known for being extremely dangerous to aircraft since the ice can effectively 'remould' the shape of the <a href="/wiki/Airfoil" title="Airfoil">airfoil</a> and flight <a href="/wiki/Flight_control_surfaces" title="Flight control surfaces">control surfaces</a>. (See <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_icing" title="Atmospheric icing">atmospheric icing</a>.)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Mechanism">Mechanism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Freezing_rain&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Mechanism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Ice_Storm_Chart.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="719" data-file-width="643" height="224" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e5/Ice_Storm_Chart.svg/200px-Ice_Storm_Chart.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e5/Ice_Storm_Chart.svg/300px-Ice_Storm_Chart.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e5/Ice_Storm_Chart.svg/400px-Ice_Storm_Chart.svg.png 2x" width="200"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Ice_Storm_Chart.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Temperature versus height diagram for different types of precipitation. The red line shows how freezing rain forms, from <a href="/wiki/Snow" title="Snow">snow</a> through the warm layer and then into the "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Supercooled" title="Supercooled">supercooled</a> stage."</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Freezing rain is often associated with the approach of a <a href="/wiki/Warm_front" title="Warm front">warm front</a>, when subfreezing air (temperatures at or below freezing) is trapped in the lowest levels of the atmosphere while warm air <a href="/wiki/Advection" title="Advection">advects</a> in aloft.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> This happens, for instance, when a low pressure system moves from the <a href="/wiki/Mississippi_River" title="Mississippi River">Mississippi River</a> Valley toward the <a href="/wiki/Appalachian_Mountains" title="Appalachian Mountains">Appalachian Mountains</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Saint_Lawrence_River" title="Saint Lawrence River">Saint Lawrence River</a> Valley of <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North America</a> during the cold season, with a strong high pressure system sitting further east. This setup is known as <a href="/wiki/Cold-air_damming" title="Cold-air damming">cold-air damming</a>, and is characterized by very cold and dry air at the surface within the region of high pressure. The warm air from the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_of_Mexico" title="Gulf of Mexico">Gulf of Mexico</a> is often the fuel for freezing precipitation.</p>
<p>Freezing rain develops when falling snow encounters a layer of warm air aloft, typically around the 800 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Millibar" title="Millibar">mbar</a> (800 <a href="/wiki/Pascal_(unit)" title="Pascal (unit)">hPa</a>) level, causing the snow to melt and become rain. As the rain continues to fall, it passes through a layer of subfreezing air just above the surface and cools to a temperature below freezing (0 °C or 32 °F). If this layer of subfreezing air is sufficiently deep, the raindrops may have time to freeze into <a href="/wiki/Ice_pellets" title="Ice pellets">ice pellets</a> (sleet) before reaching the ground. However, if the subfreezing layer of air at the surface is very shallow, the rain drops falling through it will not have time to freeze and they will hit the ground as <a href="/wiki/Supercooling" title="Supercooling">supercooled</a> rain. When these supercooled drops make contact with the ground, power lines, tree branches, aircraft, or anything else below 0 °C (32 °F), a portion of the drops instantly freezes, forming a thin film of ice, hence freezing rain.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Observations">Observations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Freezing_rain&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Observations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">

<p>Surface observations by manned or automatic stations are the only direct confirmation of freezing rain. One can never <i>see</i> directly freezing rain, rain or snow on <a href="/wiki/Weather_radar" title="Weather radar">weather radars</a>, <a href="/wiki/Doppler_effect" title="Doppler effect">Doppler</a> or conventional. However, it is possible to estimate the area covered by freezing rain with <a href="/wiki/Radar" title="Radar">radars</a> indirectly.</p>
<p>The intensity of the radar echoes (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Reflectivity" title="Reflectivity">reflectivity</a>) is proportional to the form (water or ice) of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Precipitation_(meteorology)" title="Precipitation (meteorology)">precipitation</a> and its diameter. In fact, rain has much stronger reflective power than snow but its diameter is much smaller. So the reflectivity of rain coming from melted snow is only slightly higher. However, in the layer where the snow is melting, the wet flakes still have a large diameter and are coated with water so the returns to the radar is much stronger.</p>
<p>The presence of this <a href="/wiki/Weather_radar#Bright_band" title="Weather radar">brightband</a> indicates that there is a warm layer above ground where snow melts. This could be producing rain on the ground or the possibility of freezing rain if the temperature is below freezing. This <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Artifact_(observational)" title="Artifact (observational)">artifact</a> can be located, like on the image at left, with a cross-section through radar data. The height and slope of the brightband will give clues to the extent of the region where melting occurs. Then it is possible to associate this clue with surface observations and numerical models prediction to produce output such as the ones seen on television weather programs that divide radar echoes into rain, mixed and snow precipitations.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="At_ground_level">At ground level</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Freezing_rain&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: At ground level">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Freezing rain often causes major power outages by forming <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Glaze_ice" title="Glaze ice">glaze ice</a>. When the freezing rain or drizzle is light and not prolonged, the ice formed is thin. It usually causes only minor damage, relieving trees of their dead branches etc.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> When large quantities accumulate, however, it is one of the most dangerous types of winter hazard.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> When the ice layer exceeds approximately 6.4 mm (0.25 in)<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>, tree limbs with branches heavily coated in ice can break off under the enormous weight and fall onto power lines. Windy conditions and <a href="/wiki/Lightning" title="Lightning">lightning</a>, when present, will exacerbate the damage. Power lines coated with ice become extremely heavy, causing support poles, insulators and lines to break. The ice that forms on roadways makes vehicle travel dangerous. Unlike snow, wet ice provides almost no traction, and vehicles will slide even on gentle slopes. Because freezing rain does not hit the ground as an ice pellet (called "sleet") but still as a rain droplet, it conforms to the shape of the ground, or object such as a tree branch or car. This makes one thick layer of <a href="/wiki/Ice" title="Ice">ice</a>, often called "glaze".</p>

<p>Freezing rain and glaze on a large scale is called an <a href="/wiki/Ice_storm" title="Ice storm">ice storm</a>. Effects on plants can be severe, as they cannot support the weight of the ice. Trees may snap as they are dormant and fragile during winter weather. Pine trees are also victims of ice storms as their needles will catch the ice, but not be able to support the weight. In February 1994, a severe ice storm caused over $1 billion in damage in the Southern United States, primarily in Mississippi, Tennessee, Alabama, and Western North Carolina, especially the Appalachians. One particularly severe ice storm struck eastern <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a> and northern parts of <a href="/wiki/New_York_(state)" title="New York (state)">New York</a> and <a href="/wiki/New_England" title="New England">New England</a> in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/North_American_ice_storm_of_1998" title="North American ice storm of 1998">North American ice storm of 1998</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Aircraft">Aircraft</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Freezing_rain&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Aircraft">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Freezing rain is considered to be an extreme hazard to aircraft, as it causes very rapid structural icing. Most helicopters and small airplanes lack the necessary <a href="/wiki/De-ice" title="De-ice">deicing</a> equipment to fly in freezing rain of any intensity, and heavy freezing rain can overwhelm even the most sophisticated deicing systems on large airplanes. Icing can increase an aircraft's weight but not typically enough to cause a hazard. The main danger comes from the ice changing the shape of its airfoils. This will reduce lift and increase drag. All three factors increase stalling speed and reduce aircraft performance, making it very difficult to climb or even maintain level altitude.</p>
<p>An aircraft can most easily evade freezing rain by moving into warmer air — under most conditions, this would require aircraft to descend, which it can usually do safely and easily even with a moderate accumulation of structural ice. However, freezing rain is accompanied by a <a href="/wiki/Inversion_(meteorology)" title="Inversion (meteorology)">temperature inversion</a> aloft, meaning that aircraft actually need to <i>climb</i> to move into warmer air — a potentially difficult and dangerous task with even a small amount of ice accumulation.</p>
<p>For example, in 1994, <a href="/wiki/American_Eagle_Flight_4184" title="American Eagle Flight 4184">American Eagle Flight 4184</a> encountered heavy air traffic and poor weather that postponed the arrival of this flight at <a href="/wiki/Chicago" title="Chicago">Chicago</a>'s <a href="/wiki/O%27Hare_International_Airport" title="O'Hare International Airport">O'Hare International Airport</a>, where it was to have landed en route from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Indianapolis,_Indiana" title="Indianapolis, Indiana">Indianapolis, Indiana</a>. The ATR-72, a twin-engine turboprop carrying 68 people, entered a holding pattern 105 km or 65 mi southeast of O'Hare. As the plane circled, supercooled cloud droplets, freezing rain or <a href="/wiki/Freezing_drizzle" title="Freezing drizzle">freezing drizzle</a> formed a ridge of ice on the upper surface of its wings, eventually causing the aircraft's autopilot to suddenly disconnect and the pilots to lose control. The ATR disintegrated on impact with a field below; all passengers and crew were killed.</p>
