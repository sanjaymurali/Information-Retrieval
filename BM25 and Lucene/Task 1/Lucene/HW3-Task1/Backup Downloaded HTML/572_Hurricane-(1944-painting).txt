<p><i><b>Hurricane</b></i> is a 1944 <a href="/wiki/Oil_painting" title="Oil painting">oil painting</a> by American artist <a href="/wiki/John_Marin" title="John Marin">John Marin</a>, located in the <a href="/wiki/Indianapolis_Museum_of_Art" title="Indianapolis Museum of Art">Indianapolis Museum of Art</a>, which is in <a href="/wiki/Indianapolis" title="Indianapolis">Indianapolis</a>, <a href="/wiki/Indiana" title="Indiana">Indiana</a>. He used quick brushstrokes and thickly daubed paint to depict a turbulent ocean such as he experienced near his summer home in <a href="/wiki/Maine" title="Maine">Maine</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lee_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-lee-1">[1]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hurricane_(1944_painting)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Although Marin is best known for his <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Watercolor" title="Watercolor">watercolors</a>, beginning in the 1930s he developed an interest in the more expressive brushwork and rugged quality of oil paint. Marin was influenced by <a href="/wiki/Cubism" title="Cubism">Cubism</a>, with its flattened spaces and angularity. He honed a style of frenetic brushwork to create this dynamic image of water, wind, and clouds. He was so successful in his recreation of nature that a sailor purportedly declared it the first time he had "really seen a sea painted as the sea really is."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lee_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-lee-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Historical_information">Historical information</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hurricane_(1944_painting)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Historical information">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><i>Hurricane</i> was painted the same year the <a href="/wiki/1944_Great_Atlantic_hurricane" title="1944 Great Atlantic hurricane">Great Atlantic hurricane</a> smashed into Maine.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-green_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-green-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Location_history">Location history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hurricane_(1944_painting)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Location history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><i>Hurricane</i> was first displayed in 1944 in the famous <a href="/wiki/Alfred_Stieglitz#The_Intimate_Gallery_and_An_American_Place_.281925.E2.80.931937.29" title="Alfred Stieglitz">An American Place</a> gallery. It was borrowed from the IMA to be part of an 1987 retrospective of Marin's work.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> In 2011, <i>Hurricane</i> was loaned out again for a traveling exhibit called "John Marin: Modernism at Midcentury," of which it was a highlight.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> This exhibition took it to the <a href="/wiki/Portland_Museum_of_Art" title="Portland Museum of Art">Portland Museum of Art</a> in <a href="/wiki/Portland,_Maine" title="Portland, Maine">Portland, Maine</a>; the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Amon_Carter_Museum" title="Amon Carter Museum">Amon Carter Museum</a> in <a href="/wiki/Fort_Worth,_Texas" title="Fort Worth, Texas">Fort Worth, Texas</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Addison_Gallery_of_American_Art" title="Addison Gallery of American Art">Addison Gallery of American Art</a> in <a href="/wiki/Andover,_Massachusetts" title="Andover, Massachusetts">Andover, Massachusetts</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Acquisition">Acquisition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hurricane_(1944_painting)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Acquisition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Mrs. Caroline Marmon Fesler acquired <i>Hurricane</i> the same year it was painted, from Marin's long-time friend and art dealer, <a href="/wiki/Alfred_Stieglitz" title="Alfred Stieglitz">Alfred Stieglitz</a>. He congratulated Mrs. Fesler on her purchase, writing "The <i>Hurricane</i> is certainly a masterpiece, Every one agrees with every one else as to that." It was donated to the IMA as part of her estate in 1961, and was given the accession number 61.42.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ima_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-ima-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
