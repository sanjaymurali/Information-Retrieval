<p>The <b>1933 Tampico hurricane</b> was one of two storms in the <a href="/wiki/1933_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="1933 Atlantic hurricane season">1933 Atlantic hurricane season</a> to reach <a href="/wiki/List_of_Category_5_Atlantic_hurricanes" title="List of Category 5 Atlantic hurricanes">Category 5</a> intensity on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saffir-Simpson_Hurricane_Scale" title="Saffir-Simpson Hurricane Scale">Saffir-Simpson Hurricane Scale</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[nb 1]</a></sup> It developed on September 16 near the <a href="/wiki/Lesser_Antilles" title="Lesser Antilles">Lesser Antilles</a>, and slowly intensified while moving across the <a href="/wiki/Caribbean_Sea" title="Caribbean Sea">Caribbean Sea</a>. Becoming a hurricane on September 19, its strengthening rate increased while passing south of <a href="/wiki/Jamaica" title="Jamaica">Jamaica</a>. Two days later, the hurricane reached peak winds, estimated at 160 mph (260 km/h). After weakening, it made <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Landfall_(meteorology)" title="Landfall (meteorology)">landfall</a> on the <a href="/wiki/Yucat%C3%A1n_Peninsula" title="Yucatán Peninsula">Yucatán Peninsula</a>, destroying several houses. One person was killed offshore <a href="/wiki/Progreso,_Yucat%C3%A1n" title="Progreso, Yucatán">Progreso, Yucatán</a> during the storm.</p>
<p>Over land, the hurricane weakened to a tropical storm, although it re-intensified slightly in the Gulf of Mexico. On September 25, it made a second landfall just south of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tampico,_Tamaulipas" title="Tampico, Tamaulipas">Tampico, Tamaulipas</a> with winds at around 110 mph (180 km/h), and it quickly dissipated over land. Damage was heaviest there, estimated at $5 million (1933 <a href="/wiki/United_States_dollar" title="United States dollar">USD</a>) and there were hundreds of deaths. About 75% of the houses in Tampico were damaged, including about 50% of houses that had severe to total destruction to their roofs. The destruction prompted the declaration of <a href="/wiki/Martial_law" title="Martial law">martial law</a>, and there was a curfew instated.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Meteorological_history">Meteorological history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=1933_Tampico_hurricane&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Meteorological history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:1933_Atlantic_hurricane_15_track.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="633" data-file-width="1024" height="136" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a7/1933_Atlantic_hurricane_15_track.png/220px-1933_Atlantic_hurricane_15_track.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a7/1933_Atlantic_hurricane_15_track.png/330px-1933_Atlantic_hurricane_15_track.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a7/1933_Atlantic_hurricane_15_track.png/440px-1933_Atlantic_hurricane_15_track.png 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:1933_Atlantic_hurricane_15_track.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Map plotting the track and intensity of the storm, according to the <a href="/wiki/Saffir%E2%80%93Simpson_scale" title="Saffir–Simpson scale">Saffir–Simpson scale</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>A tropical disturbance moved westward across the tropical Atlantic Ocean in mid-September. On September 16, it was estimated that a tropical depression <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclogenesis" title="Tropical cyclogenesis">developed</a> about 185 mi (300 km) east-northeast of <a href="/wiki/Tobago" title="Tobago">Tobago</a>. It moved west-northwestward through the <a href="/wiki/Lesser_Antilles" title="Lesser Antilles">Lesser Antilles</a>, passing about 14 mi (22 km) south of <a href="/wiki/Grenada" title="Grenada">Grenada</a>; however, the system was very weak, and the island reported east winds of only 12 mph (19 km/h). After entering the <a href="/wiki/Caribbean_Sea" title="Caribbean Sea">Caribbean Sea</a>, the depression intensified into a tropical storm on September 18, and a day later it became a hurricane. On September 20 it passed to the south of Jamaica while quickly strengthening. At 0000 <a href="/wiki/Coordinated_Universal_Time" title="Coordinated Universal Time">UTC</a> on September 21, a ship in the <a href="/wiki/Eye_(cyclone)" title="Eye (cyclone)">eye</a> reported a <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_pressure" title="Atmospheric pressure">barometric pressure</a> of 929 mbar (27.4 inHg). Ordinarily, the pressure reading would suggest winds of 153 mph (246 km/h); however, observations from the ship indicated the hurricane was smaller than normal, with a <a href="/wiki/Radius_of_maximum_wind" title="Radius of maximum wind">radius of maximum winds</a> of 8 mi (13 km). As a result, the peak winds were estimated at 160 mph (260 km/h), or a <a href="/wiki/List_of_Category_5_Atlantic_hurricanes" title="List of Category 5 Atlantic hurricanes">Category 5</a> on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saffir-Simpson_Hurricane_Scale" title="Saffir-Simpson Hurricane Scale">Saffir-Simpson Hurricane Scale</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-meta_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-meta-4">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>After peaking in intensity, the hurricane continued to the northwest. Early on September 22, it passed about 50 mi (80 km)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[nb 2]</a></sup> south of <a href="/wiki/Cozumel" title="Cozumel">Cozumel</a> before making <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Landfall_(meteorology)" title="Landfall (meteorology)">landfall</a> on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Yucat%C3%A1n_peninsula" title="Yucatán peninsula">Yucatán peninsula</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-meta_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-meta-4">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-5">[4]</a></sup> Its landfall intensity was unknown; the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atlantic_hurricane_reanalysis" title="Atlantic hurricane reanalysis">Atlantic hurricane reanalysis</a> in 2012 suggested that the storm could have retained Category 5 status until landfall, although local observations did not validate the theory. In the original analysis of the season, the landfall intensity was estimated at 105 mph (165 km/h). As a compromise, the 2012 reanalysis estimated the hurricane struck land with winds of 140 mph (230 km/h). The cyclone rapidly weakened while crossing the Yucatán peninsula, and on September 23 it emerged into the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_of_Mexico" title="Gulf of Mexico">Gulf of Mexico</a> with winds of 65 mph (105 km/h). It quickly reintensified into a hurricane while moving toward the northeast Mexican coastline. At around 0000 UTC on September 25, the hurricane made its final landfall just south of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tampico,_Tamaulipas" title="Tampico, Tamaulipas">Tampico, Tamaulipas</a> with winds of 110 mph (180 km/h); this was based on a ship in the eye reporting a pressure of 960 mbar (28 inHg). Within 12 hours, the storm dissipated over land.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-meta_4-3"><a href="#cite_note-meta-4">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Impact">Impact</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=1933_Tampico_hurricane&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Impact">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>While passing south of Jamaica, the hurricane produced high surf in <a href="/wiki/Kingston,_Jamaica" title="Kingston, Jamaica">Kingston</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-5">[4]</a></sup> Heavy rains from the storm affected much of the island, which disrupted travel and caused one mudslide.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[5]</a></sup> In Cozumel offshore the Yucatán Peninsula, winds reached 76 mph (122 km/h) from the southeast.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-meta_4-4"><a href="#cite_note-meta-4">[3]</a></sup> There, the hurricane destroyed a fishing pier and several houses.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-eve_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-eve-8">[6]</a></sup> While the storm crossed the peninsula, <a href="/wiki/Progreso,_Yucat%C3%A1n" title="Progreso, Yucatán">Progreso, Yucatán</a> reported east winds of 55 mph (89 km/h).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-meta_4-5"><a href="#cite_note-meta-4">[3]</a></sup> Offshore Progreso, the storm capsized a boat, killing one person.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-eve_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-eve-8">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>Before the hurricane made its final landfall, the threat of heavy rainfall prompted evacuations around Tampico,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-eve_8-2"><a href="#cite_note-eve-8">[6]</a></sup> just 10 days after another hurricane hit the same region.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_5-3"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-5">[4]</a></sup> Near Tampico, a ship reported winds of 81 mph (130 km/h), and a station in Tampico recorded winds of 53 mph (85 km/h).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-meta_4-6"><a href="#cite_note-meta-4">[3]</a></sup> High winds cut power lines and blew off or heavily damaged the roofs of half of the city's houses.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-apa927_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-apa927-9">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mont_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-mont-10">[8]</a></sup> A high accompanying <a href="/wiki/Storm_surge" title="Storm surge">storm surge</a> washed away many people from their homes,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-apa927_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-apa927-9">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nature_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-nature-11">[9]</a></sup> and several barges were washed away.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mont_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-mont-10">[8]</a></sup> The harbor was filled with debris after the storm, rendering it unusable.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ellen_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-ellen-12">[10]</a></sup> Portions of the city were flooded up to 15 ft (4.6 m) deep.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-up925_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-up925-13">[11]</a></sup> According to news reports, the hurricane damaged about 75% of Tampico.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-apa927_9-2"><a href="#cite_note-apa927-9">[7]</a></sup> Majority of the damage was to poorly constructed houses,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mont_10-2"><a href="#cite_note-mont-10">[8]</a></sup> although a large hospital in Tampico was also destroyed,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pain_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-pain-14">[12]</a></sup> killing 87 people.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-calg_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-calg-15">[13]</a></sup> Roads were blocked throughout the city, and the river was closed to boating.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mont_10-3"><a href="#cite_note-mont-10">[8]</a></sup> The rail line was impacted, which disrupted relief supplies from reaching the region.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ellen_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-ellen-12">[10]</a></sup> Outside of the city, the hurricane increased levels along the <a href="/wiki/P%C3%A1nuco_River" title="Pánuco River">Pánuco</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tames%C3%AD_River" title="Tamesí River">Tamesí</a> rivers,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-up925_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-up925-13">[11]</a></sup> which remained above flood stage for several days.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-port_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-port-16">[14]</a></sup> West of Tampico, the hurricane flooded the entire city of <a href="/wiki/C%C3%A1rdenas,_San_Luis_Potos%C3%AD" title="Cárdenas, San Luis Potosí">Cárdenas, San Luis Potosí</a>, killing 20 and injuring 200 people.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mont_10-4"><a href="#cite_note-mont-10">[8]</a></sup> In <a href="/wiki/P%C3%A1nuco,_Veracruz" title="Pánuco, Veracruz">Pánuco, Veracruz</a>, about 5,000 people were left homeless.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ellen_12-2"><a href="#cite_note-ellen-12">[10]</a></sup> In <a href="/wiki/San_Luis_Potos%C3%AD" title="San Luis Potosí">San Luis Potosí</a> state, 30 people were killed when a dam burst. Heavy rainfall in <a href="/wiki/Monterrey" title="Monterrey">Monterrey</a> caused rivers to flood. Damage spread as far as the west coast of Mexico.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pain_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-pain-14">[12]</a></sup> Throughout the country, air travel was disrupted, and several train lines were washed out,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mont_10-5"><a href="#cite_note-mont-10">[8]</a></sup> leaving three trains missing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pain_14-2"><a href="#cite_note-pain-14">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>Initial news reports suggested a death toll of up to 5,000 people, and the storm was considered "the greatest disaster in recent Mexican history".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-apa927_9-3"><a href="#cite_note-apa927-9">[7]</a></sup> Two days after the storm dissipated, the death toll was set at 54, with 850 people injured and potentially thousands that were buried.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ellen_12-3"><a href="#cite_note-ellen-12">[10]</a></sup> Search and rescue teams dug through the debris of the Tampico to find survivors and victims.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[15]</a></sup> In 1997, the <a href="/wiki/National_Hurricane_Center" title="National Hurricane Center">National Hurricane Center</a> listed the death toll between 184–200,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[16]</a></sup> and damage was estimated at $5 million (1933 USD).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nature_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-nature-11">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the aftermath of the storm, food and medical supplies rapidly diminished.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ellen_12-4"><a href="#cite_note-ellen-12">[10]</a></sup> After the storm dissipated, doctors and nurses traveled to the city to assist in the aftermath, while trains carried food and water.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-apa927_9-4"><a href="#cite_note-apa927-9">[7]</a></sup> A train from Monterrey to Tampico carried food, medicine, and soldiers to assist in rebuilding, but was delayed by ongoing flooding.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-port_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-port-16">[14]</a></sup> Military planes were utilized to transport aid.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-calg_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-calg-15">[13]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Martial_law" title="Martial law">Martial law</a> was declared in Tampico following the storm, and officials imposed a 7 p.m. curfew. Uninjured citizens helped clear the roads. President <a href="/wiki/Abelardo_L._Rodr%C3%ADguez" title="Abelardo L. Rodríguez">Abelardo L. Rodríguez</a> asked the governors of the Mexican states to send aid and for residents to send money to the <a href="/wiki/Bank_of_Mexico" title="Bank of Mexico">Bank of Mexico</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mont_10-6"><a href="#cite_note-mont-10">[8]</a></sup> Following the storm, residents overcrowded the buildings that remained standing in Tampico.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[17]</a></sup></p>
