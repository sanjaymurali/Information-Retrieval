<p><b>Space Shuttle <i>Atlantis</i></b> (<a href="/wiki/Orbiter_Vehicle_Designation" title="Orbiter Vehicle Designation">Orbiter Vehicle Designation</a>: <b>OV‑104</b>) is a <a href="/wiki/Space_Shuttle_orbiter" title="Space Shuttle orbiter">Space Shuttle orbiter</a> belonging to the <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">National Aeronautics and Space Administration</a> (NASA), the <a href="/wiki/Spaceflight" title="Spaceflight">spaceflight</a> and <a href="/wiki/Space_exploration" title="Space exploration">space exploration</a> agency of the United States.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ov104_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-ov104-2">[2]</a></sup> Constructed by the <a href="/wiki/Rockwell_International" title="Rockwell International">Rockwell International</a> company in <a href="/wiki/Southern_California" title="Southern California">Southern California</a> and delivered to the <a href="/wiki/Kennedy_Space_Center" title="Kennedy Space Center">Kennedy Space Center</a> in Eastern <a href="/wiki/Florida" title="Florida">Florida</a> in April 1985, <i>Atlantis</i> is the fourth operational and the second-to-last Space Shuttle built.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Its maiden flight was <a href="/wiki/STS-51-J" title="STS-51-J">STS-51-J</a> from 3 to 7 October 1985.</p>
<p><i>Atlantis</i> embarked on its 33rd and final mission, also the final mission of a space shuttle, <a href="/wiki/STS-135" title="STS-135">STS-135</a>, on 8 July 2011. <a href="/wiki/STS-134" title="STS-134">STS-134</a> by <a href="/wiki/Space_Shuttle_Endeavour" title="Space Shuttle Endeavour"><i>Endeavour</i></a> was expected to be the final flight before STS-135 was authorized in October 2010. STS-135 took advantage of the processing for the STS-335 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Launch_On_Need" title="Launch On Need">Launch On Need</a> mission that would have been necessary if STS-134's crew became stranded in orbit.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> <i>Atlantis</i> landed for the final time at the Kennedy Space Center on 21 July 2011.</p>
<p>By the end of its final mission, <i>Atlantis</i> had orbited the <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a> a total of 4,848 times, traveling nearly 126,000,000 mi (203,000,000 km) or more than 525 times the distance from the Earth to the <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">Moon</a>.</p>
<p><i>Atlantis</i> is named after <a href="/wiki/RV_Atlantis" title="RV Atlantis">RV <i>Atlantis</i></a>, a two-masted sailing ship that operated as the primary research vessel for the <a href="/wiki/Woods_Hole_Oceanographic_Institution" title="Woods Hole Oceanographic Institution">Woods Hole Oceanographic Institution</a> from 1930 to 1966.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>

<ul>
<li>Weight (with three shuttle main engines): 151,315 pounds (69 t)</li>

<li><i>Atlantis</i> was completed in about half the time it took to build <a href="/wiki/Space_Shuttle_Columbia" title="Space Shuttle Columbia">Space Shuttle <i>Columbia</i></a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Atlantis_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Atlantis-8">[8]</a></sup></li>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Notable_missions">Notable missions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Space_Shuttle_Atlantis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Notable missions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:STS-132_Atlantis_at_ISS_1.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2847" data-file-width="4288" height="146" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e8/STS-132_Atlantis_at_ISS_1.jpg/220px-STS-132_Atlantis_at_ISS_1.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e8/STS-132_Atlantis_at_ISS_1.jpg/330px-STS-132_Atlantis_at_ISS_1.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e8/STS-132_Atlantis_at_ISS_1.jpg/440px-STS-132_Atlantis_at_ISS_1.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:STS-132_Atlantis_at_ISS_1.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<i>Atlantis</i> docked to the International Space Station during STS-132 mission.</div>

<p>Space Shuttle <i>Atlantis</i> lifted off on its maiden voyage on 3 October 1985, on mission <a href="/wiki/STS-51-J" title="STS-51-J">STS-51-J</a>, the second dedicated Department of Defense flight.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> It flew one other mission, <a href="/wiki/STS-61-B" title="STS-61-B">STS-61-B</a>, the second night launch in the shuttle program, before the <a href="/wiki/Space_Shuttle_Challenger_disaster" title="Space Shuttle Challenger disaster">Space Shuttle <i>Challenger</i> disaster</a> temporarily grounded the shuttle fleet in 1986. Among the five Space Shuttles flown into space, <i>Atlantis</i> conducted a subsequent mission in the shortest time after the previous mission (turnaround time) when it launched in November 1985 on STS-61-B, only 50 days after its previous mission, STS-51-J in October 1985. <i>Atlantis</i> was then used for ten flights between 1988 and 1992. Two of these, both flown in 1989, deployed the planetary probes <i><a href="/wiki/Magellan_(spacecraft)" title="Magellan (spacecraft)">Magellan</a></i> to <a href="/wiki/Venus" title="Venus">Venus</a> (on <a href="/wiki/STS-30" title="STS-30">STS-30</a>) and <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Galileo_probe" title="Galileo probe">Galileo</a></i> to <a href="/wiki/Jupiter" title="Jupiter">Jupiter</a> (on <a href="/wiki/STS-34" title="STS-34">STS-34</a>). With STS-30 <i>Atlantis</i> became the first shuttle to launch an interplanetary probe.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>During another mission, <a href="/wiki/STS-37" title="STS-37">STS-37</a> flown in 1991, <i>Atlantis</i> deployed the <a href="/wiki/Compton_Gamma_Ray_Observatory" title="Compton Gamma Ray Observatory">Compton Gamma Ray Observatory</a>. Beginning in 1995 with <a href="/wiki/STS-71" title="STS-71">STS-71</a>, <i>Atlantis</i> made seven straight flights to the former <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russian</a> <a href="/wiki/Space_station" title="Space station">space station</a> <a href="/wiki/Mir" title="Mir">Mir</a> as part of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shuttle-Mir_Program" title="Shuttle-Mir Program">Shuttle-Mir Program</a>. STS-71 marked a number of firsts in human spaceflight: 100th U.S. manned space flight; first U.S. shuttle-Russian Space Station Mir docking and joint on-orbit operations; and first on-orbit changeout of shuttle crew.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> When linked, <i>Atlantis</i> and <i>Mir</i> together formed the largest spacecraft in orbit at the time.</p>

<p>Shuttle <i>Atlantis</i> also delivered several vital components for the construction of the <a href="/wiki/International_Space_Station" title="International Space Station">International Space Station</a> (ISS). During the February 2001 mission <a href="/wiki/STS-98" title="STS-98">STS-98</a> to the ISS, <i>Atlantis</i> delivered the <a href="/wiki/Destiny_(ISS_module)" title="Destiny (ISS module)">Destiny Module</a>, the primary operating facility for U.S. research payloads aboard the ISS.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> The five hour 25 minute third spacewalk performed by astronauts <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Robert_L._Curbeam" title="Robert L. Curbeam">Robert Curbeam</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thomas_D._Jones" title="Thomas D. Jones">Thomas Jones</a> during STS-98 marked NASA's 100th extra vehicular activity in space.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Quest_Joint_Airlock" title="Quest Joint Airlock">Quest Joint Airlock</a>, was flown and installed to the ISS by <i>Atlantis</i> during the mission <a href="/wiki/STS-104" title="STS-104">STS-104</a> in July 2001.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> The successful installation of the airlock gave on-board space station crews the ability to stage repair and maintenance spacewalks outside the ISS using U.S. <a href="/wiki/Extravehicular_Mobility_Unit" title="Extravehicular Mobility Unit">EMU</a> or Russian <a href="/wiki/Orlan_space_suit" title="Orlan space suit">Orlan</a> space suits. The first mission flown by <i>Atlantis</i> after the <a href="/wiki/Space_Shuttle_Columbia_disaster" title="Space Shuttle Columbia disaster">Space Shuttle Columbia disaster</a> was <a href="/wiki/STS-115" title="STS-115">STS-115</a>, conducted during September 2006.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> The mission carried the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/ISS_Truss" title="ISS Truss">P3/P4 truss</a> segments and solar arrays to the ISS. On ISS assembly flight <a href="/wiki/STS-122" title="STS-122">STS-122</a> in February 2008, <i>Atlantis</i> delivered the <a href="/wiki/Columbus_(ISS_module)" title="Columbus (ISS module)">Columbus laboratory</a> to the ISS.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> Columbus laboratory is the largest single contribution to the ISS made by the <a href="/wiki/European_Space_Agency" title="European Space Agency">European Space Agency</a> (ESA).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>

<p>In May 2009 <i>Atlantis</i> flew a seven-member crew to the <a href="/wiki/Hubble_Space_Telescope" title="Hubble Space Telescope">Hubble Space Telescope</a> for its Servicing Mission 4, <a href="/wiki/STS-125" title="STS-125">STS-125</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> The mission was a success, with the crew completing five space walks totaling 37 hours to install new cameras, batteries, a gyroscope and other components to the telescope. This was the final mission not to the <a href="/wiki/International_Space_Station" title="International Space Station">ISS</a>.</p>
<p>The longest mission flown using <i>Atlantis</i> was <a href="/wiki/STS-117" title="STS-117">STS-117</a> which lasted almost 14 days in June 2007.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> During STS-117, Atlantis' crew added a new starboard truss segment and solar array pair (the <a href="/wiki/Integrated_Truss_Structure" title="Integrated Truss Structure">S3/S4</a> truss), folded the P6 array in preparation for its relocation and performed four spacewalks. <i>Atlantis</i> was not equipped to take advantage of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Station-to-Shuttle_Power_Transfer_System" title="Station-to-Shuttle Power Transfer System">Station-to-Shuttle Power Transfer System</a> so missions could not be extended by making use of power provided by ISS.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>During the <a href="/wiki/STS-129" title="STS-129">STS-129</a> post-flight interview on 16 November 2009 shuttle launch director Mike Leinbach said that <i>Atlantis</i> officially beat shuttle <i>Discovery</i> on the record low amount of Interim Problem Reports, with a total of just 54 listed since returning from the STS-125. He continued to add "It is due to the team and the hardware processing. They just did a great job. The record will probably never be broken again in the history of the Space Shuttle Program, so congratulations to them". During the <a href="/wiki/STS-132" title="STS-132">STS-132</a> post-launch interview on 14 May 2010, shuttle launch director Mike Leinbach said that <i>Atlantis</i> beat its previous record low amount of Interim Problem Reports, with a total of 46 listed between STS-129 and STS-132.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Orbiter_Maintenance_Down_Periods">Orbiter Maintenance Down Periods</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Space_Shuttle_Atlantis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Orbiter Maintenance Down Periods">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><i>Atlantis</i> went through two overhauls of scheduled Orbiter Maintenance Down Periods (OMDPs) during its operational history.</p>
<p><i>Atlantis</i> arrived at <a href="/wiki/Palmdale,_California" title="Palmdale, California">Palmdale, California</a> in October 1992 for OMDP-1. During that visit 165 modifications were made over the next 20 months. These included the installation of a drag chute, new plumbing lines to configure the orbiter for extended duration, more than 800 new heat tiles and blankets and new insulation for main landing gear and structural modifications to the airframe.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p>On 5 November 1997, <i>Atlantis</i> again arrived at Palmdale for OMDP-2 which was completed on 24 September 1998. The 130 modifications carried out during OMDP-2 included glass cockpit displays, replacement of TACAN navigation with GPS and ISS airlock and docking installation. Several weight reduction modifications were also performed on the orbiter including replacement of Advanced Flexible Reusable Surface Insulation (AFRSI) insulation blankets<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> on upper surfaces with FRSI. Lightweight crew seats were installed and the <a href="/wiki/Extended_Duration_Orbiter" title="Extended Duration Orbiter">Extended Duration Orbiter</a> (EDO) package installed on OMDP-1 was removed to lighten <i>Atlantis</i> to better serve its prime mission of servicing the ISS.</p>
<p>During the stand down period post <i>Columbia</i> accident, <i>Atlantis</i> went through over 75 modifications to the orbiter ranging from very minor bolt change-outs to window change-outs and different fluid systems.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p>Atlantis was known among the shuttle workforce as being more prone than the others in the fleet to problems that needed to be addressed while readying the vehicle for launch leading to some nicknaming it "<a href="/wiki/Britney_Spears" title="Britney Spears">Britney</a>".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Decommissioning">Decommissioning</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Space_Shuttle_Atlantis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Decommissioning">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Space_Shuttle_retirement" title="Space Shuttle retirement">Space Shuttle retirement</a></div>

<p>NASA initially planned to withdraw <i>Atlantis</i> from service in 2008, as the orbiter would have been due to undergo its third scheduled OMDP. However, because of the timescale of the final retirement of the shuttle fleet, this was deemed uneconomical. It was planned that <i>Atlantis</i> would be kept in near-flight condition to be used as a <a href="/wiki/Cannibalization_(parts)" title="Cannibalization (parts)">parts hulk</a> for <i>Discovery</i> and <i>Endeavour</i>. However, with the significant planned flight schedule up to 2010, the decision was taken to extend the time between OMDPs, allowing <i>Atlantis</i> to be retained for operations. <i>Atlantis</i> was subsequently swapped for one flight of each <i>Discovery</i> and <i>Endeavour</i> in the flight manifest. <i>Atlantis</i> had completed what was meant to be its last flight, <a href="/wiki/STS-132" title="STS-132">STS-132</a>, prior to the end of the shuttle program,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> but the extension of the Shuttle program into 2011 led to <i>Atlantis</i> being selected for <a href="/wiki/STS-135" title="STS-135">STS-135</a>, the final Space Shuttle mission in July 2011.</p>

<p><i>Atlantis</i> is currently displayed at the <a href="/wiki/Kennedy_Space_Center_Visitor_Complex" title="Kennedy Space Center Visitor Complex">Kennedy Space Center Visitor Complex</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> NASA Administrator <a href="/wiki/Charles_Bolden" title="Charles Bolden">Charles Bolden</a> announced the decision at an employee event held on 12 April 2011 to commemorate the 30th anniversary of the <a href="/wiki/STS-1" title="STS-1">first shuttle flight</a>: "First, here at the Kennedy Space Center where every shuttle mission and so many other historic human space flights have originated, we'll showcase my old friend, <i>Atlantis</i>."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ares3_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-ares3-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Visitor Complex displays <i>Atlantis</i> suspended with its payload bay doors opened such that it appears to be back in orbit around the Earth. A multi-story digital projection of Earth rotates behind the orbiter in a 64,000-square-foot (5,900 m<sup>2</sup>) indoor facility.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> Ground breaking of the facility occurred in 2012.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> The exhibit opened on 29 June 2013.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Crews">Crews</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Space_Shuttle_Atlantis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Crews">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:STS125_crew_portrait1.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2841" data-file-width="4288" height="146" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c5/STS125_crew_portrait1.jpg/220px-STS125_crew_portrait1.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c5/STS125_crew_portrait1.jpg/330px-STS125_crew_portrait1.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c5/STS125_crew_portrait1.jpg/440px-STS125_crew_portrait1.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>

<p>A total of 156 individuals flew with Space Shuttle <i>Atlantis</i> over the course of its 33 missions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Atlantis_Legacy_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-Atlantis_Legacy-33">[33]</a></sup> Because the shuttle sometimes flew crew members arriving and departing Mir and the ISS, not all of them launched and landed on <i>Atlantis</i>.</p>
<p>Astronaut <a href="/wiki/Clayton_Anderson" title="Clayton Anderson">Clayton Anderson</a>, ESA astronaut <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Leopold_Eyharts" title="Leopold Eyharts">Leopold Eyharts</a> and Russian cosmonauts <a href="/wiki/Nikolai_Budarin" title="Nikolai Budarin">Nikolai Budarin</a> and <a href="/wiki/Anatoly_Solovyev" title="Anatoly Solovyev">Anatoly Solovyev</a> only launched on <i>Atlantis</i>. Similarly, astronauts <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Daniel_Tani" title="Daniel Tani">Daniel Tani</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sunita_Williams" title="Sunita Williams">Sunita Williams</a>, as well as cosmonauts <a href="/wiki/Vladimir_Dezhurov" title="Vladimir Dezhurov">Vladimir Dezhurov</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gennady_Strekalov" title="Gennady Strekalov">Gennady Strekalov</a> only landed with <i>Atlantis</i>. Only 146 men and women both launched and landed aboard <i>Atlantis</i>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Some of those people flew with <i>Atlantis</i> more than once. Taking them into account, 203 total seats were filled over <i>Atlantis</i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">'</span> 33 missions. Astronaut <a href="/wiki/Jerry_L._Ross" title="Jerry L. Ross">Jerry Ross</a> holds the record for the most flights aboard <i>Atlantis</i> at five.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Atlantis_Legacy_33-1"><a href="#cite_note-Atlantis_Legacy-33">[33]</a></sup></p>
<p>Astronaut <a href="/wiki/Rodolfo_Neri_Vela" title="Rodolfo Neri Vela">Rodolfo Neri Vela</a> who flew aboard Atlantis on STS-61-B mission in 1985 became the first and so far only Mexican to have traveled to space. ESA astronaut <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dirk_D._Frimout" title="Dirk D. Frimout">Dirk Frimout</a> who flew on STS-45 as a payload specialist was the first Belgian in space. STS-46 mission specialist <a href="/wiki/Claude_Nicollier" title="Claude Nicollier">Claude Nicollier</a> was the first astronaut from Switzerland. On the same flight, astronaut <a href="/wiki/Franco_Malerba" title="Franco Malerba">Franco Malerba</a> became the first citizen of Italy to travel to space.</p>
<p>Astronaut <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Michael_Massimino" title="Michael Massimino">Michael Massimino</a> who flew on STS-125 mission became the first person to use Twitter in space in May 2009.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Atlantis_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-Atlantis-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>Having flown aboard <i>Atlantis</i> as part of the STS-132 crew in May 2010 and <i>Discovery</i> as part of the <a href="/wiki/STS-133" title="STS-133">STS-133</a> crew in February/March 2011, <a href="/wiki/Stephen_Bowen_(astronaut)" title="Stephen Bowen (astronaut)">Stephen Bowen</a> became the first NASA astronaut to be launched on consecutive missions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Problems">Problems</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Space_Shuttle_Atlantis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Problems">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Composite_overwrapped_pressure_vessels">Composite overwrapped pressure vessels</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Space_Shuttle_Atlantis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Composite overwrapped pressure vessels">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>NASA announced in 2007 that 24 helium and nitrogen gas tanks in <i>Atlantis</i> were older than their designed lifetime. These <a href="/wiki/Composite_overwrapped_pressure_vessel" title="Composite overwrapped pressure vessel">composite overwrapped pressure vessels</a> (COPV) were designed for a 10-year life and later cleared for an additional 10 years; they exceeded this life in 2005. NASA said it could not guarantee any longer that the vessels on <i>Atlantis</i> would not burst or explode under full <a href="/wiki/Pressure" title="Pressure">pressure</a>. Failure of these tanks could have damaged parts of the orbiter and even wound or kill ground personnel. An in-flight failure of a pressure vessel could have even resulted in the loss of the orbiter and its crew. NASA analyses originally assumed that the vessels would <a href="/wiki/Pressure_vessel#Leak_before_burst" title="Pressure vessel">leak before they burst</a>, but new tests showed that they could in fact burst before leaking.</p>
<p>Because the original vendor was no longer in business, and a new manufacturer could not be qualified before 2010, when the shuttles were scheduled to be retired, NASA decided to continue operations with the existing tanks. Therefore, to reduce the risk of failure and the cumulative effects of load, the vessels were maintained at 80 percent of the operating pressure as late in the launch countdown as possible, and the launch pad was cleared of all but essential personnel when pressure was increased to 100 percent. The new launch procedure was employed during some the remaining launches of <i>Atlantis</i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-aging_46-0"><a href="#cite_note-aging-46">[46]</a></sup> but was resolved when the two COPVs deemed to have the highest risk of failure were replaced.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-copv_47-0"><a href="#cite_note-copv-47">[47]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Window_damage">Window damage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Space_Shuttle_Atlantis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Window damage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>After the STS-125 mission, a work light knob was discovered jammed in the space between one of <i>Atlantis</i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">'</span>s front interior windows and the Orbiter dashboard structure. The knob was believed to have entered the space during flight, when the pressurized Orbiter was expanded to its maximum size. Then, once back on Earth, the Orbiter contracted, jamming the knob in place. Leaving "as-is" was considered unsafe for flight, and some options for removal (including window replacement) would have included a 6-month delay of <i>Atlantis</i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">'</span>s next mission (planned to be <a href="/wiki/STS-129" title="STS-129">STS-129</a>). Had the removal of the knob been unsuccessful, the worst-case scenario was that <i>Atlantis</i> could have been retired from the fleet, leaving <i>Discovery</i> and <i>Endeavour</i> to complete the manifest alone. On 29 June 2009, <i>Atlantis</i> was pressurized to 17 <a href="/wiki/Pounds_per_square_inch" title="Pounds per square inch">psi</a> (120 kPa) (3 psi above ambient), which forced the Orbiter to expand slightly. The knob was then frozen with <a href="/wiki/Dry_ice" title="Dry ice">dry ice</a>, and successfully removed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-knobremoval_48-0"><a href="#cite_note-knobremoval-48">[48]</a></sup> Small areas of damage to the window were discovered where the edges of the knob had been embedded into the pane.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-knob_49-0"><a href="#cite_note-knob-49">[49]</a></sup> Subsequent investigation of the window damage discovered a maximum defect depth of approximately 0.0003 in (7.6 <a href="/wiki/Micrometre" title="Micrometre">µm</a>), less than the reportable depth threshold of 0.0015 in (38 <a href="/wiki/Micrometre" title="Micrometre">µm</a>) and not serious enough to warrant the pane’s replacement.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-windowok_50-0"><a href="#cite_note-windowok-50">[50]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_media">In media</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Space_Shuttle_Atlantis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: In media">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><i>Atlantis</i> was the shuttle shown in the 1986 film <i><a href="/wiki/SpaceCamp" title="SpaceCamp">SpaceCamp</a></i>, starring <a href="/wiki/Kate_Capshaw" title="Kate Capshaw">Kate Capshaw</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lea_Thompson" title="Lea Thompson">Lea Thompson</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tom_Skerritt" title="Tom Skerritt">Tom Skerritt</a> and <a href="/wiki/Joaquin_Phoenix" title="Joaquin Phoenix">Joaquin Phoenix</a>. The premise of the film was a crew of students at <a href="/wiki/United_States_Space_Camp" title="United States Space Camp">United States Space Camp</a> that are accidentally launched into space on-board <i>Atlantis</i>. <i>Atlantis</i> was also featured in the 1998 film <i><a href="/wiki/Deep_Impact_(film)" title="Deep Impact (film)">Deep Impact</a></i> as the spacecraft used to shuttle the crew to the fictional <i>Messiah</i> spacecraft. It is also featured in <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Armageddon_(film)" title="Armageddon (film)">Armageddon</a></i>, a film with a similar plot, in which the shuttle is destroyed in a rogue <a href="/wiki/Meteor_shower" title="Meteor shower">meteor shower</a>. She is involved as well in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Dig" title="The Dig">The Dig</a></i>, a 1995 science fiction adventure game.</p>
<p>In September 2014 Soulvizion and Division CM presented music video based on single <a href="/wiki/Are_You_with_Me#Lost_Frequencies_version" title="Are You with Me">"Lost Frequencies - Are You With Me"</a>, where main hero is astronaut of Space Shuttle. For this video used launch footage of STS-135, historical photos from NASA and 3D animation prepared in <a href="/wiki/Blender_(software)" title="Blender (software)">Blender</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup></p>
