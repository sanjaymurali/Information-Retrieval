<p>The <b>Dean Single</b>, <b>3031 Class</b>, or <b>Achilles Class</b> was a type of <a href="/wiki/Steam_locomotive" title="Steam locomotive">steam locomotive</a> built by the British <a href="/wiki/Great_Western_Railway" title="Great Western Railway">Great Western Railway</a> between 1891 and 1899. They were designed by <a href="/wiki/William_Dean_(engineer)" title="William Dean (engineer)">William Dean</a> for passenger work. The first 30 members of the class were built as 2-2-2s of the <a href="/wiki/GWR_3001_Class" title="GWR 3001 Class">3001 Class</a>.</p>
<p>The first eight members of the class (numbers 3021-3028, built April–August 1891) were built as convertible <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/7-foot-%C2%BC-inch_gauge_railway" title="7-foot-¼-inch gauge railway"><span class="nowrap">7 ft <span class="frac"><sup>1</sup>⁄<sub>4</sub></span> in</span></a> (<span class="nowrap">2,140 mm</span>) <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Broad_gauge" title="Broad gauge">broad gauge</a> <a href="/wiki/Whyte_notation" title="Whyte notation">2-2-2</a> locomotives, being converted to standard gauge in mid-1892, at the end of broad gauge running on the Great Western Railway. A further 22 were built in late 1891 and early 1892, this time as standard gauge engines.</p>
<p>Although the <span class="nowrap">4 ft <span class="frac">8<span class="visualhide"> </span><sup>1</sup>⁄<sub>2</sub></span> in</span> (<span class="nowrap">1,435 mm</span>) 3001 class were fitted with larger boilers than earlier GWR 2-2-2 classes, the diameter of the boiler was constrained by its position between the 7 ft <span class="frac nowrap">8<span class="visualhide"> </span><sup>1</sup>⁄<sub>2</sub></span> in (2.350 m) driving wheels. Thus boiler capacity could only be increased by making the boiler longer, not wider, bringing the smokebox and cylinders in front of the leading axle.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFryer199373_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFryer199373-1">[1]</a></sup> The extra weight of the larger boilers was borne by the leading wheels, making the locomotives unstable, particularly at speed. On 16 September 1893 No. 3021 <i>Wigmore Castle</i>, hauling an express train, was derailed in <a href="/wiki/Box_Tunnel" title="Box Tunnel">Box Tunnel</a> when the front axle broke. The cause of the accident was thought to be excessive weight being carried on the front axle, so it was decided to replace the leading pair of wheels in the 3001 class with a <a href="/wiki/Bogie" title="Bogie">bogie</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHolcroft197163_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHolcroft197163-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFryer199375-76_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFryer199375-76-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the 3001 class the steam chest was located underneath the cylinders, and contained two <a href="/wiki/Slide_valve" title="Slide valve">slide valves</a>. The inverted placement of the valves allowed them to drop away from the face of the steam ports when steam was shut off, thus reducing wear.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHolcroft197162_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHolcroft197162-4">[4]</a></sup> The steam chest and valves lay above the front carrying axle, and there was sufficient clearance to allow the steam chest cover to be removed over the axle for maintenance.</p>
<p>Replacing the axle with a bogie of conventional design would have obstructed access to the port faces. Dean instead used a suspension bogie, in which the weight of the locomotive was transferred upwards to the bogie by four bolts mounted on the inside frames. The centre pin of the bogie rotated in a spring-centred block mounted beneath the steam chest on cross beams. This setup gave sufficient clearance so that, when the bolts were undone, the front end of the locomotive raised, and the bogie was run out from underneath, the steam chest cover could be removed without hindrance.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHolcroft197163_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHolcroft197163-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>No. 3021 was rebuilt as a 4-2-2 in March 1894. Between June and December 1894 the 28 remaining locomotives of the 3001 class were rebuilt.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTERussell199942_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTERussell199942-5">[5]</a></sup> The first of a further 50 new bogie singles was also built in March 1894, the last of the class being outshopped in March 1899.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFryer199376_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFryer199376-6">[6]</a></sup> These new locomotives differed from the rebuilds in having their cylinder diameter reduced from 20 to 19 inches (508 to 483 mm), and the springs for the trailing wheels located above the footplate and outside the cab, necessitating a reduced width for the latter.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFryer199377-78_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFryer199377-78-7">[7]</a></sup> The rebuilds subsequently had their cylinders lined down to 19 inches (483 mm).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFryer199378_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFryer199378-8">[8]</a></sup> The entire class, as they required it, had their driving wheels fitted with thicker tyres from 1898 onwards, increasing the wheel diameter by zero and a half inch (12.7 mm) to 7 ft <span class="frac nowrap">8<span class="visualhide"> </span><sup>1</sup>⁄<sub>2</sub></span> in (2.350 m).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFryer199378_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFryer199378-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1900, <a href="/wiki/George_Jackson_Churchward" title="George Jackson Churchward">George Jackson Churchward</a> replaced the boiler on number 3027 <i>Worcester</i> with a parallel Standard 2 boiler. Twelve further engines were similarly converted in 1905 and 1906.</p>
<p>Despite the locomotives' speed, the 4-2-2 design was soon found to be outdated and unsuitable for more modern operation. A proposal to improve their performance by fitting them with long-travel valves was found to be impracticable; the existing valves were directly driven from eccentrics mounted on the driving axle, and there was insufficient clearance to fit larger eccentrics.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHolcroft1971117_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHolcroft1971117-9">[9]</a></sup> Churchward considered rebuilding the class as <a href="/wiki/GWR_7_(Armstrong)_Class" title="GWR 7 (Armstrong) Class">Armstrong Class</a> 4-4-0s with 7 ft 2 in (2.184 m) coupled wheels. The cylinder centre line would then be 3.5 in (89 mm) above the driving centre, due to the 7 in (178 mm) difference in driving wheel diameter. This scheme was not carried through because the connecting rods would not clear the lower slide bar, and the valve gear would be out of alignment. An alternative proposal to drop the locomotive 3.5 in (89 mm), and raise the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Buffer_beam" title="Buffer beam">buffer beam</a> and <a href="/wiki/Dragbox" title="Dragbox">dragbox</a>, was also rejected on the grounds of cost. The class were gradually withdrawn between 1908 and 1915, with the last survivor, no. 3074 <i>Princess Helena</i>, being withdrawn in December 1916.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHolcroft1971117_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHolcroft1971117-9">[9]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Notable_members_of_the_class">Notable members of the class</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=GWR_3031_Class&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Notable members of the class">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>3065 <i>Duke of Connaught</i> made a record-breaking run with the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Ocean_Mail&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Ocean Mail (page does not exist)">Ocean Mail</a> on 9 May 1904, covering the distance from Bristol (Pylle Hill) to Paddington in 99 minutes 46 seconds<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTENock195432_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTENock195432-10">[10]</a></sup> as part of a run from Plymouth to Paddington in 227 minutes.</p>
<p>3041 <i>The Queen</i>, originally named <i>James Mason</i>, was an example of this class allocated to <a href="/wiki/British_Royal_Train" title="British Royal Train">Royal Train</a> duties.</p>
<p>Number 3046 <i>Lord of the Isles</i> has enjoyed a certain amount of celebrity, having been chosen as the prototype for a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tri-ang" title="Tri-ang">Tri-ang</a> model locomotive. Since then the engine has also been modelled by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/BRIO" title="BRIO">Brio</a> and <a href="/wiki/Matchbox_(brand)" title="Matchbox (brand)">Matchbox</a>. In 2006, Hornby also produced a limited edition of the same model, this time bearing the name <i><a href="/wiki/Lorna_Doone" title="Lorna Doone">Lorna Doone</a></i>.</p>

<p>None of the original class survive, but a static replica of <i>The Queen</i> was commissioned by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tussauds" title="Tussauds">Tussauds</a> for the Railways and Royalty exhibition at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Windsor_and_Eton_Central_railway_station" title="Windsor and Eton Central railway station">Windsor and Eton Central railway station</a>. The replica loco was completed in December 1982 and displayed outside Steamtown in January 1983 (where it was constructed), before being transported by road to Windsor on 12 January 1983 and arriving on 14 January. The main frames, footplate, 'boiler', smokebox, cab and splashers were fabricated by Babcock's of Tipton. The tender was modified from an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/LB%26SCR_C2x_class" title="LB&amp;SCR C2x class">LBSCR C2x</a> tender. Parts from a GWR tender, that came from the Dumbleton Hall Preservation Society, were used to provide the wheels for the front bogie and the real wheels. The top halves of the driving wheels do not exist, and were cast from 2 quarters, being bolted together to make a half, and the driving wheels also don't sit on the rail, so the loco could be wheeled into position on its front bogie and rear wheels. Some boiler fittings were obtained from the Great Western Society and sandblasted, and the dome and safety valve bonnet were made by Newcastle Metal Spinners. Tussaud's fitted smoke and steam generators, so steam was emitted from the cab, whistles, safety valves and smoke from the chimney. A sound unit was also fitted.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHinchcliffe198391.2C_92_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHinchcliffe198391.2C_92-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>The engine remains there, but the tender was scrapped to make more space for the shopping centre occupying that station building. The Bluebell Railway Atlantic Group purchased the axleboxes, springs and the complete wheel sets from the tender for use in their newbuild Atlantic project.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Models">Models</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=GWR_3031_Class&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Models">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Hornby_Railways" title="Hornby Railways">Hornby Railways</a> manufacture a model of the 3031 Class in <a href="/wiki/OO_gauge" title="OO gauge">OO gauge</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Civic_heraldry">Civic heraldry</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=GWR_3031_Class&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Civic heraldry">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The coat of arms of the old Borough of Swindon (1900–74) includes an image of 3029 <i>White Horse</i> on the shield.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBryan200824_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBryan200824-26">[26]</a></sup> The coat of arms was displayed on the splashers of the last <a href="/wiki/GWR_4073_Class" title="GWR 4073 Class">Castle Class</a> built (No. 7037 <i>Swindon</i>). <a href="/wiki/Museum_of_the_Great_Western_Railway" title="Museum of the Great Western Railway">STEAM Museum of the Great Western Railway</a> at Swindon acquired one of the splashers in 2012.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBBC_News2012_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBBC_News2012-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
