<p>The <b>1938 New England Hurricane</b> (also referred to as the <b>Great New England Hurricane</b> and <b>Long Island Express</b>) was one of the deadliest and most destructive <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone" title="Tropical cyclone">tropical cyclones</a> to strike <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Long_Island,_New_York" title="Long Island, New York">Long Island, New York</a> and <a href="/wiki/New_England" title="New England">New England</a>. The storm formed near the coast of Africa on September 9, becoming a Category 5 hurricane on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saffir-Simpson_Hurricane_Scale" title="Saffir-Simpson Hurricane Scale">Saffir-Simpson Hurricane Scale</a> before making landfall as a Category 3 hurricane<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> on <a href="/wiki/Long_Island" title="Long Island">Long Island</a> on September 21. It is estimated that the hurricane killed 682 people,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Scotti_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Scotti-2">[2]</a></sup> damaged or destroyed more than 57,000 homes, and caused property losses estimated at US$306 million ($4.7 billion in 2017).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Damaged trees and buildings were still seen in the affected areas as late as 1951.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> It remains the most powerful and deadliest hurricane in recorded New England history, eclipsed in landfall intensity perhaps only by the <a href="/wiki/Great_Colonial_Hurricane_of_1635" title="Great Colonial Hurricane of 1635">Great Colonial Hurricane of 1635</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Meteorological_history">Meteorological history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=1938_New_England_hurricane&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Meteorological history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:1938_New_England_hurricane_track.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1969" data-file-width="3000" height="144" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/38/1938_New_England_hurricane_track.png/220px-1938_New_England_hurricane_track.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/38/1938_New_England_hurricane_track.png/330px-1938_New_England_hurricane_track.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/38/1938_New_England_hurricane_track.png/440px-1938_New_England_hurricane_track.png 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:1938_New_England_hurricane_track.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Map plotting the track and intensity of the storm, according to the <a href="/wiki/Saffir%E2%80%93Simpson_scale" title="Saffir–Simpson scale">Saffir–Simpson scale</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The storm was first analyzed by ship data south of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cape_Verde_Islands" title="Cape Verde Islands">Cape Verde Islands</a> on September 9. Over the next ten days, it steadily gathered strength and slowly tracked to the west-northwest; it is estimated to have reached Category 5 intensity by September 20, while centered east of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bahamas" title="Bahamas">Bahamas</a>. It then veered northward in response to a deep trough over <a href="/wiki/Appalachia" title="Appalachia">Appalachia</a>, sparing the Bahamas, Florida, the Carolinas, and the <a href="/wiki/Mid-Atlantic_states" title="Mid-Atlantic states">Mid-Atlantic states</a>. At the same time, a high pressure system was centered north of <a href="/wiki/Bermuda" title="Bermuda">Bermuda</a>, preventing the hurricane from making an eastward turn out to sea.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gibson_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Gibson-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>Thus, the hurricane was effectively squeezed to the north between the two weather systems. This caused the storm's forward speed to increase substantially late on September 20, ultimately reaching 70 mph, the highest forward velocity ever recorded in the annals of hurricanes. This extreme forward motion was in the same general direction as the winds on the eastern side of the storm as it proceeded north; this, in turn, caused the wind speed to be far higher in areas east of the storm's eye than would be the case with a hurricane of more typical forward speed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>The storm was centered several hundred miles to the southeast of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cape_Hatteras,_North_Carolina" title="Cape Hatteras, North Carolina">Cape Hatteras</a> during the early hours of September 21, and it weakened slightly. By 8:30 am EDT, it was centered approximately 100 miles (160 km) due east of Cape Hatteras, and its forward speed had increased to well over 50 mph. This rapid movement did not permit enough time for the storm to weaken over the cooler waters before it reached <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Long_Island,_New_York" title="Long Island, New York">Long Island</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> The hurricane sped through the Virginia tidewater during the 9:00 am hour. Between 12:00 pm and 2:00 pm, the <a href="/wiki/New_Jersey" title="New Jersey">New Jersey</a> coastline and New York City caught the western edge.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-islandnet.com_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-islandnet.com-9">[9]</a></sup> At the same time, weather conditions began to deteriorate rapidly on Long Island and along the southern New England coast.</p>
<p>The hurricane made landfall at <a href="/wiki/Bellport,_New_York" title="Bellport, New York">Bellport</a> on Long Island's Suffolk County sometime between 2:10 pm and 2:40 pm as a Category 3 hurricane, with sustained winds of 120 mph.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Uptoncommerativehome_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Uptoncommerativehome-10">[10]</a></sup> It made a second landfall as a Category 3 hurricane somewhere between <a href="/wiki/Bridgeport,_Connecticut" title="Bridgeport, Connecticut">Bridgeport</a> and <a href="/wiki/New_Haven,_Connecticut" title="New Haven, Connecticut">New Haven</a>, <a href="/wiki/Connecticut" title="Connecticut">Connecticut</a> at around 4:00 pm, with sustained winds of 115 mph.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Uptoncommerativehome_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-Uptoncommerativehome-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>The storm's eye moved into western Massachusetts by 5:00 pm, and it reached Vermont by 6:00 pm. Both <a href="/wiki/Westfield,_Massachusetts" title="Westfield, Massachusetts">Westfield, Massachusetts</a> and <a href="/wiki/Dorset,_Vermont" title="Dorset, Vermont">Dorset, Vermont</a> reported calm conditions and partial clearing during passage of the eye, which is a rather unusual occurrence for a New England hurricane.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-11">[11]</a></sup> The hurricane began to lose tropical characteristics as it continued into northern Vermont, though it was still carrying hurricane-force winds. It crossed into <a href="/wiki/Quebec" title="Quebec">Quebec</a> at approximately 10:00 pm, transitioning into a <a href="/wiki/Extratropical_cyclone" title="Extratropical cyclone">post-tropical low</a>. The post-tropical remnants dissipated over northern <a href="/wiki/Ontario" title="Ontario">Ontario</a> a few days later.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Forecasting_the_storm">Forecasting the storm</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=1938_New_England_hurricane&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Forecasting the storm">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 1938, United States forecasting lagged behind forecasting in Europe, where new techniques were being used to analyze air masses, taking into account the influence of fronts. A confidential report was released by the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Forest_Service" title="United States Forest Service">United States Forest Service</a>, the parent agency of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_States_Weather_Bureau" title="United States Weather Bureau">United States Weather Bureau</a>. It described the weather bureau's forecasting as "a sorry state of affairs" where forecasters had poor training and systematic planning was not used, and where forecasters had to "scrape by" to get information wherever they could. The <a href="/wiki/Jacksonville,_Florida" title="Jacksonville, Florida">Jacksonville, Florida</a>, office of the weather bureau issued a warning on September 19 that a hurricane might hit Florida. Residents and authorities made extensive preparations, as they had endured the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Labor_Day_Hurricane" title="Labor Day Hurricane">Labor Day Hurricane</a> three years earlier. When the storm turned north, the office issued warnings for the Carolina coast and transferred authority to the bureau's headquarters in Washington.</p>
<p>At 9:00 am on September 21, the Washington office issued northeast storm warnings north of Atlantic City and south of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Block_Island,_Rhode_Island" title="Block Island, Rhode Island">Block Island, Rhode Island</a>, and southeast storm warnings from Block Island to <a href="/wiki/Eastport,_Maine" title="Eastport, Maine">Eastport, Maine</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Uptoncommerativehistory_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-Uptoncommerativehistory-12">[12]</a></sup> The advisory, however, underestimated the storm's intensity and said that it was farther south than it actually was.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Uptoncommerativehistory_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-Uptoncommerativehistory-12">[12]</a></sup> The office had yet to forward any information about the hurricane to the New York City office.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Uptoncommerativehistory_12-2"><a href="#cite_note-Uptoncommerativehistory-12">[12]</a></sup> At 10:00 am, the bureau downgraded the hurricane to a tropical storm. The 11:30 am advisory mentioned gale-force winds but nothing about a tropical storm or hurricane.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Uptoncommerativehistory_12-3"><a href="#cite_note-Uptoncommerativehistory-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>That day, 28 year-old rookie Charles Pierce was standing in for two veteran meteorologists. He concluded that the storm would be squeezed between a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/High-pressure" title="High-pressure">high-pressure</a> area located to the west and a high-pressure area to the east, and that it would be forced to ride up a <a href="/wiki/Trough_(meteorology)" title="Trough (meteorology)">trough of low pressure</a> into New England. A noon meeting was called and Pierce presented his conclusion, but he was overruled by "celebrated" chief forecaster Charles Mitchell and his senior staff. In Boston, meteorologist E.B. Rideout told his <a href="/wiki/WEZE" title="WEZE">WEEI</a> radio listeners (to the skepticism of his peers) that the hurricane would hit New England.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> At 2:00 pm, hurricane-force gusts were occurring on Long Island's South Shore and near hurricane-force gusts on the coast of Connecticut. The Washington office issued an advisory saying that the storm was 75 miles east-southeast of Atlantic City and would pass over Long Island and Connecticut. Re-analysis of the storm suggests that the hurricane was farther north (just 50 miles from Fire Island), and that it was stronger and larger than the advisory said.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Uptoncommerativehistory_12-4"><a href="#cite_note-Uptoncommerativehistory-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Impact">Impact</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=1938_New_England_hurricane&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Impact">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Buzzards_Bay_station_after_1938_hurricane_postcard.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="981" data-file-width="1520" height="142" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cc/Buzzards_Bay_station_after_1938_hurricane_postcard.jpg/220px-Buzzards_Bay_station_after_1938_hurricane_postcard.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cc/Buzzards_Bay_station_after_1938_hurricane_postcard.jpg/330px-Buzzards_Bay_station_after_1938_hurricane_postcard.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cc/Buzzards_Bay_station_after_1938_hurricane_postcard.jpg/440px-Buzzards_Bay_station_after_1938_hurricane_postcard.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Buzzards_Bay_station_after_1938_hurricane_postcard.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
A postcard view of flooding from the hurricane at <a href="/wiki/Buzzards_Bay_station" title="Buzzards Bay station">Buzzards Bay station</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The majority of the storm damage was from <a href="/wiki/Storm_surge" title="Storm surge">storm surge</a> and wind. Damage was estimated at $308 million, the equivalent of $5.1 billion adjusted for inflation in 2016 dollars, making it among the most costly hurricanes to strike the U.S. mainland.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> It is estimated that, if an identical hurricane had struck in 2005, it would have caused $39.2 billion in damage due to changes in population and infrastructure.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>Approximately 600 people died in the storm in New England, most in <a href="/wiki/Rhode_Island" title="Rhode Island">Rhode Island</a>, and up to 100 people elsewhere in the path of the storm.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> An additional 708 people were reported injured.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-damagecaused_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-damagecaused-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>In total, 4,500 cottages, farms, and other homes were reported destroyed and 25,000 homes were damaged. Other damages included 26,000 automobiles destroyed and 20,000 electrical poles toppled. The hurricane also devastated the forests of the Northeast, knocking down an estimated two billion trees in New York and New England.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-damagecaused_17-1"><a href="#cite_note-damagecaused-17">[17]</a></sup> Freshwater flooding was minimal, however, as the quick passage of the storm decreased local rainfall totals, with only a few small areas receiving over 10 inches (250 mm).</p>
<p>Over 35% of New England's total forest area was affected. In all, over 2.7 billion board feet of trees fell because of the storm, although 1.6 billion board feet of the trees were salvaged.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> The Northeastern Timber Salvage Administration (NETSA) was established to deal with the extreme fire hazard that the fallen timber had created.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> In many locations, roads from the fallen tree removal were visible decades later, and some became trails still used today. The <a href="/wiki/New_York,_New_Haven_and_Hartford_Railroad" title="New York, New Haven and Hartford Railroad">New York, New Haven and Hartford Railroad</a> from New Haven to Providence was particularly hard hit, as countless bridges along the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shore_Line" title="Shore Line">Shore Line</a> were destroyed or flooded, severing rail connections to badly affected cities such as <a href="/wiki/Westerly,_Rhode_Island" title="Westerly, Rhode Island">Westerly, Rhode Island</a>.</p>
<p>Due to the lack of technology back in 1938, Long Island residents were not warned of the hurricane's arrival,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rather_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-Rather-20">[20]</a></sup> leaving no time to prepare or evacuate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Flotteron_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-Flotteron-21">[21]</a></sup> Long Island was struck first, before New England and Quebec, earning the storm the nickname the "Long Island Express."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Flotteron_21-1"><a href="#cite_note-Flotteron-21">[21]</a></sup> The winds reached up to 150 mph with waves surging to around 25–35 feet high.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-The_Long_Island_Express_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-The_Long_Island_Express-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Yale" title="Yale">Yale</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Harvard" title="Harvard">Harvard</a> both owned large forests managed by their forestry departments, but both forests were wiped out by the hurricane. However, Yale had a backup forest at Great Mountain in northwestern Connecticut which was spared from the totality of the damages, and they were able to keep their forestry program running, which maintains operation today. Harvard's program, however, was reduced as a result.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="New_Jersey">New Jersey</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=1938_New_England_hurricane&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: New Jersey">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The western side of the hurricane caused sustained tropical storm-force winds, high waves, and storm surge along much of the New Jersey coast.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-islandnet.com_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-islandnet.com-9">[9]</a></sup> The surge destroyed much of the boardwalk in <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_City,_New_Jersey" title="Atlantic City, New Jersey">Atlantic City</a>, and the bridge collapsed between Atlantic City and <a href="/wiki/Brigantine,_New_Jersey" title="Brigantine, New Jersey">Brigantine</a>, stranding at least 2,000. Additionally, the surge inundated several coastal communities; <a href="/wiki/Wildwood,_New_Jersey" title="Wildwood, New Jersey">Wildwood</a> was under 3 feet (0.91 m) of water at the height of the storm, and the boardwalk was destroyed in <a href="/wiki/Bay_Head,_New_Jersey" title="Bay Head, New Jersey">Bay Head</a> and dozens of cottages washed into the ocean. Crops sustained wind damage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> The maximum recorded wind gust was 70 mph at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sandy_Hook,_New_Jersey" title="Sandy Hook, New Jersey">Sandy Hook</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-islandnet.com_9-2"><a href="#cite_note-islandnet.com-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="New_York_City_and_western_Long_Island">New York City and western Long Island</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=1938_New_England_hurricane&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: New York City and western Long Island">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The metropolitan area escaped the worst of the wind and storm surge because it was hit by the storm's weaker western side. Winds were recorded at 60 mph at <a href="/wiki/Central_Park" title="Central Park">Central Park</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Battery_Park" title="Battery Park">Battery Park</a> recorded sustained winds of 70 mph with gusts to 80 mph, and a gust of 90 mph was recorded 500 feet above ground at the <a href="/wiki/Daily_News_Building" title="Daily News Building">Daily News Building</a>. Winds were estimated at 120 mph on top of the <a href="/wiki/Empire_State_Building" title="Empire State Building">Empire State Building</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Uptoncommerativehome_10-2"><a href="#cite_note-Uptoncommerativehome-10">[10]</a></sup> The highest winds were from the north to northwest on the back side of the storm.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Time_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-Time-25">[25]</a></sup> The storm surge was 8½ feet at the Battery and the Mean Low Water storm tide was 16¾ feet at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Willets_Point" title="Willets Point">Willets Point</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Uptoncommerativehome_10-3"><a href="#cite_note-Uptoncommerativehome-10">[10]</a></sup> In New York Harbor, the waters rose 7 feet in a half-hour.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Uptoncommerativehome_10-4"><a href="#cite_note-Uptoncommerativehome-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>In New York City and Long Island, schools were dismissed early.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Uptoncommerativehistory_12-5"><a href="#cite_note-Uptoncommerativehistory-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Williston_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-Williston-26">[26]</a></sup> Extensive street flooding occurred because debris blocked drains.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Uptoncommerativehistory_12-6"><a href="#cite_note-Uptoncommerativehistory-12">[12]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/East_River" title="East River">East River</a> flowed three blocks and flooded a <a href="/wiki/Consolidated_Edison" title="Consolidated Edison">Consolidated Edison</a> (Con Ed) plant at 133rd Street, causing power to fail in Manhattan north of 59th Street and in the Bronx for several minutes to a few hours. Railroad and ferry services were suspended for a time. The <a href="/wiki/Staten_Island_Ferry" title="Staten Island Ferry">Staten Island Ferry</a> boat <i>Knickerbocker</i> got stuck in the terminal with 200 passengers aboard.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Uptoncommerativehistory_12-7"><a href="#cite_note-Uptoncommerativehistory-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Time_25-1"><a href="#cite_note-Time-25">[25]</a></sup> Bridges and tunnels into Manhattan were closed until the following afternoon. 95% of <a href="/wiki/Nassau_County,_New_York" title="Nassau County, New York">Nassau County</a> lost power, where floods brought traffic to a halt.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Time_25-2"><a href="#cite_note-Time-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Manhasset_Bay" title="Manhasset Bay">Manhasset Bay</a>, almost 400 boats were ripped from their moorings and smashed or sunk, with more than 100 washing up on the beach by the <a href="/wiki/Port_Washington,_New_York" title="Port Washington, New York">Port Washington</a> Yacht Club. Similar scenes occurred in other locations on the north shore. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/J.P._Morgan" title="J.P. Morgan">J.P. Morgan</a> estate in <a href="/wiki/Glen_Cove,_New_York" title="Glen Cove, New York">Glen Cove</a> was heavily damaged. The wife of New York City mayor <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fiorello_La_Guardia" title="Fiorello La Guardia">Fiorello La Guardia</a> was forced to wait out the storm on the second floor of their <a href="/wiki/Northport,_New_York" title="Northport, New York">Northport</a> cottage. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mitchel_Field" title="Mitchel Field">Mitchel Field</a> army airfield was buffeted by winds of nearly 100 mph and was under knee-deep water.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> In <a href="/wiki/Williston_Park,_New_York" title="Williston Park, New York">Williston Park</a>, residents of 50 homes needed to be rescued by rowboat when heavy rain the previous few days combined with the rain from the hurricane to overflow a pond.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Scotti_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Scotti-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Williston_26-1"><a href="#cite_note-Williston-26">[26]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AmericanExperience_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-AmericanExperience-29">[29]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Eastern_Long_Island">Eastern Long Island</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=1938_New_England_hurricane&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Eastern Long Island">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Eastern Long Island experienced the worst of the storm. The Dune Road area of <a href="/wiki/Westhampton_Beach,_New_York" title="Westhampton Beach, New York">Westhampton Beach</a> was obliterated, resulting in 29 deaths. There were 21 other deaths through the rest of the east end of Long Island. The storm surge temporarily turned <a href="/wiki/Montauk,_New_York" title="Montauk, New York">Montauk</a> into an island as it flooded across the South Fork at <a href="/wiki/Napeague,_New_York" title="Napeague, New York">Napeague</a> and obliterated the tracks of the <a href="/wiki/Long_Island_Rail_Road" title="Long Island Rail Road">Long Island Rail Road</a>.</p>
<p>Long Island was hit hard being exposed to the storm due to its shorelines. The estimated storm tide was 15 feet in this region. A mean low water storm tide of 8 feet was recorded at <a href="/wiki/Port_Jefferson,_New_York" title="Port Jefferson, New York">Port Jefferson</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Uptoncommerativehome_10-5"><a href="#cite_note-Uptoncommerativehome-10">[10]</a></sup> About 50 people perished in the storm's wake.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rather_20-1"><a href="#cite_note-Rather-20">[20]</a></sup> All the shore lines were very vulnerable to the high winds and flooding, and anyone near the shores was directly in harm's way.</p>
<p>Ten new inlets were created on eastern Long Island.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Uptoncommerativehome_10-6"><a href="#cite_note-Uptoncommerativehome-10">[10]</a></sup> The surge rearranged the sand at the Cedar Point Lighthouse so that the island became connected to what is now <a href="/wiki/Cedar_Point_County_Park" title="Cedar Point County Park">Cedar Point County Park</a>. The surging water created the <a href="/wiki/Shinnecock_Inlet" title="Shinnecock Inlet">Shinnecock Inlet</a> by carving out a large section of barrier island separating Shinnecock Bay from the Atlantic. The storm toppled the landmark steeple of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/First_Presbyterian_Church_(Sag_Harbor)" title="First Presbyterian Church (Sag Harbor)">Old Whaler's Church</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceB_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceB-32">[32]</a></sup> which was the tallest building in <a href="/wiki/Sag_Harbor,_New_York" title="Sag Harbor, New York">Sag Harbor</a>. The steeple has not been rebuilt.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Wading_River,_New_York" title="Wading River, New York">Wading River</a> suffered substantial damage. The storm blew down the movie theater on Front Street in Greenport on the North Fork of Long Island. The fishing industry was destroyed, as was half of the apple crop.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Uptoncommerativehome_10-7"><a href="#cite_note-Uptoncommerativehome-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Rhode_Island">Rhode Island</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=1938_New_England_hurricane&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Rhode Island">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>The storm surge hit <a href="/wiki/Westerly,_Rhode_Island" title="Westerly, Rhode Island">Westerly, Rhode Island</a> at 3:50 pm, resulting in 100 deaths.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hurr1938_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-hurr1938-33">[33]</a></sup> The tide was higher than usual because of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Autumnal_equinox" title="Autumnal equinox">autumnal equinox</a> and <a href="/wiki/Full_moon" title="Full moon">full moon</a>, and the hurricane produced storm tides of 14 to 18 feet (5 m) along most of the Connecticut coast, with 18 to 25-foot (8 m) tides from <a href="/wiki/New_London,_Connecticut" title="New London, Connecticut">New London</a> east to <a href="/wiki/Cape_Cod" title="Cape Cod">Cape Cod</a>—including the entire coastline of Rhode Island.</p>
<p>The storm surge was especially violent along the Rhode Island shore, sweeping hundreds of summer cottages out to sea. Low-lying <a href="/wiki/Block_Island" title="Block Island">Block Island</a> was almost completely underwater, and many drowned. As the surge drove northward through <a href="/wiki/Narragansett_Bay" title="Narragansett Bay">Narragansett Bay</a>, it was restricted by the Bay's funnel shape and rose to 15.8 feet above normal spring tides, resulting in more than 13 feet (4.0 m) of water in some areas of downtown <a href="/wiki/Providence,_Rhode_Island" title="Providence, Rhode Island">Providence</a>. Several motorists were drowned in their automobiles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Weather_History_of_the_.2738_Hurricane_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-Weather_History_of_the_.2738_Hurricane-34">[34]</a></sup> In <a href="/wiki/Jamestown,_Rhode_Island" title="Jamestown, Rhode Island">Jamestown</a>, seven children were killed when their school bus was blown into Mackerel Cove.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Newport38_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-Newport38-35">[35]</a></sup> Many stores in downtown Providence were looted by mobs, often before the flood waters had fully subsided and due in part to the economic difficulties of the <a href="/wiki/Great_Depression" title="Great Depression">Great Depression</a>.</p>
<p>Many homes and structures were destroyed along the coast, as well as many structures inland along the hurricane's path. Entire beach communities were obliterated on the coast of Rhode Island. <a href="/wiki/Napatree_Point" title="Napatree Point">Napatree Point</a> was completely swept away, a small cape that housed nearly 40 families between the Atlantic Ocean and <a href="/wiki/Little_Narragansett_Bay" title="Little Narragansett Bay">Little Narragansett Bay</a> just off of <a href="/wiki/Watch_Hill,_Rhode_Island" title="Watch Hill, Rhode Island">Watch Hill</a>. Today, Napatree is a wildlife refuge with no human inhabitants. One house in <a href="/wiki/Charlestown,_Rhode_Island" title="Charlestown, Rhode Island">Charlestown, Rhode Island</a> was lifted and deposited across the street, where it stood until it was demolished in August 2011. Even to this day, concrete staircases and boardwalk bases destroyed by the hurricane can be found when sand levels are low on some beaches. The boardwalk along Easton's Beach in Newport was completely destroyed by the storm.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Newport38_35-1"><a href="#cite_note-Newport38-35">[35]</a></sup></p>
<p>A few miles from <a href="/wiki/Conanicut_Island" title="Conanicut Island">Conanicut Island</a>, <a href="/wiki/Whale_Rock_Light" title="Whale Rock Light">Whale Rock Light</a> was swept off its base and into the raging waves, killing lighthouse keeper Walter Eberle. His body was never found. The <a href="/wiki/Prudence_Island_Light" title="Prudence Island Light">Prudence Island Light</a> suffered a direct blow from the storm surge, which measured 17 feet 5 inches at Sandy Point. The masonry tower was slightly damaged. However, the adjoining light keeper's home was utterly destroyed and washed out to sea. The light keeper's wife and son were both killed, as well as the former light keeper and a couple who left their summer cottages near the lighthouse and sought shelter in what they thought was the sturdier light keeper's home. Light keeper George T. Gustavus was thrown free from the wreckage of the house and was saved by an island resident who held a branch into the water from the cliffs farther down the coast. Gustavus and Milton Chase, the owner of the island's power plant, reactivated the light during the storm by running a cable from the plant to the light and installing a light bulb, marking the first time that it was illuminated with electricity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup></p>
<p>The original parchment of the 1764 Charter of <a href="/wiki/Brown_University" title="Brown University">Brown University</a> was washed clean of its text when its vault was flooded in a Providence bank.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Newport,_Rhode_Island" title="Newport, Rhode Island">Newport</a> recorded the highest water level of the storm, at 3.53 meters above mean sea level according to a <a href="/wiki/National_Oceanic_and_Atmospheric_Administration" title="National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration">NOAA</a> study.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-aoml.noaa.gov_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-aoml.noaa.gov-38">[38]</a></sup> This storm level is 0.98 meters above the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/SLOSH" title="SLOSH">SLOSH</a> model of a 100-year storm, and one estimate is that this water level "reflects a storm occurring roughly once every 400 years."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ProJoBowman_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-ProJoBowman-39">[39]</a></sup> A study of sand deposits also gives evidence that this was the strongest hurricane to hit Rhode Island in over 300 years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-aoml.noaa.gov_38-1"><a href="#cite_note-aoml.noaa.gov-38">[38]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ProJoBowman_39-1"><a href="#cite_note-ProJoBowman-39">[39]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Fox_Point_Hurricane_Barrier" title="Fox Point Hurricane Barrier">Fox Point Hurricane Barrier</a> was completed in 1966 because of the massive flooding from the 1938 storm, and from the even higher 14.4 foot (4.4 meters) storm surge that resulted from 1954's <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Carol" title="Hurricane Carol">Hurricane Carol</a>, in hopes of preventing extreme storm surges from ever again flooding downtown Providence.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Connecticut">Connecticut</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=1938_New_England_hurricane&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Connecticut">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">

<p>Eastern Connecticut was on the eastern side of the hurricane. Long Island acted as a buffer against large ocean surges, but the waters of Long Island Sound rose to great heights. Small shoreline towns to the east of New Haven experienced much destruction from the water and winds, and the 1938 hurricane holds the record for the worst natural disaster in Connecticut's 350-year history. The mean low-water storm tide was 14.1 feet at Stamford, 12.8 feet at Bridgeport, and 10.58 feet at New London, which remains a record high.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Uptoncommerativehome_10-8"><a href="#cite_note-Uptoncommerativehome-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the shoreline towns of <a href="/wiki/Madison,_Connecticut" title="Madison, Connecticut">Madison</a>, <a href="/wiki/Clinton,_Connecticut" title="Clinton, Connecticut">Clinton</a>, <a href="/wiki/Westbrook,_Connecticut" title="Westbrook, Connecticut">Westbrook</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Old_Saybrook,_Connecticut" title="Old Saybrook, Connecticut">Old Saybrook</a>, buildings were found as wreckage across coastal roads. Actress <a href="/wiki/Katharine_Hepburn" title="Katharine Hepburn">Katharine Hepburn</a> waded to safety from her Old Saybrook beach home, narrowly escaping death. She stated in her 1991 book that 95% of her personal belongings were either lost or destroyed, including her 1932 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Academy_Award" title="Academy Award">Oscar</a>, which was later found intact.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> In <a href="/wiki/Old_Lyme,_Connecticut" title="Old Lyme, Connecticut">Old Lyme</a>, beach cottages were flattened or swept away. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/New_York,_New_Haven_%26_Hartford_Railroad" title="New York, New Haven &amp; Hartford Railroad">NYNH&amp;H</a> passenger train <i>Bostonian</i> became stuck in debris at <a href="/wiki/Stonington,_Connecticut" title="Stonington, Connecticut">Stonington</a>. Two passengers drowned while attempting to escape before the crew was able to clear the debris and get the train moving.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Uptoncommerativehistory_12-8"><a href="#cite_note-Uptoncommerativehistory-12">[12]</a></sup> Along the Stonington shorefront, buildings were swept off their foundations and found two miles (3 km) inland. Rescuers found live fish and crabs in kitchen drawers and cabinets while searching for survivors in the homes in <a href="/wiki/Mystic,_Connecticut" title="Mystic, Connecticut">Mystic</a>.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/New_London,_Connecticut" title="New London, Connecticut">New London</a> was first swept by the winds and storm surge, after which the waterfront business district caught fire and burned out of control for 10 hours. Stately homes along <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Ocean_Beach,_Connecticut&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Ocean Beach, Connecticut (page does not exist)">Ocean Beach</a> were leveled by the storm surge. The permanently anchored 240-ton <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Light_vessel" title="Light vessel">lightship</a> at the head of New London Harbor was found on a sand bar two miles (3 km) away.</p>
<p>Interior sections of the state experienced widespread flooding as the hurricane's torrential rains fell on soil already saturated from previous storms. The <a href="/wiki/Connecticut_River" title="Connecticut River">Connecticut River</a> was forced out of its banks, inundating cities and towns from <a href="/wiki/Hartford,_Connecticut" title="Hartford, Connecticut">Hartford</a> to <a href="/wiki/Middletown,_Connecticut" title="Middletown, Connecticut">Middletown</a>. Novelist <a href="/wiki/Ann_Petry" title="Ann Petry">Ann Petry</a> drew on her personal experiences of the hurricane in Old Saybrook in her 1947 novel <i>Country Place</i>. The novel is set in the immediate aftermath of World War II, but Petry identified the 1938 storm as the source for the storm that is at the center of her narrative.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Massachusetts">Massachusetts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=1938_New_England_hurricane&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Massachusetts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:201px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Marchmont_-_Winchendon_Springs_MA.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="5016" data-file-width="6646" height="150" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e4/Marchmont_-_Winchendon_Springs_MA.jpg/199px-Marchmont_-_Winchendon_Springs_MA.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e4/Marchmont_-_Winchendon_Springs_MA.jpg/299px-Marchmont_-_Winchendon_Springs_MA.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e4/Marchmont_-_Winchendon_Springs_MA.jpg/398px-Marchmont_-_Winchendon_Springs_MA.jpg 2x" width="199"/></a>

<p>The eye of the storm followed the Connecticut River north into <a href="/wiki/Massachusetts" title="Massachusetts">Massachusetts</a>, where the winds and flooding killed 99 people. In Springfield, the river rose six to 10 feet (3 m) above flood stage, causing significant damage. Up to six inches (152 mm) of rain fell across western Massachusetts, which combined with over four inches (102 mm) that had fallen a few days earlier to produce widespread flooding. Flash flooding on the Chicopee River washed away the Chicopee Falls Bridge, while the Connecticut River flooded most of the Willimansett section. Residents of <a href="/wiki/Ware,_Massachusetts" title="Ware, Massachusetts">Ware</a> were stranded for days and relied on air-dropped food and medicine. After the flood receded, the town's Main Street was a chasm in which sewer pipes could be seen.</p>
<p>To the east, the surge left <a href="/wiki/Falmouth,_Massachusetts" title="Falmouth, Massachusetts">Falmouth</a> and <a href="/wiki/New_Bedford,_Massachusetts" title="New Bedford, Massachusetts">New Bedford</a> under eight feet of water. Two-thirds of the boats sank in New Bedford harbor. Several homes washed away on Atlantic Boulevard in <a href="/wiki/Fall_River,_Massachusetts" title="Fall River, Massachusetts">Fall River</a>, whose foundations can still be found on the beach today. The <a href="/wiki/Blue_Hill_Meteorological_Observatory" title="Blue Hill Meteorological Observatory">Blue Hill Observatory</a> registered sustained winds of 121 mph (195 km/h) and a peak gust of 186 mph (299 km/h), which is the strongest hurricane-related surface wind gust ever recorded in the United States.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> A 50-foot wave, the tallest of the storm, was recorded at <a href="/wiki/Gloucester,_Massachusetts" title="Gloucester, Massachusetts">Gloucester</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Uptoncommerativehome_10-9"><a href="#cite_note-Uptoncommerativehome-10">[10]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Vermont">Vermont</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=1938_New_England_hurricane&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Vermont">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The storm entered <a href="/wiki/Vermont" title="Vermont">Vermont</a> as a Category 1 hurricane at approximately 6:00 pm EDT, reaching northern Vermont, <a href="/wiki/Burlington,_Vermont" title="Burlington, Vermont">Burlington</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Lake_Champlain" title="Lake Champlain">Lake Champlain</a> around 8:00 pm.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AmericanExperience_29-1"><a href="#cite_note-AmericanExperience-29">[29]</a></sup> Hurricane-force winds caused extensive damage to trees, buildings, and power lines. Over 2,000 miles (3,200 km) of public roads were blocked, and it took months for crews to reopen some of the roads. In <a href="/wiki/Montpelier,_Vermont" title="Montpelier, Vermont">Montpelier</a>, 120 miles (190 km) from the nearest coast, salt spray was seen on windows.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> A train was derailed in <a href="/wiki/Castleton,_Vermont" title="Castleton, Vermont">Castleton</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AmericanExperience_29-2"><a href="#cite_note-AmericanExperience-29">[29]</a></sup> The storm killed five people in Vermont. Maple and sugar groves were damaged.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> The 1938 hurricane has been the only tropical cyclone to make a direct hit on Vermont in its recorded history; it remains the only one known to have struck Vermont as a hurricane.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="New_Hampshire">New Hampshire</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=1938_New_England_hurricane&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: New Hampshire">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:NH_Hurricane-l.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="584" data-file-width="1024" height="125" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/36/NH_Hurricane-l.jpg/220px-NH_Hurricane-l.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/36/NH_Hurricane-l.jpg/330px-NH_Hurricane-l.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/36/NH_Hurricane-l.jpg/440px-NH_Hurricane-l.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:NH_Hurricane-l.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Damage done to pine forests in <a href="/wiki/Wolfeboro,_New_Hampshire" title="Wolfeboro, New Hampshire">Wolfeboro, New Hampshire</a>, 1938, by <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Peter_Roome&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Peter Roome (page does not exist)">Peter Roome</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Even though the storm center tracked further west, through Vermont, <a href="/wiki/New_Hampshire" title="New Hampshire">New Hampshire</a> received appreciable damage. As in Vermont, very high winds brought down numerous trees and electric lines, but rainfall totals in New Hampshire were significantly less than those in other states. Only 1 inch (25 mm) of rain fell in <a href="/wiki/Concord,_New_Hampshire" title="Concord, New Hampshire">Concord</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2007)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Damage at <a href="/wiki/Peterborough,_New_Hampshire" title="Peterborough, New Hampshire">Peterborough</a> was worse, however; total damage there was stated to be $500,000 (1938 dollars, $6.5 million in 2005), which included the destruction of 10 bridges. Much of the lower downtown burned because floodwaters prevented firefighters from reaching and extinguishing the blaze. Other communities also suffered considerable damage to forest resources. In New Hampshire, 13 people perished. At <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mount_Washington,_New_Hampshire" title="Mount Washington, New Hampshire">Mt. Washington</a>, winds gusted to 163 miles per hour (262 km/h) and knocked down part of a trestle on the <a href="/wiki/Mount_Washington_Cog_Railway" title="Mount Washington Cog Railway">Cog Railway</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AmericanExperience_29-3"><a href="#cite_note-AmericanExperience-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Maine">Maine</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=1938_New_England_hurricane&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Maine">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Maine" title="Maine">Maine</a>, buildings and trees were damaged and power outages occurred. Storm surge was minimal, and winds remained below hurricane strength. The storm did not claim any lives in Maine.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AmericanExperience_29-4"><a href="#cite_note-AmericanExperience-29">[29]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Maryland_and_Delaware">Maryland and Delaware</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=1938_New_England_hurricane&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Maryland and Delaware">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The western periphery of the hurricane brought heavy rain and gusty winds to Delaware and southeastern Maryland.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup> Damage, if any, is believed to have been minimal.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Quebec">Quebec</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=1938_New_England_hurricane&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Quebec">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>As the hurricane was transitioning into an <a href="/wiki/Extratropical_cyclone" title="Extratropical cyclone">extratropical cyclone</a>, it tracked into southern Quebec. By the time the system initially crossed into Canada, it continued to produce heavy rain and very strong winds, but interaction with land had taken its toll. Nevertheless, the hurricane managed to blow down numerous trees throughout the region.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup> Otherwise, damage was generally minimal.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup></p>

<li id="cite_note-7"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-7">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">Winds rotate counter-clockwise around all low pressure systems in the Northern Hemisphere. Winds on the right side of a hurricane (relative to the direction of the storm itself) are moving in the same general direction as the hurricane. Therefore, the forward motion increases the observed wind speed for points to the right of the eye of the hurricane and decreases the observed wind speed for points to the left of the eye.
