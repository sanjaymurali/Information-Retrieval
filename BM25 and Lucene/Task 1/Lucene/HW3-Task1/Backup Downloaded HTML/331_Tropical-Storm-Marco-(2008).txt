<p><b>Tropical Storm Marco</b> was the smallest <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone" title="Tropical cyclone">tropical cyclone</a> on record. The thirteenth <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone#Naming" title="Tropical cyclone">named storm</a> of the <a href="/wiki/2008_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="2008 Atlantic hurricane season">2008 Atlantic hurricane season</a>, Marco developed out of a broad <a href="/wiki/Low-pressure_area" title="Low-pressure area">area of low pressure</a> over the northwestern Caribbean during late September 2008. Influenced by a <a href="/wiki/Tropical_wave" title="Tropical wave">tropical wave</a> on October 4, a small low-level circulation center developed over <a href="/wiki/Belize" title="Belize">Belize</a>. After crossing the southern end of the <a href="/wiki/Yucat%C3%A1n_Peninsula" title="Yucatán Peninsula">Yucatán Peninsula</a> and emerging into the Bay of Campeche, the low was declared Tropical Depression Thirteen early on October 6. The depression quickly intensified into a tropical storm and was given the name <i>Marco</i> later that day. Marco reached its peak intensity with winds of 65 mph (100 km/h) early on October 7. Around this time, tropical storm force winds extended 11.5 miles (18.5 km) from the center of the storm, making Marco the smallest tropical cyclone on record.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FAQ_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-FAQ-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TCR_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-TCR-2">[2]</a></sup> Around 1200 UTC, Marco made landfall near <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Misantla,_Veracruz" title="Misantla, Veracruz">Misantla, Veracruz</a>. The storm rapidly weakened after landfall, dissipating later that day. Due to its small size, Marco caused minimal damage; however, the storm's heavy rains led to floods up to 10 feet (3.05 m) deep that covered highways and damaged homes.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Meteorological_history">Meteorological history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tropical_Storm_Marco_(2008)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Meteorological history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Marco_2008_track.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="633" data-file-width="1024" height="136" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5d/Marco_2008_track.png/220px-Marco_2008_track.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5d/Marco_2008_track.png/330px-Marco_2008_track.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5d/Marco_2008_track.png/440px-Marco_2008_track.png 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Marco_2008_track.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Map plotting the track and intensity of the storm, according to the <a href="/wiki/Saffir%E2%80%93Simpson_scale" title="Saffir–Simpson scale">Saffir–Simpson scale</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Tropical Storm Marco originated in a broad <a href="/wiki/Low-pressure_area" title="Low-pressure area">area of low pressure</a> that persisted over the northwestern Caribbean in late September 2008. On October 4, a <a href="/wiki/Tropical_wave" title="Tropical wave">tropical wave</a> reached the same area, and the system spawned a <a href="/wiki/Eye_(cyclone)" title="Eye (cyclone)">circulation center</a> over <a href="/wiki/Belize" title="Belize">Belize</a>. <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclogenesis" title="Tropical cyclogenesis">Development</a> of the low was initially inhibited by its proximity to land. As the system neared the <a href="/wiki/Bay_of_Campeche" title="Bay of Campeche">Bay of Campeche</a>, <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_convection" title="Atmospheric convection">convection</a> quickly developed around the low. At 0000 <a href="/wiki/Coordinated_Universal_Time" title="Coordinated Universal Time">UTC</a> on October 6, the low was designated as Tropical Depression Thirteen while located over <a href="/wiki/Laguna_de_T%C3%A9rminos" title="Laguna de Términos">Laguna de Términos</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TCR_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-TCR-2">[2]</a></sup> A mid-level <a href="/wiki/Ridge_(meteorology)" title="Ridge (meteorology)">ridge</a> located to the north of the depression led to movement in a general west-northwest direction. Forecasters anticipated intensification up until landfall because of the storm's well-developed <a href="/wiki/Outflow_(meteorology)" title="Outflow (meteorology)">outflow</a> and the low <a href="/wiki/Wind_shear" title="Wind shear">wind shear</a> and high <a href="/wiki/Sea_surface_temperature" title="Sea surface temperature">sea surface temperatures</a> in its path.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-D1_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-D1-3">[3]</a></sup> By 1200 UTC, the small cyclone, with a cloud shield no more than 85 miles (137 kilometers) across, was upgraded to Tropical Storm Marco.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TCR_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-TCR-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Favorable conditions for development allowed Marco to quickly intensify throughout the day on October 6. Early on October 7, Marco reached its peak intensity with winds of 65 mph (105 km/h) and a minimum pressure of 998 <a href="/wiki/Bar_(unit)" title="Bar (unit)">millibar</a> (<a href="/wiki/Pascal_(unit)" title="Pascal (unit)">hPa</a>; 29.47 <a href="/wiki/Inch_of_mercury" title="Inch of mercury">inHg</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TCR_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-TCR-2">[2]</a></sup> This was based on a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hurricane_Hunters" title="Hurricane Hunters">reconnaissance mission</a> into Marco which recorded flight-level winds of 70 mph (110 km/h), corresponding to a surface wind speed of 61 mph (98 km/h). Following the quick increase in intensity, forecasters noted the possibility of Marco intensifying into a <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone#Hurricane_or_typhoon" title="Tropical cyclone">hurricane</a> before making <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Landfall_(meteorology)" title="Landfall (meteorology)">landfall</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-D2_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-D2-4">[4]</a></sup> The storm maintained a small area of deep convection, averaging 9.2 miles (14.8 km) in diameter, as it continued moving towards the west-northwest.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-D3_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-D3-5">[5]</a></sup> Shortly after reaching peak intensity, tropical storm force winds extended 11.5 miles (18.5 km) from the center of Marco.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-D5_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-D5-6">[6]</a></sup> At 1200 UTC, the center of Marco made landfall near <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Misantla,_Veracruz" title="Misantla, Veracruz">Misantla, Veracruz</a>, with winds of 65 mph (105 km/h).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TCR_2-4"><a href="#cite_note-TCR-2">[2]</a></sup> Once inland, Marco rapidly weakened,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-D6_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-D6-7">[7]</a></sup> being downgraded to a tropical depression six hours after landfall. The small depression dissipated later that day over the mountains of Mexico.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TCR_2-5"><a href="#cite_note-TCR-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span id="Preparations,_impact_and_records"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Preparations.2C_impact_and_records">Preparations, impact and records</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tropical_Storm_Marco_(2008)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Preparations, impact and records">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

Tropical Storms Marco (center), <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Norbert_(2008)" title="Hurricane Norbert (2008)">Norbert</a> (bottom left), and the low that would eventually become <a href="/wiki/Tropical_Storm_Odile_(2008)" title="Tropical Storm Odile (2008)">Odile</a> (bottom) on October 6.</div>

</div>
<p>Upon the storm's formation, the Government of Mexico issued a <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone_warnings_and_watches#Tropical_Storm_Warning" title="Tropical cyclone warnings and watches">tropical storm warning</a> for the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_of_Mexico" title="Gulf of Mexico">Gulf of Mexico</a> from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tuxpan,_Veracruz" title="Tuxpan, Veracruz">Tuxpan</a> to Punta El Lagarto.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-public01_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-public01-8">[8]</a></sup> That afternoon the government issued a <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone_warnings_and_watches#Hurricane_Watch" title="Tropical cyclone warnings and watches">hurricane watch</a> between <a href="/wiki/Cabo_Rojo_(Mexico)" title="Cabo Rojo (Mexico)">Cabo Rojo</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Veracruz,_Veracruz" title="Veracruz, Veracruz">Veracruz</a>, and extended the tropical storm warning northward to Cabo Rojo.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-public02_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-public02-9">[9]</a></sup> Officials closed schools ahead of the storm and opened 200 shelters.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-USAT1_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-USAT1-10">[10]</a></sup> An estimated 3,000 people were evacuated from low-lying areas along the coast.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MSNBC1_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-MSNBC1-11">[11]</a></sup> Soldiers used school buses to transport evacuees to the shelters.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-USAT1_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-USAT1-10">[10]</a></sup> Marco formed in the area of Mexico's main oil-facilities,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Marco_forms_near_oil_fields_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-Marco_forms_near_oil_fields-12">[12]</a></sup> leading to the evacuation of 33 workers from four platforms. Six oil wells and a natural gas processing plant were also shut down in Veracruz.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-USAT1_10-2"><a href="#cite_note-USAT1-10">[10]</a></sup> The Mexican <a href="/wiki/Secretariat_of_Communications_and_Transportation_(Mexico)" title="Secretariat of Communications and Transportation (Mexico)">Secretariat of Communications and Transportation</a> also closed the ports of <a href="/wiki/Nautla,_Veracruz" title="Nautla, Veracruz">Nautla</a> and <a href="/wiki/Alvarado,_Veracruz" title="Alvarado, Veracruz">Alvarado</a> to small vessels as a precautionary measure.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>

Marco shortly after being upgraded to a tropical storm on October 6</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Upon landfall, heavy rains peaking at 7.9 inches (201 millimeters) in <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=El_Pujal&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="El Pujal (page does not exist)">El Pujal</a>, <a href="/wiki/San_Luis_Potos%C3%AD" title="San Luis Potosí">San Luis Potosí</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> and falling at rates up to 1 inch per hour (25.4 mm/h),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TCR_2-6"><a href="#cite_note-TCR-2">[2]</a></sup> caused some flooding in coastal towns near <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Veracruz,_Veracruz" title="Veracruz, Veracruz">Veracruz</a> as people evacuated to higher ground.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Marco_lashes_Mexico.27s_Gulf_coast_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-Marco_lashes_Mexico.27s_Gulf_coast-15">[15]</a></sup> The rains from Marco worsened flood situations in areas of Mexico already suffering from severe flooding.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-USAT1_10-3"><a href="#cite_note-USAT1-10">[10]</a></sup> Officials in Veracruz, in their post-storm damage survey, reported that two rivers, the Quilate and Tenoch, overflowed their banks due to rains produced by Marco.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> One of these rivers left the towns of Minatitlan and Hidalgotitlan under 10 ft (3 m) of water.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-USAT1_10-4"><a href="#cite_note-USAT1-10">[10]</a></sup> Highways along the coast of Veracruz were also flooded.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RW1_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-RW1-17">[17]</a></sup> Another 250 homes were flooded when a lake and a river overflowed their banks.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LJ1_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-LJ1-18">[18]</a></sup> Thirteen municipalities within Veracruz were affected by Marco. In Vega de Alatorre, 77 people were evacuated to nearby shelters after their homes were inundated with water. Three landslides were also reported in Misantla Colipa; none of them caused damage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> In all, Marco's impact was light; minimal damage was recorded, and none of the estimated 400,000 people affected by the storm sustained injury.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TCR_2-7"><a href="#cite_note-TCR-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the wake of Marco, the General Coordination of Civil Protection of the Ministry of the Interior declared a <a href="/wiki/State_of_emergency" title="State of emergency">state of emergency</a> for 48 municipalities in <a href="/wiki/Veracruz" title="Veracruz">Veracruz</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> Relief goods were distributed to the affected areas by October 9. The Government of Mexico reported that 4,700 blankets, 2,900 mattresses, 5,554 bottles of water (each containing 500 milliliters), 260,000 boxes of milk, 250,000 packages of biscuits, and 12,400 boxes of school supplies had been distributed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<p>At 0052 UTC on October 7, tropical storm force winds extended 11.5 miles (18.5 km) from the center of Marco. This made Marco the smallest tropical cyclone ever recorded, surpassing the previous record set on December 24, 1974 by <a href="/wiki/Cyclone_Tracy" title="Cyclone Tracy">Cyclone Tracy</a>, whose tropical storm-force winds extended 30 miles (48 km).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FAQ_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-FAQ-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TCR_2-8"><a href="#cite_note-TCR-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ATLHURDAT_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-ATLHURDAT-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
