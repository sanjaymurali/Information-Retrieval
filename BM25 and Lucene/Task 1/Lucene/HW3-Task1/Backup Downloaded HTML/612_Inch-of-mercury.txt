<p><b>Inch of mercury</b> (<b>inHg</b> and <b>"Hg</b>) is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Unit_of_measurement" title="Unit of measurement">unit of measurement</a> for <a href="/wiki/Pressure" title="Pressure">pressure</a>. It is still used for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Barometric_pressure" title="Barometric pressure">barometric pressure</a> in <a href="/wiki/Weather_forecasting" title="Weather forecasting">weather reports</a>, <a href="/wiki/Refrigeration" title="Refrigeration">refrigeration</a> and <a href="/wiki/Aviation" title="Aviation">aviation</a> in the <a href="/wiki/United_States_customary_units" title="United States customary units">United States</a>.</p>
<p>It is the pressure exerted by a column of <a href="/wiki/Mercury_(element)" title="Mercury (element)">mercury</a> of 1 inch (25.4 mm) in height at the <a href="/wiki/Standard_gravity" title="Standard gravity">standard acceleration of gravity</a>. Conversion to metric units depends on the temperature of mercury, and hence its density; typical conversion factors are:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NIST811_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-NIST811-1">[1]</a></sup></p>

<th>Pressure</th>

<td>3386.38 pascals</td>

<p>Aircraft <a href="/wiki/Altimeter" title="Altimeter">altimeters</a> measure the relative pressure difference between the lower ambient pressure at altitude and a calibrated reading on the ground. Within the United States and Canada, these readings are provided in inches of mercury. Ground readings vary with weather and along the route of the aircraft as it travels, so current readings are relayed periodically by air traffic control. Aircraft operating at higher altitudes (at or above what is called the <a href="/wiki/Flight_level#Transition_altitude" title="Flight level">transition altitude</a>, which varies by country) set their barometric <a href="/wiki/Altimeter" title="Altimeter">altimeters</a> to a standard pressure of 29.92 inHg (1 atm = 29.92 inHg) or 1013.25 <a href="/wiki/Pascal_(unit)" title="Pascal (unit)">hPa</a> (1 hPa = 1 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mbar" title="Mbar">mbar</a>) regardless of the actual sea level pressure. The resulting altimeter readings are known as <a href="/wiki/Flight_level" title="Flight level">flight levels</a>.</p>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Piston_engine" title="Piston engine">Piston engine</a> <a href="/wiki/Aircraft" title="Aircraft">aircraft</a> with <a href="/wiki/Constant-speed_propeller" title="Constant-speed propeller">constant-speed propellers</a> also use inches of mercury to measure <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Manifold_pressure" title="Manifold pressure">manifold pressure</a>, which is indicative of engine power produced. In automobile racing, particularly <a href="/wiki/United_States_Auto_Club" title="United States Auto Club">United States Auto Club</a> and <a href="/wiki/Champ_Car" title="Champ Car">Champ Car</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Indy_car_racing" title="Indy car racing">Indy car racing</a>, inches of mercury was the unit used to measure <a href="/wiki/Turbocharger" title="Turbocharger">turbocharger</a> inlet pressure. However, the inch of mercury is still used today in car performance modification to measure the amount of vacuum within the engine's intake manifold. This can be seen on boost/vacuum gauges.</p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Air_conditioning" title="Air conditioning">air conditioning</a> and refrigeration, inHg is often used to describe "inches of mercury vacuum", or pressures below ambient atmospheric pressure, for recovery of refrigerants from air conditioning and refrigeration systems, as well as for leak testing of systems while under a vacuum, and for dehydration of refrigeration systems. The low-side gauge in a refrigeration gauge manifold indicates pressures below ambient in "inches of mercury vacuum" (inHg), down to a 30 inHg vacuum.</p>
<p>Inches of mercury is also used in automotive cooling system vacuum test and fill tools. A technician will use this tool to remove air from modern automotive cooling systems, test the systems ability to hold vacuum and subsequently refill using the vacuum as suction for the new coolant. Typical minimum vacuum values are between 22 and 27 inHg.</p>
<p>In older literature, an "inch of mercury" is based on the height of a column of mercury at 60 °F (15.6 °C) <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NIST811_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-NIST811-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<dl>
<dd>1 inHg<sub>60 °F</sub> = 3376.85 Pa</dd>
</dl>
<p>In English units: 1 inHg<sub>60 °F</sub> = 0.489 771 <a href="/wiki/Pounds_per_square_inch" title="Pounds per square inch">psi</a>, or 2.041 771 inHg<sub>60 °F</sub> = 1 psi.</p>
