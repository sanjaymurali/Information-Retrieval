<p>The <b>Great Galveston Hurricane</b><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup>, known regionally as the Great Storm of 1900 <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>, was a Category 4 storm, with winds of up to 145 mph (233 km/h), which made landfall on September 8, 1900, in <a href="/wiki/Galveston,_Texas" title="Galveston, Texas">Galveston, Texas</a>, in the United States. It killed 6,000 to 12,000 people, making it the deadliest hurricane and natural disaster in U.S. history.</p>
<p>The hurricane appears to have started as an atmospheric trough from West Africa, causing unsettled weather in the Caribbean, and emerging into the Florida Straits as a tropical storm on September 5. Owing to contradictory forecasts, the people of Galveston felt no alarm until the official hurricane warning of September 7. The next morning, a storm surge of 15 ft (4.6 m) washed over the long, flat island-city, which was only 8 ft (2.4 m) above sea level, knocking buildings off their foundations and destroying over 3,600 homes.</p>
<p>The disaster ended the Golden Era of Galveston, as the hurricane alarmed potential investors, who turned to Houston instead. The Gulf of Mexico shoreline of Galveston island was subsequently raised by 17 ft (5.2 m) and a 10 mi (16 km) seawall erected.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Deadliest_US_hurricane">Deadliest US hurricane</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=1900_Galveston_hurricane&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Deadliest US hurricane">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Hurricane of 1900 made <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Landfall_(meteorology)" title="Landfall (meteorology)">landfall</a> on September 8, 1900, in <a href="/wiki/Galveston,_Texas" title="Galveston, Texas">Galveston, Texas</a>, in the United States.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gibson_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Gibson-6">[6]</a></sup> It had estimated winds of 145 miles per hour (233 km/h) at landfall, making it a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saffir%E2%80%93Simpson_Hurricane_Scale#Category_4" title="Saffir–Simpson Hurricane Scale">Category 4</a> storm on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saffir%E2%80%93Simpson_Hurricane_Scale" title="Saffir–Simpson Hurricane Scale">Saffir–Simpson Hurricane Scale</a>. It was the deadliest hurricane in US history, and the second costliest hurricane in U.S. history based on the dollar's 2005 value (to compare costs with those of <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Katrina" title="Hurricane Katrina">Hurricane Katrina</a> and others).</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone" title="Tropical cyclone">hurricane</a> caused great loss of life with a death toll of between 6,000 and 12,000 people;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HoT_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-HoT-7">[7]</a></sup> the number most cited in official reports is 8,000, giving the storm the third-highest number of deaths or injuries of all <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/North_Atlantic_tropical_cyclones" title="North Atlantic tropical cyclones">Atlantic hurricanes</a>, after the <a href="/wiki/Great_Hurricane_of_1780" title="Great Hurricane of 1780">Great Hurricane of 1780</a> and <a href="/wiki/1998_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="1998 Atlantic hurricane season">1998</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Mitch" title="Hurricane Mitch">Hurricane Mitch</a>. The Galveston Hurricane of 1900 is one of the deadliest <a href="/wiki/Natural_disaster" title="Natural disaster">natural disasters</a> ever to strike the United States. This loss of life can be attributed to the fact that officials for the Weather Bureau in Galveston brushed off the reports because the city had "weathered them all" and they didn't realize the threat.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> The second-deadliest storm to strike the United States, the <a href="/wiki/1928_Okeechobee_hurricane" title="1928 Okeechobee hurricane">1928 Okeechobee hurricane</a>, caused more than 2,500 deaths, and the deadliest storm of recent times, <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Katrina" title="Hurricane Katrina">Hurricane Katrina</a>, claimed the lives of approximately 1,800 people.</p>
<p>The hurricane occurred before the practice of assigning official code names to tropical storms was instituted, and thus it is commonly referred to under a variety of descriptive names. Typical names for the storm include the Galveston Hurricane of 1900, the Great Galveston Hurricane, and, especially in older documents, the Galveston Flood. It is often referred to by Galveston locals as the Great Storm or the 1900 Storm.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Meteorological_history">Meteorological history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=1900_Galveston_hurricane&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Meteorological history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:352px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:1900_Galveston_hurricane_track.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1861" data-file-width="2700" height="241" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/12/1900_Galveston_hurricane_track.png/350px-1900_Galveston_hurricane_track.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/12/1900_Galveston_hurricane_track.png/525px-1900_Galveston_hurricane_track.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/12/1900_Galveston_hurricane_track.png/700px-1900_Galveston_hurricane_track.png 2x" width="350"/></a>

Map plotting the track and intensity of the storm, according to the <a href="/wiki/Saffir%E2%80%93Simpson_scale" title="Saffir–Simpson scale">Saffir–Simpson scale</a></div>

<p>The storm's origins are unclear, because of the limited observation ability at the end of the 19th century. Ship reports were the only reliable tool for observing hurricanes at sea, and because <a href="/wiki/Wireless_telegraphy" title="Wireless telegraphy">wireless telegraphy</a> was in its infancy, these reports were not available until the ships put in at a harbor. The 1900 storm, like many powerful Atlantic hurricanes, is believed to have begun as a <a href="/wiki/Cape_Verde_hurricane" title="Cape Verde hurricane">Cape Verde–type hurricane</a>—a <a href="/wiki/Tropical_wave" title="Tropical wave">tropical wave</a> moving off the western coast of Africa. The first formal sighting of the hurricane's precursor occurred on August 27, about 1,000 miles (1,600 km) east of the <a href="/wiki/Windward_Islands" title="Windward Islands">Windward Islands</a>, when a ship recorded an area of "unsettled weather". The storm passed through the <a href="/wiki/Leeward_Islands" title="Leeward Islands">Leeward Islands</a> on August 30, probably as a tropical depression as indicated by <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_pressure" title="Atmospheric pressure">barometric pressure</a> reports from <a href="/wiki/Antigua" title="Antigua">Antigua</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sh_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-sh-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>Three days later, Antigua reported a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Severe_thunderstorm" title="Severe thunderstorm">severe thunderstorm</a> passing over, followed by the hot, humid calmness that often occurs after the passage of a tropical cyclone. By September 1, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._Weather_Bureau" title="U.S. Weather Bureau">U.S. Weather Bureau</a> observers were reporting on a "storm of moderate intensity (not a hurricane)" southeast of <a href="/wiki/Cuba" title="Cuba">Cuba</a>. Continuing westward, the storm made landfall on southwest Cuba on September 3, dropping heavy rains. On September 5, it emerged into the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Florida_Straits" title="Florida Straits">Florida Straits</a> as a tropical storm or a weak hurricane.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sh_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-sh-9">[9]</a></sup> The Weather Bureau ignored reports from Cuban meteorologists because they expected the storm to curve northeast along the coast of North America: "Assumption became fact as the official government reports stated, wrongly, that the storm was traveling northeast in the Atlantic." However, a region of high pressure had pushed the storm to the west into the Gulf of Mexico.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-met_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-met-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>The storm was reported to be north of <a href="/wiki/Key_West" title="Key West">Key West</a> on September 6,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> and in the early morning hours of Friday, September 7, the Weather Bureau office in New Orleans, Louisiana, issued a report of heavy damage along the <a href="/wiki/Louisiana" title="Louisiana">Louisiana</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mississippi" title="Mississippi">Mississippi</a> coasts. Details of the storm were not widespread; damage to telegraph lines limited communication.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Texas_Handbook_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-Texas_Handbook-12">[12]</a></sup> The Weather Bureau's central office in Washington, D.C., ordered storm warnings raised from <a href="/wiki/Pensacola,_Florida" title="Pensacola, Florida">Pensacola, Florida</a>, to Galveston. By the afternoon of September 7, large swells from the southeast were observed on the Gulf, and clouds at all altitudes began moving in from the northeast. Both of these observations are consistent with a hurricane approaching from the east. The Galveston Weather Bureau office raised its double square flags; a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hurricane_warning" title="Hurricane warning">hurricane warning</a> was in effect. The ship <i>Louisiana</i> encountered the hurricane at 1 p.m. that day after departing New Orleans. Captain Halsey estimated wind speeds of 100 mph (160 km/h). These winds correspond to a Category 2 hurricane in the modern-day <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saffir%E2%80%93Simpson_Hurricane_Scale" title="Saffir–Simpson Hurricane Scale">Saffir–Simpson Hurricane Scale</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sh_9-2"><a href="#cite_note-sh-9">[9]</a></sup></p>

<p>By early afternoon on Saturday, September 8, a steady northeastern wind had picked up. By 5 p.m., the Bureau office was recording sustained hurricane-force winds. That night, the wind direction shifted to the east, and then to the southeast as the hurricane's eye began to pass over the island just west of the city. By 11 p.m., the wind was southerly and diminishing. On Sunday morning, clear skies and a 20 mph (30 km/h) breeze off the Gulf of Mexico greeted the Galveston survivors.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sh_9-3"><a href="#cite_note-sh-9">[9]</a></sup> The storm continued on, and later tracked into <a href="/wiki/Oklahoma" title="Oklahoma">Oklahoma</a>. From there, it continued over the <a href="/wiki/Great_Lakes" title="Great Lakes">Great Lakes</a> while still sustaining winds of almost 40 mph (as recorded over <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Milwaukee,_Wisconsin" title="Milwaukee, Wisconsin">Milwaukee, Wisconsin</a>) and passed north of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/City_of_Halifax" title="City of Halifax">Halifax</a>, Nova Scotia, on September 12, 1900.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MRWUSWB_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-MRWUSWB-13">[13]</a></sup> From there it traveled into the North Atlantic where it disappeared from observations,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HoT_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-HoT-7">[7]</a></sup> after decimating a schooner fleet fishing off the coast of <a href="/wiki/Newfoundland_and_Labrador" title="Newfoundland and Labrador">Newfoundland</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Background">Background</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=1900_Galveston_hurricane&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Background">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Galveston,_Texas" title="Galveston, Texas">Galveston, Texas</a></div>
<p>At the end of the 19th century, the city of Galveston, Texas, was a booming town with a population of 36,000 residents.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> Its position on the natural harbor of <a href="/wiki/Galveston_Bay" title="Galveston Bay">Galveston Bay</a> along the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_of_Mexico" title="Gulf of Mexico">Gulf of Mexico</a> made it the center of trade and one of the biggest cities in the state of Texas. With this prosperity came a sense of complacency.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Texas_Handbook_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-Texas_Handbook-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>A quarter of a century earlier, the nearby town of <a href="/wiki/Indianola,_Texas" title="Indianola, Texas">Indianola</a> on <a href="/wiki/Matagorda_Bay" title="Matagorda Bay">Matagorda Bay</a> was undergoing its own boom and was second to Galveston among Texas port cities. Then in 1875, a powerful hurricane blew through, nearly destroying the town. Indianola was rebuilt, though a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Indianola_Hurricane_of_1886" title="Indianola Hurricane of 1886">second hurricane in 1886</a> caused residents to simply give up and move elsewhere.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> Many Galveston residents took the destruction of Indianola as an object lesson on the threat posed by hurricanes. Galveston is built on a <a href="/wiki/Galveston_Island" title="Galveston Island">low, flat island</a>, little more than a large <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sandbar" title="Sandbar">sandbar</a> along the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gulf_Coast" title="Gulf Coast">Gulf Coast</a>. These residents proposed a <a href="/wiki/Seawall" title="Seawall">seawall</a> be constructed to protect the city, but their concerns were dismissed by the majority of the population and the city's government.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>Since its formal founding in 1839, the city of Galveston had weathered numerous storms, all of which the city survived with ease. Residents believed any future storms would be no worse than previous events.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> In order to provide an official <a href="/wiki/Meteorology" title="Meteorology">meteorological</a> statement on the threat of hurricanes, Galveston Weather Bureau section director <a href="/wiki/Isaac_Cline" title="Isaac Cline">Isaac Cline</a> wrote an 1891 article in the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Galveston_Daily_News" title="Galveston Daily News">Galveston Daily News</a></i> in which he argued not only that a seawall was <i>not</i> needed to protect the city, but also that it would be impossible for a hurricane of significant strength to strike the island.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-weatherdoctor_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-weatherdoctor-18">[18]</a></sup> The seawall was not built, and development activities on the island actively increased its vulnerability to storms. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sand_dune" title="Sand dune">Sand dunes</a> along the shore were cut down to fill low areas in the city, removing what little barrier there was to the Gulf of Mexico.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Preparations">Preparations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=1900_Galveston_hurricane&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Preparations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>On September 4, the Galveston office of the <a href="/wiki/National_Weather_Service" title="National Weather Service">National Weather Bureau</a> (as it was then called) began receiving warnings from the Bureau's central office in Washington, D.C., that a "tropical storm" had moved northward over Cuba. The Weather Bureau forecasters had no way of knowing where the storm was or where it was going. At the time, they discouraged the use of terms such as <i>tornado</i> or <i>hurricane</i> to avoid panicking residents in the path of any storm event. Conditions in the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_of_Mexico" title="Gulf of Mexico">Gulf of Mexico</a> were ripe for further strengthening of the storm. The Gulf had seen little cloud cover for several weeks, and the seas were as warm as bathwater, according to one report. For a storm system that feeds off moisture, the Gulf of Mexico was enough to boost the storm from a tropical storm to a hurricane in a matter of days, with further strengthening likely.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sh_9-4"><a href="#cite_note-sh-9">[9]</a></sup></p>

<p>Weather Bureau forecasters believed the storm would travel northeast and affect the mid-Atlantic coast. "To them, the storm appeared to have begun a long turn or 'recurve' that would take it first into Florida, then drive it northeast toward an eventual exit into the Atlantic."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Larson_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-Larson-20">[20]</a></sup> Cuban forecasters adamantly disagreed, saying the hurricane would continue west. One Cuban forecaster predicted the hurricane would continue into central Texas near <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/San_Antonio,_Texas" title="San Antonio, Texas">San Antonio</a>. Early the next morning, the swells continued despite only partly cloudy skies. Largely because of the unremarkable weather, few residents heeded the warning. Few people evacuated across Galveston's bridges to the mainland, and the majority of the population was unconcerned by the rain clouds that began rolling in by midmorning.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Isaac_Cline" title="Isaac Cline">Isaac Cline</a> claimed that he took it upon himself to travel along the beach and other low-lying areas warning people personally of the storm's approach.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lasso_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-lasso-21">[21]</a></sup> These reports by Cline and his brother, Galveston meteorologist Joseph L. Cline,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Joseph_Cline_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-Joseph_Cline-22">[22]</a></sup> have been called into question in recent years, as no other survivors corroborated these accounts. In fact, Cline's role in the disaster is the subject of some controversy. Supporters point to Cline's issuing a hurricane warning without permission from the Bureau's central office;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> detractors (including author <a href="/wiki/Erik_Larson_(author)" title="Erik Larson (author)">Erik Larson</a>) point to Cline's earlier insistence that a seawall was unnecessary and his belief that an intense hurricane could not strike the island.</p>

<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Texas_evacuees">Texas evacuees</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=1900_Galveston_hurricane&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Texas evacuees">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The first train to reach Galveston left <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Houston,_Texas" title="Houston, Texas">Houston</a> on the morning of September 8 at 9:45 a.m. It found the tracks washed out, and passengers were forced to transfer to a relief train on parallel tracks to complete their journey. Even then, debris on the track slowed the train's progress to a crawl. The 95 travelers on the train from <a href="/wiki/Beaumont,_Texas" title="Beaumont, Texas">Beaumont</a> found themselves at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bolivar_Peninsula" title="Bolivar Peninsula">Bolivar Peninsula</a> waiting for the <a href="/wiki/Ferry" title="Ferry">ferry</a> that would carry them, train and all, to the island. When it arrived, the high seas forced the ferry captain to give up on his attempt to dock. The train crew attempted to return the way they had come, but rising water blocked the train's path.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<p>Ten refugees from the Beaumont train sought shelter at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Point_Bolivar_lighthouse" title="Point Bolivar lighthouse">Point Bolivar lighthouse</a> with 200 residents of <a href="/wiki/Port_Bolivar,_Texas" title="Port Bolivar, Texas">Port Bolivar</a> who were already there. The 85 who stayed with the train died when the <a href="/wiki/Storm_surge" title="Storm surge">storm surge</a> overran the tops of the cars.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-chronicle_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-chronicle-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Galveston">Galveston</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=1900_Galveston_hurricane&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Galveston">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>

<p>At the time of the 1900 storm the highest point in the city of Galveston was only 8.7 feet (2.7 m) above sea level.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-weatherdoctor_18-1"><a href="#cite_note-weatherdoctor-18">[18]</a></sup> The hurricane brought with it a <a href="/wiki/Storm_surge" title="Storm surge">storm surge</a> of over 15 feet (4.6 m)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NOAAcline_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-NOAAcline-28">[28]</a></sup> that washed over the entire island. The surge knocked buildings off their foundations and the surf pounded them to pieces. Over 3,600 homes were destroyed<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NOAAcline_28-1"><a href="#cite_note-NOAAcline-28">[28]</a></sup> and a wall of debris faced the ocean.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lasso_21-1"><a href="#cite_note-lasso-21">[21]</a></sup> The few buildings that survived, mostly solidly built mansions and houses along the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Strand_National_Historic_Landmark_District" title="Strand National Historic Landmark District">Strand District</a>, are today maintained as tourist attractions.</p>
<p>The highest measured wind speed was 100 miles per hour (160 km/h) just after 6 p.m., but the Weather Bureau's <a href="/wiki/Anemometer" title="Anemometer">anemometer</a> was blown off the building shortly after that measurement was recorded.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-weatherdoctor_18-2"><a href="#cite_note-weatherdoctor-18">[18]</a></sup> The eye passed over the city around 8 p.m. Maximum winds were estimated at 120 mph (190 km/h) at the time, though later estimates placed the hurricane at the higher Category 4 classification on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saffir%E2%80%93Simpson_Hurricane_Scale" title="Saffir–Simpson Hurricane Scale">Saffir–Simpson Hurricane Scale</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> The lowest recorded <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Barometric_pressure" title="Barometric pressure">barometric pressure</a> was 28.48 <a href="/wiki/Inch_of_mercury" title="Inch of mercury">inHg</a> (964.4 <a href="/wiki/Pascal_(unit)" title="Pascal (unit)">hPa</a>), considered at the time to be so low as to be obviously in error.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HoT_7-2"><a href="#cite_note-HoT-7">[7]</a></sup> Modern estimates later placed the storm's central pressure at 27.49 inHg (930.9 hPa), but this was subsequently adjusted to the storm's official lowest measured central pressure of 27.63 inHg (935.7 hPa).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup></p>

<p>As severe as the damage to the city's buildings was, the human toll was even greater. Because of the destruction of the bridges to the mainland and the telegraph lines, no word of the city's destruction was able to reach the mainland.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYT_bridges_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-NYT_bridges-31">[31]</a></sup> At 11 a.m. on September 9, one of the few ships at the Galveston wharfs to survive the storm, the <i>Pherabe</i>, arrived in <a href="/wiki/Texas_City,_Texas" title="Texas City, Texas">Texas City</a> on the western side of Galveston Bay. It carried six messengers from the city. When they reached the telegraph office in Houston at 3 a.m. on September 10, a short message was sent to Texas <a href="/wiki/Governor_of_Texas" title="Governor of Texas">Governor</a> <a href="/wiki/Joseph_D._Sayers" title="Joseph D. Sayers">Joseph D. Sayers</a> and U.S. President <a href="/wiki/William_McKinley" title="William McKinley">William McKinley</a>: "I have been deputized by the mayor and Citizen's Committee of Galveston to inform you that the city of Galveston is in ruins." The messengers reported an estimated five hundred dead; this was considered to be an exaggeration at the time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
<p>The citizens of Houston knew a powerful storm had blown through and had made ready to provide assistance. Workers set out by rail and ship for the island almost immediately. Rescuers arrived to find the city completely destroyed. It is believed 8,000 people—20% of the island's population—had lost their lives.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> Estimates range from 6,000 to 12,000.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HoT_7-3"><a href="#cite_note-HoT-7">[7]</a></sup> Most had <a href="/wiki/Drowning" title="Drowning">drowned</a> or been crushed as the waves pounded the <a href="/wiki/Debris" title="Debris">debris</a> that had been their homes hours earlier. Many survived the storm itself but died after several days being trapped under the wreckage of the city, with rescuers unable to reach them. The rescuers could hear the screams of the survivors as they walked on the debris trying to rescue those they could. A further 30,000 were left homeless.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup></p>

<p>The dead bodies were so numerous that burying all of them was impossible. The dead were initially weighted down on barges and dumped at sea, but when the gulf currents washed many of the bodies back onto the beach, a new solution was needed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Funeral_pyre" title="Funeral pyre">Funeral pyres</a> were set up on the beaches, or wherever dead bodies were found, and burned day and night for several weeks after the storm. The authorities passed out free whiskey to sustain the distraught men conscripted for the gruesome work of collecting and burning the dead.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-chronicle_26-1"><a href="#cite_note-chronicle-26">[26]</a></sup> More people were killed in this single storm than the total of those killed in all the tropical cyclones that have struck the United States since. This count is greater than 300 cyclones, as of 2009. The Galveston Hurricane of 1900 remains the deadliest natural disaster in U.S. history.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-randomhouse_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-randomhouse-36">[36]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Midwest">Midwest</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=1900_Galveston_hurricane&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Midwest">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The storm maintained tropical characteristics and Saffir–Simpson tropical storm-force winds (39–50 mph) when it crossed the Upper Midwest, with more than 4 <a href="/wiki/Inch" title="Inch">inches</a> (100 <a href="/wiki/Millimetre" title="Millimetre">mm</a>) of rain being reported over most of the southern third of Wisconsin. Wind created problems all over, including over Lake Michigan, and other areas in the path of the storm had similar problems. (Tropical storms reach Wisconsin and surrounding areas to the east, west, and south once every 50 years on average with others in the 19th century, 1961, and (subject to debate) <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Ike" title="Hurricane Ike">Hurricane Ike</a> in 2008; <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tropical_depressions" title="Tropical depressions">tropical depressions</a> hit twice to three times every two years; and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Subtropical_storm" title="Subtropical storm">subtropical storms</a> and depressions hit equally or more rarely than their tropical counterparts.)<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="New_York_City">New York City</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=1900_Galveston_hurricane&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: New York City">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The rapidly moving storm was still exhibiting winds of 65 mph (105 km/h) by the time it reached New York City on September 12, 1900.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/The_New_York_Times" title="The New York Times">The New York Times</a></i> reported that pedestrian-walking became difficult and that one death was attributed to the storm. A sign pole, snapped by wind, landed on a 23-year-old man, crushing his skull and killing him instantly, while two others were knocked unconscious. Awnings and signs on many buildings broke and the canvas roofing at the Fire Department headquarters was blown off.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NY_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-NY-38">[38]</a></sup></p>
<p>Closer to the waterfront, along the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Battery_Park" title="Battery Park">Battery seawall</a>, waves and tides were reported to be some of the highest in recent memory of the fishermen and sailors. Spray and debris were thrown over the wall, making working along the waterfront dangerous. Small craft in <a href="/wiki/New_York_Harbor" title="New York Harbor">New York Harbor</a> were thrown off course and tides and currents in the <a href="/wiki/Hudson_River" title="Hudson River">Hudson River</a> made navigation difficult. In <a href="/wiki/Brooklyn" title="Brooklyn">Brooklyn</a>, <i>The New York Times</i> reported that trees were uprooted, signs and similar structures were blown down, and yachts were torn from moorings with some suffering severe damage. Because of the direction of the wind, <a href="/wiki/Coney_Island" title="Coney Island">Coney Island</a> escaped the fury of the storm, though a bathing pavilion at <a href="/wiki/Bath_Beach,_Brooklyn" title="Bath Beach, Brooklyn">Bath Beach</a> suffered damage from wind and waves.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NY_38-1"><a href="#cite_note-NY-38">[38]</a></sup></p>

<p>From September 12–14, the <a href="/wiki/Post-tropical_cyclone" title="Post-tropical cyclone">extratropical remnants</a> of the Galveston hurricane affected six <a href="/wiki/Provinces_and_territories_of_Canada" title="Provinces and territories of Canada">Canadian provinces</a>, resulting in severe damage and extensive loss of life. Peak winds reached 50–80 mph (80–125 km/h) in <a href="/wiki/Toronto" title="Toronto">Toronto</a>. During the storm, a fire broke out at a flour mill in <a href="/wiki/Paris,_Ontario" title="Paris, Ontario">Paris, Ontario</a>, resulting in $350,000 in damage to the mill and 50 other stores and offices. Total crop damage in <a href="/wiki/Ontario" title="Ontario">Ontario</a> alone amounted to $1 million (1900 USD). Maximum rainfall reached 3.9 inches (100 mm) in <a href="/wiki/Perc%C3%A9,_Quebec" title="Percé, Quebec">Percé, Quebec</a>. The majority of loss of life occurred due to numerous shipwrecks off the coast of <a href="/wiki/Saint_Pierre_and_Miquelon" title="Saint Pierre and Miquelon">Saint Pierre and Miquelon</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Newfoundland" title="Newfoundland">Newfoundland</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Prince_Edward_Island" title="Prince Edward Island">Prince Edward Island</a>. The overall death toll in Canada is estimated to be between 52 and 232, making this at least the eighth deadliest hurricane to affect Canada. The large discrepancy between the fatality figures is due to the fact that many people were reported missing in Saint Pierre and Miquelon, and later presumed to be dead.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ECC_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-ECC-39">[39]</a></sup></p>

<p>In less than 24 hours the city of Galveston was effectively obliterated. The final death toll is not known with certainty but the most conservative estimate is around 6,000. Most historians believe the loss of life to be in the area of 8,000 with some suggesting as many as 12,000 perished. With the city in ruins and rail lines to the mainland destroyed, the survivors had little to live on until relief arrived.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CNN_43-0"><a href="#cite_note-CNN-43">[43]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Rebuilding">Rebuilding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=1900_Galveston_hurricane&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Rebuilding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Survivors set up temporary shelters in surplus <a href="/wiki/United_States_Army" title="United States Army">U.S. Army</a> tents along the shore. They were so numerous that observers began referring to Galveston as the "White City on the Beach". Others constructed so-called "storm lumber" homes, using salvageable material from the debris to build shelter.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup></p>
<p>Reporter <a href="/wiki/Winifred_Bonfils" title="Winifred Bonfils">Winifred Bonfils</a>, a young journalist working for <a href="/wiki/William_Randolph_Hearst" title="William Randolph Hearst">William Randolph Hearst</a>, was the first reporter on the line at the flood's aftermath. She delivered an exclusive set of reports and Hearst sent relief supplies by train.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup></p>
<p>By September 12, Galveston received its first post-storm mail. The next day, basic water service was restored, and <a href="/wiki/Western_Union" title="Western Union">Western Union</a> began providing minimal <a href="/wiki/Telegraphy" title="Telegraphy">telegraph</a> service. Within three weeks, cotton was again being shipped out of the port.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NOAAcline_28-2"><a href="#cite_note-NOAAcline-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<p>Before the Hurricane of 1900, Galveston was considered to be a beautiful and prestigious city and was known as the "<a href="/wiki/Ellis_Island" title="Ellis Island">Ellis Island</a> of the West" and the "<a href="/wiki/Wall_Street" title="Wall Street">Wall Street</a> of the Southwest".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GALVESTON.COM_46-0"><a href="#cite_note-GALVESTON.COM-46">[46]</a></sup> However, after the storm, development shifted north to Houston, which was enjoying the benefits of the <a href="/wiki/Petroleum" title="Petroleum">oil</a> boom. The dredging of the <a href="/wiki/Houston_Ship_Channel" title="Houston Ship Channel">Houston Ship Channel</a> in 1909 and 1914 ended Galveston's hopes of regaining its former status as a major commercial center.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Protection">Protection</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=1900_Galveston_hurricane&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Protection">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>To prevent future storms from causing destruction like that of the 1900 hurricane, many improvements to the island were made. The first 3 miles (4.8 km) of the <a href="/wiki/Galveston_Seawall" title="Galveston Seawall">Galveston Seawall</a>, 17-foot (5 m) high, were built beginning in 1902 under the direction of <a href="/wiki/Henry_Martyn_Robert" title="Henry Martyn Robert">Henry Martyn Robert</a>. An all-weather bridge was constructed to the mainland to replace the ones destroyed in the storm.</p>
<p>The most dramatic effort to protect the city was its raising. Dredged sand was used to raise the city of Galveston by as much as 17 feet (5.2 m) above its previous elevation. Over 2,100 buildings were raised in the process by the process of pumping sand underneath,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lasso_21-2"><a href="#cite_note-lasso-21">[21]</a></sup> including the 3,000-ton St. Patrick's Church. The seawall and raising of the island were jointly named a <a href="/wiki/List_of_Historic_Civil_Engineering_Landmarks" title="List of Historic Civil Engineering Landmarks">National Historical Civil Engineering Landmark</a> by the <a href="/wiki/American_Society_of_Civil_Engineers" title="American Society of Civil Engineers">American Society of Civil Engineers</a> in 2001.</p>
<p>In 1915, a storm similar in strength and track to the 1900 hurricane struck Galveston. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/1915_Galveston_Hurricane" title="1915 Galveston Hurricane">1915 storm</a> brought a 12-ft (4-m) storm surge that tested the new seawall. Although 53 people on <a href="/wiki/Galveston_Island" title="Galveston Island">Galveston Island</a> lost their lives in the 1915 storm, this was a great reduction from the thousands who died in 1900.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Galveston city government was reorganized into a <a href="/wiki/City_commission_government" title="City commission government">commission government</a>, a newly devised structure wherein the government is made of a small group of commissioners, each responsible for one aspect of governance. This was prompted by fears that the existing city council would be unable to handle the problem of rebuilding the city.</p>
<p>Today, Galveston is home to a cruise port, two universities, and a major insurance corporation. Homes and other buildings that survived the hurricane have been preserved, and give much of the city a <a href="/wiki/Victorian_architecture" title="Victorian architecture">Victorian</a> look. The seawall, since extended to 10 miles (16 km), is now an attraction itself, as hotels and tourist attractions have been built along its length in seeming defiance of future storms.</p>
<p>The last reported survivor of the Galveston Hurricane of 1900, Mrs. Maude Conic of <a href="/wiki/Wharton,_Texas" title="Wharton, Texas">Wharton, Texas</a>, died November 14, 2004, at the claimed age of 116,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup> although the 1910 census and other records indicate she was younger than that.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Historiography">Historiography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=1900_Galveston_hurricane&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Historiography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Historiography" title="Historiography">historiography</a>, the hurricane and the rebuilding afterward divide what is known as the <i>Golden Era</i> (1875–1900) from the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Open_Era_of_Galveston" title="Open Era of Galveston">Open Era</a></i> (1920–1957) of Galveston. The most important long-term impact of the hurricane was to confirm fears that Galveston was a dangerous place to make major investments in shipping and manufacturing operations; the economy of the Golden Era was no longer possible as investors fled. In 1920, <a href="/wiki/Prohibition_in_the_United_States" title="Prohibition in the United States">Prohibition</a> and tax law enforcement opened up new opportunities for <a href="/wiki/Organized_crime" title="Organized crime">criminal enterprises</a> related to gambling and <a href="/wiki/Rum-running" title="Rum-running">bootlegging</a> in the city. Galveston rapidly became a prime <a href="/wiki/Resort" title="Resort">resort</a> destination enabled by the <i>open</i> <a href="/wiki/Vice" title="Vice">vice</a> businesses on the island. This new entertainment-based economy brought decades-long prosperity to the island.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Museum">Museum</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=1900_Galveston_hurricane&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Museum">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Galveston Historical Foundation maintains the Texas Seaport Museum at Pier 21 in the port of Galveston. Included in the museum is a documentary titled <i>The Great Storm</i>, that gives a recounting of the 1900 hurricane.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_popular_culture">In popular culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=1900_Galveston_hurricane&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: In popular culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul>
<li><b>1934</b>: "<a href="/wiki/Wasn%27t_That_a_Mighty_Storm" title="Wasn't That a Mighty Storm">Wasn't That a Mighty Storm</a>" is an American folk song concerning the 1900 Galveston hurricane that originated as a <a href="/wiki/Spiritual_(music)" title="Spiritual (music)">spiritual</a> and was revived and popularized by <a href="/wiki/Eric_Von_Schmidt" title="Eric Von Schmidt">Eric Von Schmidt</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tom_Rush" title="Tom Rush">Tom Rush</a> in the 1960s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup></li>
<li><b>1935</b>: Film director <a href="/wiki/King_Vidor" title="King Vidor">King Vidor</a> was born in Galveston and survived the great Galveston Hurricane of 1900. Based on that experience, he published a fictionalized account of that cyclone, titled "Southern Storm", for the May 1935 issue of <a href="/wiki/Esquire_(magazine)" title="Esquire (magazine)"><i>Esquire</i> magazine</a>. <a href="/wiki/Erik_Larson_(author)" title="Erik Larson (author)">Erik Larson</a> excerpts a passage from that article in his 1999 book, <i><a href="/wiki/Isaac%27s_Storm" title="Isaac's Storm">Isaac's Storm</a></i>:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-randomhouse_36-1"><a href="#cite_note-randomhouse-36">[36]</a></sup>
<blockquote>
<p>I remember now that it seemed as if we were in a bowl looking up toward the level of the sea. As we stood there in the sandy street, my mother and I, I wanted to take my mother's hand and hurry her away. I felt as if the sea was going to break over the edge of the bowl and come puring down upon us.</p>
</blockquote>
</li>
<li><b>1946</b>: Meteorologist Joseph L.
