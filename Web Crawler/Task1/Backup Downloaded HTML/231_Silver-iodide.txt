<p><b>Silver iodide</b> is an <a href="/wiki/Inorganic_compound" title="Inorganic compound">inorganic compound</a> with the formula <a href="/wiki/Silver" title="Silver">Ag</a><a href="/wiki/Iodide" title="Iodide">I</a>. The compound is a bright yellow solid, but samples almost always contain impurities of metallic silver that give a gray coloration. The silver contamination arises because AgI is highly <a href="/wiki/Photosensitivity" title="Photosensitivity">photosensitive</a>. This property is exploited in silver-based <a href="/wiki/Photography" title="Photography">photography</a>. Silver iodide is also used as an <a href="/wiki/Antiseptic" title="Antiseptic">antiseptic</a> and in <a href="/wiki/Cloud_seeding" title="Cloud seeding">cloud seeding</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Structure">Structure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Silver_iodide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Structure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The structure adopted by silver iodide is temperature dependent:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li>Below 420 K, the β-phase of AgI, with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wurtzite_(crystal_structure)" title="Wurtzite (crystal structure)">wurtzite</a> structure, is most stable. This phase is encountered in nature as the mineral <a href="/wiki/Iodargyrite" title="Iodargyrite">iodargyrite</a>.</li>
<li>Above 420 K, the α-phase becomes more stable. This motif is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Body-centered_cubic" title="Body-centered cubic">body-centered cubic</a> structure which has the silver centers distributed randomly between 6 octahedral, 12 tetrahedral and 24 trigonal sites.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> At this temperature, Ag<sup>+</sup> ions can move rapidly through the solid, allowing <a href="/wiki/Fast_ion_conductor" title="Fast ion conductor">fast ion conduction</a>. The transition between the β and α forms represents the melting of the silver (cation) sublattice. The <a href="/wiki/Entropy_of_fusion" title="Entropy of fusion">entropy of fusion</a> for α-AgI is approximately half that for <a href="/wiki/Sodium_chloride" title="Sodium chloride">sodium chloride</a> (a typical ionic solid). This can be rationalized by considering the AgI crystalline lattice to have already "partly melted" in the transition between α and β polymorphs.</li>
<li>A metastable γ-phase also exists below 420 K with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Zincblende_(crystal_structure)" title="Zincblende (crystal structure)">zinc blende structure</a>.</li>
</ul>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Preparation_and_properties">Preparation and properties</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Silver_iodide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Preparation and properties">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Silver iodide is prepared by reaction of an iodide solution (e.g., <a href="/wiki/Potassium_iodide" title="Potassium iodide">potassium iodide</a>) with a solution of silver ions (e.g., <a href="/wiki/Silver_nitrate" title="Silver nitrate">silver nitrate</a>). A yellowish solid quickly <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Precipitate" title="Precipitate">precipitates</a>. The solid is a mixture of the two principal phases. Dissolution of the AgI in <a href="/wiki/Hydroiodic_acid" title="Hydroiodic acid">hydroiodic acid</a>, followed by dilution with water precipitates β-AgI. Alternatively, dissolution of AgI in a solution of concentrated silver nitrate followed by dilution affords α-AgI.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> If the preparation is not conducted in the absence of sunlight, the solid darkens rapidly, the light causing the reduction of ionic silver to metallic. The photosensitivity varies with sample purity.</p>

<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Crystalline_structure" title="Crystalline structure">crystalline structure</a> of β-AgI is similar to that of ice, allowing it to induce <a href="/wiki/Freezing" title="Freezing">freezing</a> by the process known as heterogeneous <a href="/wiki/Nucleation" title="Nucleation">nucleation</a>. Approximately 50,000 kg are used for <a href="/wiki/Cloud_seeding" title="Cloud seeding">cloud seeding</a> annually, each seeding experiment consuming 10–50 grams.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ullmann_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ullmann-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Safety">Safety</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Silver_iodide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Safety">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Extreme exposure can lead to <a href="/wiki/Argyria" title="Argyria">argyria</a>, characterized by localized discoloration of body tissue.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NLM-toxnet_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-NLM-toxnet-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
