<p><a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone" title="Tropical cyclone">Tropical cyclones</a> are officially ranked on one of five <b>tropical cyclone intensity scales</b>, according to their maximum sustained winds and which <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone_basins" title="Tropical cyclone basins">tropical cyclone basin(s)</a> they are located in. Only a few scales of classifications are used officially by the meteorological agencies monitoring the tropical cyclones, but some alternative scales also exist, such as <a href="/wiki/Accumulated_cyclone_energy" title="Accumulated cyclone energy">accumulated cyclone energy</a>, the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Power_Dissipation_Index&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Power Dissipation Index (page does not exist)">Power Dissipation Index</a>, the Integrated Kinetic Energy Index, and the <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Severity_Index" title="Hurricane Severity Index">Hurricane Severity Index</a>.</p>
<p>Tropical cyclones that develop in the <a href="/wiki/Northern_Hemisphere" title="Northern Hemisphere">Northern Hemisphere</a> are officially classified, by the warning centres on one of three intensity scales. Tropical cyclones or subtropical cyclones that exist, within the <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Ocean" title="Atlantic Ocean">North Atlantic Ocean</a> or the North-eastern <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Ocean" title="Pacific Ocean">Pacific Ocean</a>, are classified as either tropical depressions or tropical storms. Should a system intensify further and become a hurricane, then it will be classified on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saffir%E2%80%93Simpson_hurricane_wind_scale" title="Saffir–Simpson hurricane wind scale">Saffir–Simpson hurricane wind scale</a>, which is based on the estimated maximum sustained winds over a 1-minute period. In the Western Pacific, the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Typhoon_Committee&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Typhoon Committee (page does not exist)">Typhoon Committee</a> uses four separate classifications for tropical cyclones that exist within the basin, which are based on the estimated maximum sustained winds over a 10-minute period.</p>
<p>The India Meteorological Department's scale uses 7 different classifications for systems, within the North Indian Ocean and are based on the systems estimated 3-minute maximum sustained winds. Tropical cyclones that develop in the <a href="/wiki/Southern_Hemisphere" title="Southern Hemisphere">Southern Hemisphere</a> are only officially classified by the warning centres on off two scales, which are both based on 10-minute sustained wind speeds. The Australian tropical cyclone intensity scale which is used to classify systems, within the Australian or South Pacific tropical cyclone basins. The scale used to classify systems in the <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone_basins" title="Tropical cyclone basins">South-West Indian Ocean</a> and is adapted by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Meteo_France" title="Meteo France">Meteo France</a>, for use in various French territories, including <a href="/wiki/New_Caledonia" title="New Caledonia">New Caledonia</a> and <a href="/wiki/French_Polynesia" title="French Polynesia">French Polynesia</a>.</p>
<p>The definition of <a href="/wiki/Maximum_sustained_wind" title="Maximum sustained wind">sustained winds</a> recommended by the <a href="/wiki/World_Meteorological_Organization" title="World Meteorological Organization">World Meteorological Organization</a> (WMO) and used by most weather agencies is that of a 10-minute average at a height of 10 <a href="/wiki/Metre" title="Metre">m</a> (33 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Foot_(length)" title="Foot (length)">ft</a>). However, the Saffir–Simpson Hurricane Scale is based on wind speed measurements averaged over a 1-minute period, at 10 m (33 ft) above the surface. The scale used by RSMC New Delhi applies a 3-minute averaging period, and the Australian scale is based on both 3-second <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wind_gust" title="Wind gust">wind gusts</a> and maximum sustained winds averaged over a 10-minute interval. These make direct comparisons between basins difficult.</p>
<p>Within all basins tropical cyclones are named when the sustained winds hit 35 kn (40 mph; 65 km/h).</p>

<p>Tropical cyclones are defined as being warm cored, non-frontal synoptic cyclones, that develop over tropical or subtropical waters, with organized <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_convection" title="Atmospheric convection">atmospheric convection</a> and have a definite cyclonic surface wind circulation. They are classified by the wind speeds located around the circulation centre and are ranked, by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/World_Meteorological_Organisation" title="World Meteorological Organisation">World Meteorological Organisation</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Regional_Specialized_Meteorological_Center" title="Regional Specialized Meteorological Center">Regional Specialized Meteorological Centers</a> on one of five tropical cyclone scales. The scale used for a particular tropical cyclone depends on what basin the system is located in; with for example the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saffir%E2%80%93Simpson_hurricane_wind_scale" title="Saffir–Simpson hurricane wind scale">Saffir–Simpson hurricane wind scale</a> and the Australian tropical cyclone intensity scales both used in the Western Hemisphere. All of the scales rank tropical cyclones using their maximum sustained winds, which are either observed, measured or estimated using various techniques, over a period between one and ten minutes.</p>

<p>Tropical cyclones that occur within the Northern Hemisphere to the east of the <a href="/wiki/180th_meridian" title="180th meridian">anti-meridian</a>, are officially monitored by either the <a href="/wiki/National_Hurricane_Center" title="National Hurricane Center">National Hurricane Center</a> or the <a href="/wiki/Central_Pacific_Hurricane_Center" title="Central Pacific Hurricane Center">Central Pacific Hurricane Center</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ATL.2FEPAC_TCOP_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-ATL.2FEPAC_TCOP-1">[1]</a></sup> Within the region a tropical cyclone is defined to be a warm cored, non-frontal synoptic disturbance, that develops over tropical or subtropical waters, with organized atmospheric convection and a closed well defined circulation centre.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ATL.2FEPAC_TCOP_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-ATL.2FEPAC_TCOP-1">[1]</a></sup> The region also defines a subtropical cyclone as a non-frontal low pressure disturbance, that has the characteristics of both tropical and extratropical cyclones.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ATL.2FEPAC_TCOP_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-ATL.2FEPAC_TCOP-1">[1]</a></sup> Once either of these classifications are met, then advisories are initiated and the warning centers will classify the system as either a tropical or subtropical depression, if the one-minute sustained winds estimated or measured as less than 33 kn (38 mph; 62 km/h).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ATL.2FEPAC_TCOP_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-ATL.2FEPAC_TCOP-1">[1]</a></sup> However, if a tropical disturbance is capable of producing tropical storm or hurricane conditions on land within 48 hours, then advisories will be initiated and it will be classified as a potential tropical cyclone.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ATL.2FEPAC_TCOP_1-4"><a href="#cite_note-ATL.2FEPAC_TCOP-1">[1]</a></sup> Should the system intensify further or already have one-minute sustained winds of 34–63 kn (39–73 mph; 63–118 km/h), then it will be called either a tropical or subtropical storm and <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone_naming" title="Tropical cyclone naming">assigned a name</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ATL.2FEPAC_TCOP_1-5"><a href="#cite_note-ATL.2FEPAC_TCOP-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Should the tropical system further intensify and have winds estimated or measured, as greater than 64 kn (74 mph; 119 km/h), then it will be called a hurricane and classified on the <a href="/wiki/Saffir%E2%80%93Simpson_scale" title="Saffir–Simpson scale">Saffir–Simpson hurricane wind scale</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ATL.2FEPAC_TCOP_1-6"><a href="#cite_note-ATL.2FEPAC_TCOP-1">[1]</a></sup> The lowest classification on the SSHWS is a Category 1 hurricane, which has winds of between 64–82 kn (74–95 mph, 119–153 km/h).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ATL.2FEPAC_TCOP_1-7"><a href="#cite_note-ATL.2FEPAC_TCOP-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NHC_SSHWS_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-NHC_SSHWS-2">[2]</a></sup> Should the hurricane intensify further then it will be rated as a Category 2 hurricane, if it has winds of between 83–95 kn (96–110 mph, 154–177 km/h).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ATL.2FEPAC_TCOP_1-8"><a href="#cite_note-ATL.2FEPAC_TCOP-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NHC_SSHWS_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-NHC_SSHWS-2">[2]</a></sup> When a system becomes a Category 3 hurricane with winds of between 96–112 kn (111–129 mph, 178–208 km/h), it is considered to be a major hurricane by the warning centers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NHC_SSHWS_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-NHC_SSHWS-2">[2]</a></sup> A Category 4 hurricane has winds of 113–136 kn (130–156 mph, 209–251 km/h), while a Category 5 hurricane has winds of at least 137 kn (157 mph, 252 km/h).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ATL.2FEPAC_TCOP_1-9"><a href="#cite_note-ATL.2FEPAC_TCOP-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NHC_SSHWS_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-NHC_SSHWS-2">[2]</a></sup> A post tropical cyclone is a system that has weakened, into a remnant low or has dissipated and formal advisories are usually discontinued at this stage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ATL.2FEPAC_TCOP_1-10"><a href="#cite_note-ATL.2FEPAC_TCOP-1">[1]</a></sup> However, advisories may continue if the post tropical cyclone, poses a significant threat to life and property.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ATL.2FEPAC_TCOP_1-11"><a href="#cite_note-ATL.2FEPAC_TCOP-1">[1]</a></sup> They may also continue if the remnants of the system have a chance of regeneration and producing tropical storm or hurricane-force winds over land within 48 hours.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ATL.2FEPAC_TCOP_1-12"><a href="#cite_note-ATL.2FEPAC_TCOP-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>The SSHS was originally created using both wind speed and storm surge, but since the relationship between wind speed and storm surge is not necessarily definite, the scale was changed to the "Saffir–Simpson Hurricane Wind Scale" (SSHWS), based entirely on wind speed.</p>
<p>Although increasing echelons of the scale correspond to stronger winds, the rankings are not absolute in terms of effects. Lower-category storms can inflict greater damage than higher-category storms, depending on factors such as local terrain, population density and total rainfall. For instance, a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saffir%E2%80%93Simpson_Hurricane_Scale#Category_2" title="Saffir–Simpson Hurricane Scale">Category 2</a> hurricane that strikes a major urban area will likely do more damage than a large Category 5 hurricane that strikes a mostly rural region. In fact, tropical systems of less than hurricane strength can produce significant damage and human casualties, especially from flooding and landslides.</p>
<p>Historically, the term <i>great hurricane</i> was used to describe storms that possessed winds of at least 110 <a href="/wiki/Knot_(unit)" title="Knot (unit)">kn</a> (125 mph; 200 km/h), large radii (over 160 <a href="/wiki/Kilometre" title="Kilometre">km</a> / 100 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Statute_mile" title="Statute mile">mi</a>) and that caused large amounts of destruction. This term fell into disuse after the introduction of the Saffir–Simpson scale in the early 1970s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Great_Hurricane_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Great_Hurricane-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>A minor change to the scale was made ahead of the 2012 hurricane season, with the wind speeds for categories 3–5 tweaked to eliminate the rounding errors, that had occurred during previous seasons when a hurricane had wind speeds of 115 kn (130 mph; 215 km/h).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SSHWS_PIS_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-SSHWS_PIS-4">[4]</a></sup></p>

<p>Tropical cyclones that occur within the Northern Hemisphere between the <a href="/wiki/180th_meridian" title="180th meridian">anti-meridian</a> and <a href="/wiki/100th_meridian_east" title="100th meridian east">100°E</a>, are officially monitored by the <a href="/wiki/Japan_Meteorological_Agency" title="Japan Meteorological Agency">Japan Meteorological Agency</a> (JMA, RSMC Tokyo).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TCOM_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-TCOM-5">[5]</a></sup> Within the region a tropical cyclone is defined to be a non-frontal synoptic scale cyclone originating over tropical or sub-tropical waters, with organized convection and a definite cyclonic surface wind circulation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TCOM_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-TCOM-5">[5]</a></sup> The lowest classification used by the Typhoon Committee is a tropical depression, which has 10-minute sustained winds of less than 33 kn (17 m/s; 38 mph; 61 km/h).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TCOM_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-TCOM-5">[5]</a></sup> Should the tropical depression intensify further it is named and classified as a tropical storm, which has winds speeds between 33–47 kn (17–24 m/s; 38–54 mph; 61–87 km/h).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TCOM_5-3"><a href="#cite_note-TCOM-5">[5]</a></sup> Should the system continue to intensify further then it will be classified as a severe tropical storm, which has winds speeds between 48–63 kn (25–32 m/s; 55–72 mph; 89–117 km/h).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TCOM_5-4"><a href="#cite_note-TCOM-5">[5]</a></sup> The highest classification on the Typhoon Committee's scale is a typhoon, which has winds speeds greater than 64 kn (33 m/s; 74 mph; 119 km/h).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TCOM_5-5"><a href="#cite_note-TCOM-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/China_Meteorological_Administration" title="China Meteorological Administration">China Meteorological Administration</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Hong_Kong_Observatory" title="Hong Kong Observatory">Hong Kong Observatory</a> (HKO), <a href="/wiki/PAGASA" title="PAGASA">PAGASA</a> and the JMA, all divide the typhoon category further for domestic purposes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TCOM_5-6"><a href="#cite_note-TCOM-5">[5]</a></sup> The JMA divides the typhoon category into three categories, with a 10-minute maximum wind speed below 84 kn (43 m/s; 97 mph; 156 km/h) assigned for the (strong) typhoon category. A very strong typhoon has wind speeds between 85–104 kn (44–54 m/s; 98–120 mph; 157–193 km/h), while a violent typhoon has wind speeds of 105 kn (54 m/s; 121 mph; 194 km/h) or greater.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TCOM_5-7"><a href="#cite_note-TCOM-5">[5]</a></sup> The HKO and the CMA also divide the typhoon category into three categories, with both assigning a maximum wind speed of 80 kn (41 m/s; 92 mph; 150 km/h) to the typhoon category. A severe typhoon has wind speeds of 85–104 kn (44–54 m/s; 98–120 mph; 157–193 km/h), while a super typhoon has winds of 100 kn (51 m/s; 120 mph; 190 km/h).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TCOM_5-8"><a href="#cite_note-TCOM-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> During May 2015 PAGASA introduced the term Super Typhoon and used it for systems with winds greater than 120 kn (62 m/s; 140 mph; 220 km/h).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>In addition to the national meteorological services of each nation, the United States Joint Typhoon Warning Center monitors the basin, and issues warnings on significant tropical cyclones for the United States Government.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JTWC_Products_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-JTWC_Products-8">[8]</a></sup> These warnings use a 1-minute sustained wind speed and can be compared to the Saffir–Simpson hurricane wind scale, however, the JTWC uses their own scale for intensity classifications in this basin.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JTWC_FAQs_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-JTWC_FAQs-9">[9]</a></sup> These classifications are Tropical Depression, Tropical Storm, Typhoon and Super Typhoon.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JTWC_FAQs_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-JTWC_FAQs-9">[9]</a></sup> In addition, the Taiwan <a href="/wiki/Central_Weather_Bureau" title="Central Weather Bureau">Central Weather Bureau</a> has its own scale in Chinese but uses the Typhoon Committee scale in English.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>

<p>Any tropical cyclone that develops within the North Indian Ocean between <a href="/wiki/100th_meridian_east" title="100th meridian east">100°E</a> and <a href="/wiki/45th_meridian_east" title="45th meridian east">45°E</a> is monitored by the <a href="/wiki/India_Meteorological_Department" title="India Meteorological Department">India Meteorological Department</a> (IMD, RSMC New Delhi).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NIO_TCOP_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-NIO_TCOP-11">[11]</a></sup> Within the region a tropical cyclone is defined as being a non frontal synoptic scale cyclone, that originates over tropical or subtropical waters with organized convection and a definite cyclonic surface wind circulation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NIO_TCOP_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-NIO_TCOP-11">[11]</a></sup> The lowest official classification used in the North Indian Ocean is a Depression, which has 3-minute sustained wind speeds of between 17–27 kn (20–31 mph; 31–49 km/h).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NIO_TCOP_11-2"><a href="#cite_note-NIO_TCOP-11">[11]</a></sup> Should the depression intensify further then it will become a Deep Depression, which has winds between 28–33 kn (32–38 mph; 50–61 km/h).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NIO_TCOP_11-3"><a href="#cite_note-NIO_TCOP-11">[11]</a></sup> The system will be classified as a cyclonic storm and assigned a name by the IMD, if it should develop gale-force wind speeds of between 34–47 kn (39–54 mph; 62–88 km/h).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NIO_TCOP_11-4"><a href="#cite_note-NIO_TCOP-11">[11]</a></sup> Severe Cyclonic Storms have storm force wind speeds of between 48–63 kn (55–72 mph; 89–117 km/h), while Very Severe Cyclonic Storms have hurricane-force winds of 64–89 kt (73–102 mph; 118–166 km/h). Extremely Severe Cyclonic Storms have hurricane-force winds of 90–119 kn (166–221 km/h, 104–137 mph).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NIO_TCOP_11-5"><a href="#cite_note-NIO_TCOP-11">[11]</a></sup> The highest classification used in the North Indian Ocean is a Super Cyclonic Storm, which have hurricane-force winds of above 120 kn (138 mph; 222 km/h).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NIO_TCOP_11-6"><a href="#cite_note-NIO_TCOP-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>Historically, a system has been classified as a depression if it is an area where the barometric pressure is low compared with its surroundings.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IMD_BT_Report_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-IMD_BT_Report-12">[12]</a></sup> Other classifications historically used include: cyclonic storm where the winds did not exceed <a href="/wiki/Beaufort_scale" title="Beaufort scale">force 10</a> on the Beaufort scale and a Cyclone where the winds are either force 11 and 12 on the Beaufort scale.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IMD_BT_Report_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-IMD_BT_Report-12">[12]</a></sup> Between 1924 and 1988, tropical cyclones were classified into four categories: depression, deep depression, cyclonic storms and severe cyclonic storms.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IMD_BT_Report_12-2"><a href="#cite_note-IMD_BT_Report-12">[12]</a></sup> However, a change was made during 1988 to introduce the category "severe cyclonic storm with core of hurricane winds" for tropical cyclones, with wind speeds of more than 64 kn (74 mph; 119 km/h).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IMD_BT_Report_12-3"><a href="#cite_note-IMD_BT_Report-12">[12]</a></sup> During 1999 the categories Very Severe Cyclonic Storm and Super Cyclonic Storm were introduced, while the severe cyclonic storm with a core of hurricane winds category was eliminated.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IMD_BT_Report_12-4"><a href="#cite_note-IMD_BT_Report-12">[12]</a></sup> During 2015 another modification to the intensity scale took place, with the IMD calling a system with 3-minute maximum sustained wind speeds between 90–119 kn (166–221 km/h, 104–137 mph): an extremely severe cyclonic storm.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> The United States Joint Typhoon Warning Center also monitors the basin, and issues warnings on significant tropical cyclones on behalf of the United States Government.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JTWC_Products_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-JTWC_Products-8">[8]</a></sup> These warnings use a 1-minute sustained wind speed and can be compared to the Saffir–Simpson hurricane wind scale, however, regardless of intensity in this basin the JTWC labels all systems as tropical cyclones.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JTWC_FAQs_9-2"><a href="#cite_note-JTWC_FAQs-9">[9]</a></sup></p>

<p>Any tropical cyclone that develops within the Southern Hemisphere between Africa and <a href="/wiki/90th_meridian_east" title="90th meridian east">90°E</a> is monitored by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Meteo_France" title="Meteo France">Meteo France</a>'s La Reunion tropical cyclone centre (MFR, RSMC La Reunion).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TCOP-SWIO_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-TCOP-SWIO-14">[14]</a></sup> Within the region a tropical disturbance is defined to be a non-frontal synoptic scale low pressure area, originating over tropical or sub-tropical waters with organized convection and definite cyclonic surface wind circulation with the average wind speed estimated to be not exceeding 27 knots (50 km/h)).</p>
<p>A tropical disturbance is MFR's generic term for a non-frontal area of low pressure that has organized convection and definite cyclonic surface wind circulation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TCOP-SWIO_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-TCOP-SWIO-14">[14]</a></sup> The system should be estimated to have wind speeds of less than 28 knots (50 km/h, 32 mph).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TCOP-SWIO_14-2"><a href="#cite_note-TCOP-SWIO-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>A system is designated as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tropical_depression" title="Tropical depression">tropical depression</a> or a subtropical depression when it reaches wind speeds above 28 knots (50 km/h, 32 mph). Should a tropical depression reach wind speeds of 35 knots (65 km/h, 40 mph) then it will be classified as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Moderate_Tropical_Storm" title="Moderate Tropical Storm">moderate tropical storm</a> and assigned a name by either the Sub Regional Center in <a href="/wiki/Mauritius" title="Mauritius">Mauritius</a> or <a href="/wiki/Madagascar" title="Madagascar">Madagascar</a>. No matter how strong a subtropical system is in this basin, it is always designated as a subtropical depression.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MFR_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-MFR-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>Should the named storm intensify further and reach winds speeds of 48 knots (89 km/h, 55 mph), then it will be classified as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Severe_Tropical_Storm" title="Severe Tropical Storm">severe tropical storm</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MFR_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-MFR-15">[15]</a></sup> A severe tropical storm is designated as a <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone" title="Tropical cyclone">tropical cyclone</a> when it reaches wind speeds of 64 knots (118 km/h, 74 mph).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TCOP-SWIO_14-3"><a href="#cite_note-TCOP-SWIO-14">[14]</a></sup> Should a tropical cyclone intensify further and reach wind speeds of 90 knots (166 km/h, 103 mph), it will be classified as an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Intense_Tropical_Cyclone" title="Intense Tropical Cyclone">intense tropical cyclone</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TCOP-SWIO_14-4"><a href="#cite_note-TCOP-SWIO-14">[14]</a></sup> A <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Very_Intense_Tropical_Cyclone" title="Very Intense Tropical Cyclone">very intense tropical cyclone</a> is the highest category on the South-West Indian Ocean Tropical Cyclone scale, and has winds of over 115 knots (212 km/h, 132 mph).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MFR_15-2"><a href="#cite_note-MFR-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>At the tenth RA I tropical cyclone committee held during 1991, it was recommended that the intensity classifications be changed ahead of the 1993–94 tropical cyclone season.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-92-93_SS_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-92-93_SS-16">[16]</a></sup> Specifically it was decided that the classifications: Weak Tropical Depression, Moderate Tropical Depression and Severe Tropical Depression would be changed to Tropical Depression, Moderate Tropical Storm and Severe Tropical Storm.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-92-93_SS_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-92-93_SS-16">[16]</a></sup> This change was implemented ahead of the <a href="/wiki/1993%E2%80%9394_South-West_Indian_Ocean_cyclone_season" title="1993–94 South-West Indian Ocean cyclone season">1993–94 tropical cyclone season</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-92-93_SS_16-2"><a href="#cite_note-92-93_SS-16">[16]</a></sup> The United States Joint Typhoon Warning Center also monitors the basin, and issues warnings on significant tropical cyclones on behalf of the United States Government.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JTWC_Products_8-2"><a href="#cite_note-JTWC_Products-8">[8]</a></sup> These warnings use a 1-minute sustained wind speed and can be compared to the Saffir–Simpson hurricane wind scale, however, regardless of intensity in this basin the JTWC labels all systems as tropical cyclones.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JTWC_FAQs_9-3"><a href="#cite_note-JTWC_FAQs-9">[9]</a></sup></p>

<p>Tropical cyclones that occur within the Southern Hemisphere to the east of 90°E, are officially monitored by one or more tropical cyclone warning centres.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SPAC_TCOP_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-SPAC_TCOP-17">[17]</a></sup> These are run by the <a href="/wiki/Fiji_Meteorological_Service" title="Fiji Meteorological Service">Fiji Meteorological Service</a>, New Zealand's <a href="/wiki/MetService" title="MetService">MetService</a>, Indonesia's <a href="/wiki/Indonesian_Agency_for_Meteorology,_Climatology_and_Geophysics" title="Indonesian Agency for Meteorology, Climatology and Geophysics">Badan Meteorologi, Klimatologi, dan Geofisika</a>, Papua New Guinea's National Weather Service, while three others are run by the Australian <a href="/wiki/Bureau_of_Meteorology" title="Bureau of Meteorology">Bureau of Meteorology</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SPAC_TCOP_17-1"><a href="#cite_note-SPAC_TCOP-17">[17]</a></sup> Within the region a tropical cyclone is defined as being a non-frontal low pressure system of synoptic scale that develops over warm waters, with a definite organized wind circulation and 10-minute sustained wind speeds of 34 kn (63 km/h; 39 mph) or greater near the centre.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SPAC_TCOP_17-2"><a href="#cite_note-SPAC_TCOP-17">[17]</a></sup> Once this definition has been met then all of the centres name the system and start to use the Australian tropical cyclone intensity scale, which measures tropical cyclones using a five category system based on 10-minute maximum sustained winds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SPAC_TCOP_17-3"><a href="#cite_note-SPAC_TCOP-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fiji_Alerts_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-Fiji_Alerts-18">[18]</a></sup> A Category 1 tropical cyclone is estimated to have 10-minute sustained wind speeds of 34–47 kn (39–54 mph; 63–87 km/h), while a Category 2 tropical cyclone is estimated to have 10-minute sustained wind speeds of 48–63 kn (55–72 mph; 89–117 km/h).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fiji_Alerts_18-1"><a href="#cite_note-Fiji_Alerts-18">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BOM_FAQ_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-BOM_FAQ-19">[19]</a></sup> When a system becomes a Category 3 tropical cyclone it is reclassified as a Severe tropical cyclone and has wind speeds of 64–85 kn (74–98 mph; 119–157 km/h).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fiji_Alerts_18-2"><a href="#cite_note-Fiji_Alerts-18">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BOM_FAQ_19-1"><a href="#cite_note-BOM_FAQ-19">[19]</a></sup> A Category 4 severe tropical cyclone has winds of 86–110 kn (99–130 mph; 157–200 km/h), while the maximum rating is a Category 5 severe tropical cyclone, which has winds of at least 110 kn (130 mph; 200 km/h).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fiji_Alerts_18-3"><a href="#cite_note-Fiji_Alerts-18">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BOM_FAQ_19-2"><a href="#cite_note-BOM_FAQ-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>For systems below tropical cyclone strength there are various terms used, including Tropical Disturbance, Tropical Low and Tropical Depression.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SPAC_TCOP_17-4"><a href="#cite_note-SPAC_TCOP-17">[17]</a></sup> A tropical disturbance is defined as being a non – frontal system of synoptic scale originating over the tropics, with persistent enhanced convection and/or some indication of a circulation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SPAC_TCOP_17-5"><a href="#cite_note-SPAC_TCOP-17">[17]</a></sup> A tropical depression or tropical low is a disturbance with a defined circulation, where the central position can be estimated, and the maximum 10-minute average wind speed is less than 34 kn (39 mph; 63 km/h) near the centre.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SPAC_TCOP_17-6"><a href="#cite_note-SPAC_TCOP-17">[17]</a></sup> The FMS numbers these systems when they have a potential to develop into a tropical cyclone or persist to cause significant impact to life and property, within its area of responsibility and have been analysed for the previous 24 hours.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SPAC_TCOP_17-7"><a href="#cite_note-SPAC_TCOP-17">[17]</a></sup> The Australian tropical cyclone intensity scale was introduced by the BoM, ahead of the 1989–90 cyclone season. The United States Joint Typhoon Warning Center also monitors the basin, and issues warnings on significant tropical cyclones on behalf of the United States Government.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JTWC_Products_8-3"><a href="#cite_note-JTWC_Products-8">[8]</a></sup> These warnings use a 1-minute sustained wind speed and can be compared to the Saffir–Simpson hurricane wind scale, however, regardless of intensity in these basins the JTWC labels all systems as tropical cyclones.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JTWC_FAQs_9-4"><a href="#cite_note-JTWC_FAQs-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<div style="clear:both;"></div>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Alternative_scales">Alternative scales</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tropical_cyclone_scales&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Alternative scales">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>There are other scales that are not officially used by any of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Regional_Specialized_Meteorological_Centre" title="Regional Specialized Meteorological Centre">Regional Specialized Meteorological Centres</a> or the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tropical_Cyclone_Warning_Centre" title="Tropical Cyclone Warning Centre">Tropical Cyclone Warning Centres</a>. However they are used by other organizations, such as the <a href="/wiki/National_Oceanic_and_Atmospheric_Administration" title="National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration">National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration</a>. An example of such scale is the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Integrated_Kinetic_Energy_index&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Integrated Kinetic Energy index (page does not exist)">Integrated Kinetic Energy index</a>, which measures the destructive potential of the storm surge; it works on a scale that ranges from one to six, with six having the highest destructive potential.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IKE_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-IKE-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Accumulated_cyclone_energy" title="Accumulated cyclone energy">Accumulated cyclone energy</a> (ACE) is used by the National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration and other agencies to express the activity of individual tropical cyclones that are above tropical storm strength and entire tropical cyclone seasons.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CPC_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-CPC-21">[21]</a></sup> It is calculated by taking the squares of the estimated maximum sustained velocity of every active tropical storm (wind speed 35 knots or higher) at six-hour intervals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CPC_21-1"><a href="#cite_note-CPC-21">[21]</a></sup> The numbers are usually divided by 10,000 to make them more manageable. The unit of ACE is 10<sup>4</sup> kn<sup>2</sup>, and for use as an index the unit is assumed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CPC_21-2"><a href="#cite_note-CPC-21">[21]</a></sup> As well as being squared for ACE, wind speed can also be cubed, which is referred to as the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Power_Dissipation_Index_(PDI)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Power Dissipation Index (PDI) (page does not exist)">Power Dissipation Index (PDI)</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Severity_Index" title="Hurricane Severity Index">Hurricane Severity Index</a> (HSI) is another scale used and rates the severity of all types of tropical and subtropical cyclones based on both the intensity and the size of their wind fields.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HSI_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-HSI-23">[23]</a></sup> The HSI is a 0 to 50 point scale, allotting up to 25 points for a tropical cyclone's intensity and up to 25 points for wind field size.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HSI_23-1"><a href="#cite_note-HSI-23">[23]</a></sup> Points are awarded on a sliding scale, with the majority of points reserved for hurricane force and greater wind fields.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HSI_23-2"><a href="#cite_note-HSI-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
