<p><b>"Hurricane"</b> is a song co-written by <a href="/wiki/Thom_Schuyler" title="Thom Schuyler">Thom Schuyler</a>, <a href="/wiki/Keith_Stegall" title="Keith Stegall">Keith Stegall</a> and Stewart Harris. <a href="/wiki/Levon_Helm" title="Levon Helm">Levon Helm</a> recorded it for his 1980 album <i><a href="/wiki/American_Son_(album)" title="American Son (album)">American Son</a></i>.</p>
<p>It was later recorded by American singer <a href="/wiki/Leon_Everette" title="Leon Everette">Leon Everette</a>. It was released in July 1981 as the lead single from Everette's <a href="/wiki/RCA_Records" title="RCA Records">RCA Records</a> album <i>Hurricane</i>. It is Everette's highest-charting single, peaking at No. 4 on the <i>Billboard</i> <a href="/wiki/Hot_Country_Songs" title="Hot Country Songs">Hot Country Singles</a> chart in September 1981.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Content">Content</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hurricane_(Leon_Everette_song)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Content">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The song is about an old man who lives in the famed <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/New_Orleans,_Louisiana" title="New Orleans, Louisiana">New Orleans</a> <a href="/wiki/French_Quarter" title="French Quarter">French Quarter</a>. The man is unfazed when told that a hurricane was about to hit the city; even when "a man from Chicago" claims that the levees need to be raised, he claims that the levees will hold and the man will be "on his way to Illinois".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pittsburgh_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-pittsburgh-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Critical_reception">Critical reception</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hurricane_(Leon_Everette_song)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Critical reception">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Jerry Sharpe of <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Pittsburgh_Press" title="The Pittsburgh Press">The Pittsburgh Press</a></i> wrote that the song "defeats the standard old formulas for successful country music lyrics — no love story, no sex, no booze, no tragedy."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pittsburgh_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-pittsburgh-3">[3]</a></sup> An uncredited review in <i><a href="/wiki/Billboard_(magazine)" title="Billboard (magazine)">Billboard</a></i> said that "Everette's distinctive vocals are the perfect vehicle for this tale of man's struggle against the elements."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Dave Marsh was less favorable in <i>The New Rolling Stone Record Guide</i>, calling Everette a "poor man's <a href="/wiki/Johnny_Lee_(singer)" title="Johnny Lee (singer)">Johnny Lee</a>" and said the song was "almost an interesting ballad".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
