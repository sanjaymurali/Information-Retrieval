<p><b>Georgian Bay</b> (<a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">French</a>: <i>Baie Georgienne</i>) is a large bay of <a href="/wiki/Lake_Huron" title="Lake Huron">Lake Huron</a>, located entirely within <a href="/wiki/Ontario" title="Ontario">Ontario</a>, <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a>. The main body of the bay lies east of the <a href="/wiki/Bruce_Peninsula" title="Bruce Peninsula">Bruce Peninsula</a> and <a href="/wiki/Manitoulin_Island" title="Manitoulin Island">Manitoulin Island</a>. To its northwest is the <a href="/wiki/North_Channel_(Ontario)" title="North Channel (Ontario)">North Channel</a>.</p>
<p>Georgian Bay is surrounded by (listed <a href="/wiki/Clockwise" title="Clockwise">clockwise</a>) the districts of <a href="/wiki/Manitoulin_District" title="Manitoulin District">Manitoulin</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sudbury_District,_Ontario" title="Sudbury District, Ontario">Sudbury</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Parry_Sound_District,_Ontario" title="Parry Sound District, Ontario">Parry Sound</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Muskoka_District,_Ontario" title="Muskoka District, Ontario">Muskoka</a>, as well as the more populous counties of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Simcoe_County,_Ontario" title="Simcoe County, Ontario">Simcoe</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Grey_County,_Ontario" title="Grey County, Ontario">Grey</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bruce_County,_Ontario" title="Bruce County, Ontario">Bruce</a>. The Main Channel separates the Bruce Peninsula from Manitoulin Island and connects Georgian Bay to the rest of Lake Huron. The <a href="/wiki/North_Channel_(Ontario)" title="North Channel (Ontario)">North Channel</a>, located between Manitoulin Island and the Sudbury District, west of <a href="/wiki/Killarney,_Ontario" title="Killarney, Ontario">Killarney</a>, was once a popular route for <a href="/wiki/Steamship" title="Steamship">steamships</a> and is now used by a variety of <a href="/wiki/Pleasure_craft" title="Pleasure craft">pleasure craft</a> to travel to and from Georgian Bay.</p>
<p>The shores and waterways of the Georgian Bay are the traditional domain of the <a href="/wiki/Anishinaabe" title="Anishinaabe">Anishinaabeg</a> <a href="/wiki/First_Nations" title="First Nations">First Nations</a> peoples to the north and <a href="/wiki/Wyandot_people" title="Wyandot people">Huron</a>-<a href="/wiki/Petun" title="Petun">Petun</a> (Wyandot) to the south. The bay was thus a major <a href="/wiki/Algonquian_peoples" title="Algonquian peoples">Algonquian</a>-Huron trade route. <a href="/wiki/Samuel_de_Champlain" title="Samuel de Champlain">Samuel de Champlain</a>, the first European to explore and map the area in 1615–1616, called it "<i>La Mer douce</i>" (the calm sea), also a reference to the bay's freshwater.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HAC_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-HAC-1">[1]</a></sup> It was named "Georgian Bay" (after <a href="/wiki/George_IV_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="George IV of the United Kingdom">King George IV</a>) by Lieutenant <a href="/wiki/Henry_Wolsey_Bayfield" title="Henry Wolsey Bayfield">Henry Wolsey Bayfield</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Navy" title="Royal Navy">Royal Navy</a> in 1822.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geography">Geography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Georgian_Bay&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Geography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Great_Lakes_Lake_Huron_Georgian_Bay.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="406" data-file-width="581" height="140" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0c/Great_Lakes_Lake_Huron_Georgian_Bay.png/200px-Great_Lakes_Lake_Huron_Georgian_Bay.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0c/Great_Lakes_Lake_Huron_Georgian_Bay.png/300px-Great_Lakes_Lake_Huron_Georgian_Bay.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0c/Great_Lakes_Lake_Huron_Georgian_Bay.png/400px-Great_Lakes_Lake_Huron_Georgian_Bay.png 2x" width="200"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Great_Lakes_Lake_Huron_Georgian_Bay.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Main body of Georgian Bay highlighted on the map of the Great Lakes</div>

</div>
<p>Georgian Bay is about 190 kilometres (120 mi) long by 80 kilometres (50 mi) wide.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> It covers approximately 15,000 square kilometres (5,800 sq mi), making it nearly 80% the size of <a href="/wiki/Lake_Ontario" title="Lake Ontario">Lake Ontario</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> If Georgian Bay were considered a lake in its own right, it would be the fourth largest lake located entirely within Canada after <a href="/wiki/Great_Bear_Lake" title="Great Bear Lake">Great Bear Lake</a>, <a href="/wiki/Great_Slave_Lake" title="Great Slave Lake">Great Slave Lake</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lake_Winnipeg" title="Lake Winnipeg">Lake Winnipeg</a>.</p>
<p>Eastern Georgian Bay is part of the southern edge of the <a href="/wiki/Canadian_Shield" title="Canadian Shield">Canadian Shield</a>, granite bedrock exposed by the glaciers at the end of the last <a href="/wiki/Ice_age" title="Ice age">ice age</a>, about 11,000 years ago. The granite rock formations and windswept <a href="/wiki/Pinus_strobus" title="Pinus strobus">eastern white pine</a> are characteristic of the islands and much of the shoreline of the bay. The rugged beauty of the area inspired landscapes by artists of the <a href="/wiki/Group_of_Seven_(artists)" title="Group of Seven (artists)">Group of Seven</a>. The western part of the bay, from <a href="/wiki/Collingwood,_Ontario" title="Collingwood, Ontario">Collingwood</a> north, and including <a href="/wiki/Manitoulin_Island" title="Manitoulin Island">Manitoulin</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Drummond_Island" title="Drummond Island">Drummond</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cockburn_Island_(Ontario)" title="Cockburn Island (Ontario)">Cockburn</a> and <a href="/wiki/St._Joseph_Island_(Ontario)" title="St. Joseph Island (Ontario)">St. Joseph</a> islands, borders the <a href="/wiki/Niagara_Escarpment" title="Niagara Escarpment">Niagara Escarpment</a>. Because of its size and narrowness of the straits joining it with the rest of Lake Huron, which is analogous to if not as pronounced as the separation of Lake Huron and Lake Michigan,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Georgian Bay is sometimes called the "sixth Great Lake".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Barry1995_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Barry1995-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>There are tens of thousands of islands in Georgian Bay. Most of these islands are along the east side of the bay and are collectively known as the "<a href="/wiki/Thirty_Thousand_Islands" title="Thirty Thousand Islands">Thirty Thousand Islands</a>", including the larger <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Parry_Island,_Ontario" title="Parry Island, Ontario">Parry Island</a>. Manitoulin Island, lying along the northern side of the bay, is the world's largest island in a freshwater lake. The <a href="/wiki/Trent%E2%80%93Severn_Waterway" title="Trent–Severn Waterway">Trent–Severn Waterway</a> connects Georgian Bay to <a href="/wiki/Lake_Ontario" title="Lake Ontario">Lake Ontario</a>, running from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Port_Severn,_Ontario" title="Port Severn, Ontario">Port Severn</a> in the southeastern corner of Georgian Bay through <a href="/wiki/Lake_Simcoe" title="Lake Simcoe">Lake Simcoe</a> into Lake Ontario near <a href="/wiki/Trenton,_Ontario" title="Trenton, Ontario">Trenton</a>. Further north, <a href="/wiki/Lake_Nipissing" title="Lake Nipissing">Lake Nipissing</a> drains into Georgian Bay through the <a href="/wiki/French_River_(Ontario)" title="French River (Ontario)">French River</a>. In October 2004, the <a href="/wiki/Georgian_Bay_Littoral" title="Georgian Bay Littoral">Georgian Bay Littoral</a> was declared a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Biosphere_Reserve" title="Biosphere Reserve">Biosphere Reserve</a> by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_Nations_Educational,_Scientific_and_Cultural_Organization" title="United Nations Educational, Scientific and Cultural Organization">UNESCO</a>.</p>

<p>Archeological records reveal an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aboriginal_peoples_in_Canada" title="Aboriginal peoples in Canada">Aboriginal</a> presence in the southern regions of the Canadian Shield dating from 11,000 years ago. Evidence of later <a href="/wiki/Paleo-Indians" title="Paleo-Indians">Paleo-Indian</a> settlements have been found on <a href="/wiki/Manitoulin_Island" title="Manitoulin Island">Manitoulin Island</a> and near <a href="/wiki/Killarney,_Ontario" title="Killarney, Ontario">Killarney</a>. At the time of European <a href="/wiki/First_contact_(anthropology)" title="First contact (anthropology)">contact</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Ojibwe" title="Ojibwe">Ojibwe</a> and <a href="/wiki/Odawa" title="Odawa">Ottawa</a> <a href="/wiki/First_Nations" title="First Nations">First Nations</a>, both of whom call themselves <a href="/wiki/Anishinaabe" title="Anishinaabe">Anishinaabe</a> (plural: <i>Anishinaabeg</i>), lived along the northern, eastern and western shores of Georgian Bay. The <a href="/wiki/Wyandot_people" title="Wyandot people">Huron</a> (or Wendat) and <a href="/wiki/Petun" title="Petun">Tionontati</a> inhabited the lands along the southern coast, having migrated from the northern shores of Lake Ontario. Names of islands such as "Manitoulin" (from <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gitchi_Manitou" title="Gitchi Manitou">Gitchi Manitou</a></i>, the Great Spirit who left the bay as a source of life for the first people) and "Giant's Tomb" are indicative of the richness of the cultural history of the area. Aboriginal communities continue to live on their territories and practise their cultural traditions.</p>
<p>The first <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">European</a> to visit this area was likely <a href="/wiki/%C3%89tienne_Br%C3%BBl%C3%A9" title="Étienne Brûlé">Étienne Brûlé</a>, who at age less than 20, in 1610 was sent to live as an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Interpreter" title="Interpreter">interpreter</a> trainee with the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Onontchataronon&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Onontchataronon (page does not exist)">Onontchataronon</a>, an <a href="/wiki/Algonquian_peoples" title="Algonquian peoples">Algonquian</a> people of the <a href="/wiki/Ottawa_River" title="Ottawa River">Ottawa River</a>. They travelled every winter to live with the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Arendarhonon&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Arendarhonon (page does not exist)">Arendarhonon</a> people of the Huron (<i>Wendat</i>) confederacy at the southern end of Georgian Bay, in the area now called <a href="/wiki/Wyandot_people" title="Wyandot people">"Huronia"</a>. Brulé returned to the Arendarhonon the following year. At the same time another young interpreter trainee, a youth remembered only as Thomas, who was employed by the French <a href="/wiki/Surgeon" title="Surgeon">surgeon</a> and <a href="/wiki/Merchant" title="Merchant">trader</a> Daniel Boyer, also likely made it to Huronia, in the company of the Onontchataronon, another member of the confederacy.</p>
<p>In 1615, Brulé's employer, the <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">French</a> explorer <a href="/wiki/Samuel_de_Champlain" title="Samuel de Champlain">Samuel de Champlain</a>, made his own visit to Georgian Bay and overwintered in Huronia. He was preceded that summer by a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/R%C3%A9collet" title="Récollet">Récollet</a> <a href="/wiki/Missionary" title="Missionary">missionary</a>, <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Le_Caron" title="Joseph Le Caron">Joseph Le Caron</a>, who would live among the Huron in 1615–1616 and 1623–1624. Another Récollet missionary, Gabriel Sagard, lived there from 1623–34. The French <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jesuit" title="Jesuit">Jesuit</a> <a href="/wiki/Jean_de_Br%C3%A9beuf" title="Jean de Brébeuf">Jean de Brébeuf</a> began a mission in Huronia in 1626. In 1639 he oversaw the building of the mission fort of Sainte-Marie, Ontario's first European settlement, at what is now the town of <a href="/wiki/Midland,_Ontario" title="Midland, Ontario">Midland</a>.</p>
<p>The reconstructed Jesuit mission, <a href="/wiki/Sainte-Marie_among_the_Hurons" title="Sainte-Marie among the Hurons">Sainte-Marie among the Hurons</a>, is now a historic park operated by the province of Ontario. Also nearby is the <a href="/wiki/Martyrs%27_Shrine" title="Martyrs' Shrine">Martyrs' Shrine</a>, a Catholic church dedicated to the <a href="/wiki/Canadian_Martyrs" title="Canadian Martyrs">Canadian Martyrs</a>, Jesuits who were killed during <a href="/wiki/Iroquois" title="Iroquois">Iroquois</a> warfare against the Huron around Georgian Bay in the 17th century. <a href="/wiki/Penetanguishene" title="Penetanguishene">Penetanguishene</a>, an Ojibwe village located at the southern tip of the bay near present-day Midland, was developed as a <a href="/wiki/Naval_base" title="Naval base">naval base</a> in 1793 by <a href="/wiki/John_Graves_Simcoe" title="John Graves Simcoe">John Graves Simcoe</a>, first <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lieutenant_Governor_of_Upper_Canada" title="Lieutenant Governor of Upper Canada">Lieutenant Governor of Upper Canada</a>.</p>
<p>In 1814, during the <a href="/wiki/War_of_1812" title="War of 1812">War of 1812</a> between Great Britain and the United States, one of the battles was fought in southern Georgian Bay. On August 17, at the mouth of the <a href="/wiki/Nottawasaga_River" title="Nottawasaga River">Nottawasaga River</a> near Wasaga Beach, the British <a href="/wiki/Schooner" title="Schooner">schooner</a> <a href="/wiki/Nancy_(1789_ship)" title="Nancy (1789 ship)">HMS <i>Nancy</i></a> was sunk by three American vessels. Several weeks later, <i>Nancy</i> was avenged when British boarding parties in the <a href="/wiki/De_Tour_Passage_Underwater_Preserve" title="De Tour Passage Underwater Preserve">De Tour Passage</a> surprised and captured two of the three American vessels.</p>
<p>Georgian Bay was first charted in 1815 by Captain <a href="/wiki/William_Fitzwilliam_Owen" title="William Fitzwilliam Owen">William Fitzwilliam Owen</a>, who called it Lake Manitoulin. Captain <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Henry_Bayfield" title="Henry Bayfield">Henry Bayfield</a>, who made more detailed charts of the bay, renamed it in 1822 after <a href="/wiki/George_IV_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="George IV of the United Kingdom">King George IV</a>. His charts are the basis for those in use today.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Canadian_Hydrographic_Service" title="Canadian Hydrographic Service">Canadian Hydrographic Service</a> traces its history back to 1883, when it was originally established as the Georgian Bay Survey, tasked with charting and improving knowledge of the bay after a steamship wrecked there the previous year, killing 150 of its passengers.</p>
<p>Over the years, 32 lighthouses were built on Georgian Bay. Six of them were designed with limestone towers; these were built in the 1850s and are known collectively as the Imperial Towers. Some of the 32 can be toured by the public, some cannot, and some are accessible only by tour boats or private boat.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Legend_of_Kitchikewana">Legend of Kitchikewana</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Georgian_Bay&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Legend of Kitchikewana">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">

<p><a href="/wiki/Wyandot_people" title="Wyandot people">Wyandot</a> legend tells of a god called Kitchikewana, who was large enough to guard the whole of the Georgian Bay. Kitchikewana was known for his great temper, and his tribe decided the best way to calm him was with a wife. They held a grand celebration, and many women came. Kitchikewana met a woman named Wanakita here. He decided that this was the woman he wanted to marry, and started planning the wedding immediately after she left. But when she was invited back, she told Kitchikewana that she was already engaged. Enraged, Kitchikewana destroyed all the decorations, running to one end of <a href="/wiki/Beausoleil_Island" title="Beausoleil Island">Beausoleil Island</a> and grabbing a large ball of earth. Running to the other end, he tossed it into the Great Lakes. Thus, the 30,000 Islands were created. The indentations left behind by his fingers form the five bays of Georgian Bay: Midland Bay, <a href="/wiki/Penetanguishene" title="Penetanguishene">Penetang Bay</a>, Hog Bay, Sturgeon Bay, and <a href="/wiki/Matchedash_Bay" title="Matchedash Bay">Matchedash Bay</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> He then lay down to sleep and sleeps there still as <a href="/wiki/Giants_Tomb_Island" title="Giants Tomb Island">Giant's Tomb Island</a>.</p>
<p>The town of Penetanguishene now has a large statue of Kitchikewana on its main street. There is a YMCA summer camp for youth located on Beausoleil Island, in southern Georgian Bay, named after Kitchikewana.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> YMCA Camp Kitchikewana, or Kitchi for short, has been located in <a href="/wiki/Georgian_Bay_Islands_National_Park" title="Georgian Bay Islands National Park">Georgian Bay Islands National Park</a> since 1919. Originally operated by the Midland YMCA, it is now the residential camp for youth from the YMCA of Simcoe/Muskoka.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Settlements">Settlements</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Georgian_Bay&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Settlements">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p><a href="/wiki/Owen_Sound" title="Owen Sound">Owen Sound</a> is the largest city on the bay, long having served as a shipping and rail depot for the <a href="/wiki/Great_Lakes#Geography" title="Great Lakes">Upper Great Lakes</a>. The towns of <a href="/wiki/Midland,_Ontario" title="Midland, Ontario">Midland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Penetanguishene" title="Penetanguishene">Penetanguishene</a>, <a href="/wiki/Severn,_Ontario" title="Severn, Ontario">Port Severn</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Georgian_Bay,_Ontario" title="Georgian Bay, Ontario">Honey Harbour</a> are at the southeastern end of the bay and are popular sites for summer cottages, as are the many bays and islands on the eastern coast. <a href="/wiki/Collingwood,_Ontario" title="Collingwood, Ontario">Collingwood</a>, <a href="/wiki/Meaford,_Ontario" title="Meaford, Ontario">Meaford</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Wasaga_Beach" title="Wasaga Beach">Wasaga Beach</a> are located at the southern end of the bay, around <a href="/wiki/Nottawasaga_Bay" title="Nottawasaga Bay">Nottawasaga Bay</a>. Owen Sound, <a href="/wiki/Wiarton,_Ontario" title="Wiarton, Ontario">Wiarton</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Lion%27s_Head,_Ontario" title="Lion's Head, Ontario">Lion's Head</a> are located on the <a href="/wiki/Bruce_Peninsula" title="Bruce Peninsula">Bruce Peninsula</a> along the southern and southwestern shores of the bay, while <a href="/wiki/Tobermory,_Ontario" title="Tobermory, Ontario">Tobermory</a> is located at the northern tip of the Bruce Peninsula on the Main Channel. The passenger ferry <a href="/wiki/MS_Chi-Cheemaun" title="MS Chi-Cheemaun">MS <i>Chi-Cheemaun</i></a> travels from Tobermory across the Main Channel to <a href="/wiki/Tehkummah" title="Tehkummah">South Baymouth</a> on Manitoulin Island. <a href="/wiki/Parry_Sound,_Ontario" title="Parry Sound, Ontario">Parry Sound</a>, the world's deepest freshwater port,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> is located on the eastern shore of the bay.</p>
<p>There are communities of summer cottages on the north and east shore and on the adjacent 30,000 Islands. These include areas such as Cognashene, Wah Wah Taysee, <a href="/wiki/The_Archipelago" title="The Archipelago">Sans Souci</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pointe_au_Baril,_Ontario" title="Pointe au Baril, Ontario">Pointe au Baril</a> and <a href="/wiki/Byng_Inlet_(Ontario)" title="Byng Inlet (Ontario)">Byng Inlet</a>. Most of these cottages are accessible only by water.</p>
