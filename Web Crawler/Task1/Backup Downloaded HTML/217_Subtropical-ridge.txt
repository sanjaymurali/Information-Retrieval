<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Subtropical ridge</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

<p>The <b>subtropical ridge</b>, also known as the <b>subtropical high</b> or <b>horse latitudes</b>, is a significant belt of atmospheric <a href="/wiki/High-pressure_area" title="High-pressure area">high pressure</a> situated around the latitudes of <a href="/wiki/30th_parallel_north" title="30th parallel north">30°N</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Northern_Hemisphere" title="Northern Hemisphere">Northern Hemisphere</a> and <a href="/wiki/30th_parallel_south" title="30th parallel south">30°S</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Southern_Hemisphere" title="Southern Hemisphere">Southern Hemisphere</a>. It is the product of the <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_circulation" title="Atmospheric circulation">global air circulation</a> cell known as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hadley_Cell" title="Hadley Cell">Hadley Cell</a>. The subtropical ridge is characterized by mostly calm winds, which act to reduce <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Air_quality" title="Air quality">air quality</a> under its axis by causing <a href="/wiki/Fog" title="Fog">fog</a> overnight, and <a href="/wiki/Haze" title="Haze">haze</a> during daylight hours as a result of the stable atmosphere found near its location. The air descending from the upper troposphere flows out from its center at surface level toward the upper and lower <a href="/wiki/Latitude" title="Latitude">latitudes</a> of each hemisphere, creating both the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Trade_wind" title="Trade wind">trade winds</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Westerlies" title="Westerlies">westerlies</a>. The subtropical ridge moves poleward during the summer, reaching its most northern latitude in early fall, before moving equatorward during the cold season. The El Niño southern <a href="/wiki/Climate" title="Climate">climate</a> oscillation (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/ENSO" title="ENSO">ENSO</a>) can displace the northern hemisphere subtropical ridge, with La Niñas allowing for a more northerly axis for the ridge, while El Niños show flatter, more southerly ridges. The change of the ridge position during ENSO cycles changes the tracks of <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone" title="Tropical cyclone">tropical cyclones</a> that form around their equatorward and western peripheries. As the subtropical ridge varies in position and strength, it can enhance or depress <a href="/wiki/Monsoon" title="Monsoon">monsoon</a> regimes around their low-latitude periphery. The term "<a href="/wiki/Horse_latitudes" title="Horse latitudes">horse latitudes</a>" refers to and is synonymous with the subtropical ridge or subtropical high.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Formation">Formation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Subtropical_ridge&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Formation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Heating of the earth near the equator leads to large amounts of convection along the <a href="/wiki/Monsoon_trough" title="Monsoon trough">monsoon trough</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Intertropical_convergence_zone" title="Intertropical convergence zone">Intertropical convergence zone</a>. This air mass rises to the lower stratosphere where it diverges, moving away from the equator in the upper troposphere in both northerly and southerly directions. As it moves towards the mid-latitudes on both sides of the equator, the air cools and sinks. The resulting air mass subsidence creates a subtropical ridge of high pressure near the 30th parallel in both hemispheres. At the surface level, the sinking air diverges again with some returning to the equator, completing the Hadley circulation. This circulation on each side of the equator is known as the <a href="/wiki/Hadley_cell" title="Hadley cell">Hadley cell</a> and leads to the formation of the subtropical ridge.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> Many of the world's deserts are caused by these climatological <a href="/wiki/High-pressure_area" title="High-pressure area">high-pressure areas</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Migration">Migration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Subtropical_ridge&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Migration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Subtropicalridge2000091412.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="282" data-file-width="622" height="113" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8e/Subtropicalridge2000091412.jpg/250px-Subtropicalridge2000091412.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8e/Subtropicalridge2000091412.jpg/375px-Subtropicalridge2000091412.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8e/Subtropicalridge2000091412.jpg/500px-Subtropicalridge2000091412.jpg 2x" width="250"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Subtropicalridge2000091412.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The subtropical ridge shows up as a large area of black (dryness) on this water vapor satellite image from September 2000.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The subtropical ridge starts migrating poleward in late spring reaching its zenith in early autumn before retreating equatorward during the late <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fall" title="Fall">fall</a>, winter, and early spring. The equatorward migration of the subtropical ridge during the cold season is due to increasing north-south temperature differences between the poles and tropics.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> The latitudinal movement of the subtropical ridge is strongly correlated with the progression of the <a href="/wiki/Monsoon_trough" title="Monsoon trough">monsoon trough</a> or <a href="/wiki/Intertropical_Convergence_Zone" title="Intertropical Convergence Zone">Intertropical Convergence Zone</a>.</p>
<p>Most <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone" title="Tropical cyclone">tropical cyclones</a> form on the side of the subtropical ridge closer to the <a href="/wiki/Equator" title="Equator">equator</a>, then move poleward past the ridge axis before recurving into the main belt of the Westerlies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> When the subtropical ridge shifts due to ENSO, so will the preferred tropical cyclone tracks. Areas west of <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a> and <a href="/wiki/Korea" title="Korea">Korea</a> tend to experience many fewer September–November tropical cyclone impacts during <a href="/wiki/El_Ni%C3%B1o" title="El Niño">El Niño</a> and neutral years, while mainland <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a> experiences much greater landfall frequency during <a href="/wiki/La_Ni%C3%B1a" title="La Niña">La Niña</a> years. During El Niño years, the break in the subtropical ridge tends to lie near <a href="/wiki/130th_meridian_east" title="130th meridian east">130°E</a>, which would favor the Japanese archipelago, while in La Niña years the formation of tropical cyclones, along with the subtropical ridge position, shift west, which increases the threat to China.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> In the Atlantic basin, the subtropical ridge position tends to lie about 5 degrees farther south during El Niño years, which leads to a more southerly recurvature for tropical cyclones during those years.</p>
<p>When the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atlantic_Multidecadal_Oscillation" title="Atlantic Multidecadal Oscillation">Atlantic Multidecadal Oscillation</a>'s mode is favorable to <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclogenesis" title="Tropical cyclogenesis">tropical cyclone development</a> (1995–present), it amplifies the subtropical ridge across the central and eastern Atlantic.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Role_in_weather_formation_and_air_quality">Role in weather formation and air quality</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Subtropical_ridge&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Role in weather formation and air quality">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Air_quality" title="Air quality">Air quality</a></div>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Monsoon" title="Monsoon">Monsoon</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Subtropridgejulyna.gif"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="445" data-file-width="625" height="178" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f2/Subtropridgejulyna.gif/250px-Subtropridgejulyna.gif" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f2/Subtropridgejulyna.gif/375px-Subtropridgejulyna.gif 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f2/Subtropridgejulyna.gif/500px-Subtropridgejulyna.gif 2x" width="250"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Subtropridgejulyna.gif" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Mean July subtropical ridge position.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>When the subtropical ridge in the northwest Pacific is stronger than normal, it leads to a wet <a href="/wiki/Monsoon" title="Monsoon">monsoon</a> season for <a href="/wiki/Asia" title="Asia">Asia</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> The subtropical ridge position is linked to how far northward monsoon moisture and <a href="/wiki/Thunderstorm" title="Thunderstorm">thunderstorms</a> extend into the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>. The subtropical ridge across <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North America</a> typically migrates far enough northward to begin monsoon conditions across the Desert Southwest from July to September.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> When the subtropical ridge is farther north than normal towards the <a href="/wiki/Four_Corners" title="Four Corners">Four Corners</a>, monsoon thunderstorms can spread northward into <a href="/wiki/Arizona" title="Arizona">Arizona</a>. When the high pressure moves south, its circulation cuts off the moisture and the atmosphere dries out across the Desert Southwest, causing a break in the monsoon regime.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>On the subtropical ridges western edge (eastern coast of continents), the high pressure cell creates a southerly flow of tropical air toward the lower east sides of continents in the summer months. In the United States the subtropical ridge <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bermuda_High" title="Bermuda High">Bermuda High</a> helps create the hot, sultry summers with daily thunderstorms typical of the Gulf and Atlantic states. This flow pattern also occurs on the eastern coasts of continents in other subtropical climates such as <a href="/wiki/South_China" title="South China">South China</a>, southern Japan, central-eastern South America, southern <a href="/wiki/Queensland" title="Queensland">Queensland</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/KwaZulu_Natal" title="KwaZulu Natal">KwaZulu Natal</a> province in South Africa.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>When surface winds become light, the subsidence produced directly under the subtropical ridge can lead to a buildup of particulates in urban areas under the ridge, leading to widespread <a href="/wiki/Haze" title="Haze">haze</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> If the low level <a href="/wiki/Relative_humidity" title="Relative humidity">relative humidity</a> rises towards 100 percent overnight, <a href="/wiki/Fog" title="Fog">fog</a> can form.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
