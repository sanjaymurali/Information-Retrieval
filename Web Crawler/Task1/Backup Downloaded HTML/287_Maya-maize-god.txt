<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Maya maize god</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">
					Jump to:					<a href="#mw-head">navigation</a>, 					<a href="#p-search">search</a>
</div>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Maya_maize_god.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="421" data-file-width="497" height="212" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b1/Maya_maize_god.jpg/250px-Maya_maize_god.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b1/Maya_maize_god.jpg/375px-Maya_maize_god.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/b/b1/Maya_maize_god.jpg 2x" width="250"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Maya_maize_god.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Fig. 1: Tonsured Maize God as a patron of the scribal arts, Classic period</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Like other <a href="/wiki/Mesoamerica" title="Mesoamerica">Mesoamerican</a> people, the traditional <a href="/wiki/Maya_civilization" title="Maya civilization">Mayas</a> recognize in their staple crop, <a href="/wiki/Maize" title="Maize">maize</a>, a vital force with which they strongly identify. This is clearly shown by their mythological traditions. According to the 16th-century <a href="/wiki/Popol_Vuh" title="Popol Vuh">Popol Vuh</a>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hero_Twins" title="Hero Twins">Hero Twins</a> have maize plants for alter egos and man himself is created from maize. The discovery and opening of the Maize Mountain - the place where the corn seeds are hidden - is still one of the most popular of Maya tales. In the Classic period (200-900 AD), the maize deity shows aspects of a <a href="/wiki/Culture_hero" title="Culture hero">culture hero</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Female_and_male_maize_deities">Female and male maize deities</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Maya_maize_god&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Female and male maize deities">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In Mayan oral tradition, maize is usually personified as a woman<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> - like <a href="/wiki/Rice" title="Rice">rice</a> in Southeast Asia, or <a href="/wiki/Wheat" title="Wheat">wheat</a> in <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greece" title="Ancient Greece">ancient Greece</a> and Rome. The acquisition of this woman through bridal capture constitutes one of the basic Mayan myths.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Thompson_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Thompson-2">[2]</a></sup> In contrast to this, the pre-Spanish Mayan aristocracy appears to have primarily conceived of maize as male. The classic period distinguished two male forms: a foliated (leafy) maize god and a <a href="/wiki/Tonsure" title="Tonsure">tonsured</a> one.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Taube_1985_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Taube_1985-3">[3]</a></sup> The foliated god is present in the so-called maize tree (Temple of the Foliated Cross, <a href="/wiki/Palenque" title="Palenque">Palenque</a>), its cobs being shaped like the deity's head. A male maize deity representing the foliated type and labeled God E is present in the three extant Maya books of undisputed authenticity.</p>
<p>Whereas the foliated maize god is a one-dimensional vegetation spirit, the tonsured maize god's functions are much more diverse. When performing ritually, the latter typically wears a netted jade skirt and a belt with a large <a href="/wiki/Spondylus" title="Spondylus">spondylus</a> shell covering the loins. On <a href="/wiki/Maya_stelae" title="Maya stelae">stelae</a>, it is a queen rather than a king that tends to represent the tonsured maize god. The queen thus appears as a maize goddess, in accordance with the Mayan narrative traditions mentioned above.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Late_pre-classic_and_classic_Mayan_maize_mythology">Late pre-classic and classic Mayan maize mythology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Maya_maize_god&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Late pre-classic and classic Mayan maize mythology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:152px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:SAN_FRAN-1.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="8402" data-file-width="4201" height="300" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/f/f3/SAN_FRAN-1.jpg/150px-SAN_FRAN-1.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/f/f3/SAN_FRAN-1.jpg/225px-SAN_FRAN-1.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/f/f3/SAN_FRAN-1.jpg/300px-SAN_FRAN-1.jpg 2x" width="150"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:SAN_FRAN-1.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Fig. 2: San Francisco Capstone depicting the Tonsured Maize God residing in a well.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Many classic Mayan paintings, particularly those on vases, testify to the existence of a rich mythology centered on the tonsured maize god. The late pre-classic murals of <a href="/wiki/San_Bartolo_(Maya_site)" title="San Bartolo (Maya site)">San Bartolo</a> demonstrate its great antiquity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Saturno_2005_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Saturno_2005-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Taube_2010_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Taube_2010-5">[5]</a></sup> Several theories, with varying degrees of ethnographic support, have been formulated to account for episodes such as the maize deity's resurrection from a turtle, his canoe voyage, and his transformation into a cacao tree.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Popol_Vuh_twin_myth_extension">Popol Vuh twin myth extension</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Maya_maize_god&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Popol Vuh twin myth extension">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The tonsured maize god is often accompanied by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hero_Twins" title="Hero Twins">hero twins</a>. Following <a href="/wiki/Karl_Taube" title="Karl Taube">Karl Taube</a>, many scholars (such as <a href="/wiki/Michael_D._Coe" title="Michael D. Coe">Michael D. Coe</a>) believe that the resurrected tonsured maize god of the classic period corresponds to the father of the hero twins in the <a href="/wiki/Popol_Vuh" title="Popol Vuh">Popol Vuh</a> called <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hun-Hunahpu" title="Hun-Hunahpu">Hun-Hunahpu</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Taube_1985_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-Taube_1985-3">[3]</a></sup> However, this generally accepted identification has also been contested.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Braakhuis_2009_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Braakhuis_2009-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cosmological_creation_myth">Cosmological creation myth</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Maya_maize_god&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Cosmological creation myth">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Linda_Schele" title="Linda Schele">Linda Schele</a>'s emphasis on creation has led to a series of interconnected hypotheses all involving the cosmological centrality of the tonsured maize god (or "first father"), to wit: his establishment of the so-called "three-stone hearth" (assumed to represent a constellation);<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Freidel_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Freidel-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> his raising of the world tree;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Freidel_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-Freidel-7">[7]</a></sup> his "dance of creation";<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Freidel_7-2"><a href="#cite_note-Freidel-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Taube_2009_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Taube_2009-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> and his stance as an acrobat, which (more or less coinciding with representations of a crocodile tree) seems to evoke the central world tree.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> The maize god's presence in the San Bartolo arrangement of five world trees has been interpreted as his establishment of the world.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Seasonal_myth">Seasonal myth</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Maya_maize_god&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Seasonal myth">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Another theory, formulated by <a href="/wiki/Simon_Martin_(Mayanist)" title="Simon Martin (Mayanist)">Simon Martin</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Martin_2006_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-Martin_2006-13">[13]</a></sup> focuses on the tonsured maize god's interaction with an aged jaguar deity of trade, <a href="/wiki/God_L" title="God L">God L</a>. This interaction is related to the hero's transformation into a cacao tree conceived as a "trophy tree." God L is assumed to have presided over the dry season dedicated to long-distance trade, warfare, and the cacao harvest, and the Tonsured Maize God over the wet season and the growth of the maize. The onset of the two seasons is thought to be symbolized by the defeat of the maize deity and of God L, respectively.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Gulf_Coast_maize_myth">Gulf Coast maize myth</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Maya_maize_god&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Gulf Coast maize myth">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In many scenes, an aquatic environment strongly comes to the fore (see fig. 2), most famously in the maize deity's resurrection from the carapace of a turtle that is floating on the waters. Braakhuis<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Braakhuis_2009_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-Braakhuis_2009-6">[6]</a></sup> pointed out that such an environment also characterizes an important maize myth shared by many ethnic groups (such as <a href="/wiki/Huastec_people" title="Huastec people">Huaxtecs</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Totonacs" title="Totonacs">Totonacs</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nahuas" title="Nahuas">Nahuas</a> and <a href="/wiki/Zoque_people" title="Zoque people">Zoques</a>) inhabiting Mexico's Gulf Coast. The fact that this myth focuses on a male, rather than a female maize deity, while at the same time establishing an intimate connection between the maize god and the turtle, is adduced in support of the idea that the classic Mayas once formed part of the same narrative tradition. More in particular, the Pre-Classic <a href="/wiki/San_Bartolo_(Maya_site)" title="San Bartolo (Maya site)">San Bartolo</a> Mayan maize deity dancing with a turtle drum amidst aquatic deities may have a connection with a <a href="/wiki/Zoque_people" title="Zoque people">Zoque</a> (<a href="/wiki/Popoluca" title="Popoluca">Popoluca</a>) version of the Gulf Coast maize myth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Braakhuis_2009_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-Braakhuis_2009-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Taube_2009_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-Taube_2009-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Braakhuis_2014_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-Braakhuis_2014-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Names_and_calendar_functions">Names and calendar functions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Maya_maize_god&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Names and calendar functions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Several designations for the pre-Spanish maize god occur in the <i>Book of Chilam Balam</i> of Chumayel. They include <i>ah mun</i> (tender green shoot) <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> and <i>zac uac nal</i> (white six new corn) or <i>uac chuaac nal</i> (six tall new corn).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Thompson_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Thompson-2">[2]</a></sup> In the wake of Schele, the tonsured maize god (hypothetically equated with Hun-Hunahpu) has often been nicknamed "first father." The classic name of the tonsured maize god, which usually includes the numeral "One", is not known with certainty. Schele's "Hun-Nal-Ye" used to be popular; more recently, "Ixim" (maize grains) and "Nal" (wet ear of corn) are being considered.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Zender.2C_Marc_2014_1.E2.80.9314_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-Zender.2C_Marc_2014_1.E2.80.9314-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>In a general sense, maize relates to the day Q'an (ripe or ripeness). The appearance of the tonsured maize god is connected to the base date of the Long Count, 4 Ahau 8 Cumku. The head of the tonsured maize god serves to denote the number 1, that of the foliated maize god the number 8.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Zender.2C_Marc_2014_1.E2.80.9314_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-Zender.2C_Marc_2014_1.E2.80.9314-16">[16]</a></sup> The tonsured maize god is sometimes found associated with the lunar crescent and may therefore have played a role in the divisions of the lunar count; his head seems to occur in glyph C of the Lunar Series (see also <a href="/wiki/Maya_moon_goddess" title="Maya moon goddess">Maya moon goddess</a>).</p>
