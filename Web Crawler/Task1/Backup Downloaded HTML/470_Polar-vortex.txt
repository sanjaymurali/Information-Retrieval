<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Polar vortex</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

<p>A <b>polar vortex</b> is an upper level <a href="/wiki/Low-pressure_area" title="Low-pressure area">low-pressure area</a> lying near the Earth's pole. There are two polar vortices in the <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a>'s atmosphere, which overlie the <a href="/wiki/North_Pole" title="North Pole">North</a>, and <a href="/wiki/South_Pole" title="South Pole">South Poles</a>. Each polar vortex is a persistent, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Synoptic_scale" title="Synoptic scale">large-scale</a>, low pressure zone that rotates counter-clockwise at the North Pole (called a <a href="/wiki/Cyclone" title="Cyclone">cyclone</a>), and clockwise at the South Pole. The bases of the two polar vortices are located in the middle and upper <a href="/wiki/Troposphere" title="Troposphere">troposphere</a> and extend into the <a href="/wiki/Stratosphere" title="Stratosphere">stratosphere</a>. Beneath that lies a large mass of cold, dense arctic air. The vortices weaken and strengthen from year to year. When the vortex of the arctic is strong it is well defined, there is a single vortex and the arctic air is well contained; when weaker, which it generally is, it will break into two or more vortices; when very weak, the flow of arctic air becomes more disorganized and masses of cold arctic air can push equatorward, bringing with it a rapid and sharp temperature drop. The interface between the cold dry air mass of the pole and the warm moist air mass further south defines the location of the polar front. The polar front is centered, roughly at 60° latitude. A polar vortex strengthens in the winter and weakens in the summer due to its dependence on the temperature difference between the equator and the poles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HB_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-HB-1">[1]</a></sup> The vortices span less than 1,000 kilometers (620 miles) in diameter within which they rotate counter-clockwise in the <a href="/wiki/Northern_Hemisphere" title="Northern Hemisphere">Northern Hemisphere</a>, and in a clockwise fashion in the <a href="/wiki/Southern_Hemisphere" title="Southern Hemisphere">Southern Hemisphere</a>. As with other cyclones, their rotation is driven by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coriolis_effect" title="Coriolis effect">Coriolis effect</a>.</p>
<p>When the polar vortex is strong, there is a single vortex with a jet stream that is "well constrained" near the polar front. When the northern vortex weakens, it separates into two or more vortices, the strongest of which are near <a href="/wiki/Baffin_Island" title="Baffin Island">Baffin Island</a>, <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a> and the other over northeast <a href="/wiki/Siberia" title="Siberia">Siberia</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-glossvortex_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-glossvortex-2">[2]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Antarctic" title="Antarctic">Antarctic</a> vortex of the Southern Hemisphere is a single low pressure zone that is found near the edge of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ross_ice_shelf" title="Ross ice shelf">Ross ice shelf</a> near 160 west longitude. When the polar vortex is strong, the mid-latitude <a href="/wiki/Westerlies" title="Westerlies">Westerlies</a> (winds at the surface level between 30° and 60° latitude from the west) increase in strength and are persistent. When the polar vortex is weak, high pressure zones of the mid latitudes may push poleward, moving the polar vortex, jet stream, and polar front equatorward. The jet stream is seen to "buckle" and deviate south. This rapidly brings cold dry air into contact with the warm, moist air of the mid latitudes, resulting in a rapid and dramatic change of weather known as a "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cold_snap" title="Cold snap">cold snap</a>".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Ozone_depletion" title="Ozone depletion">Ozone depletion</a> occurs within the polar vortices – particularly over the Southern Hemisphere – reaching a maximum depletion in the spring.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Polar_vortex&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The polar vortex was first described as early as 1853.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> The phenomenon's sudden stratospheric warming (SSW) develops during the winter in the Northern Hemisphere and was discovered in 1952 with <a href="/wiki/Radiosonde" title="Radiosonde">radiosonde</a> observations at altitudes higher than 20 km.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>The phenomenon was mentioned frequently in the news and weather media in the <a href="/wiki/Early_2014_North_American_cold_wave" title="Early 2014 North American cold wave">cold North American winter of 2013-2014</a>, popularizing the term as an explanation of very cold temperatures.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Identification">Identification</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Polar_vortex&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Identification">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Polar cyclones are low pressure zones embedded within the polar air masses, and exist year-round. The stratospheric polar vortex develops at latitudes above the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Subtropical_jet_stream" title="Subtropical jet stream">subtropical jet stream</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> Horizontally, most polar vortices have a radius of less than 1,000 kilometres (620 mi).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pause_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-pause-8">[8]</a></sup> Since polar vortices exist from the stratosphere downward into the mid-troposphere,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-glossvortex_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-glossvortex-2">[2]</a></sup> a variety of heights/pressure levels are used to mark its position. The 50 mb pressure surface is most often used to identify its stratospheric location.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> At the level of the tropopause, the extent of closed contours of <a href="/wiki/Potential_temperature" title="Potential temperature">potential temperature</a> can be used to determine its strength. Others have used levels down to the 500 hPa pressure level (about 5,460 metres (17,910 ft) above sea level during the winter) to identify the polar vortex.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Duration_and_power">Duration and power</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Polar_vortex&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Duration and power">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Polarvortexwinter.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1029" data-file-width="1800" height="126" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8c/Polarvortexwinter.jpg/220px-Polarvortexwinter.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8c/Polarvortexwinter.jpg/330px-Polarvortexwinter.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8c/Polarvortexwinter.jpg/440px-Polarvortexwinter.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Polarvortexwinter.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Polar vortex and weather impacts due to stratospheric warming</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Polar vortices are weakest during summer and strongest during winter. <a href="/wiki/Extratropical_cyclone" title="Extratropical cyclone">Extratropical cyclones</a> that migrate into higher latitudes when the polar vortex is weak can disrupt the single vortex creating smaller vortices (<a href="/wiki/Cold-core_low" title="Cold-core low">cold-core lows</a>) within the polar air mass.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> Those individual vortices can persist for more than a month.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pause_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-pause-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Volcano" title="Volcano">Volcanic</a> eruptions in the <a href="/wiki/Tropics" title="Tropics">tropics</a> can lead to a stronger polar vortex during winter for as long as two years afterwards.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> The strength and position of the polar vortex shapes the flow pattern in a broad area about it. An index which is used in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Northern_hemisphere" title="Northern hemisphere">northern hemisphere</a> to gauge its magnitude is the <a href="/wiki/Arctic_oscillation" title="Arctic oscillation">Arctic oscillation</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>When the Arctic vortex is at its strongest, there is a single vortex, but normally, the Arctic vortex is elongated in shape, with two cyclone centers, one over Baffin Island in <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a> and the other over northeast <a href="/wiki/Siberian_High" title="Siberian High">Siberia</a>. When the Arctic pattern is at its weakest, subtropic air masses can intrude poleward causing the Arctic air masses to move equatorward, as during the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Winter_1985_Arctic_outbreak" title="Winter 1985 Arctic outbreak">Winter 1985 Arctic outbreak</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-roanoke_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-roanoke-14">[14]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Antarctic" title="Antarctic">Antarctic</a> polar vortex is more pronounced and persistent than the <a href="/wiki/Arctic" title="Arctic">Arctic</a> one. In the Arctic the distribution of land masses at high latitudes in the Northern Hemisphere gives rise to <a href="/wiki/Rossby_wave" title="Rossby wave">Rossby waves</a> which contribute to the breakdown of the polar vortex, whereas in the Southern Hemisphere the vortex is less disturbed. The breakdown of the polar vortex is an extreme event known as a <a href="/wiki/Sudden_stratospheric_warming" title="Sudden stratospheric warming">sudden stratospheric warming</a>, here the vortex completely breaks down and an associated warming of 30–50 °C (54–90 °F) over a few days can occur.</p>
<p>The waxing and waning of the polar vortex is driven by the movement of mass and the transfer of heat in the polar region. In the autumn, the <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/Special:Search/circumpolar" title="wikt:Special:Search/circumpolar">circumpolar</a> winds increase in speed and the polar vortex rises into the <a href="/wiki/Stratosphere" title="Stratosphere">stratosphere</a>. The result is the polar air forms a coherent rotating air mass: the polar vortex. As winter approaches, the vortex core cools, the winds decrease, and the vortex energy declines. Once late winter and early spring approach the vortex is at its weakest. As a result, during late winter, large fragments of the vortex air can be diverted into lower latitudes by stronger weather systems intruding from those latitudes. In the lowest level of the stratosphere, strong <a href="/wiki/Potential_vorticity" title="Potential vorticity">potential vorticity</a> gradients remain, and the majority of that air remains confined within the polar air mass into December in the Southern Hemisphere and April in the Northern Hemisphere, well after the breakup of the vortex in the mid-stratosphere.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>The breakup of the northern polar vortex occurs between mid March to mid May. This event signifies the transition from winter to spring, and has impacts on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydrological_cycle" title="Hydrological cycle">hydrological cycle</a>, growing seasons of vegetation, and overall ecosystem productivity. The timing of the transition also influences changes in sea ice, ozone, air temperature, and cloudiness. Early and late polar breakup episodes have occurred, due to variations in the stratospheric flow structure and upward spreading of planetary waves from the troposphere.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (April 2016)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup> As a result of increased waves into the vortex, the vortex experiences more rapid warming than normal, resulting in an earlier breakup and spring. When the breakup comes early, it is characterized by with persistent of remnants of the vortex. When the breakup is late, the remnants dissipate rapidly. When the breakup is early, there is one warming period from late February to middle March. When the breakup is late, there are two warming periods, one January, and one in March. Zonal mean temperature, wind, and <a href="/wiki/Geopotential" title="Geopotential">geopotential</a> height exert varying deviations from their normal values before and after early breakups, while the deviations remain constant before and after late breakups. Scientists are connecting a delay in the Arctic vortex breakup with a reduction of planetary wave activities, few stratospheric sudden warming events, and depletion of ozone.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (April 2016)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Sudden_stratospheric_warming" title="Sudden stratospheric warming">Sudden stratospheric warming</a> events are associated with weaker polar vortices. This warming of stratospheric air can reverse the circulation in the Arctic Polar Vortex from counter-clockwise to clockwise.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> These changes aloft force changes in the troposphere below.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> An example of an effect on the troposphere is the change in speed of the Atlantic Ocean circulation pattern. A soft spot just south of Greenland is where the initial step of <a href="/wiki/Downwelling" title="Downwelling">downwelling</a> occurs, nicknamed the "Achilles Heel of the North Atlantic". Small amounts of heating or cooling traveling from the polar vortex can trigger or delay <a href="/wiki/Downwelling" title="Downwelling">downwelling</a>, altering the <a href="/wiki/North_Atlantic_Current" title="North Atlantic Current">Gulf Stream Current</a> of the Atlantic, and the speed of other ocean currents. Since all other oceans depend on the Atlantic Ocean's movement of heat energy, climates across the planet can be dramatically affected. The weakening or strengthening of the polar vortex can alter the sea circulation more than a mile beneath the waves.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> Strengthening storm systems within the troposphere that cool the poles, intensify the polar vortex. <a href="/wiki/La_Ni%C3%B1a" title="La Niña">La Niña</a>–related climate anomalies significantly strengthen the polar vortex.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> Intensification of the polar vortex produces changes in relative humidity as downward intrusions of dry, stratospheric air enter the vortex core. With a strengthening of the vortex comes a longwave cooling due to a decrease in water vapor concentration near the vortex. The decreased water content is a result of a lower <a href="/wiki/Tropopause" title="Tropopause">tropopause</a> within the vortex, which places dry stratospheric air above moist tropospheric air.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> Instability is caused when the vortex tube, the line of concentrated <a href="/wiki/Vorticity" title="Vorticity">vorticity</a>, is displaced. When this occurs, the vortex rings become more unstable and prone to shifting by planetary waves.The planetary wave activity in both hemispheres varies year-to-year, producing a corresponding response in the strength and temperature of the polar vortex.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> The number of waves around the perimeter of the vortex are related to the core size; as the vortex core decreases, the number of waves increase.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<p>The degree of the mixing of polar and mid-latitude air depends on the evolution and position of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Polar_night_jet" title="Polar night jet">polar night jet</a>. In general, the mixing is less inside the vortex than outside. Mixing occurs with unstable planetary waves that are characteristic of the middle and upper stratosphere in winter. Prior to vortex breakdown, there is little transport of air out of the Arctic Polar Vortex due to strong barriers above 420 km (261 miles). The polar night jet which exists below this, is weak in the early winter. As a result, it does not deviate any descending polar air, which then mixes with air in the mid-latitudes. In the late winter, air parcels do not descend as much, reducing mixing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> After the vortex is broken up, the ex-vortex air is dispersed into the middle latitudes within a month.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JGR2012_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-JGR2012-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<p>Sometimes, a mass of the polar vortex breaks off before the end of the final warming period. If large enough, the piece can move into Canada and the Midwestern, Central, Southern, and Northeastern United States. This diversion of the polar vortex can occur due to the displacement of the polar jet stream; for example, the significant northwestward direction of the polar jet stream in the western part of the United States during the winters of 2013–2014, and 2014-2015. This caused warm, dry conditions in the west, and cold, snowy conditions in the east.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> Occasionally, the high-pressure air mass, called the Greenland Block, can cause the polar vortex to divert to the south, rather than follow its normal path over the North Atlantic.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></p>

<p>A study in 2001 found that stratospheric circulation can have anomalous effects on weather regimes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Baldwin_2001_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-Baldwin_2001-29">[29]</a></sup> In the same year researchers found a statistical correlation between weak polar vortex and outbreaks of severe cold in the Northern Hemisphere.<sup more recent years scientists identified interactions with <a href="/wiki/Arctic_sea_ice_decline" title="Arctic sea ice decline">Arctic sea ice decline</a>, reduced snow cover, <a href="/wiki/Evapotranspiration" title="Evapotranspiration">evapotranspiration</a> patterns, <a href="/wiki/North_Atlantic_oscillation" title="North Atlantic oscillation">NAO</a> anomalies or weather anomalies which are linked to the polar vortex and <a href="/wiki/Jet_stream" title="Jet stream">jet stream</a> configuration.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Baldwin_2001_29-1"><a href="#cite_note-Baldwin_2001-29">[29]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Song_2004_31-1"><a href="#cite_note-Song_2004-31">[31]</a></sup><sup class="reference" <p>The general assumption is that reduced snow cover and sea ice reflect less sunlight and therefore evaporation and transpiration increases, which in turn alters the pressure and temperature gradient of the polar vortex, causing it to weaken or collapse. This becomes apparent when the jet stream amplitude increases (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Meanders" title="Meanders">meanders</a>) over the northern hemisphere, causing <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rossby_waves" title="Rossby waves">Rossby waves</a> to propagate farther to the south or north, which in turn transports warmer air to the north pole and polar air into lower latitudes. The jet stream amplitude increases with a weaker polar vortex, hence increases the chance for weather systems to become blocked. A recent blocking event emerged when a high-pressure over Greenland steered <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Sandy" title="Hurricane Sandy">Hurricane Sandy</a> into the northern <a href="/wiki/Mid-Atlantic_states" title="Mid-Atlantic states">Mid-Atlantic states</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Ozone_depletion">Ozone depletion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Polar_vortex&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Ozone depletion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The chemistry of the Antarctic polar vortex has created severe <a href="/wiki/Ozone_depletion" title="Ozone depletion">ozone depletion</a>. The nitric acid in <a href="/wiki/Polar_stratospheric_cloud" title="Polar stratospheric cloud">polar stratospheric clouds</a> reacts with <a href="/wiki/Chlorofluorocarbon" title="Chlorofluorocarbon">chlorofluorocarbons</a> to form <a href="/wiki/Chlorine" title="Chlorine">chlorine</a>, which <a href="/wiki/Catalysis" title="Catalysis">catalyzes</a> the photochemical destruction of <a href="/wiki/Ozone" title="Ozone">ozone</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> Chlorine concentrations build up during the polar winter, and the consequent ozone destruction is greatest when the sunlight returns in spring.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> These clouds can only form at temperatures below about −80 °C (−112 °F). Since there is greater air exchange between the Arctic and the mid-latitudes, ozone depletion at the north pole is much less severe than at the south.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> Accordingly, the seasonal reduction of ozone levels over the Arctic is usually characterized as an "ozone dent", whereas the more severe ozone depletion over the Antarctic is considered an "ozone hole". This said, chemical ozone destruction in the 2011 Arctic polar vortex attained, for the first time, a level clearly identifiable as an Arctic "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ozone_hole" title="Ozone hole">ozone hole</a>".<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>

<p>Other astronomical bodies are also known to have polar vortices, including <a href="/wiki/Venus" title="Venus">Venus</a> (double vortex—that is, two polar vortices at a pole),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Mars" title="Mars">Mars</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jupiter" title="Jupiter">Jupiter</a>, <a href="/wiki/Saturn" title="Saturn">Saturn</a>, and Saturn's moon <a href="/wiki/Titan_(moon)" title="Titan (moon)">Titan</a>.</p>
