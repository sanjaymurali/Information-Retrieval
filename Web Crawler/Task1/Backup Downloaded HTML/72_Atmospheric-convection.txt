<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Atmospheric convection</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">

<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="thumb tright">

<p><b>Atmospheric convection</b> is the result of a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Air_parcel" title="Air parcel">parcel</a>-environment instability, or temperature difference, layer in the atmosphere. Different <a href="/wiki/Lapse_rate" title="Lapse rate">lapse rates</a> within dry and moist air lead to instability. Mixing of air during the day which expands the height of the <a href="/wiki/Planetary_boundary_layer" title="Planetary boundary layer">planetary boundary layer</a> leads to increased winds, <a href="/wiki/Cumulus_cloud" title="Cumulus cloud">cumulus cloud</a> development, and decreased surface <a href="/wiki/Dew_point" title="Dew point">dew points</a>. Moist convection leads to <a href="/wiki/Thunderstorm" title="Thunderstorm">thunderstorm</a> development, which is often responsible for <a href="/wiki/Severe_weather" title="Severe weather">severe weather</a> throughout the world. Special threats from thunderstorms include <a href="/wiki/Hail" title="Hail">hail</a>, <a href="/wiki/Downburst" title="Downburst">downbursts</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Tornado" title="Tornado">tornadoes</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Overview">Overview</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Atmospheric_convection&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Overview">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>There are a few general <a href="/wiki/Archetype" title="Archetype">archetypes</a> of atmospheric instability that are used to explain <a href="/wiki/Convection" title="Convection">convection</a> (or lack thereof). A necessary (but not sufficient) condition for convection is that the environmental <a href="/wiki/Lapse_rate" title="Lapse rate">lapse rate</a> (the rate of decrease of temperature with height) is steeper than the lapse rate experienced by a rising parcel of air. When this condition is met, upward-displaced air parcels can become buoyant and thus experience a further upward force. Buoyant convection begins at the <a href="/wiki/Level_of_free_convection" title="Level of free convection">level of free convection (LFC)</a>, above which an air parcel may ascend through the <a href="/wiki/Free_convective_layer" title="Free convective layer">free convective layer (FCL)</a> with positive buoyancy. Its buoyancy turns negative at the <a href="/wiki/Equilibrium_level" title="Equilibrium level">equilibrium level (EL)</a>, but the parcel's vertical momentum may carry it to the <a href="/wiki/Maximum_parcel_level" title="Maximum parcel level">maximum parcel level (MPL)</a> where the negative buoyancy decelerates the parcel to a stop. Integrating the buoyancy force over the parcel's vertical displacement yields Convective Available Potential Energy (CAPE), the Joules of energy available per kilogram of potentially buoyant air. CAPE is an upper limit for an ideal undilute parcel, and the square root of twice the CAPE is sometimes called a thermodynamic speed limit for updrafts, based on the simple <a href="/wiki/Kinetic_energy" title="Kinetic energy">kinetic energy equation</a>.</p>
<p>However, such buoyant <a href="/wiki/Acceleration" title="Acceleration">acceleration</a> concepts give an oversimplified view of convection. <a href="/wiki/Drag_(physics)" title="Drag (physics)">Drag</a> is an opposite force to counter buoyancy <a class="external autonumber" href="http://www.flame.org/~cdoswell/publications/Overview_Chapter.pdf" rel="nofollow">[1]</a>, so that parcel ascent occurs under a balance of forces, like the <a href="/wiki/Terminal_velocity" title="Terminal velocity">terminal velocity</a> of a falling object. Buoyancy may be reduced by <a href="/wiki/Entrainment_(meteorology)" title="Entrainment (meteorology)">entrainment</a>, which dilutes the parcel with environmental air. See the <a href="/wiki/Convective_available_potential_energy" title="Convective available potential energy">CAPE</a>, <a href="/wiki/Buoyancy" title="Buoyancy">buoyancy</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Air_parcel" title="Air parcel">parcel</a> links for a more in depth mathematical explanation of these processes.</p>
<p>Atmospheric convection is called <i>deep</i> when it extends from near the surface to above the 500 hPa level, generally stopping at the <a href="/wiki/Tropopause" title="Tropopause">tropopause</a> at around 200 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/HPa" title="HPa">hPa</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> Most atmospheric deep convection occurs in the <a href="/wiki/Tropics" title="Tropics">tropics</a> as the rising branch of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hadley_circulation" title="Hadley circulation">Hadley circulation</a>; and represents a strong local coupling between the surface and the upper troposphere which is largely absent in winter midlatitudes. Its counterpart in the ocean (deep convection downward in the water column) only occurs at a few locations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> While less dynamically important than in the atmosphere, such oceanic convection is responsible for the worldwide existence of cold water in the lowest layers of the ocean.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Initiation">Initiation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Atmospheric_convection&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Initiation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thermal_column" title="Thermal column">thermal column</a> (or thermal) is a vertical section of rising air in the lower altitudes of the Earth's atmosphere. Thermals are created by the uneven heating of the Earth's surface from solar radiation. The Sun warms the ground, which in turn warms the air directly above it. The warmer air expands, becoming less dense than the surrounding air mass, and creating a <a href="/wiki/Thermal_low" title="Thermal low">thermal low</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> The mass of lighter air rises, and as it does, it cools due to its expansion at lower high-altitude pressures. It stops rising when it has cooled to the same temperature as the surrounding air. Associated with a thermal is a downward flow surrounding the thermal column. The downward moving exterior is caused by colder air being displaced at the top of the thermal. Another convection-driven weather effect is the <a href="/wiki/Sea_breeze" title="Sea breeze">sea breeze</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jet_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Jet-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Thunderstorms">Thunderstorms</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Atmospheric_convection&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Thunderstorms">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Thunderstorm" title="Thunderstorm">Thunderstorm</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:502px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Thunderstorm_formation.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="554" data-file-width="1087" height="255" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c8/Thunderstorm_formation.jpg/500px-Thunderstorm_formation.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c8/Thunderstorm_formation.jpg/750px-Thunderstorm_formation.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c8/Thunderstorm_formation.jpg/1000px-Thunderstorm_formation.jpg 2x" width="500"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Thunderstorm_formation.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Stages of a thunderstorm's life.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Warm air has a lower density than cool air, so warm air rises within cooler air,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> similar to <a href="/wiki/Hot_air_balloon" title="Hot air balloon">hot air balloons</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Clouds form as relatively warmer air carrying moisture rises within cooler air. As the moist air rises, it cools causing some of the <a href="/wiki/Water_vapor" title="Water vapor">water vapor</a> in the rising packet of air to <a href="/wiki/Condensation" title="Condensation">condense</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> When the moisture condenses, it releases energy known as <a href="/wiki/Latent_heat" title="Latent heat">latent heat</a> of vaporization which allows the rising packet of air to cool less than its surrounding air,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> continuing the cloud's ascension. If enough <a href="/wiki/Convective_available_potential_energy" title="Convective available potential energy">instability</a> is present in the atmosphere, this process will continue long enough for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cumulonimbus" title="Cumulonimbus">cumulonimbus clouds</a> to form, which support lightning and thunder. Generally, thunderstorms require three conditions to form: moisture, an unstable airmass, and a lifting force (heat).</p>
<p>All <a href="/wiki/Thunderstorm" title="Thunderstorm">thunderstorms</a>, regardless of type, go through three stages: the <b>developing stage</b>, the <b>mature stage</b>, and the <b>dissipation stage</b>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Extreme_Weather_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Extreme_Weather-11">[11]</a></sup> The average thunderstorm has a 24 km (15 mi) diameter. Depending on the conditions present in the atmosphere, these three stages take an average of 30 minutes to go through.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tsbasics_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-tsbasics-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>There are four main types of thunderstorms: single-cell, multicell, squall line (also called multicell line) and supercell. Which type forms depends on the instability and relative wind conditions at different layers of the atmosphere ("<a href="/wiki/Wind_shear" title="Wind shear">wind shear</a>"). Single-cell thunderstorms form in environments of low vertical wind shear and last only 20–30 minutes. Organized thunderstorms and thunderstorm clusters/lines can have longer life cycles as they form in environments of significant vertical wind shear, which aids the development of stronger updrafts as well as various forms of severe weather. The supercell is the strongest of the thunderstorms, most commonly associated with large hail, high winds, and tornado formation.</p>
<p>The latent heat release from condensation is the determinate between significant convection and almost no convection at all. The fact that air is generally cooler during winter months, and therefore cannot hold as much water vapor and associated latent heat, is why significant convection (thunderstorms) are infrequent in cooler areas during that period. <a href="/wiki/Thundersnow" title="Thundersnow">Thundersnow</a> is one situation where forcing mechanisms provide support for very steep environmental lapse rates, which as mentioned before is an archetype for favored convection. The small amount of latent heat released from air rising and condensing moisture in a thundersnow also serves to increase this convective potential, although minimally. There are also three types of thunderstorms: orographic, air mass, and frontal.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Boundaries_and_forcing">Boundaries and forcing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Atmospheric_convection&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Boundaries and forcing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Despite the fact that there might be a layer in the atmosphere that has positive values of CAPE, if the parcel does not reach or begin rising to that level, the most significant convection that occurs in the FCL will not be realized. This can occur for numerous reasons. Primarily, it is the result of a cap, or <a href="/wiki/Convective_inhibition" title="Convective inhibition">convective inhibition (CIN/CINH)</a>. Processes that can erode this inhibition are heating of the Earth's surface and forcing. Such forcing mechanisms encourage upward vertical velocity, characterized by a speed that is relatively low to what you find in a thunderstorm updraft. Because of this, it is not the actual air being pushed to its LFC that "breaks through" the inhibition, but rather the forcing cools the inhibition adiabatically. This would counter, or "erode" the increase of temperature with height that is present during a capping inversion.</p>
<p>Forcing mechanisms that can lead to the eroding of inhibition are ones that create some sort of evacuation of mass in the upper parts of the atmosphere, or a surplus of mass in the low levels of the atmosphere, which would lead to upper level divergence or lower level convergence, respectively. Upward vertical motion will often follow. Specifically, a <a href="/wiki/Cold_front" title="Cold front">cold front</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sea_breeze" title="Sea breeze">sea/lake breeze</a>, <a href="/wiki/Outflow_boundary" title="Outflow boundary">outflow boundary</a>, or forcing through vorticity dynamics (<a href="/wiki/Positive_vorticity_advection" title="Positive vorticity advection">differential positive vorticity advection</a>) of the atmosphere such as with troughs, both <a href="/wiki/Shortwave_(meteorology)" title="Shortwave (meteorology)">shortwave</a> and <a href="/wiki/Rossby_wave" title="Rossby wave">longwave</a>. <a href="/wiki/Jet_stream" title="Jet stream">Jet streak</a> dynamics through the imbalance of Coriolis and pressure gradient forces, causing <a href="/wiki/Geostrophic_wind" title="Geostrophic wind">subgeostrophic and supergeostrophic flows</a>, can also create upward vertical velocities.There are numerous other atmospheric setups in which upward vertical velocities can be created.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Concerns_regarding_severe_deep_moist_convection">Concerns regarding severe deep moist convection</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Atmospheric_convection&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Concerns regarding severe deep moist convection">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Buoyancy is key to thunderstorm growth and is necessary for any of the severe threats within a thunderstorm. There are other processes, not necessarily thermodynamic, that can increase updraft strength. These include <a href="/wiki/Mesocyclone" title="Mesocyclone">updraft rotation</a>, low level convergence, and evacuation of mass out of the top of the updraft via strong upper level winds and the <a href="/wiki/Jet_stream" title="Jet stream">jet stream</a>.</p>

<p>Like other precipitation in cumulonimbus clouds hail begins as water droplets. As the droplets rise and the temperature goes below freezing, they become <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Supercooled" title="Supercooled">supercooled</a> <a href="/wiki/Water" title="Water">water</a> and will freeze on contact with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Condensation_nuclei" title="Condensation nuclei">condensation nuclei</a>. A cross-section through a large hailstone shows an onion-like structure. This means the hailstone is made of thick and translucent layers, alternating with layers that are thin, white and opaque. Former theory suggested that hailstones were subjected to multiple descents and ascents, falling into a zone of humidity and refreezing as they were uplifted. This up and down motion was thought to be responsible for the successive layers of the hailstone. New research (based on theory and field study) has shown this is not necessarily true.</p>
<p>The storm's <a href="/wiki/Vertical_draft" title="Vertical draft">updraft</a>, with upwardly directed wind speeds as high as 180 kilometres per hour (110 mph),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ncar_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-ncar-14">[14]</a></sup> blow the forming hailstones up the cloud. As the hailstone ascends it passes into areas of the cloud where the concentration of humidity and supercooled water droplets varies. The hailstone’s growth rate changes depending on the variation in humidity and supercooled water droplets that it encounters. The accretion rate of these water droplets is another factor in the hailstone’s growth. When the hailstone moves into an area with a high concentration of water droplets, it captures the latter and acquires a translucent layer. Should the hailstone move into an area where mostly water vapour is available, it acquires a layer of opaque white ice.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nelson_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-Nelson-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>Furthermore, the hailstone’s speed depends on its position in the cloud’s updraft and its mass. This determines the varying thicknesses of the layers of the hailstone. The accretion rate of supercooled water droplets onto the hailstone depends on the relative velocities between these water droplets and the hailstone itself. This means that generally the larger hailstones will form some distance from the stronger updraft where they can pass more time growing<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nelson_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-Nelson-15">[15]</a></sup> As the hailstone grows it releases <a href="/wiki/Latent_heat" title="Latent heat">latent heat</a>, which keeps its exterior in a liquid phase. Undergoing 'wet growth', the outer layer is <i>sticky</i>, or more adhesive, so a single hailstone may grow by collision with other smaller hailstones, forming a larger entity with an irregular shape.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Brimlow_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-Brimlow-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>The hailstone will keep rising in the thunderstorm until its mass can no longer be supported by the updraft. This may take at least 30 minutes based on the force of the updrafts in the hail-producing thunderstorm, whose top is usually greater than 10 kilometres (6.2 mi) high. It then falls toward the ground while continuing to grow, based on the same processes, until it leaves the cloud. It will later begin to melt as it passes into air above freezing temperature<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>Thus, a unique trajectory in the thunderstorm is sufficient to explain the layer-like structure of the hailstone. The only case in which we can discuss multiple trajectories is in a multicellular thunderstorm where the hailstone may be ejected from the top of the "mother" cell and captured in the updraft of a more intense "daughter cell". This however is an exceptional case.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nelson_15-2"><a href="#cite_note-Nelson-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Downburst">Downburst</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Atmospheric_convection&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Downburst">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A downburst is created by a column of sinking air that, after hitting ground level, spreads out in all directions and is capable of producing damaging straight-line winds of over 240 kilometres per hour (150 mph), often producing damage similar to, but distinguishable from, that caused by <a href="/wiki/Tornado" title="Tornado">tornadoes</a>. This is because the physical properties of a downburst are completely different from those of a tornado. Downburst damage will radiate from a central point as the descending column spreads out when impacting the surface, whereas tornado damage tends towards convergent damage consistent with rotating winds. To differentiate between tornado damage and damage from a downburst, the term <b>straight-line winds</b> is applied to damage from microbursts.</p>
<p>Downbursts are particularly strong <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Updraft_and_Downdraft" title="Updraft and Downdraft">downdrafts</a> from thunderstorms. Downbursts in air that is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Precipitation_(meteorology)" title="Precipitation (meteorology)">precipitation</a> free or contains <a href="/wiki/Virga" title="Virga">virga</a> are known as <b>dry downbursts</b>;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-micro_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-micro-18">[18]</a></sup> those accompanied with precipitation are known as <b>wet downbursts</b>. Most downbursts are less than 4 kilometres (2.5 mi) in extent: these are called <a href="/wiki/Microburst" title="Microburst">microbursts</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Macroburst_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-Macroburst-19">[19]</a></sup> Downbursts larger than 4 kilometres (2.5 mi) in extent are sometimes called <b>macrobursts</b>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Macroburst_19-1"><a href="#cite_note-Macroburst-19">[19]</a></sup> Downbursts can occur over large areas. In the extreme case, a <a href="/wiki/Derecho" title="Derecho">derecho</a> can cover a huge area more than 320 kilometres (200 mi) wide and over 1,600 kilometres (990 mi) long, lasting up to 12 hours or more, and is associated with some of the most intense straight-line winds,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> but the generative process is somewhat different from that of most downbursts.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Tornadoes">Tornadoes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Atmospheric_convection&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Tornadoes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Tornado" title="Tornado">Tornado</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">

<p>A tornado is a dangerous rotating column of air in contact with both the surface of the earth and the base of a <a href="/wiki/Cumulonimbus_cloud" title="Cumulonimbus cloud">cumulonimbus cloud</a> (thundercloud) or a <a href="/wiki/Cumulus_cloud" title="Cumulus cloud">cumulus cloud</a>, in rare cases. Tornadoes come in many sizes but typically form a visible <a href="/wiki/Funnel_cloud" title="Funnel cloud">condensation funnel</a> whose narrowest end reaches the earth and surrounded by a cloud of <a href="/wiki/Debris" title="Debris">debris</a> and <a href="/wiki/Dust" title="Dust">dust</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Renno_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-Renno-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p>Tornadoes wind speeds generally average between 64 kilometres per hour (40 mph) and 180 kilometres per hour (110 mph). They are approximately 75 metres (246 ft) across and travel a few kilometers before dissipating. Some attain wind speeds in excess of 480 kilometres per hour (300 mph), may stretch more than a 1.6 kilometres (0.99 mi) across, and maintain contact with the ground for more than 100 kilometres (62 mi).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SPC_FAQ_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-SPC_FAQ-22">[22]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fastest_wind_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-fastest_wind-23">[23]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-widest_tornado_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-widest_tornado-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<p>Tornadoes, despite being one of the most destructive weather phenomena are generally short-lived. A long-lived tornado generally lasts no more than an hour, but some have been known to last for 2 hours or longer (for example, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tri-state_tornado" title="Tri-state tornado">Tri-state tornado</a>). Due to their relatively short duration, less information is known about the development and formation of tornadoes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tornadoes2008_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-Tornadoes2008-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Measurement">Measurement</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Atmospheric_convection&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Measurement">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The potential for convection in the atmosphere is often measured by an atmospheric temperature/dewpoint profile with height. This is often displayed on a <a href="/wiki/Skew-T_log-P_diagram" title="Skew-T log-P diagram">Skew-T</a> chart or other similar thermodynamic diagram. These can be plotted by a <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_sounding" title="Atmospheric sounding">measured sounding analysis</a>, which is the sending of a <a href="/wiki/Radiosonde" title="Radiosonde">radiosonde</a> attached to a balloon into the atmosphere to take the measurements with height. <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_model" title="Atmospheric model">Forecast models</a> can also create these diagrams, but are less accurate due to model uncertainties and biases, and have lower spatial resolution. Although, the temporal resolution of forecast model soundings is greater than the direct measurements, where the former can have plots for intervals of up to every 3 hours, and the latter as having only 2 per day (although when a convective event is expected a special sounding might be taken outside of the normal schedule of 00Z and then 12Z.).<a class="external autonumber" href="http://www.stormchaser.niu.edu/machine/fcstsound.html" rel="nofollow">[2]</a></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_forecasting_concerns">Other forecasting concerns</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Atmospheric_convection&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Other forecasting concerns">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Atmospheric convection can also be responsible for and have implications on a number of other weather conditions. A few examples on the smaller scale would include: Convection mixing the planetary boundary layer (PBL) and allowing drier air aloft to the surface thereby decreasing dew points, creating cumulus-type clouds which can limit a small amount of sunshine, increasing surface winds, making outflow boundaries/and other smaller boundaries more diffuse, and the eastward propagation of the dryline during the day. On the larger scale, rising of air can lead to warm core surface lows, often found in the desert southwest.</p>
