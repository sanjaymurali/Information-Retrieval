<p>The <b>Honda CBR600F</b>, known as the 'Hurricane' in the US market, is a sports <a href="/wiki/Motorcycle" title="Motorcycle">motorcycle</a> made by <a href="/wiki/Honda" title="Honda">Honda</a> from 1987 to 1990. In Austria and Mexico, a smaller version, the <b>CBR500F</b>, was marketed. In 2011, Honda released an all-new model of the same name.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> The original CBR600F along with the CBR750F and <a href="/wiki/Honda_CBR1000F" title="Honda CBR1000F">CBR1000F</a> was Honda's first <a href="/wiki/Inline-four_engine" title="Inline-four engine">inline four-cylinder</a>, <a href="/wiki/Motorcycle_fairing" title="Motorcycle fairing">fully faired</a> sport bike. It had a liquid-cooled 85 hp (63 kW) <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/DOHC" title="DOHC">DOHC</a> 16-valve engine, and a six-speed transmission and an ignition with <a href="/wiki/Capacitor_discharge_ignition" title="Capacitor discharge ignition">CDI</a>, 2 <a href="/wiki/Ignition_coil" title="Ignition coil">coils</a>. The Honda Hurricane was introduced as the newest style of sport bike.</p>

<p>The <b>Honda CBR600 F2</b> was made from 1991 to 1994. It was introduced to replace the CBR600 Hurricane, or F1, and was considered one of Honda's most modern and innovative <a href="/wiki/Types_of_motorcycles#Sport_bike" title="Types of motorcycles">sport bikes</a>. Development of the second generation CBR began in early 1989. Hurricane LPL Ishikawa led the development of the new bike, known internally as MV9 but also called the F2, an alphanumeric that led to its official name: CBR600F2.</p>
<p>The F2's development began with meetings to discuss concepts and sketches for the new bike. A few months later, an F2 prototype was produced. Painted black, the bike looked faster and sleeker than the more blocky first generation bike, and test results were superior: few motorcycles in the 1990s had the performance ability of the F2.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-review_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-review-3">[3]</a></sup></p>

<p>The <b>CBR600F3</b> is the third generation of the CBR600F series. It replaced the F2, and was produced from 1995 to 1998. It had a modified engine, ram-air intake and cartridge forks. The 1997 and 1998 models also came with a deeper oil pan, sleeker tail fairings, seat and tail light, and a revised engine head netting about a 5 hp gain over 95/96 models. The CDI and ram-air system were also revised to allowed for smoother throttle curve over the 95/96 models, which could sometimes be jerky. The last made 1998 models came with another revised engine head which resulted in a slight power gain. In 1999, it was replaced by the CBR600 F4.</p>
<p>In 1996 and 1998 the CBR600F3 was also sold in "Smokin' Joe's Replica" versions in the USA, Canada and Australia (1998 only).</p>
<p><i><a href="/wiki/Cycle_World" title="Cycle World">Cycle World</a></i> tested the F3's acceleration from 0 to 60 miles per hour (0 to 97 km/h) at 3 seconds and 0 to <span class="frac nowrap"><sup>1</sup>⁄<sub>4</sub></span> mile (0.00 to 0.40 km) at 10.9 seconds at 125.28 mph (201.62 km/h).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/Motorcycle_Consumer_News" title="Motorcycle Consumer News">Motorcycle Consumer News</a></i> recorded a 0 to 60 mph time of 3.7 seconds and a <span class="nowrap"> </span><span class="frac nowrap"><sup>1</sup>⁄<sub>4</sub></span> mile time of 11.03 seconds at 124.06 mph (199.66 km/h)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>

<p>The <b>Honda CBR600F4</b> was produced between 1999 and 2000. It is the last of the CBR600 series of Honda sportbikes to be <a href="/wiki/Carburetor" title="Carburetor">carbureted</a>. An aluminium twin spar frame which reduced frame weight was used, and the engine crankcase was designed to share swing-arm pivot. Through a reduction of internal friction and weight, combined with larger valves, shorter stroke, and a bigger bore, higher revs were enabled. The spark plug caps had Ignition coils built into them. Slightly larger carbs were added and the oil cooler was now located by the oil filter. A new suspension had larger 43 mm forks and used Fireblade parts. The front brakes were upgraded, and the rear rim increased to 5.5” with new 3-spoke wheels. The F4 was fitted with Honda’s HISS engine immobiliser system.</p>
<p>The phrase "Dedicated to Super Evaluators Dirk Vandenberg and <a href="/wiki/Josef_Boyd" title="Josef Boyd">Josef Boyd</a>" are embossed in raised letters on the inside of the Honda CBR600F4's upper fairing;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CBR600F4.com_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-CBR600F4.com-6">[6]</a></sup> the dedication is to two of Honda R&amp;D's senior product developers who were killed during the final testing of this model.</p>

<p>The <b>Honda CBR600F4i</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Types_of_motorcycles#Sport_bike" title="Types of motorcycles">sport bike</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PerfIndexMCN_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-PerfIndexMCN-7">[7]</a></sup> that was produced by <a href="/wiki/Honda" title="Honda">Honda</a> from 2001 to 2006. Two UK versions of the F4i were released in 2001. The CBR600F F1 and onwards had the banana style seat found on pre PGM FI bikes whilst the CBR600F FS1/FS2 (the F sport) were sold with the two tier seat units, black frames and various other performance upgrades as found in the US model CBR600F4I. The F sport was discontinued upon the arrival of the CBR600RR in 2003. In 2003, Honda introduced the <a href="/wiki/Honda_CBR600RR" title="Honda CBR600RR">CBR600RR</a>, as a higher-performance race-ready, race replica alternative to Honda's more street oriented 600-class sport bike, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Honda_CBR600F4i" title="Honda CBR600F4i">CBR600F4i</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cameron2003_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Cameron2003-8">[8]</a></sup> In 2004, the one-piece "banana" seat model replaced the split seat model. This bike sacrificed a little performance but gained a more comfortable riding position with the one piece seat, in addition to the clip-ons mounted above the top fork clamp already and also replacing the black frame with one in silver.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tips_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-tips-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sport_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-sport-10">[10]</a></sup> As well as an engine configuration better suited to everyday riding. This model is the same as the CBR600F1 and onwards that was sold in the UK from 2001.The F4i remained available for three more years before being discontinued, with the 2006 model being the final one to be released. No major updates were made in these final years except for color scheme changes.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Honda_CBR600F&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>For the 2001 model year Honda released an upgraded version of the popular <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Honda_CBR600F4" title="Honda CBR600F4">CBR600F4</a> with intentions of becoming more competitive in the middleweight sport bike class. The 370 lb (170 kg) (<a href="/wiki/Motorcycle_testing_and_measurement" title="Motorcycle testing and measurement">dry</a>) F4i is a modified F4 with numerous engine, chassis and bodywork changes.</p>
<p>The 2001 US F4i had a new subframe which raises the seat by five millimeters allows for more storage room and improved two-tier seat.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-first_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-first-11">[11]</a></sup> The new tail unit has less padding and a higher perch for the passenger. The F4i's tail-light is smaller with a new dual-bulb configuration. The F4i had a new dash layout with a large analog tachometer. The new LCD digital display had a speedometer, odometer, clock, engine temperature read-out, amber shift light, and trip meters.The single largest change aside from the styling is the addition of high-pressure (50 psi).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-firstlooks_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-firstlooks-12">[12]</a></sup> programmed fuel injection — thus the model designation "F4i". In a number of countries, the bike was sold in both the "normal" and "Sport" variants, the Sport having a two-part seat, no grab rail and no main stand (though the main stand mounting holes remained). Fuel injection allows for more precise fuel metering and delivery over a wider rpm range, while providing better throttle response and reducing emissions. The injectors reside one per cylinder and work with 38 mm throttle bodies. Each injector has four nozzles, and together the injectors add up to five horsepower over the F4.</p>
<p>Weight was also reduced. The rear wheel was made lighter, and the front wheel.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tips_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-tips-9">[9]</a></sup> The rotor carriers moved out closer to the brake calipers to reduce weight and to improve rigidity. But stopping power still was not on par with other bikes in the class. There is additional bracing on the steering head for more response, better feedback and feel from the front end. The suspension has also been tweaked with less high-speed damping and a little more low-speed damping with the shock and fork being more street-bias.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hondareview_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-hondareview-13">[13]</a></sup></p>

<p>Additional engine changes include a lighter camshaft sprocket and increased valve spring pressure (two springs per intake valve) which allow for higher revving. There are new piston rings that slide with less friction and increased internal engine oil flow. Redline is now 14,200 rpm,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tips_9-2"><a href="#cite_note-tips-9">[9]</a></sup> 700 rpm higher than the previous year's F4.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-com_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-com-14">[14]</a></sup> To increase the bike's pulling capabilities at high speeds, 5th and 6th gears have been shortened slightly and the rear sprocket was enlarged from 45 teeth to 46.Also adding one clutch plate for greater durability to now making 8.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-com_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-com-14">[14]</a></sup> The oiling holes in the camshafts have been enlarged by 0.5 mm (to 2.5 mm) and piston ring friction has been decreased to aid cooling at the higher rev ceiling.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-com_14-2"><a href="#cite_note-com-14">[14]</a></sup> Spring pressures on the intake and exhaust valves have been increased to avoid valve float. Also, there are now two valve springs (inner and outer) on the intake side instead of the single item that resides on the exhaust side.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>The F4i's new bodywork carries a more racy look and provides a 3% reduction in drag. It also houses a new dual headlight front cowl design which uses 40% brighter H7 bulbs compared to the old H4 bulbs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-com_14-3"><a href="#cite_note-com-14">[14]</a></sup> The headlights are dual multi-reflector units covered by a one-piece flexiglass lens. The turn stalks are shorter and the mirrors are now positioned higher and closer to the rider. The elimination of carburetors allowed for a slightly larger air box and a larger 4.8 US gal (18 l; 4.0 imp gal) fuel tank. And a fuel consumption of 36.7 mpg<sub>‑US</sub> (6.41 L/100 km; 44.1 mpg<sub>‑imp</sub>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MCN-Index_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-MCN-Index-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2001 and 2002, in recognition of Honda's association with MotoGP champion Valentino Rossi, and its 500th motorcycle Grand Prix victory, the CBR600F4i was also released in Europe and Australia (2002 only) in two "Rossi Replica" versions.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2011_CBR600F">2011 CBR600F</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Honda_CBR600F&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: 2011 CBR600F">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 2011, Honda released a new <b>Honda CBR600F</b> model built in Italy for the European markets that was not released in America. It continues the legacy of the CBR model F line.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-have_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-have-17">[17]</a></sup> To honor the German rider Stefan Bradl, Honda Germany launched a special edition of the CBR600F the CBR600F LCR. It featured an Arrow titanium exhaust Progrip handlebar grips, a rear hugger and single seat cover and an LCR sticker kit as well as combined braking and ABS.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lcr_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-lcr-18">[18]</a></sup> The CBR600F shares most of the components of the naked CB600F 'Hornet' such as inverted fork though longer by 50mm and aluminium Mono-backbone frame.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rivalry_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-rivalry-19">[19]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-james_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-james-20">[20]</a></sup> But wraps it in a multi-layered full fairing that lowers drag and protects the rider from windblast as well as new instruments, bars, and tank. It also retains a similar relaxed seating position to minimise rider fatigue.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Honda_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-Honda-21">[21]</a></sup> The 2011 CBR600F also shares the engine of the highly revised CB600F model that came out in April 2007. This engine is a detuned version of that which is available in the 2007 CBR600RR giving a maximum output power of approximately 102 bhp (76 kW) at 12,000 rpm and 47.94 lb·ft (65.00 N·m) of torque at 10,500 rpm.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2014_CBR650F">2014 CBR650F</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Honda_CBR600F&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: 2014 CBR650F">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <b>Honda CBR650F</b> is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sport_touring" title="Sport touring">sport touring</a> motorcycle, part of the Honda <a href="/wiki/Honda_CBR_series" title="Honda CBR series">CBR series</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cbr_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-cbr-22">[22]</a></sup> manufactured by <a href="/wiki/Honda" title="Honda">Honda</a> for their global market. It was released in 2014 alongside its <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Naked_bike" title="Naked bike">naked</a> sibling, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Honda_CB650F" title="Honda CB650F">CB650F</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-News_Releases_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-News_Releases-23">[23]</a></sup> The bike replaces the 2011–2013 <a href="/wiki/Honda_CBR600F#2011_CBR600F" title="Honda CBR600F">CBR600F</a>. It is powered by a liquid-cooled <a href="/wiki/Four-stroke_engine" title="Four-stroke engine">four-stroke</a> 648.7 cc (39.6 cu in), <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/DOHC" title="DOHC">DOHC</a>, 16-valve, <a href="/wiki/Inline-four_engine" title="Inline-four engine">inline-four engine</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Powersports_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-Powersports-24">[24]</a></sup> Though bike is new from the ground up, it has 19 years of development.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cbr_22-1"><a href="#cite_note-cbr-22">[22]</a></sup> The motor which looks a lot like an early model CBR600RR is aimed at more mid range and low end than the typical <i>600 cc</i> supersport bikes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-review_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-review-3">[3]</a></sup> It has about the same specs as a F2. And like the F2, it has a steel twin spar frame.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ride_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-ride-25">[25]</a></sup> With a wet weight much like the F2 as well at 460 lb (210 kg).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-review_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-review-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
