<p><b>Expedition 26</b> was the 26th long-duration mission to the <a href="/wiki/International_Space_Station" title="International Space Station">International Space Station</a>. The expedition's first three crew members – one US astronaut and two Russian cosmonauts – arrived at the station on board <a href="/wiki/Soyuz_TMA-01M" title="Soyuz TMA-01M">Soyuz TMA-01M</a> on 10 October 2010. Expedition 26 officially began the following month on 26 November,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-crew1_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-crew1-2">[2]</a></sup> when half of the crew of the previous mission, <a href="/wiki/Expedition_25" title="Expedition 25">Expedition 25</a>, returned to Earth on board <a href="/wiki/Soyuz_TMA-19" title="Soyuz TMA-19">Soyuz TMA-19</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-crew1_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-crew1-2">[2]</a></sup> The rest of the Expedition 26 crew – one US astronaut, one Russian cosmonaut and one <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/ESA" title="ESA">ESA</a> astronaut – joined the trio already on board when their spacecraft, <a href="/wiki/Soyuz_TMA-20" title="Soyuz TMA-20">Soyuz TMA-20</a>, docked with the station on 17 December 2010.</p>
<p>The commander of Expedition 25, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Douglas_Wheelock" title="Douglas Wheelock">Douglas Wheelock</a>, handed over command of the station to Expedition 26 commander <a href="/wiki/Scott_Kelly_(astronaut)" title="Scott Kelly (astronaut)">Scott Kelly</a> on 24 November 2010. The 26 crew was joined by the crew of <a href="/wiki/STS-133" title="STS-133">STS-133</a> on 26 February 2011,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> and was supplied by the ESA's <a href="/wiki/Johannes_Kepler_ATV" title="Johannes Kepler ATV"><i>Johannes Kepler</i></a> unmanned resupply craft, which arrived on 24 February. Expedition 26 ended on 16 March 2011 with the departure of Soyuz TMA-01M.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="MagISStra">MagISStra</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Expedition_26&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: MagISStra">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>ESA astronaut Paolo Nespoli's mission to the space station was named <i>MagISStra</i>. The name combines the word <i>magistra</i>, meaning "female teacher" in <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a>, with the acronym "ISS", as suggested by Antonella Pezzani of Italy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Spacewalks">Spacewalks</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Expedition_26&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Spacewalks">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Two Russian spacewalks were scheduled for Expedition 26.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> The first, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Russian_EVA-27" title="Russian EVA-27">Russian EVA-27</a>, was conducted Friday, 21 January 2011.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> The second spacewalk, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Russian_EVA-28" title="Russian EVA-28">Russian EVA-28</a>, was conducted on the date of 16 February 2011. Cosmonauts <a href="/wiki/Oleg_Skripochka" title="Oleg Skripochka">Oleg Skripochka</a> and <a href="/wiki/Dmitri_Kondratyev" title="Dmitri Kondratyev">Dmitri Kondratyev</a> conducted both spacewalks.</p>
