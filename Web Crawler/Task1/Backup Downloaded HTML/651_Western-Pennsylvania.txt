<p><b>Western Pennsylvania</b> refers to the western third of the state of <a href="/wiki/Pennsylvania" title="Pennsylvania">Pennsylvania</a> in the United States. <a href="/wiki/Pittsburgh" title="Pittsburgh">Pittsburgh</a> is the region's principal city, with a <a href="/wiki/Metropolitan_area" title="Metropolitan area">metropolitan area</a> population of about 2.4 million people, and serves as its economic and cultural center. <a href="/wiki/Erie,_Pennsylvania" title="Erie, Pennsylvania">Erie</a>, <a href="/wiki/Altoona,_Pennsylvania" title="Altoona, Pennsylvania">Altoona</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Johnstown,_Pennsylvania" title="Johnstown, Pennsylvania">Johnstown</a> are its other metropolitan centers. As of the 2010 census, Western Pennsylvania's total population is nearly 4 million.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Although the Commonwealth does not designate Western Pennsylvania as an official region, since colonial times it has retained a distinct identity not only because of its <a href="/wiki/Geographical_distance" title="Geographical distance">geographical distance</a> from <a href="/wiki/Philadelphia" title="Philadelphia">Philadelphia</a>, the beginning of Pennsylvania settlement, but especially because of its topographical separation from the east by virtue of the <a href="/wiki/Appalachian_Mountains" title="Appalachian Mountains">Appalachian Mountains</a>, which characterize much of the western region. In the 18th century, this separateness caused some to rally for the formation of a 14th state in this region named <a href="/wiki/Westsylvania" title="Westsylvania">Westsylvania</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> The strong cultural identity of Western Pennsylvania is reinforced by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pennsylvania_Supreme_Court" title="Pennsylvania Supreme Court">state supreme court</a> holding sessions in Pittsburgh, in addition to <a href="/wiki/Harrisburg,_Pennsylvania" title="Harrisburg, Pennsylvania">Harrisburg</a> and Philadelphia.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Counties">Counties</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Western_Pennsylvania&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Counties">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/List_of_counties_in_Pennsylvania" title="List of counties in Pennsylvania">List of counties in Pennsylvania</a></div>
<p>Since at least the early 20th century, scholarly books such as <i>Guidebook to Historic Western Pennsylvania</i>, published by the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Pittsburgh_Press" title="University of Pittsburgh Press">University of Pittsburgh Press</a> (1938), formally define the region as the 26 counties west of the Appalachian divide, a <a href="/wiki/Meridian_(geography)" title="Meridian (geography)">meridian</a> from the north at <a href="/wiki/McKean_County,_Pennsylvania" title="McKean County, Pennsylvania">McKean County</a> down and along McKean's eastern border and ending in the south at <a href="/wiki/Bedford_County,_Pennsylvania" title="Bedford County, Pennsylvania">Bedford County</a>.</p>
<p>In alphabetical order those counties are:</p>
<div class="div-col columns column-width" style="-moz-column-width: 10em; -webkit-column-width: 10em; column-width: 10em;">
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Allegheny_County,_Pennsylvania" title="Allegheny County, Pennsylvania">Allegheny</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Armstrong_County,_Pennsylvania" title="Armstrong County, Pennsylvania">Armstrong</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Beaver_County,_Pennsylvania" title="Beaver County, Pennsylvania">Beaver</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Bedford_County,_Pennsylvania" title="Bedford County, Pennsylvania">Bedford</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Blair_County,_Pennsylvania" title="Blair County, Pennsylvania">Blair</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Butler_County,_Pennsylvania" title="Butler County, Pennsylvania">Butler</a></li>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Western_Pennsylvania&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Long recognized as a powerhouse of American industry, Western Pennsylvania is a large geophysical and socio-economic entity. It encompasses that portion of the state to the west of the Appalachian divide and included within the Mississippi drainage system of rivers.</p>
<p>The largest rivers in this area are the <a href="/wiki/Allegheny_River" title="Allegheny River">Allegheny River</a>, which flows southward from the <a href="/wiki/New_York_(state)" title="New York (state)">New York</a> border, and the <a href="/wiki/Monongahela_River" title="Monongahela River">Monongahela River</a>, which flows northward from <a href="/wiki/West_Virginia" title="West Virginia">West Virginia</a>. These two rivers meet in <a href="/wiki/Downtown_Pittsburgh" title="Downtown Pittsburgh">Downtown Pittsburgh</a> and join to form the <a href="/wiki/Ohio_River" title="Ohio River">Ohio River</a>, which from that point flows an additional 981 miles (1,579 km) southwest to the <a href="/wiki/Mississippi_River" title="Mississippi River">Mississippi River</a>. The juncture of the Allegheny and Monongahela was historically regarded as strategic and the gateway to the interior of the continent from the east. At various times this juncture has been called the Forks of the Ohio, <a href="/wiki/Fort_Duquesne" title="Fort Duquesne">Fort Duquesne</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fort_Pitt_(Pennsylvania)" title="Fort Pitt (Pennsylvania)">Fort Pitt</a>, the Golden Triangle, and today, at its apex, <a href="/wiki/Point_State_Park" title="Point State Park">Point State Park</a>. Incredibly, after several decades of border war and 150 years of high-rent city-center urbanization, the original 1764 blockhouse from Fort Pitt still stands here and is one of the oldest buildings in the region.</p>
<p>Other notable rivers are the <a href="/wiki/Youghiogheny_River" title="Youghiogheny River">Youghiogheny River</a>, flowing north from West Virginia and western <a href="/wiki/Maryland" title="Maryland">Maryland</a> to join the Monongahela just upriver of Pittsburgh, and which was the early route of penetration into Western Pennsylvania, the <a href="/wiki/Kiskiminetas_River" title="Kiskiminetas River">Kiskiminetas River</a>, <a href="/wiki/French_Creek_(Allegheny_River)" title="French Creek (Allegheny River)">French Creek</a>, a major passageway between Lake Erie and the Allegheny River for the Indians and early French explorers and traders, and the small <a href="/wiki/Oil_Creek_(Allegheny_River)" title="Oil Creek (Allegheny River)">Oil Creek</a> in Crawford and Venango counties, where slicks gave an indication of petroleum reserves and in whose watershed the first oil well in the United States was drilled.</p>
<p>The highest point in Pennsylvania, <a href="/wiki/Mount_Davis_(Pennsylvania)" title="Mount Davis (Pennsylvania)">Mount Davis</a>, reaches 3,213 feet (979 m), and is located near the southern border of the state in Somerset County, approximately 100 miles (160 km) east of the southwestern corner, where the <a href="/wiki/Appalachian_Mountains" title="Appalachian Mountains">Appalachian Mountains</a> enter Pennsylvania from the south.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gnis_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-gnis-1">[1]</a></sup> To the west and north of this point lies the <a href="/wiki/Allegheny_Plateau" title="Allegheny Plateau">Allegheny Plateau</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Dissected_plateau" title="Dissected plateau">dissected plateau</a> so eroded that it appears to be an interminable series of high hills and steep valleys. The peaks in the area are among the lowest in the East Coast highlands, but what they lack in height they make up in wide extent of land covered, which forms a vast formidable barrier for mile upon mile to overland travel from the coast.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Distinctiveness">Distinctiveness</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Western_Pennsylvania&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Distinctiveness">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Western Pennsylvania is distinctive from the rest of the state due to several important and complex factors:</p>
<ul>
<li>The initial difficulty of transportation access from the east involved many miles of seemingly endless parallel ridges of the Appalachian Mountains, and then the broken hills and valleys of the <a href="/wiki/Allegheny_Plateau" title="Allegheny Plateau">Allegheny Plateau</a>, all of which were covered in thick forests. Indeed, the initial method of access from areas east of the Appalachians, was to travel southbound outside of Pennsylvania, then follow the Potomac River northwest through Maryland and Virginia, and then re-enter the state in its southwest corner. Various methods of more direct transport were later tried, including a canal system westbound over the mountains and then, later, the <a href="/wiki/Pennsylvania_Railroad" title="Pennsylvania Railroad">Pennsylvania Railroad</a> which extended the railroad systems of the East Coast west to Pittsburgh and the <a href="/wiki/Ohio_River" title="Ohio River">Ohio Valley</a>. Perhaps the best known transportation innovation to simplify access to this area is the famous <a href="/wiki/Pennsylvania_Turnpike" title="Pennsylvania Turnpike">Pennsylvania Turnpike</a>, the first modern limited access highway in North America.</li>
<li>The initial problem was economic marketing of a limited number of goods that could stand such high freight costs. The insensitivity of the new U.S. Federal Government to the marketing problems in the west led to the <a href="/wiki/Whiskey_Rebellion" title="Whiskey Rebellion">Whiskey Rebellion</a> in Western Pennsylvania, an event which seriously challenged the political viability of the new American nation. Later marketing arrangements turned to access via the <a href="/wiki/Ohio_River" title="Ohio River">Ohio River</a>, with Pittsburgh a barge and steamboat center of the mid-continent. Today, Pittsburgh is still strongly oriented to the rivers; the port of Pittsburgh ranks No. 13 by tonnage in the USA and even surpasses the Port of Philadelphia in tonnage, due to the heavy shipping of bulk coal by <a href="/wiki/Barge" title="Barge">barge</a> inland on the rivers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> Locally, a system of agriculture arose suitable to Western Pennsylvania's rugged terrain, emphasizing animal husbandry and dairying but with few exportable vegetable crops. The search for some sort of exportable agricultural specialty perhaps also encouraged the rise of the sauce industry and its first location at Sharpsburg in what was later to become the large <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/H.J._Heinz_Company" title="H.J. Heinz Company">H.J. Heinz Company</a>.</li>
<li>The search for exploitable resources first resulted with the development of huge <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bituminous" title="Bituminous">bituminous coal</a> or "soft" <a href="/wiki/Coal" title="Coal">coal</a> deposits in the area for use in a growing iron foundry sector. However, it was not until the realization by <a href="/wiki/Andrew_Carnegie" title="Andrew Carnegie">Andrew Carnegie</a>, that Western Pennsylvania possessed an optimum location for a very large scale American <a href="/wiki/Steel" title="Steel">steel</a> sector, that this area, especially Pittsburgh, became known for the industrial specialty that characterizes it today. The region also had large <a href="/wiki/Glass" title="Glass">glass</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pottery" title="Pottery">pottery</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Brickmaking" title="Brickmaking">brickmaking</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Ceramic" title="Ceramic">ceramic</a> industries, which took advantage of the coal and the sand and clay in local soils. The local glass industry produced 45 percent of the nation's output in glass by the 1860s and more than 80 percent of the output by the 1920s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> The rise and subsequent <a href="/wiki/Rust_Belt" title="Rust Belt">decline of the American steel industry</a> at Pittsburgh introduces a host of complex economic concepts necessary to understand why that particular activity centered in this particular place, including the notions of classical Weberian location analysis for more than one input, the Pittsburgh Plus system for maintaining advantageous freight costs to ship to the market, <a href="/wiki/Vertical_integration" title="Vertical integration">vertical integration</a> and supply innovations such as the development of the <a href="/wiki/Mesabi_Range" title="Mesabi Range">Mesabi Range</a>, the ore freighter as a transport vehicle, and the construction of the Soo Locks. Other necessary economic concepts for description could well include <a href="/wiki/Economies_of_scale" title="Economies of scale">economies of scale</a>, <a href="/wiki/Diseconomies_of_scale" title="Diseconomies of scale">diseconomies of scale</a>, monopoly (or cartel) price equilibrium, and "dumping". Labor relations problems historically were frequent in the earlier steel sector, and mention should be made of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_Steel_Workers_of_America" title="United Steel Workers of America">United Steel Workers of America</a>, as well as the contemporary issue of "legacy costs" arising from heavy entitlements to a large retired labor force after sharply downsizing to today's level of employment.</li>
<li>Other exploitable resources in Western Pennsylvania were also distinct. One was the drilling of the <a href="/wiki/Drake_Well_Museum" title="Drake Well Museum">first oil well</a> in the world at <a href="/wiki/Titusville,_Pennsylvania" title="Titusville, Pennsylvania">Titusville</a> and the rise of the US petroleum industry. Another was widespread deforestation of the outlying areas and their subsequent reforestation under <a href="/wiki/Gifford_Pinchot" title="Gifford Pinchot">Gifford Pinchot</a>, who instituted the first large scale government sponsored timber management effort in the USA. During this time of intensive exploitation of forests a whole new sector, the wood chemistry industry, appeared and then later vanished. Finally, mention should also be made of management in the forested areas of a large animal population which supports the famous "Pennsylvania deer-hunting" cultural ethos. Indeed, the first day of deer-hunting season is a de facto unofficial holiday in much of the central and northern regions of the state, when absence from work or school is generally tolerated with no explanation necessary.</li>
<li>Since the early 1950s, a renaissance occurred in the development of cultural institutions and abatement of pollution in Pittsburgh and its surrounding area. The effects of this increase in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Livability" title="Livability">livability</a> are particularly apparent in the Golden Triangle district of <a href="/wiki/Downtown_Pittsburgh" title="Downtown Pittsburgh">Downtown Pittsburgh</a>, which at one point had been plagued with so much industrial haze that drivers used their headlights in mid-day. However, this social improvement has not always been accompanied by a serious plan of regional economic development to assess what, precisely, should fill the income void after the departure of steel. In addition, the city of Pittsburgh continues to become de-populated and has recently been put under state supervision of its finances.</li>
<li>Culturally, the distinctiveness of Western Pennsylvania is underlined by the existence of a unique local dialect called "Pittsburghese" or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pittsburgh_English" title="Pittsburgh English">Pittsburgh English</a>, sometimes affectionately termed the "yinzer" dialect, due to its use of the term "yins" (also spelled "yunz, "yinz", "youns", etc.) as the plural form of "you". This is probably a legacy of <a href="/wiki/Ulster_Scots_people" title="Ulster Scots people">Ulster-Scots</a> settlement in the area. Western Pennsylvanians also refer to soft drinks as "pop" while in the eastern half of the state it is referred to as "soda."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Erie_Triangle" title="Erie Triangle">Erie Triangle</a> and the city of <a href="/wiki/Erie,_Pennsylvania" title="Erie, Pennsylvania">Erie</a> give Pennsylvania a port along the <a href="/wiki/Great_Lakes" title="Great Lakes">Great Lakes</a>. This region in unique within western Pennsylvania in that it maintains stronger geographic and cultural connections with western New York than it does with western Pennsylvania, due in large part to its identification with the eastern Great Lakes region, rather than Appalachia. This is reflected in the city and county of Erie sharing far more similarities with <a href="/wiki/Buffalo,_New_York" title="Buffalo, New York">Buffalo</a> and southwestern New York than it does with Pittsburgh and western Pennsylvania. The Erie region is also known for its distinct agriculture, centered on grapes and other fruit, due to the moderating climatic influence, in both summer and fall, of <a href="/wiki/Lake_Erie" title="Lake Erie">Lake Erie</a>. (In winter, the area is often inundated with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lake_effect_snow" title="Lake effect snow">"lake-effect" snow</a>.) There are also small commercial fresh-water fisheries and many streams and smaller lakes with a variety of fish to catch, including yellow perch and walleye.</li>
<li>In <a href="/wiki/Stonycreek_Township,_Somerset_County,_Pennsylvania" title="Stonycreek Township, Somerset County, Pennsylvania">Stonycreek Township</a> is the <a href="/wiki/Flight_93_National_Memorial" title="Flight 93 National Memorial">memorial and crash site</a> of <a href="/wiki/United_Airlines_Flight_93" title="United Airlines Flight 93">United Airlines Flight 93</a>, the "Let's Roll" flight which occurred on 9/11/2001 after passengers attempted to overpower the plane's hijackers. The site is an informal patriotic shrine with many hand-made mementos voluntarily gracing the area. There is a movement to add the site to the National Park System. It is a startling coincidence that the Stoneycreek site is comparatively close to the other centuries-earlier locations of military engagements in Western Pennsylvania, such as <a href="/wiki/Fort_Duquesne" title="Fort Duquesne">Fort Duquesne</a> and the area of the <a href="/wiki/Whiskey_Rebellion" title="Whiskey Rebellion">Whiskey Rebellion</a>. This can in part be explained by the fact that all these locations were on a strategic route from eastern settlements in Pennsylvania and Virginia (and, later, Washington, D.C.) to the West.</li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Sports">Sports</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Western_Pennsylvania&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Sports">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Pittsburgh boasts three major league sports teams: the <a href="/wiki/Pittsburgh_Steelers" title="Pittsburgh Steelers">Pittsburgh Steelers</a> of the <a href="/wiki/National_Football_League" title="National Football League">National Football League</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Pittsburgh_Pirates" title="Pittsburgh Pirates">Pittsburgh Pirates</a> of <a href="/wiki/Major_League_Baseball" title="Major League Baseball">Major League Baseball</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Pittsburgh_Penguins" title="Pittsburgh Penguins">Pittsburgh Penguins</a> of the <a href="/wiki/National_Hockey_League" title="National Hockey League">National Hockey League</a>. Meanwhile, the <a href="/wiki/Pittsburgh_Panthers" title="Pittsburgh Panthers">Pittsburgh Panthers</a> is a <a href="/wiki/NCAA_Division_I" title="NCAA Division I">NCAA Division I</a> college team. Erie and Johnstown both have junior ice hockey teams as well. The <a href="/wiki/Erie_Otters" title="Erie Otters">Erie Otters</a> play in the <a href="/wiki/Ontario_Hockey_League" title="Ontario Hockey League">Ontario Hockey League</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Johnstown_Tomahawks" title="Johnstown Tomahawks">Johnstown Tomahawks</a> play in the <a href="/wiki/North_American_Hockey_League" title="North American Hockey League">North American Hockey League</a>.</p>
