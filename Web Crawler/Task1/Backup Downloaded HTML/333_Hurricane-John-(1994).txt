<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Hurricane John (1994)</h1>

<p><b>Hurricane John</b>, also known as <b>Typhoon John</b>, was both the longest-lasting and the farthest-traveling <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone" title="Tropical cyclone">tropical cyclone</a> ever observed. John formed during the <a href="/wiki/1994_Pacific_hurricane_season" title="1994 Pacific hurricane season">1994 Pacific hurricane season</a>, which had above average activity due to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/El_Ni%C3%B1o_Southern_Oscillation" title="El Niño Southern Oscillation">El Niño</a> of 1994–95<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> and peaked as a Category 5 hurricane on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saffir%E2%80%93Simpson_hurricane_wind_scale" title="Saffir–Simpson hurricane wind scale">Saffir–Simpson hurricane wind scale</a>, the highest categorization for hurricanes.</p>
<p>Over the course of its existence, it followed a 7,165-mile (13,280-km) path from the eastern Pacific to the western Pacific and back to the central Pacific, lasting 31 days in total.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-report1_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-report1-2">[2]</a></sup> Because it existed in both the eastern and western Pacific, John was one of a small number of tropical cyclones to be designated as both a hurricane and a typhoon. Despite lasting for a full month, John barely affected land at all, bringing only minimal effects to the <a href="/wiki/Hawaiian_Islands" title="Hawaiian Islands">Hawaiian Islands</a> and the United States <a href="/wiki/Military_base" title="Military base">military base</a> on <a href="/wiki/Johnston_Atoll" title="Johnston Atoll">Johnston Atoll</a>. Its remnants later affected Alaska.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Meteorological_history">Meteorological history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hurricane_John_(1994)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Meteorological history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:John_1994_track.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="633" data-file-width="1024" height="136" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1f/John_1994_track.png/220px-John_1994_track.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1f/John_1994_track.png/330px-John_1994_track.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1f/John_1994_track.png/440px-John_1994_track.png 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:John_1994_track.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Map plotting the track and intensity of the storm, according to the <a href="/wiki/Saffir%E2%80%93Simpson_scale" title="Saffir–Simpson scale">Saffir–Simpson scale</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The origins of Hurricane John were thought by the United States <a href="/wiki/National_Hurricane_Center" title="National Hurricane Center">National Hurricane Center</a> (NHC) to be from a <a href="/wiki/Tropical_wave" title="Tropical wave">tropical wave</a> that moved off the coast of Africa on July 25, 1994.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-John_NHC_TCR_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-John_NHC_TCR-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MWR_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-MWR-4">[4]</a></sup> The wave subsequently moved across the Atlantic Ocean and Caribbean without distinction, before it crossed Central America and moved into the Eastern Pacific Ocean on or around August 8.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-John_NHC_TCR_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-John_NHC_TCR-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MWR_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-MWR-4">[4]</a></sup> Upon entering the Eastern Pacific the wave gradually developed, before the NHC initiated advisories on the system and designated it as Tropical Depression Ten-E during August 11.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-John_Dis_1_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-John_Dis_1-5">[5]</a></sup> The system was at this time moving westwards and located around 345 miles (555 km) to the south-southeast of <a href="/wiki/Acapulco" title="Acapulco">Acapulco</a>, Mexico.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-John_NHC_TCR_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-John_NHC_TCR-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MWR_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-MWR-4">[4]</a></sup> Quickly developing banding features and well-defined outflow, it was upgraded to a tropical storm and named John later that day.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-report1_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-report1-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>A strong ridge of high pressure over the northeastern Pacific Ocean forced John westward, where upper level <a href="/wiki/Wind_shear" title="Wind shear">wind shear</a> kept John a tropical storm. Intensity fluctuated considerably, however, as shear levels varied. More than once, shear cleared away most of the clouds above John and nearly caused it to weaken to a tropical depression.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-report1_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-report1-2">[2]</a></sup> However, after eight days of slow westward movement across the Pacific Ocean, shear lessened greatly on August 19, and John intensified significantly and was designated as a hurricane at 1700 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pacific_Time_zone" title="Pacific Time zone">PDT</a>. During an eighteen-hour period between August 19 and August 20, John further strengthened from a weak Category 1 hurricane to a major Category 3 hurricane. Around 1100 PDT on August 20, it crossed into the central Pacific, the first of three basin crosses John would make.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-report1_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-report1-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>After entering the central Pacific, John left the area monitored by the NHC and was instead monitored by the <a href="/wiki/Central_Pacific_Hurricane_Center" title="Central Pacific Hurricane Center">Central Pacific Hurricane Center</a> (CPHC). As it moved slowly westward, Hurricane John continued to strengthen considerably in an increasingly favorable environment well south of the <a href="/wiki/Hawaiian_Islands" title="Hawaiian Islands">Hawaiian Islands</a>; on 22 August John was designated a Category 5 hurricane on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saffir%E2%80%93Simpson_hurricane_scale" title="Saffir–Simpson hurricane scale">Saffir–Simpson hurricane scale</a> (the highest classification for hurricanes) and later that day (by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hawaii_Standard_Time" title="Hawaii Standard Time">Hawaii Standard Time</a>) reached its peak winds of 175 miles per hour (280 km/h).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-report2_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-report2-6">[6]</a></sup> Also on August 22 (by Hawaii Standard Time), John made its closest approach to the Hawaiian Islands, 345 miles (500 km) to the south. John had threatened to turn north and affect the islands days before, but the ridge of high pressure that typically shields the islands from hurricanes kept John on its southerly path. Nonetheless, heavy rains and wind from the outer bands of John affected the islands.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-report2_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-report2-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>With the Hawaiian Islands behind it, John began a slow turn to the north, taking near-direct aim at <a href="/wiki/Johnston_Atoll" title="Johnston Atoll">Johnston Atoll</a>, a small group of islands populated only by a United States military base. The storm slowly weakened from its peak as a Category 5 hurricane in the face of increasing shear, dropping down to a Category 1 hurricane with 90 miles per hour (145 km/h) maximum winds. On August 25 local time, John made its closest approach to the Johnston Atoll only 15 miles (24 km) to the north. On Johnston Atoll, sustained winds were reported up to 60 miles per hour (95 km/h), the equivalent of a strong tropical storm, and gusts up to 75 miles per hour (120 km/h) were recorded.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Centralreport_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Centralreport-7">[7]</a></sup></p>

<p>Clearing Johnston Atoll, John turned to the northwest and began strengthening again as shear decreased. On August 27 local time, John reached a secondary peak strength of 135 miles per hour (210 km/h), and shortly thereafter it crossed the <a href="/wiki/International_Date_Line" title="International Date Line">International Date Line</a> at approximately 22° <a href="/wiki/Latitude" title="Latitude">N</a> and came under the surveillance of the <a href="/wiki/Guam" title="Guam">Guam</a> branch of the <a href="/wiki/Joint_Typhoon_Warning_Center" title="Joint Typhoon Warning Center">Joint Typhoon Warning Center</a> (JTWC). By crossing into the western Pacific, John also became a typhoon and was referred to as Typhoon John during its time in the western Pacific.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Centralreport_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-Centralreport-7">[7]</a></sup> Immediately after crossing the Date Line, John again weakened and its forward motion stalled. By September 1, John had weakened to a tropical storm and was nearly motionless just west of the Date Line. There, John lingered for six days while performing a multi-day <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Clockwise_and_counterclockwise" title="Clockwise and counterclockwise">counterclockwise</a> loop. On September 7, a trough moved into the area and quickly moved John to the northeast. John crossed the Date Line again on September 8 and reentered the central Pacific.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Centralreport_7-2"><a href="#cite_note-Centralreport-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>After reentering the central Pacific, John briefly reached a tertiary peak strength of 90 miles per hour (145 km/h), a strong Category 1 hurricane, well to the north of <a href="/wiki/Midway_Atoll" title="Midway Atoll">Midway Island</a>. However, the trough was rapidly pulling apart John's structure, and the cold waters of the northern central Pacific were not conducive to a tropical cyclone. On September 10, the 120th advisory was released on the system, finally declaring John to have become <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Extratropical" title="Extratropical">extratropical</a> approximately 1000 miles (1600 km) south of <a href="/wiki/Unalaska_Island" title="Unalaska Island">Unalaska Island</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Centralreport_7-3"><a href="#cite_note-Centralreport-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Forecasting_difficulties">Forecasting difficulties</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hurricane_John_(1994)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Forecasting difficulties">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>During John's time in the Western North Pacific, the <a href="/wiki/Joint_Typhoon_Warning_Center" title="Joint Typhoon Warning Center">Joint Typhoon Warning Center</a> (JTWC) had particular difficulty in forecasting and even estimating the strength of John. John weakened considerably after entering the Western North Pacific, and, before estimates were later revised, four consecutive advisories were issued that declared John a tropical depression. Each of these advisories called for imminent dissipation. As John persisted and did not dissipate as the JTWC had predicted, it was upgraded to a minimal tropical storm in the next advisory. At the same time, however, two separate ship reports indicated that John had sustained winds of at least 55 <a href="/wiki/Knot_(unit)" title="Knot (unit)">knots</a> (100 km/h, 65 mph), far stronger than the advisory strength of 35 knots (65 km/h, 40 mph). John would go on to restrengthen into a strong Category 1 hurricane after reentering the Central North Pacific, defying all JTWC predictions. After later reanalysis, the JTWC raised the estimated wind speeds of John for every advisory from 1200 UTC September 1 to its final advisory exactly a week later by at least 5 knots (9 km/h, 6 mph) and as much as 25 knots (46 km/h, 29 mph).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JTWCreport_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-JTWCreport-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Records">Records</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hurricane_John_(1994)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Records">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Its 31-day existence made John the longest-lasting tropical cyclone recorded in both the Pacific Ocean and worldwide, surpassing both <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Tina_(1992)" title="Hurricane Tina (1992)">Hurricane Tina</a>'s previous record in the Pacific of 24 days in the <a href="/wiki/1992_Pacific_hurricane_season" title="1992 Pacific hurricane season">1992 season</a> and the <a href="/wiki/1899_San_Ciriaco_hurricane" title="1899 San Ciriaco hurricane">1899 San Ciriaco hurricane</a>'s previous world record of 28 days in the <a href="/wiki/1899_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="1899 Atlantic hurricane season">1899 Atlantic season</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-noaafaq1_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-noaafaq1-9">[9]</a></sup> In addition, despite its slow movement throughout much of its path, John was the farthest-traveling tropical cyclone in both Pacific Ocean and worldwide, with a distance traveled of 7,165 miles (13,280 km), out-distancing previous record holders <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Fico" title="Hurricane Fico">Hurricane Fico</a> in the Pacific of 4,700 miles (8,700 km) in the <a href="/wiki/1978_Pacific_hurricane_season" title="1978 Pacific hurricane season">1978 season</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Faith" title="Hurricane Faith">Hurricane Faith</a> worldwide of 6,850 miles (12,700 km) in the <a href="/wiki/1966_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="1966 Atlantic hurricane season">1966 Atlantic season</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-noaafaq2_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-noaafaq2-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>Pressure readings from John's peak are not consistently available as the CPHC did not monitor pressures at the time, but <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Air_Force_Reserve" title="Air Force Reserve">Air Force Reserve</a> aircraft did measure a surface pressure of 929 <a href="/wiki/Bar_(unit)" title="Bar (unit)">mbar</a> (<a href="/wiki/Pascal_(unit)" title="Pascal (unit)">hPa</a>), making John one of the most intense hurricanes recorded in the central Pacific; both <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Emilia_(1994)" title="Hurricane Emilia (1994)">Hurricane Emilia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Gilma_(1994)" title="Hurricane Gilma (1994)">Hurricane Gilma</a> recorded a lower pressure in the central Pacific earlier in the 1994 season, but with lower wind speeds. (Intensity is measured by minimum central pressure, which correlates with but is not directly linked to wind speeds). John was also only the fourth Category 5 hurricane recorded in the central Pacific (the first was <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hurricane_Patsy_(1959)" title="Hurricane Patsy (1959)">Hurricane Patsy</a> in <a href="/wiki/1959_Pacific_hurricane_season" title="1959 Pacific hurricane season">1959</a>, the second was Hurricane Emilia and the third one was Hurricane Gilma, both earlier in 1994), and possessed the highest recorded wind speed in a central Pacific hurricane, 175 mph (280 km/h).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-report2_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-report2-6">[6]</a></sup> Since 1994, only one Category 5 hurricane, <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Ioke" title="Hurricane Ioke">Hurricane Ioke</a>, has formed in or entered into the Central Pacific; Ioke, like Emilia and Gilma, had a lower central pressure but lower wind speeds than John. However, John's pressure record is incomplete; the 929 mbar reading was only measured when the winds were 160 mph; there is no pressure reading when it had winds of 175 mph, so it could have been stronger than Emilia, Gilma or Ioke.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EPAC_HURDAT_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-EPAC_HURDAT-11">[11]</a></sup> John was the first Eastern Pacific hurricane to also exist as a typhoon, a feat only matched by <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Genevieve_(2014)" title="Hurricane Genevieve (2014)">Hurricane Genevieve</a> in <a href="/wiki/2014_Pacific_hurricane_season" title="2014 Pacific hurricane season">2014</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>Additionally, counting only storms recognized by all north Pacific official <a href="/wiki/Regional_Specialized_Meteorological_Center" title="Regional Specialized Meteorological Center">Regional Specialized Meteorological Centers</a>, John was only the third of five tropical cyclones to exist in all three <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone_basins" title="Tropical cyclone basins">tropical cyclone basins</a> in the Pacific Ocean. The others were <a href="/wiki/1986_Pacific_typhoon_season#Severe_Tropical_Storm" title="1986 Pacific typhoon season">Tropical Storm Georgette</a> and hurricanes <a href="/wiki/1994_Pacific_hurricane_season#Hurricane_Li" title="1994 Pacific hurricane season">Li</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Dora_(1999)" title="Hurricane Dora (1999)">Dora</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Genevieve_(2014)" title="Hurricane Genevieve (2014)">Genevieve</a> in <a href="/wiki/1986_Pacific_hurricane_season" title="1986 Pacific hurricane season">1986</a>, <a href="/wiki/1994_Pacific_hurricane_season" title="1994 Pacific hurricane season">earlier in 1994</a>, <a href="/wiki/1999_Pacific_hurricane_season" title="1999 Pacific hurricane season">1999</a> and <a href="/wiki/2014_Pacific_hurricane_season" title="2014 Pacific hurricane season">2014</a>, respectively.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CPHC_Archive_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-CPHC_Archive-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-best_track_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-best_track-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CPHC_Rebest_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-CPHC_Rebest-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JMA_BT_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-JMA_BT-16">[16]</a></sup> Also, John was the fifth of seven tropical cyclones to enter the central Pacific from the western Pacific. Hurricanes <a href="/wiki/1959_Pacific_hurricane_season#Hurricane_Patsy" title="1959 Pacific hurricane season">Patsy</a> and <a href="/wiki/1985_Pacific_hurricane_season#Hurricane_Skip" title="1985 Pacific hurricane season">Skip</a> and tropical storms <a href="/wiki/1968_Pacific_hurricane_season#Tropical_Storm_Virginia" title="1968 Pacific hurricane season">Virginia</a> and <a href="/wiki/1980_Pacific_hurricane_season#Tropical_Storm_Carmen" title="1980 Pacific hurricane season">Carmen</a> in <a href="/wiki/1959_Pacific_hurricane_season" title="1959 Pacific hurricane season">1959</a>, <a href="/wiki/1985_Pacific_hurricane_season" title="1985 Pacific hurricane season">1985</a>, <a href="/wiki/1968_Pacific_hurricane_season" title="1968 Pacific hurricane season">1968</a> and <a href="/wiki/1980_Pacific_hurricane_season" title="1980 Pacific hurricane season">1980</a>, respectively, had done so previously, and only <a href="/wiki/1996_Pacific_hurricane_season#Tropical_Depression_Seventeen-W" title="1996 Pacific hurricane season">Tropical Depression 17W</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tropical_Storm_Omeka" title="Tropical Storm Omeka">Tropical Storm Omeka</a> in <a href="/wiki/1996_Pacific_hurricane_season" title="1996 Pacific hurricane season">1996</a> and <a href="/wiki/2010_Pacific_hurricane_season" title="2010 Pacific hurricane season">2010</a>, respectively, did so later.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Centralreport_7-4"><a href="#cite_note-Centralreport-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CPHC_Archive_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-CPHC_Archive-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CPHC_Rebest_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-CPHC_Rebest-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JMA_BT_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-JMA_BT-16">[16]</a></sup> Finally, John was one of only five tropical cyclones to cross the Date Line twice, alongside hurricanes Patsy and Skip, Tropical Storm Carmen and Tropical Depression 17W.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CPHC_Archive_13-2"><a href="#cite_note-CPHC_Archive-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hawaii_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hawaii-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Impact">Impact</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hurricane_John_(1994)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Impact">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Hurricane_John_23_aug_1994_1739Z.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="608" data-file-width="540" height="191" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ab/Hurricane_John_23_aug_1994_1739Z.jpg/170px-Hurricane_John_23_aug_1994_1739Z.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ab/Hurricane_John_23_aug_1994_1739Z.jpg/255px-Hurricane_John_23_aug_1994_1739Z.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ab/Hurricane_John_23_aug_1994_1739Z.jpg/340px-Hurricane_John_23_aug_1994_1739Z.jpg 2x" width="170"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Hurricane_John_23_aug_1994_1739Z.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Satellite image of Hurricane John as a Category 5 storm with winds of 165 mph (270 km/h)</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>John affected both the <a href="/wiki/Hawaiian_Islands" title="Hawaiian Islands">Hawaiian Islands</a> and <a href="/wiki/Johnston_Atoll" title="Johnston Atoll">Johnston Atoll</a>, but only lightly. While John passed over 345 miles (550 km) to the south of Hawai<span class="unicode">ʻ</span>i, the islands did experience strengthened trade winds and rough surf along the southeast- and south-facing shores, and, while moving westward, on west-facing shores as well.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Centralreport_7-5"><a href="#cite_note-Centralreport-7">[7]</a></sup> The waves, ranging from 6 to 10 ft (1.8 to 3.0 m) in height, flooded beach parks in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kailua-Kona,_Hawai%27i" title="Kailua-Kona, Hawai'i">Kailua-Kona</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ncdc_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-ncdc-18">[18]</a></sup> Additionally, heavy rains on the <a href="/wiki/Hawaii_(island)" title="Hawaii (island)">Big Island of Hawai<span class="unicode">ʻ</span>i</a> caused minor, localized flooding and some short-term road closures. No deaths, injuries or significant damages were reported in Hawai<span class="unicode">ʻ</span>i.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Centralreport_7-6"><a href="#cite_note-Centralreport-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>Although John passed within 25 km (16 mi) of <a href="/wiki/Johnston_Atoll" title="Johnston Atoll">Johnston Atoll</a>, it had weakened greatly to a Category 1 system by closest approach.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Centralreport_7-7"><a href="#cite_note-Centralreport-7">[7]</a></sup> Prior to the storm's arrival, waves between 20 and 30 ft (6.1 and 9.1 m) were reported on the island.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> Additionally, in the Northern Hemisphere, the strongest winds and heaviest rain lie to the north of a tropical cyclone, so the atoll, which lay to the south of the storm's path, was spared the brunt of the storm. Nonetheless, the 1,100-man personnel for the United States military base on Johnston Atoll had been evacuated to <a href="/wiki/Honolulu" title="Honolulu">Honolulu</a> as a precaution while John approached. Damage to structures was considerable, but the size of the island and relative functionality of the base led to low damage; monetary losses were estimated at close to $15 million (1994 US$).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Centralreport_7-8"><a href="#cite_note-Centralreport-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>The remnants of John moved through the <a href="/wiki/Aleutian_Islands" title="Aleutian Islands">Aleutian Islands</a>, producing a wind gust of 46 mph (74 km/h) in <a href="/wiki/Unalaska,_Alaska" title="Unalaska, Alaska">Unalaska</a>. The storm brought a plume of warm air, and two stations recorded a high temperature of 66 °F (19 °C).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
