<p><i><b>The Day After Tomorrow</b></i> is a 2004 American science-fiction disaster film co-written, directed, and produced by <a href="/wiki/Roland_Emmerich" title="Roland Emmerich">Roland Emmerich</a> and starring <a href="/wiki/Dennis_Quaid" title="Dennis Quaid">Dennis Quaid</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jake_Gyllenhaal" title="Jake Gyllenhaal">Jake Gyllenhaal</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ian_Holm" title="Ian Holm">Ian Holm</a>, <a href="/wiki/Emmy_Rossum" title="Emmy Rossum">Emmy Rossum</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Sela_Ward" title="Sela Ward">Sela Ward</a>. The film depicts catastrophic climatic effects following the <a href="/wiki/Shutdown_of_thermohaline_circulation" title="Shutdown of thermohaline circulation">disruption of the North Atlantic Ocean circulation</a> in a series of <a href="/wiki/Extreme_weather" title="Extreme weather">extreme weather</a> events that usher in <a href="/wiki/Global_cooling" title="Global cooling">global cooling</a> and lead to a new <a href="/wiki/Ice_age" title="Ice age">ice age</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> Filmed in <a href="/wiki/Toronto" title="Toronto">Toronto</a> and <a href="/wiki/Montreal" title="Montreal">Montreal</a>, it is the highest-grossing Hollywood film made in Canada (adjusted for <a href="/wiki/Inflation" title="Inflation">inflation</a>).</p>
<p>Originally slated for release in the summer of 2003, <i>The Day After Tomorrow</i> opened in <a href="/wiki/Mexico_City" title="Mexico City">Mexico City</a> on May 17, 2004, and was released worldwide from May 26 to May 28 (except in South Korea and Japan, where it was released on June 4 and 5). A major commercial success, the film became the <a href="/wiki/2004_in_film#Highest-grossing_films" title="2004 in film">sixth highest-grossing film of 2004</a>. It received mixed reviews upon release, with critics highly praising the film's special effects but criticizing numerous scientific inaccuracies.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Plot">Plot</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Day_After_Tomorrow&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Plot">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Jack Hall, an American <a href="/wiki/Paleoclimatology" title="Paleoclimatology">paleoclimatologist</a>, and his colleagues Frank and Jason, drill for ice-core samples in the <a href="/wiki/Larsen_Ice_Shelf" title="Larsen Ice Shelf">Larsen Ice Shelf</a> for the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/NOAA" title="NOAA">NOAA</a>, when the shelf breaks apart. Jack warns of <a href="/wiki/Global_warming" title="Global warming">global warming</a> to a <a href="/wiki/United_Nations" title="United Nations">UN</a> conference in <a href="/wiki/New_Delhi" title="New Delhi">New Delhi</a>, but <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/US_Vice_President" title="US Vice President">US Vice President</a> Raymond Becker dismisses his concerns. Professor Terry Rapson of the Hedland Centre in <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a> shares Jack’s views of an inevitable climate shift and they become friends. Several <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Buoys" title="Buoys">buoys</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Ocean" title="Atlantic Ocean">Atlantic Ocean</a> show a severe ocean temperature drop, Rapson concluding Jack’s theories are correct. Jack and Rapson’s teams, along with <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">NASA</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Meteorologist" title="Meteorologist">meteorologist</a> Janet Tokada, build a forecast model based on Jack’s research.</p>
<p>Eventually, a <a href="/wiki/Storm" title="Storm">storm</a> system develops in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Northern_hemisphere" title="Northern hemisphere">northern hemisphere</a>, splitting into three superstorms above <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a>, Scotland, and <a href="/wiki/Siberia" title="Siberia">Siberia</a>. The subzero temperatures of the superstorms' <a href="/wiki/Eye_(cyclone)" title="Eye (cyclone)">eyes</a> cause <a href="/wiki/Flash_freezing" title="Flash freezing">flash freezing</a>. Meanwhile, the weather becomes increasingly bad around the world, <a href="/wiki/Tokyo" title="Tokyo">Tokyo</a> is struck by a giant hail storm and <a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles" title="Los Angeles">Los Angeles</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hollywood" title="Hollywood">Hollywood</a> are devastated by a tornado outbreak.</p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Manhattan" title="Manhattan">Manhattan</a>, Jack’s son Sam, and his friends Brian, J.D., and Laura Chapman participate in an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Academic_decathlon" title="Academic decathlon">academic decathlon</a>. The city begins to <a href="/wiki/Flood" title="Flood">flood</a> with rainfall, followed by an enormous <a href="/wiki/Storm_surge" title="Storm surge">storm surge</a>, trapping Sam’s group in the <a href="/wiki/New_York_Public_Library" title="New York Public Library">New York Public Library</a>. Sam contacts Jack and his mother Lucy, a <a href="/wiki/Physician" title="Physician">doctor</a>, Jack advising him to stay inside, and promises to rescue him. Rapson and his team perish in the European storm, while Lucy remains in a hospital caring for bed-ridden children, until saved by the authorities.</p>
<p>Upon Jack’s suggestion, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/US_President" title="US President">US President</a> Blake orders the southern states to be evacuated into <a href="/wiki/Mexico" title="Mexico">Mexico</a>, the northern half doomed to be hit by the superstorm. Blake is caught in it and dies, making Becker the <a href="/wiki/United_States_presidential_line_of_succession" title="United States presidential line of succession">new President</a>. Jack, Jason, and Frank make their way Manhattan, but Frank falls through the <a href="/wiki/Skylight" title="Skylight">skylight</a> of a <a href="/wiki/Shopping_mall" title="Shopping mall">mall</a>, cutting his rope to prevent his friends from falling in after him. In the library, most survivors decide to head south against Sam’s warnings, and later freeze to death. Sam, his friends, and other likeminded survivors remain inside, burning books to stay warm.</p>
<p>Lauren, wounded during the flooding, develops <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Blood_poisoning" title="Blood poisoning">blood poisoning</a> from her injury. Sam, Brian, and J.D. search a Russian cargo vessel, that drifted into the city, for <a href="/wiki/Penicillin" title="Penicillin">penicillin</a>. They encounter a pack of <a href="/wiki/Gray_wolf" title="Gray wolf">wolves</a> which escaped from <a href="/wiki/Central_Park_Zoo" title="Central Park Zoo">Central Park Zoo</a>. The eye of the North American storm arrives, freezing Manhattan, but Sam’s group escape into the library just in time. Jack and Jason also survive it. Days later, the superstorms dissipate. Jack and Jason reach the library, discovering Sam and the others have survived.</p>
<p>Becker, in his first address as President, apologises on television for his ignorance, vowing to send helicopters to rescue survivors in the northern states. Jack and Sam’s group are picked up in Manhattan, where many people have survived. On the <a href="/wiki/International_Space_Station" title="International Space Station">International Space Station</a>, <a href="/wiki/Astronaut" title="Astronaut">astronauts</a> look down in awe at the frozen Earth, now free of <a href="/wiki/Pollution" title="Pollution">pollution</a>.</p>

<p><i>The Day After Tomorrow</i> was inspired by <i><a href="/wiki/Coast_to_Coast_AM" title="Coast to Coast AM">Coast to Coast AM</a></i> talk-radio host <a href="/wiki/Art_Bell" title="Art Bell">Art Bell</a> and <a href="/wiki/Whitley_Strieber" title="Whitley Strieber">Whitley Strieber</a>'s book, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Coming_Global_Superstorm" title="The Coming Global Superstorm">The Coming Global Superstorm</a></i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> and Strieber wrote the film's <a href="/wiki/Novelization" title="Novelization">novelization</a>. <a href="/wiki/Arnold_Federbush" title="Arnold Federbush">Arnold Federbush</a>'s 1978 novel, <i>Ice!</i>, and Douglas Orgill and <a href="/wiki/John_Gribbin" title="John Gribbin">John Gribbin</a>'s <i>The Sixth Winter</i> (published in 1979) have similar themes. Before and during the film's release, members of environmental and political advocacy groups distributed pamphlets to moviegoers describing the possible effects of global warming. Although the film depicts effects of global warming predicted by scientists (such as rising sea levels, more destructive storms, and disruption of ocean currents and weather patterns), it depicts their occurrence more rapidly and severely than what is considered scientifically plausible; the theory that a superstorm could create rapid worldwide <a href="/wiki/Climate_change" title="Climate change">climate change</a> does not appear in the scientific literature.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>To choose a studio, writer Michael Wimer created an auction. A copy of the script was sent to all major studios along with a term sheet. They had 24 hours to decide whether to produce the movie with Roland Emmerich directing. Fox Studios was the only studio to accept the terms.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Visual_effects">Visual effects</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Day_After_Tomorrow&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Visual effects">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><i>The Day After Tomorrow</i> is well-known for its special effects and <a href="/wiki/Computer-generated_imagery" title="Computer-generated imagery">CGI</a>. The movie features 416 visual effects shots, with nine effects houses and over 1,000 artists working on the film for over a year to bring its disaster sequences to life.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reception">Reception</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Day_After_Tomorrow&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Reception">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Box_office">Box office</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Day_After_Tomorrow&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Box office">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The film ranked #2 at the box office (behind <i><a href="/wiki/Shrek_2" title="Shrek 2">Shrek 2</a></i>) over its four-day <a href="/wiki/Memorial_Day" title="Memorial Day">Memorial Day</a> opening, grossing $85,807,341. led the per-theater average, with a four-day average of $25,053 (compared to <i>Shrek 2</i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">'</span>s four-day average of $22,633). At the end of its theatrical run, the film grossed $186,740,799 domestically and $544,272,402 worldwide. It was the second-highest opening-weekend film not to lead at the box office; <i><a href="/wiki/Inside_Out_(2015_film)" title="Inside Out (2015 film)">Inside Out</a></i> surpassed it in June 2015.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BoxOfficeMojo_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-BoxOfficeMojo-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p><i>The Day After Tomorrow</i> is the eighth-highest-grossing film which did not lead the United States box office its opening weekend, behind <i>Inside Out</i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Sing_(2016_American_film)" title="Sing (2016 American film)">Sing</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/My_Big_Fat_Greek_Wedding" title="My Big Fat Greek Wedding">My Big Fat Greek Wedding</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Alvin_and_the_Chipmunks_(film)" title="Alvin and the Chipmunks (film)">Alvin and the Chipmunks</a></i> and <a href="/wiki/Alvin_and_the_Chipmunks:_The_Squeakquel" title="Alvin and the Chipmunks: The Squeakquel">its sequel</a>, <i><a href="/wiki/Sherlock_Holmes_(2009_film)" title="Sherlock Holmes (2009 film)">Sherlock Holmes</a></i>, and <i><a href="/wiki/Ice_Age:_Dawn_of_the_Dinosaurs" title="Ice Age: Dawn of the Dinosaurs">Ice Age: Dawn of the Dinosaurs</a></i>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> It is fourth worldwide in that category, behind <i><a href="/wiki/Ice_Age:_Dawn_of_the_Dinosaurs" title="Ice Age: Dawn of the Dinosaurs">Ice Age: Dawn of the Dinosaurs</a></i>, <i>Inside Out</i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Casino_Royale_(2006_film)" title="Casino Royale (2006 film)">Casino Royale</a></i>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Critical_reaction">Critical reaction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Day_After_Tomorrow&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Critical reaction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><i>The Day After Tomorrow</i> received mixed reviews from critics, who praised its visual effects and criticized its writing and scientific inaccuracy. <a href="/wiki/Review_aggregator" title="Review aggregator">Review aggregator</a> <a href="/wiki/Rotten_Tomatoes" title="Rotten Tomatoes">Rotten Tomatoes</a> rated the film at 45%, with an average rating of 5.3 out of 10. According to the website, it is "A ludicrous popcorn flick filled with clunky dialogues, but spectacular visuals save it from being a total disaster."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Roger_Ebert" title="Roger Ebert">Roger Ebert</a> of the <i><a href="/wiki/Chicago_Sun-Times" title="Chicago Sun-Times">Chicago Sun-Times</a></i> described the film as "profoundly silly" but nonetheless said the film was effective and praised the special effects. He gave it three stars out of four.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Political_and_scientific_criticism">Political and scientific criticism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Day_After_Tomorrow&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Political and scientific criticism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Emmerich did not deny that his casting of a weak president and the resemblance of vice-president <a href="/wiki/Kenneth_Welsh" title="Kenneth Welsh">Kenneth Welsh</a> to <a href="/wiki/Dick_Cheney" title="Dick Cheney">Dick Cheney</a> were intended to criticize the <a href="/wiki/Climate_change_policy_of_the_George_W._Bush_administration" title="Climate change policy of the George W. Bush administration">climate change policy of the George W. Bush administration</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-USAToday_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-USAToday-8">[8]</a></sup> Responding to claims of insensitivity in his inclusion of scenes of a devastated <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York City</a> less than three years after the <a href="/wiki/September_11_attacks" title="September 11 attacks">September 11 attacks</a>, Emmerich said that it was necessary to showcase the increased unity of people in the face of disaster because of the attacks.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Black_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Black-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-UGO_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-UGO-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CC_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-CC-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>Some scientists criticized the film's scientific aspects. Paleoclimatologist and professor of earth and planetary science at <a href="/wiki/Harvard_University" title="Harvard University">Harvard University</a> <a href="/wiki/Daniel_P._Schrag" title="Daniel P. Schrag">Daniel P. Schrag</a> said, "On the one hand, I'm glad that there's a big-budget movie about something as critical as climate change. On the other, I'm concerned that people will see these over-the-top effects and think the whole thing is a joke ... We are indeed experimenting with the Earth in a way that hasn't been done for millions of years. But you're not going to see another <a href="/wiki/Ice_age" title="Ice age">ice age</a> – at least not like that."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-USAToday_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-USAToday-8">[8]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/J._Marshall_Shepherd" title="J. Marshall Shepherd">J. Marshall Shepherd</a>, a research meteorologist at the <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">NASA</a> <a href="/wiki/Goddard_Space_Flight_Center" title="Goddard Space Flight Center">Goddard Space Flight Center</a>, expressed a similar sentiment: "I'm heartened that there's a movie addressing real climate issues. But as for the science of the movie, I'd give it a D minus or an F. And I'd be concerned if the movie was made to advance a political agenda."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-USAToday_8-2"><a href="#cite_note-USAToday-8">[8]</a></sup> According to <a href="/wiki/University_of_Victoria" title="University of Victoria">University of Victoria</a> climatologist <a href="/wiki/Andrew_Weaver" title="Andrew Weaver">Andrew Weaver</a>, "It's <i><a href="/wiki/The_Towering_Inferno" title="The Towering Inferno">The Towering Inferno</a></i> of climate science movies, but I'm not losing any sleep over a new ice age, because it's impossible."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-USAToday_8-3"><a href="#cite_note-USAToday-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Patrick_J._Michaels" title="Patrick J. Michaels">Patrick J. Michaels</a>, a former research professor of <a href="/wiki/Environmental_science" title="Environmental science">environmental science</a> at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Virginia" title="University of Virginia">University of Virginia</a> who rejects the scientific consensus<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> on <a href="/wiki/Global_warming" title="Global warming">global warming</a>, called the film "propaganda" in a <i><a href="/wiki/USA_Today" title="USA Today">USA Today</a></i> editorial: "As a scientist, I bristle when lies dressed up as 'science' are used to influence political discourse."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> College instructor and retired NASA Office of Inspector General senior special agent Joseph Gutheinz called <i>The Day After Tomorrow</i> "a cheap thrill ride, which many weak-minded people will jump on and stay on for the rest of their lives" in a <i>Space Daily</i> editorial.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>When paleoclimatologist William Hyde of <a href="/wiki/Duke_University" title="Duke University">Duke University</a> was asked on <a href="/wiki/Usenet" title="Usenet">Usenet</a> if he would see the film, he answered that he would not unless someone offered him $100.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> Subscribers to the newsgroup took up the challenge and, despite Hyde's protests, raised the $100.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> Hyde's review on Google Groups criticized the film's depiction of weather which stopped at national borders; it was "to climate science as <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Frankenstein_(1931)" title="Frankenstein (1931)">Frankenstein</a></i> is to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heart_transplant" title="Heart transplant">heart transplant</a> surgery".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>Stefan Rahmstorf of the <a href="/wiki/Potsdam_Institute_for_Climate_Impact_Research" title="Potsdam Institute for Climate Impact Research">Potsdam Institute for Climate Impact Research</a>, an expert on <a href="/wiki/Thermohaline_circulation" title="Thermohaline circulation">thermohaline circulation</a> and its effect on climate, said after a talk with scriptwriter Jeffrey Nachmanoff at the film's <a href="/wiki/Berlin" title="Berlin">Berlin</a> preview:</p>
<dl>
<dd>"Clearly this is a disaster movie and not a scientific documentary, [and] the film makers have taken a lot of artistic license. But the film presents an opportunity to explain that some of the basic background is right: humans are indeed increasingly changing the climate and this is quite a dangerous experiment, including some risk of abrupt and unforeseen changes ... Luckily it is extremely unlikely that we will see major ocean circulation changes in the next couple of decades (I’d be just as surprised as Jack Hall if they did occur); at least most scientists think this will only become a more serious risk towards the end of the century. And the consequences would certainly not be as dramatic as the 'superstorm' depicted in the movie. Nevertheless, a major change in ocean circulation is a risk with serious and partly unpredictable consequences, which we should avoid. And even without events like ocean circulation changes, climate change is serious enough to demand decisive action."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></dd>
</dl>
<p>Environmental activist and <i><a href="/wiki/The_Guardian" title="The Guardian">Guardian</a></i> columnist <a href="/wiki/George_Monbiot" title="George Monbiot">George Monbiot</a> called <i>The Day After Tomorrow</i> "a great movie and lousy science".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2008, <a href="/wiki/Yahoo!" title="Yahoo!">Yahoo!</a> Movies listed <i>The Day After Tomorrow</i> as one of its top-10 scientifically inaccurate films.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> It was criticized for depicting <a href="/wiki/Meteorology" title="Meteorology">meteorological</a> phenomena as occurring over the course of hours, instead of decades or centuries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> A 2015 <i>Washington Post</i> article reported on a paper published in <i>Nature Scientific Reports</i> which indicated that global temperatures could drop relatively rapidly due to a temporary shutdown of the Atlantic Meridional Overturning Circulation caused by global warming.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Home_media">Home media</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Day_After_Tomorrow&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Home media">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><span id="DVD"></span> The film was released on VHS and DVD October 12, 2004 and was released in <a href="/wiki/High-definition_video" title="High-definition video">high-definition video</a> on <a href="/wiki/Blu-ray" title="Blu-ray">Blu-ray</a> in North America on October 2, 2007 and in the United Kingdom on April 28, 2008, in <a href="/wiki/1080p" title="1080p">1080p</a> with a lossless <a href="/wiki/DTS-HD_Master_Audio" title="DTS-HD Master Audio">DTS-HD Master Audio</a> track and few bonus features. DVD sales were $110 million, bringing the film's gross to $652,771,772.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
