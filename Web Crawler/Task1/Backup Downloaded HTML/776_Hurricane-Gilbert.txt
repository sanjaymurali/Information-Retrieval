<p><b>Hurricane Gilbert</b> was the <a href="/wiki/List_of_costliest_Atlantic_hurricanes" title="List of costliest Atlantic hurricanes">costliest</a> <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_hurricane" title="Atlantic hurricane">Atlantic hurricane</a> on record until surpassed by <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Hugo" title="Hurricane Hugo">Hugo</a> of <a href="/wiki/1989_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="1989 Atlantic hurricane season">the following year</a> as well as the most intense Atlantic hurricane until <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Wilma" title="Hurricane Wilma">Hurricane Wilma</a>. Gilbert was an extremely powerful <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone" title="Tropical cyclone">tropical cyclone</a> that formed during the <a href="/wiki/1988_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="1988 Atlantic hurricane season">1988 Atlantic hurricane season</a> and peaked as a <a href="/wiki/List_of_Category_5_Atlantic_hurricanes" title="List of Category 5 Atlantic hurricanes">Category 5</a> strength hurricane that brought widespread destruction to the Caribbean and the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_of_Mexico" title="Gulf of Mexico">Gulf of Mexico</a>. Gilbert was also one of the largest tropical cyclones ever observed in the Atlantic basin. At one point, its tropical storm-force winds measured 575 mi (925 km) in diameter. In addition, Gilbert was the most intense tropical cyclone in recorded history to strike Mexico.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HURDAT_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-HURDAT-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>The tenth <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_named_tropical_cyclones" title="List of named tropical cyclones">named storm</a> and third hurricane of the 1988 <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="Atlantic hurricane season">Atlantic hurricane season</a>, Gilbert developed from a <a href="/wiki/Tropical_wave" title="Tropical wave">tropical wave</a> on September 8 while located 400 mi (640 km) east of <a href="/wiki/Barbados" title="Barbados">Barbados</a>. Following intensification into a tropical storm the next day, Gilbert steadily strengthened as it tracked west-northwestward into the Caribbean Sea. On September 10, Gilbert attained hurricane intensity, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rapid_deepening" title="Rapid deepening">rapidly intensified</a> into a Category 3 hurricane on September 11. After striking <a href="/wiki/Jamaica" title="Jamaica">Jamaica</a> the following day, rapid intensification occurred once again, and the storm became a Category 5 hurricane on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saffir%E2%80%93Simpson_hurricane_wind_scale" title="Saffir–Simpson hurricane wind scale">Saffir–Simpson hurricane wind scale</a> late on September 13. Gilbert then weakened slightly, and made landfall on the <a href="/wiki/Yucat%C3%A1n_Peninsula" title="Yucatán Peninsula">Yucatán Peninsula</a> later that day while maintaining Category 5 intensity. After landfall, Gilbert weakened rapidly over the Yucatán Peninsula, and emerged into the Gulf of Mexico as a Category 2 storm on September 15. Gradual intensification occurred as Gilbert tracked across the Gulf of Mexico, and the storm made landfall as a Category 3 hurricane in mainland Mexico on September 16. The hurricane gradually weakened after landfall, and eventually dissipated on September 19 over the Midwestern United States.</p>
<p>Gilbert wrought havoc in the Caribbean and the Gulf of Mexico for nearly nine days. In total, it killed 318 people and caused about $7.1 billion (equivalent to $19 billion in 2016) in damages over the course of its path. As a result of the extensive damage caused by Gilbert, the <a href="/wiki/World_Meteorological_Organization" title="World Meteorological Organization">World Meteorological Organization</a> <a href="/wiki/List_of_retired_Atlantic_hurricane_names" title="List of retired Atlantic hurricane names">retired</a> the name in the spring of 1989; it was replaced with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tropical_Storm_Gordon_(disambiguation)" title="Tropical Storm Gordon (disambiguation)">Gordon</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Meteorological_history">Meteorological history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hurricane_Gilbert&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Meteorological history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Gilbert_1988_track.png"><img alt="This image is a satellite tracking of Hurricane Gilbert. Map plotting the track and intensity of the storm, according to the <a href="/wiki/Saffir%E2%80%93Simpson_scale" title="Saffir–Simpson scale">Saffir–Simpson scale</a></div>

<p>The origins of Hurricane Gilbert trace back to an easterly <a href="/wiki/Tropical_wave" title="Tropical wave">tropical wave</a>—an elongated <a href="/wiki/Trough_(meteorology)" title="Trough (meteorology)">low-pressure area</a> moving from east to west—that exited the northwestern coast of Africa on September 3, 1988. Over the subsequent days, the wave traversed the tropical Atlantic and <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclogenesis" title="Tropical cyclogenesis">developed</a> a broad wind <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_circulation" title="Atmospheric circulation">circulation</a> extending just north of the equator. The system remained disorganized until September 8, when <a href="/wiki/Weather_satellite" title="Weather satellite">satellite images</a> showed a defined circulation center approaching the <a href="/wiki/Windward_Islands" title="Windward Islands">Windward Islands</a>. The following day, the <a href="/wiki/National_Hurricane_Center" title="National Hurricane Center">National Hurricane Center (NHC)</a> classified it as the twelfth <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone" title="Tropical cyclone">tropical depression</a> of the annual hurricane season using the <a href="/wiki/Dvorak_technique" title="Dvorak technique">Dvorak technique</a>, when it was located about 400 mi (640 km) east of <a href="/wiki/Barbados" title="Barbados">Barbados</a>. The depression proceeded toward the west-northwest, and while moving through the <a href="/wiki/Lesser_Antilles" title="Lesser Antilles">Lesser Antilles</a> near <a href="/wiki/Martinique" title="Martinique">Martinique</a>, it gained enough strength to be designated as Tropical Storm Gilbert.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-prelim01_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-prelim01-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>After becoming a tropical storm, Gilbert underwent a period of significant strengthening. Passing to the south of <a href="/wiki/Hispaniola" title="Hispaniola">Hispaniola</a>, it became a hurricane late on September 10 and further strengthened to Category 3 intensity on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saffir-Simpson_hurricane_wind_scale" title="Saffir-Simpson hurricane wind scale">Saffir–Simpson scale</a> the next day. At that time, Gilbert was classified as a <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone" title="Tropical cyclone">major hurricane</a> with sustained winds of 125 mph (205 km/h) and a minimum <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_pressure" title="Atmospheric pressure">barometric pressure</a> 960 <a href="/wiki/Bar_(unit)" title="Bar (unit)">mbar</a> (<a href="/wiki/Pascal_(unit)" title="Pascal (unit)">hPa</a>; 28.35 <a href="/wiki/Inch_of_mercury" title="Inch of mercury">inHg</a>). On September 12, the hurricane made <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Landfall_(meteorology)" title="Landfall (meteorology)">landfall</a> on the eastern coast of Jamaica at this intensity; its 15 mi (25 km)-wide <a href="/wiki/Eye_(cyclone)" title="Eye (cyclone)">eye</a> moved from east to west across the entire length of the island.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-prelim01_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-prelim01-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>

<p>Gilbert <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rapid_deepening" title="Rapid deepening">strengthened rapidly</a> after emerging from the coast of Jamaica. As the hurricane brushed the <a href="/wiki/Cayman_Islands" title="Cayman Islands">Cayman Islands</a>, a reporting station on <a href="/wiki/Grand_Cayman" title="Grand Cayman">Grand Cayman</a> recorded a wind gust of 156 mph (252 km/h) as the storm passed just to the southeast on September 13. Explosive intensification continued until Gilbert reached a minimum <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_pressure" title="Atmospheric pressure">pressure</a> of 888 mbar (hPa; 26.22 inHg) with <a href="/wiki/Maximum_sustained_wind" title="Maximum sustained wind">maximum sustained flight-level winds</a> of 185 mph (295 km/h), having intensified by 72 mbar in a space of 24 hours.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[nb 1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-prelim01_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-prelim01-2">[2]</a></sup> This pressure was the lowest ever observed in the <a href="/wiki/Western_Hemisphere" title="Western Hemisphere">Western Hemisphere</a> and made Gilbert the most intense <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/North_Atlantic_tropical_cyclone" title="North Atlantic tropical cyclone">Atlantic hurricane</a> on record until it was surpassed by Hurricane Wilma in 2005.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HURDAT_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-HURDAT-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Gilbert made landfall for a second time on the island of <a href="/wiki/Cozumel" title="Cozumel">Cozumel</a>, and subsequently made landfall on Mexico's Yucatán Peninsula on September 14 as a Category 5 hurricane,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-prelim01_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-prelim01-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-prelim02_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-prelim02-6">[5]</a></sup> making it the first Category 5 hurricane to make landfall in the Atlantic basin since <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_David" title="Hurricane David">Hurricane David</a> hit Hispanola in <a href="/wiki/1979_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="1979 Atlantic hurricane season">1979</a>; the minimum pressure at landfall in Cozumel was estimated to be 900 mbar (26.6 inHg), along with maximum sustained winds of 160 mph (260 km/h).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-prelim02_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-prelim02-6">[5]</a></sup> The storm weakened quickly while crossing land before it emerged into the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_of_Mexico" title="Gulf of Mexico">Gulf of Mexico</a> as a Category 2 hurricane.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-prelim09_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-prelim09-7">[6]</a></sup> Gilbert restrengthened rapidly, however, and made landfall for a final time as a Category 3 hurricane near <a href="/wiki/La_Pesca" title="La Pesca">La Pesca</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tamaulipas" title="Tamaulipas">Tamaulipas</a> on September 16, with winds of about 125 mph (201 km/h).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-prelim01_2-4"><a href="#cite_note-prelim01-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>On September 17, Gilbert brushed the inland city of <a href="/wiki/Monterrey" title="Monterrey">Monterrey</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nuevo_Le%C3%B3n" title="Nuevo León">Nuevo León</a> before taking a sharp turn to the north. The storm spawned 29 <a href="/wiki/Tornado" title="Tornado">tornadoes</a> in <a href="/wiki/Texas" title="Texas">Texas</a> on September 18 before moving across <a href="/wiki/Oklahoma" title="Oklahoma">Oklahoma</a> and being absorbed by a low-pressure system over <a href="/wiki/Missouri" title="Missouri">Missouri</a> on September 19, finally becoming <a href="/wiki/Extratropical_cyclone" title="Extratropical cyclone">extratropical</a> over <a href="/wiki/Lake_Michigan" title="Lake Michigan">Lake Michigan</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-prelim01_2-5"><a href="#cite_note-prelim01-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Preparations">Preparations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hurricane_Gilbert&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Preparations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Gilbert_16_sept_1988_2226Z.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="640" data-file-width="600" height="235" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/24/Gilbert_16_sept_1988_2226Z.jpg/220px-Gilbert_16_sept_1988_2226Z.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/24/Gilbert_16_sept_1988_2226Z.jpg/330px-Gilbert_16_sept_1988_2226Z.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/24/Gilbert_16_sept_1988_2226Z.jpg/440px-Gilbert_16_sept_1988_2226Z.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>

<p>Late on September 10, a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone_watches_and_warnings#Tropical_Storm_Warning" title="Tropical cyclone watches and warnings">tropical storm warning</a> was issued by the National Hurricane Center for the southern coast of the Dominican Republic alongside a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone_watches_and_warnings#Hurricane_Watch" title="Tropical cyclone watches and warnings">hurricane watch</a> for the <a href="/wiki/Barahona_Province" title="Barahona Province">Barahona</a> Peninsula. The hurricane watch for Barahona was upgraded to a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone_watches_and_warnings#Hurricane_Warning" title="Tropical cyclone watches and warnings">hurricane warning</a> early on September 11. Later that day, hurricane watches were posted for the Dominican Republic's southern coast, Jamaica, and the southern coast of Cuba east of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cabo_Cruz" title="Cabo Cruz">Cabo Cruz</a>; the hurricane watch in Jamaica was upgraded to a hurricane warning by the end of the day. Hurricane warnings for the southern coast of Haiti were also posted on September 11.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-prelim11_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-prelim11-8">[7]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Cayman_Airways" title="Cayman Airways">Cayman Airways</a> evacuated residents from the <a href="/wiki/Cayman_Islands" title="Cayman Islands">Cayman Islands</a> ahead of Gilbert.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-news_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-news-9">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>On September 12, a hurricane watch was issued for the Cayman Islands, and the hurricane watch for the southern coast of Cuba was extended to <a href="/wiki/Cienfuegos" title="Cienfuegos">Cienfuegos</a>, with the portion of the watch east of <a href="/wiki/Camag%C3%BCey" title="Camagüey">Camagüey</a> upgraded to a hurricane warning. That evening, the Yucatán Peninsula was placed under a hurricane watch between <a href="/wiki/Felipe_Carrillo_Puerto,_Quintana_Roo" title="Felipe Carrillo Puerto, Quintana Roo">Felipe Carrillo Puerto</a> and <a href="/wiki/Progreso,_Yucat%C3%A1n" title="Progreso, Yucatán">Progreso</a>. This area included the resort cities of <a href="/wiki/Canc%C3%BAn" title="Cancún">Cancún</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cozumel" title="Cozumel">Cozumel</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-prelim11_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-prelim11-8">[7]</a></sup> The following day, hurricane watches were posted for <a href="/wiki/Pinar_del_R%C3%ADo" title="Pinar del Río">Pinar del Río</a> and <a href="/wiki/Isla_de_la_Juventud" title="Isla de la Juventud">Isla de la Juventud</a>, and the Cayman Islands were placed under a hurricane warning.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-prelim11_8-2"><a href="#cite_note-prelim11-8">[7]</a></sup> The watches in western Cuba and the Yucatán Peninsula were replaced with warnings at about mid-day September 13.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-prelim12_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-prelim12-10">[9]</a></sup> As Gilbert approached the Yucatán Peninsula on September 14, the hurricane warning in the region was extended to cover the entire coast between <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chetumal,_Quintana_Roo" title="Chetumal, Quintana Roo">Chetumal</a> and <a href="/wiki/Champot%C3%B3n,_Campeche" title="Champotón, Campeche">Champotón</a>, while a hurricane watch was posted for the northern district of <a href="/wiki/Belize" title="Belize">Belize</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-prelim12_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-prelim12-10">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>Once Gilbert entered the Gulf of Mexico on September 15, hurricane watches were posted for the portion of the shore between <a href="/wiki/Port_Arthur,_Texas" title="Port Arthur, Texas">Port Arthur</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tampico,_Tamaulipas" title="Tampico, Tamaulipas">Tampico</a>. Around noon that day, the hurricane watch was upgraded to a hurricane warning between Tampico and <a href="/wiki/Port_O%27Connor,_Texas" title="Port O'Connor, Texas">Port O'Connor</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-prelim12_10-2"><a href="#cite_note-prelim12-10">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Governor_of_Texas" title="Governor of Texas">Texas governor</a> <a href="/wiki/Bill_Clements" title="Bill Clements">Bill Clements</a> issued a decree allowing municipalities to lift laws in the name of public safety, including <a href="/wiki/Contraflow_lane_reversal" title="Contraflow lane reversal">contraflow lane reversals</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[10]</a></sup> and speed limits.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[11]</a></sup></p>

<p>Gilbert claimed 318 lives, mostly in Mexico. Exact monetary damage figures are not available, but the total for all areas affected by Gilbert is estimated to be near $7.1 billion (1988 <a href="/wiki/United_States_dollar" title="United States dollar">USD</a>). A preliminary report assessed Gilbert as the costliest Atlantic hurricane of all time with an initial damage total of over <span class="sortkey" style="display:none">7010100000000000000♠</span>US$10 billion,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Thomas_C._Hayes_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-Thomas_C._Hayes-18">[17]</a></sup> though it was not later confirmed.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Eastern_Caribbean_and_Venezuela">Eastern Caribbean and Venezuela</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hurricane_Gilbert&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Eastern Caribbean and Venezuela">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>As a tropical storm, Gilbert brought high winds and heavy rains to many of the eastern Caribbean islands.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AssociatedPress915_17-2"><a href="#cite_note-AssociatedPress915-17">[16]</a></sup> In St. Lucia heavy rains peaking at 12.8 in (330 mm) in <a href="/wiki/Castries" title="Castries">Castries</a> resulted in flash flooding and mudslides, though no major structural damage was reported.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-UPI911_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-UPI911-19">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-StLuciaUPI_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-StLuciaUPI-20">[19]</a></sup> At <a href="/wiki/Hewanorra_International_Airport" title="Hewanorra International Airport">Hewanorra International Airport</a>, a dam ruptured and flooded one of the runways.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-UPI911_19-1"><a href="#cite_note-UPI911-19">[18]</a></sup> Offshore, six fishermen went missing as Gilbert approached the <a href="/wiki/Lesser_Antilles" title="Lesser Antilles">Lesser Antilles</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-StLuciaUPI_20-1"><a href="#cite_note-StLuciaUPI-20">[19]</a></sup> Banana crop losses from the storm in St. Lucia reached $740,000,with <a href="/wiki/Guadeloupe" title="Guadeloupe">Guadeloupe</a>, <a href="/wiki/Saint_Vincent_and_the_Grenadines" title="Saint Vincent and the Grenadines">St. Vincent</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Dominica" title="Dominica">Dominica</a> reporting similar damage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AssociatedPress915_17-3"><a href="#cite_note-AssociatedPress915-17">[16]</a></sup> Several mudslides were reported in Dominica, though no damage resulted from them. Roughly 5 in (130 mm) of rain fell in <a href="/wiki/Barbados" title="Barbados">Barbados</a>, leading to flash floods and prompting officials to close schools and government offices.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-UPI911_19-2"><a href="#cite_note-UPI911-19">[18]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/United_States_Virgin_Islands" title="United States Virgin Islands">U.S. Virgin Islands</a> experienced widespread power outages and flooding, with many residents losing electricity for several days. Damage was less severe in the nearby <a href="/wiki/British_Virgin_Islands" title="British Virgin Islands">British Virgin Islands</a>, where only some flooding and power outages took place. In Puerto Rico, dozens of small communities lost power and agricultural losses reached $200,000.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AssociatedPress915_17-4"><a href="#cite_note-AssociatedPress915-17">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>In Venezuela, <a href="/wiki/Rainband" title="Rainband">outflow bands</a> from Gilbert produced extreme torrential rain which triggered widespread flash floods and <a href="/wiki/Landslide" title="Landslide">landslides</a> in the northern part of the country, killing five people and leaving hundreds homeless.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1988mwr_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-1988mwr-21">[20]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[21]</a></sup> Damage from the storm was estimated at $3 million.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[22]</a></sup> A combined and confirmed death toll of seven dead from the <a href="/wiki/Dominican_Republic" title="Dominican Republic">Dominican Republic</a> and <a href="/wiki/Venezuela" title="Venezuela">Venezuela</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[23]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Hispaniola">Hispaniola</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hurricane_Gilbert&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Hispaniola">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Heavy rains from the outer bands of Hurricane Gilbert triggered significant flooding in the <a href="/wiki/Dominican_Republic" title="Dominican Republic">Dominican Republic</a> and <a href="/wiki/Haiti" title="Haiti">Haiti</a>. At least nine people perished in the Dominican Republic as many rivers, including the <a href="/wiki/Yuna_River" title="Yuna River">Yuna</a>, overtopped their banks.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-UPIHispaniola1_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-UPIHispaniola1-15">[14]</a></sup> The main electrical relay station in <a href="/wiki/Santo_Domingo" title="Santo Domingo">Santo Domingo</a> was damaged by the storm, causing a temporary blackout for much of the city.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-StPetersburgHispaniola1_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-StPetersburgHispaniola1-25">[24]</a></sup> Losses in the country were estimated in the millions of dollars.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-UPIHispaniola1_15-2"><a href="#cite_note-UPIHispaniola1-15">[14]</a></sup> In nearby Haiti, more substantial losses took place; 53 people died,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-emdat_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-emdat-14">[13]</a></sup> including 10 offshore. Most of the casualties took place in the southern part of the country. The port of <a href="/wiki/Jacmel" title="Jacmel">Jacmel</a> was reportedly destroyed by 10 ft (3.0 m) waves stirred up by the hurricane.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-UPIHispaniola1_15-3"><a href="#cite_note-UPIHispaniola1-15">[14]</a></sup> In light of extensive damage, the <a href="/wiki/Government_of_Haiti" title="Government of Haiti">government of Haiti</a> declared a <a href="/wiki/State_of_emergency" title="State of emergency">state of emergency</a> for the entire southern peninsula.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-StPetersburgHispaniola1_25-1"><a href="#cite_note-StPetersburgHispaniola1-25">[24]</a></sup> Losses throughout Haiti were estimated at $91.2 million.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-emdat_14-2"><a href="#cite_note-emdat-14">[13]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Jamaica">Jamaica</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hurricane_Gilbert&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Jamaica">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">

<p>Hurricane Gilbert produced a 19 ft (5.8 m) <a href="/wiki/Storm_surge" title="Storm surge">storm surge</a> and brought up to 823 millimetres (32.4 in) of rain in the mountainous areas of Jamaica,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[25]</a></sup> causing inland flash flooding. 49 people died.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1988mwr_21-1"><a href="#cite_note-1988mwr-21">[20]</a></sup> Prime Minister <a href="/wiki/Edward_Seaga" title="Edward Seaga">Edward Seaga</a> stated that the hardest hit areas near where Gilbert made landfall looked "like <a href="/wiki/Hiroshima" title="Hiroshima">Hiroshima</a> after the <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_weapon" title="Nuclear weapon">atom bomb</a>."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[26]</a></sup> The storm left $4 billion (1988 USD) in damage from destroyed crops, buildings, houses, roads, and small aircraft.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-The_Storm_and_its_Effects_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-The_Storm_and_its_Effects-28">[27]</a></sup> Two people eventually had to be rescued because of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mudslide" title="Mudslide">mudslides</a> triggered by Gilbert and were sent to the hospital. The two people were reported to be fine. No planes were going in and out of <a href="/wiki/Kingston,_Jamaica" title="Kingston, Jamaica">Kingston</a>, and telephone lines were jammed from Jamaica to <a href="/wiki/Florida" title="Florida">Florida</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-news_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-news-9">[8]</a></sup></p>

<p>As Gilbert lashed <a href="/wiki/Kingston,_Jamaica" title="Kingston, Jamaica">Kingston</a>, its winds knocked down power lines, uprooted trees, and flattened fences. On the north coast, 20 feet (6.1 m) waves hit <a href="/wiki/Ocho_Rios" title="Ocho Rios">Ocho Rios</a>, a popular tourist resort where hotels were evacuated. <a href="/wiki/Norman_Manley_International_Airport" title="Norman Manley International Airport">Kingston's airport</a> reported severe damage to its aircraft, and all Jamaica-bound flights were cancelled at <a href="/wiki/Miami_International_Airport" title="Miami International Airport">Miami International Airport</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-news_9-2"><a href="#cite_note-news-9">[8]</a></sup> Unofficial estimates state that at least 30 people were killed around the island. Estimated property damage reached more than $200 million. More than 100,000 houses were destroyed or damaged and the country's <a href="/wiki/Banana" title="Banana">banana</a> crop was largely destroyed. Hundreds of miles of roads and highways were also heavily damaged.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[28]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hurricane_Hunters" title="Hurricane Hunters">Reconnaissance flights</a> over remote parts of Jamaica reported that eighty percent of the homes on the island had lost their roofs. The <a href="/wiki/Poultry" title="Poultry">poultry</a> industry was also wiped out; the damage from agricultural loss reached $500 million (1988 USD). Hurricane Gilbert was the most destructive storm in the history of Jamaica and the most severe storm since <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Charlie_(1951)" title="Hurricane Charlie (1951)">Hurricane Charlie</a> in <a href="/wiki/1951_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="1951 Atlantic hurricane season">1951</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[29]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cayman_Islands">Cayman Islands</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hurricane_Gilbert&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Cayman Islands">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Gilbert passed 30 miles (48 km) to the south of the Cayman Islands early on September 13, with one reported gust of 157 mph (253 km/h). However, the islands largely escaped the hurricane due to Gilbert's quick forward motion. Damage was mitigated because the depth of the water surrounding the islands limited the height of the storm surge to 5 ft (1.5 m) There was very severe damage to crops, trees, pastures, and a number of private homes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hurricanecity.2FCayman_Islands_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hurricanecity.2FCayman_Islands-31">[30]</a></sup> At least 50 people were left homeless and losses were expected to be in the millions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-UPIHispaniola1_15-4"><a href="#cite_note-UPIHispaniola1-15">[14]</a></sup></p>

<p>Across parts of northern Central America, heavy rains from the outer bands of Hurricane Gilbert triggered deadly flash floods. Its rainfall and high winds reached <a href="/wiki/Guatemala" title="Guatemala">Guatemala</a>, <a href="/wiki/Belize" title="Belize">Belize</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Honduras" title="Honduras">Honduras</a>. In Honduras, at least eight people were killed and 6,000 were left homeless. Additionally, approximately 27,000 acres (11,000 hectares) of crops were flooded.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[31]</a></sup> Sixteen people perished in Guatemala and another five died in Nicaragua, leaving a total of 21 people dead in Central America.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[32]</a></sup></p>

<p>35,000 people were left homeless and 83 ships sank when Gilbert struck the Yucatán Peninsula. 60,000 homes were destroyed, and damage was estimated at between $1 and 2 billion (1989 US$).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mexican_Hurricanes.2FGilbert_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-Mexican_Hurricanes.2FGilbert-34">[33]</a></sup> In the Cancún region, Gilbert produced waves 7 m (23 ft) high, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Beach_erosion" title="Beach erosion">washing away</a> 60% of the city's beaches; the <a href="/wiki/Storm_surge" title="Storm surge">storm surge</a> from the storm penetrated up to 5 km (3.1 mi) inland.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[34]</a></sup> A further loss of $87 million (1989 <a href="/wiki/United_States_dollar" title="United States dollar">USD</a>) due to a decline in tourism was estimated for the months of October, November and December in 1988.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Canc.C3.BAn_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-Canc.C3.BAn-36">[35]</a></sup> Rainfall in the Yucatán Peninsula peaked at 13.78 inches (350 mm) in <a href="/wiki/Progreso,_Yucat%C3%A1n" title="Progreso, Yucatán">Progreso</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rain_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-rain-37">[36]</a></sup></p>
<p>As Gilbert lashed the third largest city of Mexico, <a href="/wiki/Monterrey" title="Monterrey">Monterrey</a>, it brought very high winds, torrential rains, and extensive flash floods. More than 60 people died from raging flood waters, and it was feared that more than 150 people died when five buses carrying evacuees were overturned in the raging floodwaters. Six policemen died when they were swept away while trying to rescue passengers on buses stranded by the Santa Catarina River.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mexican_Hurricanes.2FGilbert_34-1"><a href="#cite_note-Mexican_Hurricanes.2FGilbert-34">[33]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-300_feared_dead_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-300_feared_dead-38">[37]</a></sup> The residents of Monterrey had no power or drinking water, and most telephone lines were down. As the water receded, vehicles began appearing with their wheels up, jammed with mud and rocks. <a href="/wiki/Quintana_Roo" title="Quintana Roo">Quintana Roo</a> Governor <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Miguel_Borje&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Miguel Borje (page does not exist)">Miguel Borje</a> reported that damages in Cancún were estimated at more than 1.3 billion Mexican pesos (1988 pesos; $500 million in USD). More than 5,000 American tourists were evacuated from <a href="/wiki/Canc%C3%BAn" title="Cancún">Cancún</a>. In <a href="/wiki/Saltillo" title="Saltillo">Saltillo</a>, five people died in road accidents caused by heavy rain, and almost 1,000 were left homeless.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-300_feared_dead_38-1"><a href="#cite_note-300_feared_dead-38">[37]</a></sup> Rainfall in northeastern Mexico peaked at over 10 inches (250 mm) in localized areas of inland <a href="/wiki/Tamaulipas" title="Tamaulipas">Tamaulipas</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rain_37-1"><a href="#cite_note-rain-37">[36]</a></sup> In <a href="/wiki/Coahuila" title="Coahuila">Coahuila</a>, rainfall from Gilbert caused the deaths of 5 people who were swept away by rising waters. Among these were a paramedic and a pregnant woman who died when a Mexican <a href="/wiki/International_Red_Cross_and_Red_Crescent_Movement" title="International Red Cross and Red Crescent Movement">Red Cross</a> ambulance fell into a flooded <a href="/wiki/Arroyo_(creek)" title="Arroyo (creek)">arroyo</a> near Los Chorros after a bridge collapsed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[38]</a></sup> Gilbert dumped torrential rains and spawned some tornadoes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[39]</a></sup> In Quintana Roo, Gilbert caused significant defoliation in the jungle. The debris eventually fueled a fire in 1989, which ultimately burned 460 sq mi (1,200 km<sup>2</sup>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Natural_Hazards_Map_41-0"><a href="#cite_note-Natural_Hazards_Map-41">[40]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="United_States">United States</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hurricane_Gilbert&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: United States">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>Despite concerns that Texas might suffer a direct hit, there was only minor damage reported in southern Texas from Gilbert's landfall 60 miles (97 km) to the south. Winds gusted to hurricane force in a few places, but the main impact felt in the state was from beach erosion caused by a 3-5-foot storm surge, and tornadoes, which mainly affected the <a href="/wiki/San_Antonio" title="San Antonio">San Antonio</a> area. 29 <a href="/wiki/History_of_tropical_cyclone-spawned_tornadoes" title="History of tropical cyclone-spawned tornadoes">tornadoes were spawned</a> by Gilbert in Texas, at least two of which were killer tornadoes. Estimates ranged from 30 to more than 60 hitting 25 Texas counties. Nine of them hit <a href="/wiki/San_Antonio" title="San Antonio">San Antonio</a>, where a 59-year-old woman was killed as she slept in her mobile home. 40 tornadoes were spawned in an area from <a href="/wiki/Corpus_Christi,_Texas" title="Corpus Christi, Texas">Corpus Christi</a> and <a href="/wiki/Brownsville,_Texas" title="Brownsville, Texas">Brownsville</a> north to San Antonio and west to <a href="/wiki/Del_Rio,_Texas" title="Del Rio, Texas">Del Rio</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-severe_aftermath_42-0"><a href="#cite_note-severe_aftermath-42">[41]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[42]</a></sup> In the state, a major disaster was declared on October 5, 1988.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[43]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Oklahoma" title="Oklahoma">Oklahoma</a> recorded the highest rainfall in the United States at 8.6 inches (220 mm), in <a href="/wiki/Wichita_Mountains_Wildlife_Refuge" title="Wichita Mountains Wildlife Refuge">Wichita Mountains Wildlife Refuge</a>. Isolated locations in Texas and Oklahoma reported over 7 inches (180 mm), while moderate rainfall of up to 3 inches (76 mm) fell in central <a href="/wiki/Michigan" title="Michigan">Michigan</a>. Overall damage in the United States was estimated at $80 million (1988 USD).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dead_2011_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-Dead_2011-16">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rain_37-2"><a href="#cite_note-rain-37">[36]</a></sup></p>

<p>The overall property damage was estimated at $7.1 billion (1988 USD). Earlier estimates put property damage from Gilbert at $2.5 billion but were as high as $10 billion. A final count of Hurricane Gilbert's victims is not possible because many people remained missing in Mexico, but the total confirmed death toll was 433 people.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-severe_aftermath_42-1"><a href="#cite_note-severe_aftermath-42">[41]</a></sup> Gilbert was the worst hurricane in the history of Jamaica and the most destructive tropical cyclone on record to strike Mexico. Due to its widespread impact, extensive damage, and extreme loss of life, the name <i>Gilbert</i> was <a href="/wiki/List_of_retired_Atlantic_hurricane_names" title="List of retired Atlantic hurricane names">retired</a> in the spring of 1989 by the <a href="/wiki/World_Meteorological_Organization" title="World Meteorological Organization">World Meteorological Organization</a> and was the first name to be retired since <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Gloria" title="Hurricane Gloria">Hurricane Gloria</a> in 1985. The name will never again be used for another Atlantic hurricane.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[52]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone_naming" title="Tropical cyclone naming">name</a> was replaced by <i><a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Gordon_(1994)" title="Hurricane Gordon (1994)">Gordon</a></i> during the <a href="/wiki/1994_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="1994 Atlantic hurricane season">1994 Atlantic hurricane season</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1994names_54-0"><a href="#cite_note-1994names-54">[53]</a></sup></p>
<p>The destruction in Jamaica was so heavy that <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Lovindeer&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Lovindeer (page does not exist)">Lovindeer</a>, one of the country's leading dance hall DJs, released a single called <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Wild_Gilbert&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Wild Gilbert (page does not exist)">Wild Gilbert</a></i> a few days after the storm. It was the fastest selling reggae record in the history of <a href="/wiki/Music_of_Jamaica" title="Music of Jamaica">Jamaican music</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[54]</a></sup> In 1989, the <a href="/wiki/PBS" title="PBS">PBS</a> series <i><a href="/wiki/Nova_(TV_series)" title="Nova (TV series)">Nova</a></i> released the episode "<a href="/wiki/Hurricane!_(Nova)" title="Hurricane! (Nova)">Hurricane!</a>" that featured Gilbert (later modified in 1992 to reflect <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Andrew" title="Hurricane Andrew">Hurricane Andrew</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Iniki" title="Hurricane Iniki">Hurricane Iniki</a>).<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>On September 13, Hurricane Gilbert attained a record low <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_pressure" title="Atmospheric pressure">central pressure</a> of 888 mb (hPa; 26.22 inHg), surpassing the previous minimum of 892 hPa (26.34 inHg) set by the <a href="/wiki/1935_Labor_Day_hurricane" title="1935 Labor Day hurricane">1935 Labor Day hurricane</a>. This made it the <a href="/wiki/List_of_the_most_intense_tropical_cyclones" title="List of the most intense tropical cyclones">strongest</a> tropical cyclone <a href="/wiki/HURDAT" title="HURDAT">on record</a> in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/North_Atlantic_tropical_cyclone" title="North Atlantic tropical cyclone">north Atlantic basin</a> at the time. It was surpassed by <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Wilma" title="Hurricane Wilma">Hurricane Wilma</a> in <a href="/wiki/2005_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="2005 Atlantic hurricane season">2005</a>, which attained a central pressure of 882 hPa (26.05 inHg).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HURDAT_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-HURDAT-1">[1]</a></sup> Inflicting approximately $7.1 billion in damage, Gilbert was one of the <a href="/wiki/List_of_costliest_Atlantic_hurricanes" title="List of costliest Atlantic hurricanes">most destructive Atlantic hurricanes</a> ever observed at the time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Thomas_C._Hayes_18-1"><a href="#cite_note-Thomas_C._Hayes-18">[17]</a></sup> Gilbert is the most intense tropical cyclone on record to strike Jamaica. The storm also produced record-breaking rainfall in Jamaica, amounting to 27.56 in (700 mm). This ranked it as the <a href="/wiki/List_of_wettest_tropical_cyclones_by_country" title="List of wettest tropical cyclones by country">fourth-wettest known storm</a> to strike Jamaica; however, it has since been surpassed by three other storms.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1988mwr_21-2"><a href="#cite_note-1988mwr-21">[20]</a></sup></p>
