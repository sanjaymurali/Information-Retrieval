<p><b>Hurricane Eloise</b> was the most destructive <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone" title="Tropical cyclone">tropical cyclone</a> of the <a href="/wiki/1975_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="1975 Atlantic hurricane season">1975 Atlantic hurricane season</a>. The fifth tropical storm, fourth hurricane, and second major hurricane of the season, Eloise formed as a tropical depression on September 13 to the east of the <a href="/wiki/Virgin_Islands" title="Virgin Islands">Virgin Islands</a>. The depression tracked westward and intensified into a tropical storm while passing to the north of <a href="/wiki/Puerto_Rico" title="Puerto Rico">Puerto Rico</a>. Eloise briefly attained hurricane intensity soon thereafter, but weakened back to a tropical storm upon making <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Landfall_(meteorology)" title="Landfall (meteorology)">landfall</a> over <a href="/wiki/Hispaniola" title="Hispaniola">Hispaniola</a>. A weak and disorganized cyclone, Eloise emerged into open waters of the northern Caribbean Sea; upon striking the northern <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Yucatan_Peninsula" title="Yucatan Peninsula">Yucatan Peninsula</a>, it turned north and began to re-intensify. In the Gulf of Mexico, the cyclone quickly matured and became a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saffir-Simpson_Hurricane_Scale" title="Saffir-Simpson Hurricane Scale">Category 3 hurricane</a> on September 23. Eloise made landfall along the <a href="/wiki/Florida_Panhandle" title="Florida Panhandle">Florida Panhandle</a> west of <a href="/wiki/Panama_City,_Florida" title="Panama City, Florida">Panama City</a> before moving inland across <a href="/wiki/Alabama" title="Alabama">Alabama</a> and dissipating on September 24.</p>
<p>The storm produced torrential rainfall throughout the islands of Puerto Rico and Hispaniola, causing extensive flooding that led to severe damage and more than 40 deaths. Thousands of people in these areas became homeless as flood waters submerged numerous communities. As Eloise progressed westward, it affected Cuba to a lesser extent. In advance of the storm, about 100,000 residents evacuated from the Gulf Coast region. Upon making landfall in Florida, Eloise generated wind gusts of 155 miles per hour (249 km/h), which demolished hundreds of buildings in the area. The storm's severe winds, waves, and storm surge left numerous beaches, piers, and other coastal structures heavily impaired.</p>
<p>Wind-related damage extended into inland Alabama and Georgia. Further north, torrential rains along the entire East Coast of the United States created an unprecedented and far-reaching flooding event, especially into the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mid-Atlantic_States" title="Mid-Atlantic States">Mid-Atlantic States</a>. In that region, an additional 17 people died as a result of freshwater flooding from the post-tropical storm; infrastructural and geological effects were comparable to those from <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Agnes" title="Hurricane Agnes">Hurricane Agnes</a> several years prior. Across the United States, damage amounted to approximately $560 million. The storm killed 80 people along its entire track; due to the severe damage, the name "Eloise" was <a href="/wiki/List_of_retired_Atlantic_hurricane_names" title="List of retired Atlantic hurricane names">retired</a> from the Atlantic tropical cyclone naming lists.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Meteorological_history">Meteorological history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hurricane_Eloise&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Meteorological history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Eloise_1975_track.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="633" data-file-width="1024" height="136" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6c/Eloise_1975_track.png/220px-Eloise_1975_track.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6c/Eloise_1975_track.png/330px-Eloise_1975_track.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6c/Eloise_1975_track.png/440px-Eloise_1975_track.png 2x" width="220"/></a>

<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Eloise_1975_track.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Map plotting the track and intensity of the storm, according to the <a href="/wiki/Saffir%E2%80%93Simpson_scale" title="Saffir–Simpson scale">Saffir–Simpson scale</a></div>

<p>The origins of Hurricane Eloise trace back to a <a href="/wiki/Tropical_wave" title="Tropical wave">tropical wave</a> that emerged from the western coast of Africa on September 6, 1975. Satellite imagery indicated that the system was initially disjointed and poorly developed, although there was evidence of a low-level circulation. The disturbance tracked westward for several days as it slowly matured. On September 13, a ship called the <i>Gulf Hansa</i> recorded winds of around 25 mph (40 km/h) and 10 feet (3.0 m) seas in association with the system. Shortly thereafter, a <a href="/wiki/Reconnaissance_aircraft" title="Reconnaissance aircraft">reconnaissance aircraft</a> found a center of circulation 575 miles (925 km) east of the <a href="/wiki/Virgin_Islands" title="Virgin Islands">Virgin Islands</a>, and it is estimated that the storm became a tropical depression at 0600 UTC.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PRELIM_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-PRELIM-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HURDAT_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-HURDAT-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>The depression continued moving towards the west as it gradually strengthened. On September 16, the system attained tropical storm status and was designated Eloise;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HURDAT_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-HURDAT-2">[2]</a></sup> accordingly, the first advisory on the system was issued by the San Juan Weather Bureau office. While in the vicinity of a strengthening <a href="/wiki/Anticyclone" title="Anticyclone">anticyclone</a> aloft, Eloise became better organized, and the storm rapidly intensified and reached Category 1 hurricane status 18 hours after being named. The cyclone soon made landfall on the <a href="/wiki/Dominican_Republic" title="Dominican Republic">Dominican Republic</a>, inhibiting further development.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PRELIM_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-PRELIM-1">[1]</a></sup> Although initially predicted to remain north of land, the storm moved across northern Hispaniola and then tracked across southeastern <a href="/wiki/Cuba" title="Cuba">Cuba</a>. After 36 hours with much of its circulation over mountainous terrain, Eloise deteriorated to a tropical storm on September 17.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PRELIM_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-PRELIM-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>The cyclone emerged over the open waters of the northern Caribbean on September 19, passing <a href="/wiki/Jamaica" title="Jamaica">Jamaica</a> to the north as it moved away from Cuba. Despite favorable upper-level conditions, its interaction with land—combined with the weakening of a <a href="/wiki/Ridge_(meteorology)" title="Ridge (meteorology)">ridge</a> to the north—left the storm's center distorted. Eloise remained a fairly disorganized tropical storm until September 20, when it approached the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Yucatan_Peninsula" title="Yucatan Peninsula">Yucatan Peninsula</a> and began to re-intensify. The storm crossed over the northern tip of the peninsula as it began to turn northward in response to an approaching <a href="/wiki/Trough_(meteorology)" title="Trough (meteorology)">trough</a>. Between September 17 and September 21, however, reports on the storm were scarce, leading to uncertainty in its exact location and strength. Upon entering the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_of_Mexico" title="Gulf of Mexico">Gulf of Mexico</a>, Eloise quickly organized. The trough enhanced the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Wind_divergence&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Wind divergence (page does not exist)">wind divergence</a> over the storm's center,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PRELIM_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-PRELIM-1">[1]</a></sup> allowing it to strengthen once again to reach hurricane force about 345 miles (555 km) south of <a href="/wiki/New_Orleans" title="New Orleans">New Orleans</a>, <a href="/wiki/Louisiana" title="Louisiana">Louisiana</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HURDAT_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-HURDAT-2">[2]</a></sup></p>

<p>On September 22, the cyclone intensified to attain Category 2 strength, and became a major hurricane of Category 3 status shortly thereafter as it turned towards the northeast.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HURDAT_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-HURDAT-2">[2]</a></sup> Several ships penetrated the storm's center during its passage through the gulf. The hurricane also moved over two experimental buoys which recorded data on the storm, aiding meteorologists in their forecasts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PRELIM_1-4"><a href="#cite_note-PRELIM-1">[1]</a></sup> Hurricane Eloise continued to strengthen until it reached its peak winds of 125 mph (205 km/h) and a minimum <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Barometric_pressure" title="Barometric pressure">barometric pressure</a> of about 955 mbar (hPa; 28.2 inHg).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HURDAT_2-4"><a href="#cite_note-HURDAT-2">[2]</a></sup> It moved ashore along the <a href="/wiki/Florida_Panhandle" title="Florida Panhandle">Florida Panhandle</a> near <a href="/wiki/Panama_City,_Florida" title="Panama City, Florida">Panama City</a> on September 23. Shortly after making landfall, the hurricane rapidly degenerated. Just six hours later, it had weakened into a tropical storm, while situated over eastern <a href="/wiki/Alabama" title="Alabama">Alabama</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PRELIM_1-5"><a href="#cite_note-PRELIM-1">[1]</a></sup> It further weakened into a tropical depression at 0000 UTC on September 24. The depression transitioned into an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Extratropical_storm" title="Extratropical storm">extratropical storm</a> over <a href="/wiki/Virginia" title="Virginia">Virginia</a>, and became indistinguishable by later that same day. The remnant moisture, however, merged with a <a href="/wiki/Weather_front" title="Weather front">weather front</a> to produce widespread and heavy precipitation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PRELIM_1-6"><a href="#cite_note-PRELIM-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HURDAT_2-5"><a href="#cite_note-HURDAT-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Preparations">Preparations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hurricane_Eloise&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Preparations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In advance of Hurricane Eloise, warnings for heavy rainfall and potential flooding were issued for <a href="/wiki/Puerto_Rico" title="Puerto Rico">Puerto Rico</a> and the Virgin Islands. A <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hurricane_warning" title="Hurricane warning">hurricane warning</a> was declared for parts of the Dominican Republic about 12 hours before landfall.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PRELIM_1-7"><a href="#cite_note-PRELIM-1">[1]</a></sup> A "hurricane emergency" was put into effect for the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oriente_(province)" title="Oriente (province)">Oriente Province</a> of Cuba, while a "state of alert" was issued for the <a href="/wiki/Camag%C3%BCey_Province" title="Camagüey Province">Camagüey Province</a>. <a href="/wiki/Cubana_de_Aviaci%C3%B3n" title="Cubana de Aviación">Cubana de Aviación</a> suspended all flights to Oriente.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>On and before September 15, there was still uncertainty as to whether Eloise would impact the United States. However, officials in Florida began taking precautionary measures.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> When the storm entered the Gulf of Mexico, forecasters suggested that the storm would continue northward and strike the area near <a href="/wiki/Mobile_Bay" title="Mobile Bay">Mobile Bay</a>. Contrary to predictions, by late on September 22, the storm had turned northeast, and some residents of Florida were still unaware of the storm's threat despite the issuance of hurricane warnings 24 hours in advance.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Barnes_2007_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Barnes_2007-5">[5]</a></sup> As a result, evacuations were delayed to an extent.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PRELIM_1-8"><a href="#cite_note-PRELIM-1">[1]</a></sup> During the morning hours of September 23, civil preparedness workers drove through coastal towns with loudspeakers advising people to seek shelter. Due of the intensity of the approaching hurricane, evacuations along the coast were ultimately thorough, despite the initial delay. It was reported that 99% of <a href="/wiki/Pensacola,_Florida" title="Pensacola, Florida">Pensacola</a> residents along the beach had left their homes, and overall, 100,000 people evacuated from areas in Louisiana through Florida.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Barnes_2007_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-Barnes_2007-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>A statement issued by the <a href="/wiki/National_Weather_Service" title="National Weather Service">National Weather Service</a> advised people in nine Florida counties to complete hurricane preparations, which included securing loose objects and moving watercraft to safety.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Homes along the coast were boarded up by their owners,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> while offshore, workers were removed from oil platforms. A spokesman for <a href="/wiki/Royal_Dutch_Shell" title="Royal Dutch Shell">Royal Dutch Shell</a> reported that 800 workers were to be evacuated.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> In New Orleans, emergency equipment was readied and inspected. The New Orleans Levee Board went into a second-stage alert on September 21, and cleared debris from floodwall openings.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Impact">Impact</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hurricane_Eloise&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Impact">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>As a weak tropical depression, the storm brought 5 to 10 inches (130 to 250 mm) of rainfall to portions of the <a href="/wiki/Leeward_Islands" title="Leeward Islands">Leeward Islands</a>, including <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/St._Kitts" title="St. Kitts">St. Kitts</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/St._Martin" title="St. Martin">St. Martin</a>. More minor amounts of precipitation fell over the northernmost islands, and winds were light in these areas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PRELIM_1-9"><a href="#cite_note-PRELIM-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Despite being only a tropical storm while passing by Puerto Rico, Eloise produced extreme amounts of rainfall on the island, peaking at 33.29 inches (846 mm) in Dos Bocas. Other totals of 10 to 20 inches (250 to 510 mm) were common.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HPC_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-HPC-10">[10]</a></sup> The heavy rains resulted in severe flash flooding which killed 34 people, mostly from drownings, and left $60 million in damages.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Barnes3_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Barnes3-11">[11]</a></sup> Several hundred people were injured, and the storm forced over 6,000 residents from their homes. Dozens of towns and villages were flooded, though <a href="/wiki/Utuado,_Puerto_Rico" title="Utuado, Puerto Rico">Utuado</a>, with a population of 35,000 at the time, was hit the hardest. The situation in that town was described as a "total disaster"; four housing developments were under water, and dozens of vehicles were washed away.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> The flood waters submerged thousands of miles of roads and put several bridges out-of-service.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-newspaper2_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-newspaper2-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>As the storm proceeded westward, it dropped heavy rainfall throughout eastern and southern Hispaniola. Widespread flooding impacted Haiti and the Dominican Republic, leaving a total of 25 people dead. Although the most intense winds remained offshore, a gust of 50 miles per hour (80 km/h) was recorded at <a href="/wiki/Cape_Enga%C3%B1o_(Dominican_Republic)" title="Cape Engaño (Dominican Republic)">Cape Engaño</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PRELIM_1-10"><a href="#cite_note-PRELIM-1">[1]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/San_Felipe_de_Puerto_Plata" title="San Felipe de Puerto Plata">Puerto Plata</a> on the northern coast of the Dominican Republic was also battered by high winds and heavy rain. Following the storm, electricity was turned off due to the danger of electrocutions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-newspaper2_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-newspaper2-13">[13]</a></sup> Despite the storm's effects across Hispaniola and Puerto Rico, no monetary damage totals are available.</p>

<p>Rain and wind from the storm affected the southern <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bahamas" title="Bahamas">Bahamas</a>, Cuba, <a href="/wiki/Jamaica" title="Jamaica">Jamaica</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Cayman_Islands" title="Cayman Islands">Cayman Islands</a>, and the northern Yucatan Peninsula. Since the storm was primarily weak while passing by these areas, no significant damage was reported.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PRELIM_1-11"><a href="#cite_note-PRELIM-1">[1]</a></sup> Eloise brought torrential rainfall and winds of 20 mph (32 km/h) to the <a href="/wiki/Guantanamo_Bay_Naval_Base" title="Guantanamo Bay Naval Base">Guantanamo Bay Naval Base</a> in southeastern Cuba, inflicting $65,000 in damage. Personnel on the base were moved to designated hurricane shelters in advance of the storm.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Florida">Florida</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hurricane_Eloise&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Florida">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Eloise came ashore along the coast of northern Florida as a Category 3 storm producing winds of 90 mph (140 km/h) with gusts that reached 155 mph (249 km/h).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PRELIM_1-12"><a href="#cite_note-PRELIM-1">[1]</a></sup> Sustained winds were likely higher, but due to the sparsity of recording stations, few official records exist. The winds in the area were reportedly the strongest of the century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Barnes_2007_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-Barnes_2007-5">[5]</a></sup> Hurricane-force winds occurred from <a href="/wiki/Fort_Walton_Beach,_Florida" title="Fort Walton Beach, Florida">Fort Walton Beach</a> through Panama City. Along the coast, tides ran 12 to 16 feet (3.7 to 4.9 m) above normal, peaking at 18 feet (5.5 m). Hurricane Eloise spawned several tornadoes as it pressed inland.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PRELIM_1-13"><a href="#cite_note-PRELIM-1">[1]</a></sup> In general, rainfall ranged from 4 to 8 inches (100 to 200 mm); at the <a href="/wiki/Eglin_Air_Force_Base" title="Eglin Air Force Base">Eglin Air Force Base</a> near <a href="/wiki/Valparaiso,_Florida" title="Valparaiso, Florida">Valparaiso</a>, however, the hurricane dropped 14.9 inches (380 mm) of precipitation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HPC_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-HPC-10">[10]</a></sup> The heaviest rainfall was usually confined to northwest of the storm's track, and a number of locations to the east of Eloise's center picked up less than 1 inch (25 mm) of rain.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PRELIM_1-14"><a href="#cite_note-PRELIM-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Damage from the hurricane was widespread. Fort Walton Beach, where hundreds of structures were damaged or destroyed, was hit particularly hard. In some areas, the storm surge washed away buildings demolished by the strong winds. The winds cleared certain locations of trees and buried properties and roads under sand. Throughout northwest Florida, an estimated 8,000 people suffered storm-related losses, and 500 businesses were completely destroyed. An article in the <i><a href="/wiki/Tallahassee_Democrat" title="Tallahassee Democrat">Tallahassee Democrat</a></i> reported that "Cottages, motels, restaurants, convenience stores and other beach businesses were strewn across the highway in a tangle of down power poles, lines and busted mains."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Barnes_2007_5-3"><a href="#cite_note-Barnes_2007-5">[5]</a></sup> A 2,100-acre (850 ha) shrimp farm at Panama City, the first of its kind, was effectively lost. The storm destroyed the farm's prospective initial harvest, 1,500,000 pounds (680,000 kg) of shrimp enclosed in a system of nets and enclosures. The president of the company described the subsequent events as six months of extreme turmoil in an effort to recover, followed by a quick and steady rebound. By the spring of 1976, the company became confident in financial success and full recovery.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Barnes3_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-Barnes3-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>

<p>The storm caused severe <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Beach_erosion" title="Beach erosion">beach erosion</a> in <a href="/wiki/Bay_County,_Florida" title="Bay County, Florida">Bay County</a>; approximately 801,000 <a href="/wiki/Cubic_yard" title="Cubic yard">cubic yards</a> (612,000 <a href="/wiki/Cubic_metre" title="Cubic metre">m<sup>3</sup></a>) of sand was removed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> Storm-related changes in the coastal topography resulted in extensive structural damage in the <a href="/wiki/Panama_City_Beach,_Florida" title="Panama City Beach, Florida">Panama City Beach</a> area. The most severe damage was concentrated in a 22 miles (35 km) area of the shore east of the hurricane's eye, and storm surge peaked in intensity for no more than a half hour according to preliminary estimates. Much of the resultant damage came as a result of foundation undermining, which was compared to that of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/New_England_hurricane_of_1938" title="New England hurricane of 1938">New England hurricane of 1938</a>. Although Eloise was not abnormally strong, the geographicy setting and building standards in the area were blamed for the destruction of many homes and businesses.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> Monetary losses from property damage in Panama City Beach alone totaled about $50 million.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-swash_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-swash-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>The first major storm to strike the region in 40 years, Hurricane Eloise did not directly kill anyone in the state of Florida.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> However, four deaths of an indirect nature were attributed to the hurricane;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PRELIM_1-15"><a href="#cite_note-PRELIM-1">[1]</a></sup> two of them were related to heart attacks. Numerous people sustained injuries, largely from broken glass or cleanup efforts. Overall property damage from the storm in Florida amounted to $150 million.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Barnes3_11-2"><a href="#cite_note-Barnes3-11">[11]</a></sup> In the storm's aftermath, a study of the hurricane's effect on aquatic animals living in the <i>swash zone</i> (the immediate area where land and the ocean meet) of Panama City Beach was conducted.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-swash_18-1"><a href="#cite_note-swash-18">[18]</a></sup> The study concluded that compared to 11 consecutive months of data prior to the storm, the swash zone experienced a brief influx of animal species normally found offshore. However, the number decreased to near normal shortly thereafter.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-swash_18-2"><a href="#cite_note-swash-18">[18]</a></sup> Also along the shore, the hurricane dismantled or severely impaired several piers, including the total destruction of a 300 feet (91 m) extension of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Okaloosa_Island" title="Okaloosa Island">Okaloosa Island</a> Pier built just three years earlier and part of its original span. A fishing pier at <a href="/wiki/St._Andrews_State_Park" title="St. Andrews State Park">St. Andrews State Park</a> also suffered vast damage, along with another wooden pier at <a href="/wiki/Mexico_Beach,_Florida" title="Mexico Beach, Florida">Mexico Beach</a> and the M.B. Miller Pier at Panama City Beach, which lost its end section to the storm.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Elsewhere_in_the_United_States">Elsewhere in the United States</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hurricane_Eloise&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Elsewhere in the United States">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>As the hurricane progressed inland, it passed over eastern Alabama, generating strong winds. A gust of 100 mph (160 km/h) was recorded northeast of <a href="/wiki/Ozark,_Alabama" title="Ozark, Alabama">Ozark</a>. Winds elsewhere in the state ranged from around 35 mph (56 km/h) to 88 mph (142 km/h). Precipitation in Alabama peaked at 5.54 inches (141 mm). The high winds resulted in severe damage to property and crops, amounting to $100 million.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PRELIM_1-16"><a href="#cite_note-PRELIM-1">[1]</a></sup> Eloise cut power and telephone service in the area,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> and in <a href="/wiki/Geneva_County,_Alabama" title="Geneva County, Alabama">Geneva County</a>, several people sustained storm-related injuries. As in Florida, the weakening hurricane spawned a number of tornadoes in Alabama and Georgia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> Preliminary reports indicated that every county in southeastern Alabama received some damage from the storm. The strong winds uprooted trees and knocked down powerlines. Heavy rain associated with the storm caused a leak in the <a href="/wiki/Alabama_State_Capitol" title="Alabama State Capitol">Alabama State Capitol</a> building roof.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> Gusty winds, moderate to heavy rainfall, and low pressures extended into Georgia, Louisiana, and to a lesser extent, <a href="/wiki/Mississippi" title="Mississippi">Mississippi</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PRELIM_1-17"><a href="#cite_note-PRELIM-1">[1]</a></sup></p>

<p>The remnants of Eloise interacted with another weather system, producing widespread precipitation across the Eastern United States, including portions of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ohio_Valley" title="Ohio Valley">Ohio Valley</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Mid-Atlantic_states" title="Mid-Atlantic states">Mid-Atlantic states</a>, and <a href="/wiki/New_England" title="New England">New England</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HPC_10-2"><a href="#cite_note-HPC-10">[10]</a></sup> The deluge was "almost continuous" during the period between September 22 and 26 according to a statement by the National Weather Service.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Schwartz272_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-Schwartz272-24">[24]</a></sup> One of the highest rainfall totals in association with the storm occurred in <a href="/wiki/Westminster,_Maryland" title="Westminster, Maryland">Westminster, Maryland</a>, where 14.23 inches (361 mm) of rain were recorded. Elsewhere, 7 to 10 inches (180 to 250 mm) or more of precipitation fell throughout parts of <a href="/wiki/Pennsylvania" title="Pennsylvania">Pennsylvania</a>, <a href="/wiki/New_Jersey" title="New Jersey">New Jersey</a>, <a href="/wiki/New_York_(state)" title="New York (state)">New York</a> and <a href="/wiki/Connecticut" title="Connecticut">Connecticut</a>. At least 22 states received rainfall from Hurricane Eloise and its remnant moisture.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HPC_10-3"><a href="#cite_note-HPC-10">[10]</a></sup> Nearby <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hurricane_Faye" title="Hurricane Faye">Hurricane Faye</a> may have also contributed to the heavy rainfall, although this connection was never confirmed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Barnes2_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-Barnes2-25">[25]</a></sup> In <a href="/wiki/Washington,_D.C." title="Washington, D.C.">Washington, D.C.</a>, 9.08 inches (231 mm) of rain contributed to the wettest September on record since 1934.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Virginia_hurricane_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-Virginia_hurricane-26">[26]</a></sup> The excessive rainfall led to extensive flooding throughout the region, in some areas exceeding 50- to 100-year levels.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-USGS_report_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-USGS_report-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<p>Pennsylvania and New York bore the brunt of the flooding, which culminated in loss of life and severe property damage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Barnes2_25-1"><a href="#cite_note-Barnes2-25">[25]</a></sup> Along the central <a href="/wiki/Southern_Tier" title="Southern Tier">Southern Tier</a> region of New York, the storm damaged or destroyed over 700 structures.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> Flooding throughout the Northeastern United States disabled over a dozen water plants and at least 16 sewage treatment plants, prompting a <a href="/wiki/Boil-water_advisory" title="Boil-water advisory">boil-water advisory</a> in Pennsylvania's capital city of <a href="/wiki/Harrisburg,_Pennsylvania" title="Harrisburg, Pennsylvania">Harrisburg</a>. Infrastructure further south also suffered; in <a href="/wiki/Maryland" title="Maryland">Maryland</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Monocacy_River" title="Monocacy River">Monocacy River</a>—a tributary of the <a href="/wiki/Potomac_River" title="Potomac River">Potomac</a>—swelled to 14 feet (4.3 m) above flood stage, inundating the city of <a href="/wiki/Frederick,_Maryland" title="Frederick, Maryland">Frederick</a> and compromising the city's supply of fresh drinking water.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Schwartz272_24-1"><a href="#cite_note-Schwartz272-24">[24]</a></sup> A final downpour of rain on the night of September 25–26 led to an additional 4 inches (100 mm) of rain in central Maryland triggered severe flash flooding. In some cases, this onslaught affected the same areas that were still recovering from <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Agnes" title="Hurricane Agnes">Hurricane Agnes</a> several years earlier, including <a href="/wiki/Ellicott_City,_Maryland" title="Ellicott City, Maryland">Ellicott City</a>, <a href="/wiki/Elkridge,_Maryland" title="Elkridge, Maryland">Elkridge</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Laurel,_Maryland" title="Laurel, Maryland">Laurel</a>, where two major rivers breached their banks and engulfed nearby areas. Many homes and businesses were lost, along with numerous vehicles; in the aftermath, looters entered on boats to access the devastated cities. The floodgates at the <a href="/wiki/Rocky_Gorge_Reservoir" title="Rocky Gorge Reservoir">Rocky Gorge Dam</a> in Laurel were opened, forcing 500 residents downstream to leave their homes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Schwartz2723_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-Schwartz2723-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<p>The consequences of the flooding rains were the worst seen in areas of the interior Mid-Atlantic states since Agnes, and comparisons were often drawn between the two hurricanes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Schwartz272_24-2"><a href="#cite_note-Schwartz272-24">[24]</a></sup> In Pennsylvania alone, flooding from the remnants of Eloise forced 20,000 residents out of their homes;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DC_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-DC-30">[30]</a></sup> thousands further south in the Washington, D.C. area, where severe flooding impacted the city's southern suburbs,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Schwartz273_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-Schwartz273-31">[31]</a></sup> also fled to seek refuge. Further, many motorists throughout the region became stranded on highways inundated by floodwaters.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DC_30-1"><a href="#cite_note-DC-30">[30]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Four_Mile_Run" title="Four Mile Run">Four Mile Run</a> and nearby streams overflowed and "tumbled through residential neighborhoods". Hundreds of families in the <a href="/wiki/Alexandria,_Virginia" title="Alexandria, Virginia">Alexandria</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arlington,_Virginia" title="Arlington, Virginia">Arlington, Virginia</a> area suffered flood-related losses.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Schwartz273_31-1"><a href="#cite_note-Schwartz273-31">[31]</a></sup> Across the Northeastern U.S., the storm system killed 17 people and inflicted $300 million in damage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Barnes2_25-2"><a href="#cite_note-Barnes2-25">[25]</a></sup> An instance of a storm-induced fatality is the death of a man in <a href="/wiki/White_Plains,_New_York" title="White Plains, New York">White Plains, New York</a>, who was killed by waters raging across the <a href="/wiki/Hutchinson_River_Parkway" title="Hutchinson River Parkway">Hutchinson River Parkway</a>. Agriculturally, the extended period of wet weather threatened a range of crops, including the <a href="/wiki/Rhode_Island" title="Rhode Island">Rhode Island</a> apple crop, of which 35% was feared to have been destroyed, and corn and sweet potato fields in <a href="/wiki/North_Carolina" title="North Carolina">North Carolina</a>. With ground too moist for farm machinery to operate on, harvests were postponed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Aftermath">Aftermath</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hurricane_Eloise&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Aftermath">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Hurricane_Eloise_in_Gulf_on_S2ptember_22.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1188" data-file-width="1808" height="145" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/82/Hurricane_Eloise_in_Gulf_on_S2ptember_22.jpg/220px-Hurricane_Eloise_in_Gulf_on_S2ptember_22.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/82/Hurricane_Eloise_in_Gulf_on_S2ptember_22.jpg/330px-Hurricane_Eloise_in_Gulf_on_S2ptember_22.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/82/Hurricane_Eloise_in_Gulf_on_S2ptember_22.jpg/440px-Hurricane_Eloise_in_Gulf_on_S2ptember_22.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">

Eloise on September 22 in the Gulf of Mexico</div>
</div>

<p>After touring the disaster area, Florida Governor <a href="/wiki/Reubin_Askew" title="Reubin Askew">Reubin Askew</a> noted, "I think we're going to have to take a long, close look at some of the construction [...] Some of the structures simply won't be able to be built back in the exact location where they were."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Barnes3_11-3"><a href="#cite_note-Barnes3-11">[11]</a></sup> Governor Askew recruited 400 National Guard troops to prevent looting following the storm.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> He also requested the initial declaration of five counties along the Florida panhandle as national disaster areas, and stated that he would consider adding two more counties. The declaration would make residents in the counties recognized as disaster areas eligible to receive federal aid.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> Immediately following the storm, the mayor of Panama City criticized the state of Florida for failing to provide sufficient post-storm aid.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> Despite the destruction, the storm reportedly had some economic benefits; in the midst of rebuilding and recovery, business grew, especially in and around Panama City, and people began to move into the area.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> In at least one instance, the hurricane and its associated storm surge had a lasting effect on local geography, breaching Crooked Island in Bay County to create an inlet 0.75 miles (1.21 km) wide referred to as Eloise Inlet.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> Eloise provided a comprehensive base of information on beach and dune erosion along the Florida panhandle, which aided in the programming of certain erosion prediction <a href="/wiki/Numerical_weather_prediction" title="Numerical weather prediction">numerical models</a>. In 1995, reports from the aftermath of <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Opal" title="Hurricane Opal">Hurricane Opal</a> created a more extensive collection of data.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup></p>
<p>On September 26, President <a href="/wiki/Gerald_Ford" title="Gerald Ford">Gerald Ford</a> approved the declaration for Florida,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> and later issued a separate declaration for 30 counties in Pennsylvania as the storm's flooding rains progressed northward.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pennsylvania_40-0"><a href="#cite_note-Pennsylvania-40">[40]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> Pennsylvania Lieutenant Governor <a href="/wiki/Ernest_Kline" title="Ernest Kline">Ernest Kline</a> assigned 600 National Guardsmen to assist in the evacuation of flood victims and maintain security in storm-ravaged areas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> Over $430 million in federal disaster relief was spent overall in 1975 and distributed to 92,000 families; the bulk of the funds went to recovery for areas affected by Hurricane Eloise along its entire course.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> In Maryland, Governor <a href="/wiki/Marvin_Mandel" title="Marvin Mandel">Marvin Mandel</a> placed 10 of the state's 23 counties under a state of emergency.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Schwartz272_24-3"><a href="#cite_note-Schwartz272-24">[24]</a></sup> Following the severe damage caused by Hurricane Eloise, its name was <a href="/wiki/List_of_retired_Atlantic_hurricane_names" title="List of retired Atlantic hurricane names">retired</a> at the end of the 1975 season. As such, the name was removed from the rotating lists of names used in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/North_Atlantic_hurricane" title="North Atlantic hurricane">Atlantic hurricane basin</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Retired_44-0"><a href="#cite_note-Retired-44">[44]</a></sup> However, Eloise was not replaced by any particular name due to the addition of male names into the lists in 1979.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Names1979_45-0"><a href="#cite_note-Names1979-45">[45]</a></sup></p>
