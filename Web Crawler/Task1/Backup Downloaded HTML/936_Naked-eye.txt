<p><b>Naked eye</b>, also called <b>bare eye</b> or <b>unaided eye</b>, is the practice of engaging in <a href="/wiki/Visual_perception" title="Visual perception">visual perception</a> unaided by a <a href="/wiki/Magnification" title="Magnification">magnifying</a> or <a href="/wiki/Optical_telescope#Light-gathering_power" title="Optical telescope">light-collecting</a> <a href="/wiki/Optical_instrument" title="Optical instrument">optical instrument</a>, such as a <a href="/wiki/Telescope" title="Telescope">telescope</a> or <a href="/wiki/Microscope" title="Microscope">microscope</a>. Vision corrected to normal <a href="/wiki/Visual_acuity" title="Visual acuity">acuity</a> using <a href="/wiki/Corrective_lens" title="Corrective lens">corrective lenses</a> is considered "naked". In <a href="/wiki/Astronomy" title="Astronomy">astronomy</a>, the naked eye may be used to observe events that can be viewed without equipment, such as an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Astronomical_conjunction" title="Astronomical conjunction">astronomical conjunction</a>, the passage of a <a href="/wiki/Comet" title="Comet">comet</a>, or a <a href="/wiki/Meteor_shower" title="Meteor shower">meteor shower</a>, sometimes some asteroids can be seen, including <a href="/wiki/4_Vesta" title="4 Vesta">4 Vesta</a>. Sky lore and various tests demonstrate an impressive wealth of phenomena that can be seen with the unaided eye.</p>
<p></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Basic_accuracies">Basic accuracies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Naked_eye&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Basic accuracies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The basic accuracies of the human eye are:</p>
<ul>
<li>Quick <a href="/wiki/Autofocus" title="Autofocus">autofocus</a> from distances of 25 cm (young people) to 50 cm (most people 50 years and older) to infinity.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Angular_resolution" title="Angular resolution">Angular resolution</a>: about 1 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arcminute" title="Arcminute">arcminute</a>, approximately 0.02° or 0.0003 radians,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Yanoff2009_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Yanoff2009-1">[1]</a></sup> which corresponds to 0.3 m at a 1 km distance.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Field_of_view" title="Field of view">Field of view</a> (FOV): simultaneous visual perception in an area of about 160° × 175°.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></li>
<li>Faint <a href="/wiki/Star" title="Star">stars</a> up to +8 <a href="/wiki/Apparent_magnitude" title="Apparent magnitude">magnitude</a> under a perfectly dark sky.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Photometry_(optics)" title="Photometry (optics)">Photometry</a> (brightness) to ±10% or 1% of intensity – in a range between night and day of 1:10,000,000,000.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Symmetry" title="Symmetry">Symmetries</a> of 10–20' (3–6 m per 1 km), see the measurements of <a href="/wiki/Tycho_Brahe" title="Tycho Brahe">Tycho Brahe</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Egyptians" title="Egyptians">Egyptians</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></li>
<li>Interval estimations (for example at a plan on paper) to 3–5%.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></li>
<li>Unconscious recognizing of <a href="/wiki/Motion_(physics)" title="Motion (physics)">movement</a> (that is "<a href="/wiki/Alarm_signal" title="Alarm signal">alarm</a> system" and reflexes).<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></li>
</ul>
<p>Visual perception allows a person to gain much information about their surroundings:</p>
<ul>
<li>the distances and 3-dimensional position of things and persons</li>
<li>the vertical (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Plumb_line" title="Plumb line">plumb line</a>) and the slope of plain objects</li>
<li>luminosities and colors and their changes by time and direction</li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Naked_eye_in_astronomy">Naked eye in astronomy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Naked_eye&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Naked eye in astronomy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:372px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Light_pollution_country_versus_city.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1296" data-file-width="972" height="493" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/db/Light_pollution_country_versus_city.png/370px-Light_pollution_country_versus_city.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/db/Light_pollution_country_versus_city.png/555px-Light_pollution_country_versus_city.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/db/Light_pollution_country_versus_city.png/740px-Light_pollution_country_versus_city.png 2x" width="370"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Light_pollution_country_versus_city.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
A photographic approximation of a naked eye view of the <a href="/wiki/Night_sky" title="Night sky">night sky</a> from a small rural town (top) and a <a href="/wiki/Metropolitan_area" title="Metropolitan area">metropolitan area</a> (bottom). <a href="/wiki/Light_pollution" title="Light pollution">Light pollution</a> dramatically reduces the visibility of <a href="/wiki/Star" title="Star">stars</a>.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The visibility of astronomical objects is strongly affected by <a href="/wiki/Light_pollution" title="Light pollution">light pollution</a>. Even a few hundred kilometers away from a metropolitan area where the sky can appear to be very dark, it is still the residual light pollution that sets the limit on the visibility of faint objects. For most people, these are likely to be the best observing conditions within their reach. Under such "typical" dark sky conditions, the naked eye can see stars with an <a href="/wiki/Apparent_magnitude" title="Apparent magnitude">apparent magnitude</a> up to +6<sup>m</sup>. Under perfect dark sky conditions where all light pollution is absent, stars as faint as +8<sup>m</sup> might be visible.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bortle_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bortle-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>The angular resolution of the naked eye is about 1′; however, some people have sharper vision than that. There is anecdotal evidence that people had <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Galilean_moon#Visibility" title="Galilean moon">seen</a> the Galilean <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jupiter%27s_natural_satellites" title="Jupiter's natural satellites">moons of Jupiter</a> before telescopes were invented.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Of similar magnitude, <a href="/wiki/Uranus" title="Uranus">Uranus</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vesta_(asteroid)" title="Vesta (asteroid)">Vesta</a> had most probably been seen but could not be recognized as planets because they appear so faint even at maximum brightness that their motion could not be detected. Uranus, when discovered in 1781, was the first planet discovered using <a href="/wiki/Technology" title="Technology">technology</a> (a <a href="/wiki/Telescope" title="Telescope">telescope</a>) rather than being spotted by the naked eye.</p>
<p>Theoretically, in a typical dark sky, the <a href="/wiki/Adaptation_(eye)" title="Adaptation (eye)">dark adapted</a> human eye would see the about 5,600 stars brighter than +6<sup>m</sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SIMBAD-mag6_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-SIMBAD-mag6-6">[6]</a></sup> while in perfect dark sky conditions about 45,000 stars brighter than +8<sup>m</sup> might be visible.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bortle_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-Bortle-4">[4]</a></sup> In practice, the atmospheric <a href="/wiki/Extinction_(astronomy)" title="Extinction (astronomy)">extinction</a> and dust reduces this number somewhat. In the center of a city, where the naked-eye limiting magnitude due to extreme amounts of light pollution can be +4<sup>m</sup> or less, as few as 200 to 500 stars may be visible. Colors can be seen but this is limited by the fact that the eye uses <a href="/wiki/Photoreceptor_cell" title="Photoreceptor cell">rods instead of cones</a> to view fainter stars.</p>
<p>The visibility of diffuse objects such as <a href="/wiki/Star_cluster" title="Star cluster">star clusters</a> and galaxies is much more strongly affected by light pollution than is that of planets and stars. Under typical dark conditions only a few such objects are visible. These include the <a href="/wiki/Pleiades" title="Pleiades">Pleiades</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Double_cluster" title="Double cluster">h/χ Persei</a>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Andromeda_galaxy" title="Andromeda galaxy">Andromeda galaxy</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Carina_Nebula" title="Carina Nebula">Carina Nebula</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Orion_Nebula" title="Orion Nebula">Orion Nebula</a>, <a href="/wiki/Omega_Centauri" title="Omega Centauri">Omega Centauri</a>, <a href="/wiki/47_Tucanae" title="47 Tucanae">47 Tucanae</a>, the Ptolemy Cluster <a href="/wiki/Messier_7" title="Messier 7">Messier 7</a> near the tail of <a href="/wiki/Scorpius" title="Scorpius">Scorpius</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Messier_Object_13" title="Messier Object 13">globular cluster M13</a> in <a href="/wiki/Hercules_(constellation)" title="Hercules (constellation)">Hercules</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Triangulum_Galaxy" title="Triangulum Galaxy">Triangulum Galaxy (M33)</a> is a difficult <a href="/wiki/Averted_vision" title="Averted vision">averted vision</a> object and only visible at all if it is higher than 50° in the sky. The globular clusters <a href="/wiki/Messier_3" title="Messier 3">M 3</a> in <a href="/wiki/Canes_Venatici" title="Canes Venatici">Canes Venatici</a> and <a href="/wiki/Messier_92" title="Messier 92">M 92</a> in Hercules are also visible with the naked eye under such conditions. Under really dark sky conditions, however, M33 is easy to see, even in direct vision. Many other <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Messier_objects" title="Messier objects">Messier objects</a> are also visible under such conditions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bortle_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-Bortle-4">[4]</a></sup> The most distant objects that have been seen by the naked eye are nearby bright galaxies such as <a href="/wiki/Centaurus_A" title="Centaurus A">Centaurus A</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bode%27s_Galaxy" title="Bode's Galaxy">Bode's Galaxy</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-omeara1998_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-omeara1998-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-skf_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-skf-10">[10]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Sculptor_Galaxy" title="Sculptor Galaxy">Sculptor Galaxy</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-skf_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-skf-10">[10]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Messier_83" title="Messier 83">Messier 83</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Classical_planet" title="Classical planet">Five planets</a> can be recognized as planets from <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a> with the naked eye: Mercury, Venus, Mars, Jupiter, and Saturn. Under typical dark sky conditions <a href="/wiki/Uranus" title="Uranus">Uranus</a> (magnitude +5.8) can be seen as well with averted vision. The Sun and the Moon—the remaining noticeable naked-eye objects of the solar system—are sometimes added to make seven "planets." During daylight only the <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">Moon</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">Sun</a> are obvious naked eye objects, but in many cases <a href="/wiki/Venus" title="Venus">Venus</a> can be spotted in daylight and in rarer cases <a href="/wiki/Jupiter" title="Jupiter">Jupiter</a>. Close to sunset and sunrise bright stars like <a href="/wiki/Sirius" title="Sirius">Sirius</a> or even <a href="/wiki/Canopus" title="Canopus">Canopus</a> can be spotted with the naked eye as long as one knows the exact position in which to look. Historically, the zenith of naked-eye astronomy was the work of <a href="/wiki/Tycho_Brahe" title="Tycho Brahe">Tycho Brahe</a> (1546–1601). He built an extensive observatory to make precise measurements of the heavens without any instruments for magnification. In 1610, <a href="/wiki/Galileo_Galilei" title="Galileo Galilei">Galileo Galilei</a> pointed a <a href="/wiki/Telescope" title="Telescope">telescope</a> towards the sky. He immediately discovered the <a href="/wiki/Galilean_moons" title="Galilean moons">moons of Jupiter</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Planetary_phase" title="Planetary phase">phases</a> of <a href="/wiki/Venus" title="Venus">Venus</a>, among other things.</p>
<p>Meteor showers are better observed by naked eye than with binoculars. Such showers include the <a href="/wiki/Perseids" title="Perseids">Perseids</a> (10–12 August) and the December <a href="/wiki/Geminids" title="Geminids">Geminids</a>. Some 100 <a href="/wiki/Satellite" title="Satellite">satellites</a> per night, the <a href="/wiki/International_Space_Station" title="International Space Station">International Space Station</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Milky_Way" title="Milky Way">Milky Way</a> are other popular objects visible to the naked eye.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Space.2C_geodesy_and_navigation">Space, geodesy and navigation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Naked_eye&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Space, geodesy and navigation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Many other things can be estimated without an instrument. If an arm is stretched the span of the hand corresponds to an angle of 18 to 20°. The distance of a person, just covered up by the outstretched thumbnail, is about 100 meters. The vertical can be estimated to about 2° and, in the northern hemisphere, observing the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pole_Star" title="Pole Star">Pole Star</a> and using a protractor can give the observer's geographic <a href="/wiki/Latitude" title="Latitude">latitude</a>, up to 1 degree of accuracy.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Babylonia" title="Babylonia">Babylonians</a>, <a href="/wiki/Maya_civilization" title="Maya civilization">Mayans</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Egypt" title="Ancient Egypt">ancient Egyptians</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Indian_Astronomy" title="Indian Astronomy">ancient Indians</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Chinese_astronomy" title="Chinese astronomy">Chinese</a> measured all the basics of their respective time and calendar systems by naked eye:</p>
<ul>
<li>the length of a year and a month to ±0.1 hour or to better than 1 minute (0.001%)</li>
<li>the 24 hours of a day, and the <a href="/wiki/Equinox" title="Equinox">equinoxes</a></li>
<li>the periods of the <a href="/wiki/Planet" title="Planet">planets</a> were calculated by Mayan astronomers, to within 5 to 10 minutes accuracy in the case of Venus and Mars.</li>
</ul>
<p>In a similar manner star <a href="/wiki/Occultation" title="Occultation">occultations</a> by the moon can be observed. By using a digital <a href="/wiki/Clock" title="Clock">clock</a> an accuracy of 0.2 second is possible. This represents only 200 meters at the moon's distance of 385,000 km.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Small_objects_and_maps">Small objects and maps</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Naked_eye&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Small objects and maps">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Observing a nearby small object without a <a href="/wiki/Magnifying_glass" title="Magnifying glass">magnifying glass</a> or a <a href="/wiki/Microscope" title="Microscope">microscope</a>, the size of the object depends on the viewing distance. Under normal lighting conditions (light source ~ 1000 lumens at height 600–700 mm, viewing angle ~ 35 degrees) the angular size recognized by naked eye will be round 1 arc minute = 1/60 degrees = 0.0003 radians.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Yanoff2009_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Yanoff2009-1">[1]</a></sup> At a viewing distance of 16" = ~ 400 mm, which is considered a normal reading distance in the USA, the smallest object resolution will be ~ 0.116 mm. For inspection purposes laboratories use a viewing distance of 200–250 mm,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> which gives the smallest size of the object recognizable to the naked eye of ~0.058- 0.072 mm(~55-75 micrometers). The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Accuracy" title="Accuracy">accuracy</a> of a measurement ranges from 0.1 to 0.3 mm and depends on the experience of the observer. The latter figure is the usual positional accuracy of faint details in maps and technical plans.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Environmental_pollution">Environmental pollution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Naked_eye&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Environmental pollution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Mars,_2099_(ESO).jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3456" data-file-width="5184" height="167" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ef/Mars%2C_2099_%28ESO%29.jpg/250px-Mars%2C_2099_%28ESO%29.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ef/Mars%2C_2099_%28ESO%29.jpg/375px-Mars%2C_2099_%28ESO%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ef/Mars%2C_2099_%28ESO%29.jpg/500px-Mars%2C_2099_%28ESO%29.jpg 2x" width="250"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">

The Milky Way is visible over the <a href="/wiki/Very_Large_Telescope" title="Very Large Telescope">Very Large Telescope</a>, demonstrating clear atmosphere above <a href="/wiki/Paranal_Observatory" title="Paranal Observatory">Paranal Observatory</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></div>

<p>A clean <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Earth" title="Atmosphere of Earth">atmosphere</a> is indicated by the fact that the Milky Way is visible. Comparing the zenith with the <a href="/wiki/Horizon" title="Horizon">horizon</a>, shows how the "blue quality" is degraded depending on the amount of air pollution and dust. The twinkling of a star is an indication of the <a href="/wiki/Turbulence" title="Turbulence">turbulence</a> of the air. This is of importance in <a href="/wiki/Meteorology" title="Meteorology">meteorology</a> and for the "<a href="/wiki/Astronomical_seeing" title="Astronomical seeing">seeing</a>" of astronomy.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Light_pollution" title="Light pollution">Light pollution</a> is a significant problem for amateur astronomers but becomes less late at night when many lights are shut off. Air dust can be seen even far away from a city by its "light dome".</p>
