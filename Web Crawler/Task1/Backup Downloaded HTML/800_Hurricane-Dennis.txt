<p><b>Hurricane Dennis</b> was an early-forming major <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hurricane" title="Hurricane">hurricane</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Caribbean" title="Caribbean">Caribbean</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gulf_of_Mexico" title="Gulf of Mexico">Gulf of Mexico</a> during the record-breaking <a href="/wiki/2005_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="2005 Atlantic hurricane season">2005 Atlantic hurricane season</a>. Dennis was the fourth named storm, second hurricane, and first major hurricane of the season. In July, the hurricane set several records for early season hurricane activity becoming the strongest <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/North_Atlantic_tropical_cyclone" title="North Atlantic tropical cyclone">Atlantic hurricane</a> ever to form before August; a title it held for only six days before being surpassed by <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Emily_(2005)" title="Hurricane Emily (2005)">Hurricane Emily</a>.</p>
<p>Dennis hit <a href="/wiki/Cuba" title="Cuba">Cuba</a> twice as a Category 4 hurricane on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saffir-Simpson_Hurricane_Scale" title="Saffir-Simpson Hurricane Scale">Saffir-Simpson Hurricane Scale</a>, and made landfall on the <a href="/wiki/Florida_Panhandle" title="Florida Panhandle">Florida Panhandle</a> in the United States as a Category 3 storm less than a year after <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Ivan" title="Hurricane Ivan">Hurricane Ivan</a> did so. Dennis killed 88 people—76 directly and 12 indirectly—in the U.S. and <a href="/wiki/Caribbean" title="Caribbean">Caribbean</a> and caused $2.23 billion (2005 <a href="/wiki/United_States_dollar" title="United States dollar">USD</a>) in damages to the United States, as well as an approximately equal amount of damage in the Caribbean, primarily on <a href="/wiki/Cuba" title="Cuba">Cuba</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Meteorological_history">Meteorological history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hurricane_Dennis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Meteorological history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Meteorological_history_of_Hurricane_Dennis" title="Meteorological history of Hurricane Dennis">Meteorological history of Hurricane Dennis</a></div>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Dennis_2005_track.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2920" data-file-width="3000" height="214" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/32/Dennis_2005_track.png/220px-Dennis_2005_track.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/32/Dennis_2005_track.png/330px-Dennis_2005_track.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/32/Dennis_2005_track.png/440px-Dennis_2005_track.png 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Dennis_2005_track.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Map plotting the track and intensity of the storm, according to the <a href="/wiki/Saffir%E2%80%93Simpson_scale" title="Saffir–Simpson scale">Saffir–Simpson scale</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The incipient <a href="/wiki/Tropical_wave" title="Tropical wave">tropical wave</a> that became Dennis was identified on June 26, 2005, well-inland over Africa;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TCR_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-TCR-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> it later emerged over the Atlantic Ocean on June 29 and moved briskly to the west.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TCR_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-TCR-1">[1]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Saharan_Air_Layer" title="Saharan Air Layer">Dry air</a> initially inhibited development,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> though once this abated the wave was able to consolidate into a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tropical_depression" title="Tropical depression">tropical depression</a> on July 4.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TCR_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-TCR-1">[1]</a></sup> The depression soon crossed <a href="/wiki/Grenada" title="Grenada">Grenada</a> before entering the <a href="/wiki/Caribbean_Sea" title="Caribbean Sea">Caribbean Sea</a> whereupon increasingly favorable environmental factors, such as low <a href="/wiki/Wind_shear" title="Wind shear">wind shear</a> and high <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sea_surface_temperatures" title="Sea surface temperatures">sea surface temperatures</a>, fueled intensification.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Turning west-northwest, the system achieved tropical storm status on July 5 and hurricane status the following day.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TCR_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-TCR-1">[1]</a></sup> Formation of a well-defined <a href="/wiki/Eye_(cyclone)" title="Eye (cyclone)">eye</a> and <a href="/wiki/Central_dense_overcast" title="Central dense overcast">central dense overcast</a> signaled Dennis's intensification into a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/SSHS#Category_3" title="SSHS">major hurricane</a> on July 7.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TCR_1-4"><a href="#cite_note-TCR-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Throughout this phase, the hurricane traversed the <a href="/wiki/Jamaica_Channel" title="Jamaica Channel">Jamaica Channel</a>, bringing deadly floods to both <a href="/wiki/Jamaica" title="Jamaica">Jamaica</a> and <a href="/wiki/Haiti" title="Haiti">Haiti</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TCR_1-5"><a href="#cite_note-TCR-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>The powerful storm soon struck <a href="/wiki/Granma_Province" title="Granma Province">Granma Province</a>, Cuba, as a Category 4 early on July 8; violent winds battered the province and caused extensive damage. Briefly weakening due to interaction with land, Dennis quickly regained its strength. Paralleling the western coast of Cuba, Dennis attained its peak winds of 150 mph (240 km/h) later that day before making a second <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Landfall_(meteorology)" title="Landfall (meteorology)">landfall</a> in the country, this time in <a href="/wiki/Matanzas_Province" title="Matanzas Province">Matanzas Province</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TCR_1-6"><a href="#cite_note-TCR-1">[1]</a></sup> Resumed interaction with the mountains of Cuba caused significant weakening;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> however, once Dennis emerged over the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_of_Mexico" title="Gulf of Mexico">Gulf of Mexico</a> on July 9, it was able to quickly reorganize. The hurricane reached Category 4 strength for a third time on July 10 as it approached <a href="/wiki/Florida" title="Florida">Florida</a>, attaining its lowest <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Barometric_pressure" title="Barometric pressure">barometric pressure</a> of 930 <a href="/wiki/Bar_(unit)" title="Bar (unit)">mbar</a> (<a href="/wiki/Pascal_(unit)" title="Pascal (unit)">hPa</a>; 27.46 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Inches_of_Mercury" title="Inches of Mercury">inHg</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TCR_1-7"><a href="#cite_note-TCR-1">[1]</a></sup> This ranked Dennis as the strongest pre-August hurricane in the Atlantic basin; however, this was eclipsed just six days later by <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Emily_(2005)" title="Hurricane Emily (2005)">Hurricane Emily</a> which was the first July Category 5 on record.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> Weakening ensued as the hurricane approached the <a href="/wiki/Florida_Panhandle" title="Florida Panhandle">Florida Panhandle</a>, ultimately making landfall over <a href="/wiki/Santa_Rosa_Island_(Florida)" title="Santa Rosa Island (Florida)">Santa Rosa Island</a> on July 10 as a Category 3. Dramatic weakening ensued once the cyclone moved ashore. Dennis lingered as a tropical depression and remnant low for roughly a week, traversing the <a href="/wiki/Mississippi_embayment" title="Mississippi embayment">Mississippi River Valley</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ohio_River" title="Ohio River">Ohio River Valley</a> before finally dissipating over <a href="/wiki/Ontario" title="Ontario">Ontario</a> on July 18.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TCR_1-8"><a href="#cite_note-TCR-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Preparations">Preparations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hurricane_Dennis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Preparations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Caribbean">Caribbean</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hurricane_Dennis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Caribbean">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Haiti" title="Haiti">Haiti</a> officials evacuated residents along the coastline, but noted that many were not obliging.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BBC-Haiti_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-BBC-Haiti-13">[13]</a></sup> In <a href="/wiki/Cuba" title="Cuba">Cuba</a> more than 600,000 residents were moved from their homes to government shelters or other locations in anticipation of Dennis.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MSNBC-Florida_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-MSNBC-Florida-14">[14]</a></sup> All schools were closed, and most flights in the country were suspended or cancelled.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Cayman_Islands" title="Cayman Islands">Cayman Islands</a> chapter of the Red Cross opened shelters on July 7 and placed 120 volunteers on standby. Schools and government offices closed for the duration of Dennis's passage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="United_States">United States</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hurricane_Dennis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: United States">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the United States, the governors of Florida, <a href="/wiki/Alabama" title="Alabama">Alabama</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mississippi" title="Mississippi">Mississippi</a> and <a href="/wiki/Louisiana" title="Louisiana">Louisiana</a> all declared <a href="/wiki/State_of_emergency" title="State of emergency">states of emergency</a> in their states. At 6 am CDT (2300 UTC) on July 9, 2005, all southbound lanes on <a href="/wiki/Interstate_65" title="Interstate 65">Interstate 65</a> from <a href="/wiki/Mobile,_Alabama" title="Mobile, Alabama">Mobile</a> to <a href="/wiki/Montgomery,_Alabama" title="Montgomery, Alabama">Montgomery</a>, Alabama, were closed. Traffic was redirected, making all four lanes northbound to allow evacuations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-preps_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-preps-17">[17]</a></sup> In Alabama residents in all parts of <a href="/wiki/Mobile_County,_Alabama" title="Mobile County, Alabama">Mobile County</a>, and those south of <a href="/wiki/Interstate_10_in_Alabama" title="Interstate 10 in Alabama">I-10</a> in <a href="/wiki/Baldwin_County,_Alabama" title="Baldwin County, Alabama">Baldwin County</a>, were ordered to evacuate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-as_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-as-18">[18]</a></sup> Similar orders were issued in Mississippi for parts of <a href="/wiki/Jackson_County,_Mississippi" title="Jackson County, Mississippi">Jackson</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hancock_County,_Mississippi" title="Hancock County, Mississippi">Hancock</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Harrison_County,_Mississippi" title="Harrison County, Mississippi">Harrison</a> counties; and for coastal areas in the <a href="/wiki/Florida_Panhandle" title="Florida Panhandle">Florida Panhandle</a> stretching from <a href="/wiki/Escambia_County,_Florida" title="Escambia County, Florida">Escambia County</a> to <a href="/wiki/Bay_County,_Florida" title="Bay County, Florida">Bay County</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-as_18-1"><a href="#cite_note-as-18">[18]</a></sup> Likewise, military installations such as <a href="/wiki/Naval_Air_Station_Pensacola" title="Naval Air Station Pensacola">NAS Pensacola</a>, <a href="/wiki/Naval_Air_Station_Whiting_Field" title="Naval Air Station Whiting Field">Whiting Field</a>, <a href="/wiki/Eglin_Air_Force_Base" title="Eglin Air Force Base">Eglin AFB</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hurlburt_Field" title="Hurlburt Field">Hurlburt Field</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tyndall_Air_Force_Base" title="Tyndall Air Force Base">Tyndall AFB</a> were all evacuated days before the storm. Additionally, Red Cross officials opened 87 shelters across the state which were able to hold about 14,000 evacuees.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-preps_17-1"><a href="#cite_note-preps-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>In Florida, about 50,000 tourists in the <a href="/wiki/Florida_Keys" title="Florida Keys">Keys</a> were forced to evacuate by July 8.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-keys_evacuation_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-keys_evacuation-19">[19]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/MacDill_Air_Force_Base" title="MacDill Air Force Base">MacDill Air Force Base</a> in <a href="/wiki/Tampa,_Florida" title="Tampa, Florida">Tampa</a> evacuated its aircraft to <a href="/wiki/McConnell_Air_Force_Base" title="McConnell Air Force Base">McConnell Air Force Base</a> near <a href="/wiki/Wichita,_Kansas" title="Wichita, Kansas">Wichita, Kansas</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-high_school_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-high_school-20">[20]</a></sup> 700,000 people in the Florida panhandle were evacuated in the days prior to Dennis, 100,000 of them in Escambia County alone.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-county_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-county-21">[21]</a></sup> As a result of the large evacuations, more than 200 truckloads provided about 1.8 million gallons of gasoline.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gasoline_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-gasoline-22">[22]</a></sup> The Red Cross also moved 60 mobile canteens capable of serving 30,000 hot meals each a day to the staging points of Hattiesburg and Jackson. National guardsmen were mobilized, and four emergency medical teams, each capable of setting up a small field hospital, were on standby. Also, at Eglin Air Force Base, about 20,000 military personnel were evacuated, and at Hurlburt Field, home to Air Force's 16th Special Operations Wing, a mandatory evacuation was ordered for all 15,000 airmen and their families.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-military_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-military-23">[23]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Caribbean_2">Caribbean</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hurricane_Dennis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Caribbean">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Effects_of_Hurricane_Dennis_in_Haiti" title="Effects of Hurricane Dennis in Haiti">Effects of Hurricane Dennis in Haiti</a> and <a href="/wiki/Effects_of_Hurricane_Dennis_in_Jamaica" title="Effects of Hurricane Dennis in Jamaica">Effects of Hurricane Dennis in Jamaica</a></div>
<p>Heavy rain from the outer bands of Dennis produced widespread flooding and landslides in Haiti. The resulting torrents killed at least 56 people, injured 36 others, and left 24 more missing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IFRCupdate3_24-1"><a href="#cite_note-IFRCupdate3-24">[24]</a></sup> At least nine of the fatalities occurred when a bridge collapsed in <a href="/wiki/Grand-Go%C3%A2ve" title="Grand-Goâve">Grand-Goâve</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> Extensive property damage was incurred with 929 homes destroyed and another 3,058 damaged, leaving 1,500 families homeless.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IFRCupdate3_24-2"><a href="#cite_note-IFRCupdate3-24">[24]</a></sup> Damage totaled US$50 million.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IDD_25-1"><a href="#cite_note-IDD-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<p>Dennis brought torrential rain to <a href="/wiki/Jamaica" title="Jamaica">Jamaica</a>, with accumulations peaking at 24.54 in (623 mm) in <a href="/wiki/Mavis_Bank" title="Mavis Bank">Mavis Bank</a>—a <a href="/wiki/Return_period" title="Return period">1-in-50 year event</a>. Widespread flash flooding ensued, damaging or destroying numerous homes and businesses. The overflow of multiple rivers prompted evacuations in several towns and left many stranded. <a href="/wiki/Saint_Thomas_Parish,_Jamaica" title="Saint Thomas Parish, Jamaica">Saint Thomas</a> and <a href="/wiki/Portland_Parish" title="Portland Parish">Portland</a> Parishes were hardest-hit. Overall, one person died there and damage exceeded J$2.128 billion (US$34.5 million).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TCR_1-12"><a href="#cite_note-TCR-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DenEmRpt_26-1"><a href="#cite_note-DenEmRpt-26">[26]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ODPEM_27-1"><a href="#cite_note-ODPEM-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<p>From there the storm moved to <a href="/wiki/Cuba" title="Cuba">Cuba</a>, leaving 16 people dead and $1.4 billion in damages as it roared through the island, flattening houses and downing trees and power lines. Heavy rainfall fell across the country, with amounts reaching up to 1092 mm/42.99 inches, making Dennis the wettest storm for the island since <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Flora" title="Hurricane Flora">Hurricane Flora</a> of 1963.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CUBARAIN_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-CUBARAIN-31">[31]</a></sup> According to reports from the Cuban government, 120,000 homes were damaged, 15,000 of which were destroyed. The citrus and vegetable industries were also devastated as Cuba's primary agricultural regions were the hardest hit. Nonetheless, <a href="/wiki/Fidel_Castro" title="Fidel Castro">Fidel Castro</a> publicly refused US aid after the storm in protest of the ongoing US trade embargo against Cuba, stating that, "If they offered $1 billion we would say no."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cuba-damages_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-Cuba-damages-32">[32]</a></sup> Relayed reports from Cuban meteorologists stated that a gust up to 149 mph (239 km/h) was detected at <a href="/wiki/Cienfuegos_Province" title="Cienfuegos Province">Cienfuegos</a>, 85% of the power lines were down, and extensive damage to the communications infrastructure had occurred. Dennis was more destructive than the previous year's <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Charley" title="Hurricane Charley">Hurricane Charley</a> and was widely regarded as the worst hurricane to strike Cuba since <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Flora" title="Hurricane Flora">Hurricane Flora</a> in the <a href="/wiki/1963_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="1963 Atlantic hurricane season">1963 season</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="United_States_2">United States</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hurricane_Dennis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: United States">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Effects_of_Hurricane_Dennis_in_Florida" title="Effects of Hurricane Dennis in Florida">Effects of Hurricane Dennis in Florida</a>, <a href="/wiki/Effects_of_Hurricane_Dennis_in_Alabama" title="Effects of Hurricane Dennis in Alabama">Effects of Hurricane Dennis in Alabama</a>, <a href="/wiki/Effects_of_Hurricane_Dennis_in_Georgia" title="Effects of Hurricane Dennis in Georgia">Effects of Hurricane Dennis in Georgia</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Effects_of_Hurricane_Dennis_in_Mississippi" title="Effects of Hurricane Dennis in Mississippi">Effects of Hurricane Dennis in Mississippi</a></div>
<p>In the United States, damage was not as high as originally expected, mainly because Dennis was more compact and moved more quickly than initially forecast. Dennis made landfall approximately 30 miles (48 km) to the east of where <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Ivan" title="Hurricane Ivan">Hurricane Ivan</a> had made landfall 10 months before, but did not cause as much damage as Ivan, due to its quicker speed, compact size, and because the area was not fully rebuilt. Dennis moved about 7 mph (11 km/h) faster than Ivan at landfall, and had hurricane-force winds that only extended 40 miles (65 km) from its center, compared to Ivan's 105 miles (170 km/h).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MSNBC-Florida_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-MSNBC-Florida-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BBC-landfall_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-BBC-landfall-33">[33]</a></sup> During the height of the storm, Dennis produced storm surges as high as 9 ft (3 m) in the <a href="/wiki/Apalachee_Bay" title="Apalachee Bay">Apalachee Bay</a> region, and as high as 7 ft (2 m) on the <a href="/wiki/Florida_Panhandle" title="Florida Panhandle">Florida Panhandle</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FEMA_1-year_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-FEMA_1-year-34">[34]</a></sup> and left 680,000 customers without electricity in four southern states.</p>
<p>In southern Florida, damage was mostly limited to moderate wind gusts; in <a href="/wiki/Miami-Dade_County,_Florida" title="Miami-Dade County, Florida">Miami-Dade County</a>, gusty winds knocked out several traffic lights along <a href="/wiki/U.S._Route_1_in_Florida" title="U.S. Route 1 in Florida">U.S. 1</a>, the only route to and from the <a href="/wiki/Florida_Keys" title="Florida Keys">Florida Keys</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-miami-dade_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-miami-dade-35">[35]</a></sup> A man died in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ft._Lauderdale,_Florida" title="Ft. Lauderdale, Florida">Ft. Lauderdale</a> when he stepped on a downed electrical wire and was electrocuted.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-electric_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-electric-36">[36]</a></sup> Damage was mostly minor and limited to outer rainbands and tornadoes in Central Florida. In the <a href="/wiki/Tampa_Bay" title="Tampa Bay">Tampa Bay</a> area, several tornadoes were reported to have touched down causing minor damage such as downed trees and power lines.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tampa_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-tampa-37">[37]</a></sup> The most severe damage occurred on the Florida Panhandle. At <a href="/wiki/Navarre_Beach,_Florida" title="Navarre Beach, Florida">Navarre Beach</a>, sustained winds of 98 miles per hour (158 km/h) were reported with a peak gust of 121 miles per hour (195 km/h), while a tower at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pensacola_Airport" title="Pensacola Airport">Pensacola Airport</a> reported sustained winds of 82 miles per hour (132 km/h) and a peak gust of 96 miles per hour (154 km/h).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TCR_1-13"><a href="#cite_note-TCR-1">[1]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Milton,_Florida" title="Milton, Florida">Milton</a> received 7.08 inches (180 mm) of rain, the highest reported rainfall total in Florida caused by Dennis.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rain_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-rain-38">[38]</a></sup> No significant damage was reported to most structures; however, insurers initially estimated that Dennis caused $3–$5 billion in insured damage,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dr-Gray_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-Dr-Gray-39">[39]</a></sup> or approximately $6–$10 billion total (insured damage estimates are generally held to be approximately one-half of total damages). However, the NHC's Tropical Cyclone Report reported total damage in the United States as only $2.23 billion with $1.115 billion of insured damage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TCR_1-14"><a href="#cite_note-TCR-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Alabama" title="Alabama">Alabama</a>, sustained winds reached minimal hurricane force in the interior of the state.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-chron_40-0"><a href="#cite_note-chron-40">[40]</a></sup> In total, 280,000 people in Alabama experienced power outages during the storm.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> No deaths occurred, although Dennis caused three injuries<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-disc9_42-0"><a href="#cite_note-disc9-42">[42]</a></sup> and total damage amounted to $127 million (2005 USD),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-disc9_42-1"><a href="#cite_note-disc9-42">[42]</a></sup> mostly due to structural damage. There was also severe damage to cotton crops.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ncdc2_43-0"><a href="#cite_note-ncdc2-43">[43]</a></sup> In <a href="/wiki/Mississippi" title="Mississippi">Mississippi</a>, damage was not as severe as previously anticipated.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> As Dennis impacted the state, a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Storm_tide" title="Storm tide">storm tide</a> of 2 ft (0.61 m)–4 ft (1.2 m) above normal was reported.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TCR_1-15"><a href="#cite_note-TCR-1">[1]</a></sup> Rainfall from the hurricane averaged between 1–5 inches (25–125 mm),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ncdc_46-0"><a href="#cite_note-ncdc-46">[46]</a></sup> and minimum barometric pressure of 994.2 mb was reported near <a href="/wiki/Pascagoula,_Mississippi" title="Pascagoula, Mississippi">Pascagoula</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TCR_1-16"><a href="#cite_note-TCR-1">[1]</a></sup> Wind gusts peaked at 59 mph (95 km/h) causing several hundred trees to uproot or snap, damaging a total of 21 homes and businesses.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ncdc_46-1"><a href="#cite_note-ncdc-46">[46]</a></sup></p>

<p>Dennis caused at least 10 tornadoes in the U.S., although only one of them reached F1 status on the <a href="/wiki/Fujita_scale" title="Fujita scale">Fujita scale</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TCR_1-17"><a href="#cite_note-TCR-1">[1]</a></sup> The storm dropped over 10 inches (250 mm) of rain in some areas of Alabama and Georgia (see the <a href="/wiki/File:Dennis2005rain.png" title="File:Dennis2005rain.png">rainfall graphic</a>). Parts of Georgia, which had received heavy rain just days earlier from <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Cindy_(2005)" title="Hurricane Cindy (2005)">Hurricane Cindy</a>, suffered heavy flooding, and flash-floods were reported on the outskirts of the <a href="/wiki/Atlanta" title="Atlanta">Atlanta</a> metropolitan area.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability"><span title="The material near this tag failed verification of its source citation(s). (February 2010)">not in citation given</span></a></i>]</sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dennis-SRH_47-0"><a href="#cite_note-Dennis-SRH-47">[47]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the United States, 15 storm-related deaths (14 in Florida) were reported, including one in <a href="/wiki/Walton_County,_Florida" title="Walton County, Florida">Walton County</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ledger_48-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ledger-48">[48]</a></sup> three in <a href="/wiki/Broward_County,_Florida" title="Broward County, Florida">Broward County</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TCR_1-18"><a href="#cite_note-TCR-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Miami-Herald_49-0"><a href="#cite_note-Miami-Herald-49">[49]</a></sup> three in <a href="/wiki/Charlotte_County,_Florida" title="Charlotte County, Florida">Charlotte County</a>, one each in <a href="/wiki/Nassau_County,_Florida" title="Nassau County, Florida">Nassau</a> and <a href="/wiki/Escambia_County,_Florida" title="Escambia County, Florida">Escambia</a> Counties<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Miami-Herald_49-1"><a href="#cite_note-Miami-Herald-49">[49]</a></sup> and one in <a href="/wiki/Decatur,_Georgia" title="Decatur, Georgia">Decatur, Georgia</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11-Alive_50-0"><a href="#cite_note-11-Alive-50">[50]</a></sup> In the Gulf of Mexico, the storm caused <i><a href="/wiki/Thunder_Horse_PDQ" title="Thunder Horse PDQ">Thunder Horse PDQ</a></i>, a <a href="/wiki/BP" title="BP">BP</a> <a href="/wiki/Oil_platform" title="Oil platform">oil rig</a> about 150 miles (240 km) southeast of New Orleans, Louisiana, to severely list.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Listing_51-0"><a href="#cite_note-Listing-51">[51]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Aftermath">Aftermath</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hurricane_Dennis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Aftermath">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/List_of_retired_Atlantic_hurricane_names" title="List of retired Atlantic hurricane names">List of retired Atlantic hurricane names</a></div>
<p>Because of the great amount of damage and deaths in the Caribbean and United States, the name <i>Dennis</i> was retired in the spring of 2006, and will never again be used for an Atlantic hurricane. It was replaced by <i>Don</i>, which was first used during the <a href="/wiki/2011_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="2011 Atlantic hurricane season">2011 hurricane season</a>. Dennis was one of five names to be retired in 2005—alongside <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Katrina" title="Hurricane Katrina">Katrina</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Rita" title="Hurricane Rita">Rita</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Stan" title="Hurricane Stan">Stan</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Wilma" title="Hurricane Wilma">Wilma</a>; this is the greatest such number since the implementation of retirement in 1955.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup></p>
