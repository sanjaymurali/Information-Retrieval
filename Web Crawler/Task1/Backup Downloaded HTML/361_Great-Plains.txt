<p>The <b>Great Plains</b> (sometimes simply "the Plains") is the broad expanse of flat land (a <a href="/wiki/Plain" title="Plain">plain</a>), much of it covered in <a href="/wiki/Prairie" title="Prairie">prairie</a>, <a href="/wiki/Steppe" title="Steppe">steppe</a> and <a href="/wiki/Grassland" title="Grassland">grassland</a>, that lies west of the <a href="/wiki/Mississippi_River" title="Mississippi River">Mississippi River</a> <a href="/wiki/Tallgrass_prairie" title="Tallgrass prairie">tallgrass prairie</a> states and east of the <a href="/wiki/Rocky_Mountains" title="Rocky Mountains">Rocky Mountains</a> in the United States and Canada. It embraces</p>
<ul>
<li>the entirety of the states of <a href="/wiki/Kansas" title="Kansas">Kansas</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nebraska" title="Nebraska">Nebraska</a>, <a href="/wiki/South_Dakota" title="South Dakota">South Dakota</a> and <a href="/wiki/North_Dakota" title="North Dakota">North Dakota</a>,</li>
<li>parts of the states of <a href="/wiki/Colorado" title="Colorado">Colorado</a>, <a href="/wiki/Montana" title="Montana">Montana</a>, <a href="/wiki/New_Mexico" title="New Mexico">New Mexico</a>, <a href="/wiki/Oklahoma" title="Oklahoma">Oklahoma</a>, <a href="/wiki/Texas" title="Texas">Texas</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wyoming" title="Wyoming">Wyoming</a></li>
</ul>
<p>and reaches into</p>
<ul>
<li>the southern portions of the <a href="/wiki/Provinces_and_territories_of_Canada" title="Provinces and territories of Canada">Canadian provinces</a> of <a href="/wiki/Alberta" title="Alberta">Alberta</a>, <a href="/wiki/Manitoba" title="Manitoba">Manitoba</a> and <a href="/wiki/Saskatchewan" title="Saskatchewan">Saskatchewan</a>.</li>
</ul>
<p>The region is known for supporting extensive cattle <a href="/wiki/Ranch" title="Ranch">ranching</a> and <a href="/wiki/Dryland_farming" title="Dryland farming">dry farming.</a></p>
<p>The Canadian portion of the Plains is known as the <a href="/wiki/Canadian_Prairies" title="Canadian Prairies">Prairies</a>. It covers much of Alberta and southern Saskatchewan, and a narrow band of southern Manitoba. Despite covering a relatively small geographic area, the Prairies are nevertheless home to the majority of each of the three provinces' respective populations.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Usage">Usage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Great_Plains&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Usage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The term "Great Plains" is used in the United States to describe a sub-section of the even more vast <a href="/wiki/Interior_Plains" title="Interior Plains">Interior Plains</a> physiographic division, which covers much of the interior of North America. It also has currency as a region of human geography, referring to the <a href="/wiki/Plains_Indians" title="Plains Indians">Plains Indians</a> or the Plains States.</p>
<p>In Canada the term is little used; <a href="/wiki/Natural_Resources_Canada" title="Natural Resources Canada">Natural Resources Canada</a>, the government department responsible for official mapping and equivalent to the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Geological_Survey" title="United States Geological Survey">United States Geological Survey</a>, treats the Interior Plains as one unit consisting of several related plateaux and plains. There is no region referred to as the "Great Plains" in <i><a href="/wiki/Atlas_of_Canada" title="Atlas of Canada">The Atlas of Canada</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> In terms of human geography, the term <i>prairie</i> is more commonly used in Canada, and the region is known as the <a href="/wiki/Canadian_Prairies" title="Canadian Prairies">Prairie Provinces</a> or simply "the Prairies."</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/North_American_Environmental_Atlas" title="North American Environmental Atlas">North American Environmental Atlas</a>, produced by the <a href="/wiki/Commission_for_Environmental_Cooperation" title="Commission for Environmental Cooperation">Commission for Environmental Cooperation</a>, a NAFTA agency composed of the geographical agencies of the Mexican, American, and Canadian governments, uses the "Great Plains" as an <a href="/wiki/Ecoregion" title="Ecoregion">ecoregion</a> synonymous with predominant prairies and grasslands rather than as physiographic region defined by topography.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> The Great Plains ecoregion includes five sub-regions: Temperate Prairies, West-Central Semi-Arid Prairies, South-Central Semi-Arid Prairies, Texas Louisiana Coastal Plains, and Tamaulipus-Texas Semi-Arid Plain, which overlap or expand upon other Great Plains designations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>

<p>The region is about 500 mi (800 km) east to west and 2,000 mi (3,200 km) north to south. Much of the region was home to <a href="/wiki/American_bison" title="American bison">American bison</a> herds until they were hunted to near extinction during the mid/late 19th century. It has an area of approximately 500,000 sq mi (1,300,000 km<sup>2</sup>). Current thinking regarding the geographic boundaries of the Great Plains is shown by this <a class="external text" href="http://www.unl.edu/plains/about/map.shtml" rel="nofollow">map</a> at the Center for Great Plains Studies, <a href="/wiki/University_of_Nebraska%E2%80%93Lincoln" title="University of Nebraska–Lincoln">University of Nebraska–Lincoln</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Encyclopedia_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Encyclopedia-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>The term "Great Plains", for the region west of about the <a href="/wiki/96th_meridian_west" title="96th meridian west">96th</a> or <a href="/wiki/98th_meridian_west" title="98th meridian west">98th meridian</a> and east of the Rocky Mountains, was not generally used before the early 20th century. Nevin Fenneman's 1916 study, <i>Physiographic Subdivision of the United States</i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> brought the term Great Plains into more widespread usage. Before that the region was almost invariably called the High Plains, in contrast to the lower Prairie Plains of the <a href="/wiki/Midwestern_United_States" title="Midwestern United States">Midwestern states</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Today the term "<a href="/wiki/High_Plains_(United_States)" title="High Plains (United States)">High Plains</a>" is used for a subregion of the Great Plains.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geography">Geography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Great_Plains&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Geography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Great Plains are the westernmost portion of the vast <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North American</a> <a href="/wiki/Interior_Plains" title="Interior Plains">Interior Plains</a>, which extend east to the <a href="/wiki/Appalachian_Plateau" title="Appalachian Plateau">Appalachian Plateau</a>. The <a href="/wiki/United_States_Geological_Survey" title="United States Geological Survey">United States Geological Survey</a> divides the Great Plains in the United States into ten physiographic subdivisions:</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Coteau_du_Missouri" title="Coteau du Missouri">Coteau du Missouri</a> or Missouri Plateau, glaciated – east-central South Dakota, northern and eastern North Dakota and northeastern Montana;</li>
<li>Coteau du Missouri, unglaciated – western South Dakota, northeastern Wyoming, southwestern North Dakota and southeastern Montana;</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Black_Hills" title="Black Hills">Black Hills</a> – western South Dakota;</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/High_Plains_(United_States)" title="High Plains (United States)">High Plains</a> – southeastern Wyoming, southwestern South Dakota, western Nebraska (including the <a href="/wiki/Sandhills_(Nebraska)" title="Sandhills (Nebraska)">Sand Hills</a>), eastern Colorado, western Kansas, western Oklahoma, <a href="/wiki/Eastern_New_Mexico" title="Eastern New Mexico">eastern New Mexico</a>, and northwestern Texas (including the <a href="/wiki/Llano_Estacado" title="Llano Estacado">Llano Estacado</a> and <a href="/wiki/Texas_Panhandle" title="Texas Panhandle">Texas Panhandle</a>);</li>
<li>Plains Border – central Kansas and northern Oklahoma (including the <a href="/wiki/Flint_Hills" title="Flint Hills">Flint</a>, <a href="/wiki/Red_Hills_(Kansas)" title="Red Hills (Kansas)">Red</a> and <a href="/wiki/Smoky_Hills" title="Smoky Hills">Smoky Hills</a>);</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Colorado_Piedmont" title="Colorado Piedmont">Colorado Piedmont</a> – eastern Colorado;</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Raton,_New_Mexico" title="Raton, New Mexico">Raton</a> section – northeastern New Mexico;</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Pecos_River" title="Pecos River">Pecos Valley</a> – eastern New Mexico;</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Edwards_Plateau" title="Edwards Plateau">Edwards Plateau</a> – south-central Texas; and</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Central_Texas" title="Central Texas">Central Texas</a> section – central Texas.</li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Paleontology">Paleontology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Great_Plains&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Paleontology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>During the <a href="/wiki/Cretaceous" title="Cretaceous">Cretaceous</a> Period (145–66 million years ago), the Great Plains were covered by a shallow <a href="/wiki/Inland_sea_(geology)" title="Inland sea (geology)">inland sea</a> called the <a href="/wiki/Western_Interior_Seaway" title="Western Interior Seaway">Western Interior Seaway</a>. However, during the <a href="/wiki/Late_Cretaceous" title="Late Cretaceous">Late Cretaceous</a> to the <a href="/wiki/Paleocene" title="Paleocene">Paleocene</a> (65–55 million years ago), the seaway had begun to recede, leaving behind thick marine deposits and a relatively flat terrain which the seaway had once occupied.</p>
<p>During the Cenozoic era, specifically about 25 million years ago during the <a href="/wiki/Miocene" title="Miocene">Miocene</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pliocene" title="Pliocene">Pliocene</a> epochs, the continental climate became favorable to the evolution of grasslands. Existing forest biomes declined and grasslands became much more widespread. The grasslands provided a new <a href="/wiki/Ecological_niche" title="Ecological niche">niche</a> for mammals, including many <a href="/wiki/Ungulate" title="Ungulate">ungulates</a> and <a href="/wiki/Glires" title="Glires">glires</a>, that switched from browsing diets to grazing diets. Traditionally, the spread of grasslands and the development of grazers have been strongly linked. However, an examination of mammalian teeth suggests that it is the open, gritty habitat and not the grass itself which is linked to diet changes in mammals, giving rise to the "grit, not grass" hypothesis.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Paleontology" title="Paleontology">Paleontological</a> finds in the area have yielded bones of <a href="/wiki/Mammoth" title="Mammoth">mammoths</a>, <a href="/wiki/Smilodon" title="Smilodon">saber-toothed cats</a> and other ancient animals,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> as well as dozens of other <a href="/wiki/Megafauna" title="Megafauna">megafauna</a> (large animals over 100 lb [45 kg]) – such as <a href="/wiki/Ground_sloth" title="Ground sloth">giant sloths</a>, <a href="/wiki/Evolution_of_the_horse" title="Evolution of the horse">horses</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mastodon" title="Mastodon">mastodons</a>, and <a href="/wiki/American_lion" title="American lion">American lion</a> – that dominated the area of the ancient Great Plains for thousands to millions of years. The vast majority of these animals became extinct in North America at the end of the <a href="/wiki/Pleistocene" title="Pleistocene">Pleistocene</a> (around 13,000 years ago).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Climate">Climate</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Great_Plains&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Climate">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>In general, the Great Plains have a wide variety of weather through the year, with very cold and harsh winters and very hot and humid summers. Wind speeds are often very high, especially in winter. Grasslands are among the least protected biomes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Humans have converted much of the prairies for agricultural purposes or to create pastures. The Great Plains have dust storms mostly every year or so.</p>
<p>The 100th meridian roughly corresponds with the line that divides the Great Plains into an area that receive 20 in (510 mm) or more of rainfall per year and an area that receives less than 20 in (510 mm). In this context, the High Plains, as well as <a href="/wiki/Southern_Alberta" title="Southern Alberta">Southern Alberta</a>, south-western Saskatchewan and <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Montana" title="Eastern Montana">Eastern Montana</a> are mainly <a href="/wiki/Semi-arid_climate" title="Semi-arid climate">semi arid</a> steppe land and are generally characterised by <a href="/wiki/Ranch" title="Ranch">rangeland</a> or marginal <a href="/wiki/Agriculture" title="Agriculture">farmland</a>. The region (especially the High Plains) is periodically subjected to extended periods of <a href="/wiki/Drought" title="Drought">drought</a>; high winds in the region may then generate devastating <a href="/wiki/Dust_storm" title="Dust storm">dust storms</a>. The eastern Great Plains near the eastern boundary falls in the <a href="/wiki/Humid_subtropical_climate" title="Humid subtropical climate">humid subtropical climate</a> zone in the southern areas, and the northern and central areas fall in the <a href="/wiki/Humid_continental_climate" title="Humid continental climate">humid continental climate</a>.</p>
<p>Many <a href="/wiki/Thunderstorm" title="Thunderstorm">thunderstorms</a> occur in the plains in the spring through summer. The southeastern portion of the Great Plains is the most <a href="/wiki/Tornado" title="Tornado">tornado</a> active area in the world and is sometimes referred to as <a href="/wiki/Tornado_Alley" title="Tornado Alley">Tornado Alley</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Flora">Flora</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Great_Plains&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Flora">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Great Plains are part of the floristic <a href="/wiki/North_American_Prairies_Province" title="North American Prairies Province">North American Prairies Province</a>, which extends from the Rocky Mountains to the <a href="/wiki/Appalachian_Mountains" title="Appalachian Mountains">Appalachians</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Great_Plains&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Original_American_contact">Original American contact</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Great_Plains&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Original American contact">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>The first Americans (<a href="/wiki/Paleo-Indians" title="Paleo-Indians">Paleo-Indians</a>) who arrived to the Great Plains were successive <a href="/wiki/Indigenous_peoples_of_the_Americas" title="Indigenous peoples of the Americas">indigenous</a> cultures who are known to have inhabited the Great Plains for thousands of years, over 15,000 years ago.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> Humans entered the North American continent in waves of migration, mostly over <a href="/wiki/Beringia" title="Beringia">Beringia</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Bering_Strait" title="Bering Strait">Bering Straits</a> <a href="/wiki/Land_bridge" title="Land bridge">land bridge</a>.</p>
<p>Historically the Great Plains were the range of the bison and of the culture of the <a href="/wiki/Plains_Indians" title="Plains Indians">Plains Indians</a>, whose tribes included the <a href="/wiki/Blackfoot_Confederacy" title="Blackfoot Confederacy">Blackfoot</a>, <a href="/wiki/Crow_Nation" title="Crow Nation">Crow</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sioux" title="Sioux">Sioux</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cheyenne" title="Cheyenne">Cheyenne</a>, <a href="/wiki/Arapaho" title="Arapaho">Arapaho</a>, <a href="/wiki/Comanche" title="Comanche">Comanche</a>, and others. Eastern portions of the Great Plains were inhabited by tribes who lived in semi-permanent villages of <a href="/wiki/Earth_house" title="Earth house">earth lodges</a>, such as the <a href="/wiki/Arikara" title="Arikara">Arikara</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mandan" title="Mandan">Mandan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pawnee_people" title="Pawnee people">Pawnee</a> and <a href="/wiki/Wichita_people" title="Wichita people">Wichita</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="European_contact">European contact</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Great_Plains&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: European contact">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">

<p>With the arrival of <a href="/wiki/Francisco_V%C3%A1zquez_de_Coronado" title="Francisco Vázquez de Coronado">Francisco Vázquez de Coronado</a>, a Spanish <a href="/wiki/Conquistador" title="Conquistador">conquistador</a>, the first recorded history of encounter between Europeans and Native Americans in the Great Plains occurred in Texas, Kansas and Nebraska from 1540 to 1542. In that same time period, <a href="/wiki/Hernando_de_Soto" title="Hernando de Soto">Hernando de Soto</a> crossed a west-northwest direction in what is now Oklahoma and Texas. Today this is known as the De Soto Trail. The Spanish thought the Great Plains were the location of the mythological <i><a href="/wiki/Quivira" title="Quivira">Quivira</a> and <a href="/wiki/Zuni-Cibola_Complex" title="Zuni-Cibola Complex">Cíbola</a></i>, a place said to be rich in gold.</p>
<p>Over the next one hundred years, founding of the <a href="/wiki/Fur_trade" title="Fur trade">fur trade</a> brought thousands of ethnic Europeans into the Great Plains. Fur trappers from France, Spain, Britain, Russia and the young United States made their way across much of the region, making regular contacts with Native Americans. After the United States acquired the Louisiana Purchase in 1803 and conducted the <a href="/wiki/Lewis_and_Clark_Expedition" title="Lewis and Clark Expedition">Lewis and Clark Expedition</a> in 1804–1806, more information about the Plains became available and various pioneers entered the areas.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Manuel_Lisa" title="Manuel Lisa">Manuel Lisa</a>, based in <a href="/wiki/St._Louis" title="St. Louis">St. Louis</a>, established a major fur trading site at his <a href="/wiki/Fort_Lisa_(Nebraska)" title="Fort Lisa (Nebraska)">Fort Lisa</a> on the <a href="/wiki/Missouri_River" title="Missouri River">Missouri River</a> in Nebraska. Fur trading posts were often the basis of later settlements. Through the 19th century, more European Americans and Europeans migrated to the Great Plains as part of a vast <a href="/wiki/United_States_territorial_acquisitions" title="United States territorial acquisitions">westward expansion</a> of population. New settlements became dotted across the Great Plains.</p>
<p>The new immigrants also brought diseases against which the Native Americans had no resistance. Between a half and two-thirds of the Plains Indians are thought to have died of <a href="/wiki/Smallpox" title="Smallpox">smallpox</a> by the time of the 1803 <a href="/wiki/Louisiana_Purchase" title="Louisiana Purchase">Louisiana Purchase</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Pioneer_settlement">Pioneer settlement</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Great_Plains&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Pioneer settlement">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>After 1870, the new <a href="/wiki/History_of_rail_transport_in_the_United_States" title="History of rail transport in the United States">railroads across the Plains</a> brought hunters who killed off almost all the bison for their hides. The railroads offered attractive packages of land and transportation to European farmers, who rushed to settle the land. They (and Americans as well) also took advantage of the homestead laws to obtain free farms. Land speculators and local boosters identified many potential towns, and those reached by the railroad had a chance, while the others became ghost towns. In Kansas, for example, nearly 5000 towns were mapped out, but by 1970 only 617 were actually operating. In the mid-20th century, closeness to an interstate exchange determined whether a town would flourish or struggle for business.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>Much of the Great Plains became <a href="/wiki/Open_range" title="Open range">open range</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Rangeland" title="Rangeland">rangeland</a> where <a href="/wiki/Cattle" title="Cattle">cattle</a> roamed free, hosting ranching operations where anyone was theoretically free to run cattle. In the spring and fall, ranchers held roundups where their cowboys branded new calves, treated animals and sorted the cattle for sale. Such ranching began in Texas and gradually moved northward. Between 1866 and 1895, cowboys herded 10 million cattle north to rail heads such as <a href="/wiki/Dodge_City,_Kansas" title="Dodge City, Kansas">Dodge City, Kansas</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Ogallala,_Nebraska" title="Ogallala, Nebraska">Ogallala, Nebraska</a>; from there, cattle were shipped eastward.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Cattle_drives_in_the_United_States" title="Cattle drives in the United States">Cattle drives in the United States</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Cowboy1902.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1248" data-file-width="1229" height="223" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c4/Cowboy1902.jpg/220px-Cowboy1902.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c4/Cowboy1902.jpg/330px-Cowboy1902.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c4/Cowboy1902.jpg/440px-Cowboy1902.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Cowboy1902.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Cattle herd and cowboy, circa 1902</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Many foreign investors, especially British, financed the great ranches of the era. Overstocking of the range and the terrible winter of 1886 resulted in a disaster, with many cattle starved and frozen to death. <a href="/wiki/Theodore_Roosevelt" title="Theodore Roosevelt">Theodore Roosevelt</a>, a rancher in the Dakotas, lost his entire investment; he returned east to reenter politics. From then on, ranchers generally raised feed to ensure they could keep their cattle alive over winter.</p>
<p>To allow for agricultural development of the Great Plains and house a growing population, the US passed the <a href="/wiki/Homestead_Acts" title="Homestead Acts">Homestead Acts</a> of 1862: it allowed a settler to claim up to 160 acres (65 ha) of land, provided that he lived on it for a period of five years and cultivated it. The provisions were expanded under the <a href="/wiki/Kinkaid_Act" title="Kinkaid Act">Kinkaid Act</a> of 1904 to include a homestead of an entire <a href="/wiki/Section_(United_States_land_surveying)" title="Section (United States land surveying)">section</a>. Hundreds of thousands of people claimed such homesteads, sometimes building <a href="/wiki/Sod_house" title="Sod house">sod houses</a> out of the very <a href="/wiki/Sod" title="Sod">turf</a> of their land. Many of them were not skilled <a href="/wiki/Dryland_farming" title="Dryland farming">dryland farmers</a> and failures were frequent. Much of the Plains were settled during relatively wet years. Government experts did not understand how farmers should cultivate the prairies and gave advice counter to what would have worked<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>. <a href="/wiki/History_of_Germans_in_Russia,_Ukraine_and_the_Soviet_Union" title="History of Germans in Russia, Ukraine and the Soviet Union">Germans from Russia</a> who had previously farmed, under similar circumstances, in what is now <a href="/wiki/Ukraine" title="Ukraine">Ukraine</a> were marginally more successful than other homesteaders. The <a href="/wiki/Dominion_Lands_Act" title="Dominion Lands Act">Dominion Lands Act</a> of 1871 served a similar function for establishing homesteads on the prairies in Canada.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Social_life">Social life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Great_Plains&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Social life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Grange1873.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="466" data-file-width="406" height="253" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e4/Grange1873.jpg/220px-Grange1873.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e4/Grange1873.jpg/330px-Grange1873.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/e/e4/Grange1873.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Grange1873.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Grange in session, 1873</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The railroads opened up the Great Plains for settlement, for now it was possible to ship wheat and other crops at low cost to the urban markets in the East, and Europe. Homestead land was free for American settlers. Railroads sold their land at cheap rates to immigrants in expectation they would generate traffic as soon as farms were established. Immigrants poured in, especially from Germany and Scandinavia. On the plains, very few single men attempted to operate a farm or ranch by themselves; they clearly understood the need for a hard-working wife, and numerous children, to handle the many chores, including child-rearing, feeding and clothing the family, managing the housework, feeding the hired hands, and, especially after the 1930s, handling paperwork and financial details.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> During the early years of settlement, farm women played an integral role in assuring family survival by working outdoors. After approximately one generation, women increasingly left the fields, thus redefining their roles within the family. New technology including sewing and washing machines encouraged women to turn to domestic roles. The scientific housekeeping movement, promoted across the land by the media and government extension agents, as well as county fairs which featured achievements in home cookery and canning, advice columns for women regarding farm bookkeeping, and home economics courses in the schools.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>Although the eastern image of farm life in the prairies emphasized the isolation of the lonely farmer and wife, plains residents created busy social lives for themselves. They often sponsored activities that combined work, food and entertainment such as <a href="/wiki/Barn_raising" title="Barn raising">barn raisings</a>, corn huskings, quilting bees,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> Grange meetings, church activities and school functions. Women organized shared meals and potluck events, as well as extended visits between families.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_National_Grange_of_the_Order_of_Patrons_of_Husbandry" title="The National Grange of the Order of Patrons of Husbandry">Grange</a> was a nationwide farmers' organization, they reserved high offices for women, and gave them a voice in public affairs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="After_19th_century">After 19th century</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Great_Plains&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: After 19th century">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:322px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:High_plains_fresh_groundwater_usage_2000.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="946" data-file-width="747" height="405" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a6/High_plains_fresh_groundwater_usage_2000.svg/320px-High_plains_fresh_groundwater_usage_2000.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a6/High_plains_fresh_groundwater_usage_2000.svg/480px-High_plains_fresh_groundwater_usage_2000.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a6/High_plains_fresh_groundwater_usage_2000.svg/640px-High_plains_fresh_groundwater_usage_2000.svg.png 2x" width="320"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:High_plains_fresh_groundwater_usage_2000.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Withdrawal rates from the <a href="/wiki/Ogallala_Aquifer" title="Ogallala Aquifer">Ogallala Aquifer</a>.</div>

<p>The region roughly centered on the <a href="/wiki/Oklahoma_Panhandle" title="Oklahoma Panhandle">Oklahoma Panhandle</a>, including southeastern Colorado, southwestern Kansas, the <a href="/wiki/Texas_Panhandle" title="Texas Panhandle">Texas Panhandle</a>, and extreme northeastern New Mexico was known as the <a href="/wiki/Dust_Bowl" title="Dust Bowl">Dust Bowl</a> during the late 1920s and early 1930s. The effect of an extended drought, inappropriate cultivation, and financial crises of the <a href="/wiki/Great_Depression" title="Great Depression">Great Depression</a>, forced many farmers off the land throughout the Great Plains.</p>
<p>From the 1950s on, many areas of the Great Plains have become productive crop-growing areas because of extensive <a href="/wiki/Irrigation" title="Irrigation">irrigation</a> on large landholdings. The United States is a major exporter of agricultural products. The southern portion of the Great Plains lies over the <a href="/wiki/Ogallala_Aquifer" title="Ogallala Aquifer">Ogallala Aquifer</a>, a huge underground layer of water-bearing strata dating from the <a href="/wiki/Quaternary_glaciation" title="Quaternary glaciation">last ice age</a>. <a href="/wiki/Center_pivot_irrigation" title="Center pivot irrigation">Center pivot irrigation</a> is used extensively in drier sections of the Great Plains, resulting in <a href="/wiki/Aquifer" title="Aquifer">aquifer</a> depletion at a rate that is greater than the ground's ability to recharge.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Population_decline">Population decline</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Great_Plains&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Population decline">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Depopulation_of_the_Great_Plains" title="Depopulation of the Great Plains">Depopulation of the Great Plains</a></div>
<p>The rural Plains have lost a third of their population since 1920. Several hundred thousand square miles of the Great Plains have fewer than 6 inhabitants per square mile (2.3 inhabitants per square kilometer)—the density standard <a href="/wiki/Frederick_Jackson_Turner" title="Frederick Jackson Turner">Frederick Jackson Turner</a> used to declare the <a href="/wiki/Frontier" title="Frontier">American frontier</a> "closed" in 1893. Many have fewer than 2 inhabitants per square mile (0.77 inhabitants per square kilometer). There are more than 6,000 <a href="/wiki/Ghost_town" title="Ghost town">ghost towns</a> in the state of Kansas alone, according to Kansas historian <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Daniel_Fitzgerald_(writer)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Daniel Fitzgerald (writer) (page does not exist)">Daniel Fitzgerald</a>. This problem is often exacerbated by the consolidation of farms and the difficulty of attracting modern industry to the region. In addition, the smaller school-age population has forced the consolidation of school districts and the closure of high schools in some communities. The continuing population loss has led some to suggest that the current use of the drier parts of the Great Plains is not sustainable,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> and there has been a proposal – the "<a href="/wiki/Buffalo_Commons" title="Buffalo Commons">Buffalo Commons</a>" – to return approximately 139,000 sq mi (360,000 km<sup>2</sup>) of these drier parts to native prairie land.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Wind_power">Wind power</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Great_Plains&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Wind power">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:GreenMountainWindFarm_Fluvanna_2004.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1360" data-file-width="2048" height="146" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8b/GreenMountainWindFarm_Fluvanna_2004.jpg/220px-GreenMountainWindFarm_Fluvanna_2004.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8b/GreenMountainWindFarm_Fluvanna_2004.jpg/330px-GreenMountainWindFarm_Fluvanna_2004.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8b/GreenMountainWindFarm_Fluvanna_2004.jpg/440px-GreenMountainWindFarm_Fluvanna_2004.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:GreenMountainWindFarm_Fluvanna_2004.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/Wind_farm" title="Wind farm">Wind farm</a> in the plains of <a href="/wiki/West_Texas" title="West Texas">West Texas</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The Great Plains contribute substantially to <a href="/wiki/Wind_power_in_the_United_States" title="Wind power in the United States">wind power in the United States</a>. In July 2008, oilman turned wind-farm developer <a href="/wiki/T._Boone_Pickens" title="T. Boone Pickens">T. Boone Pickens</a> called for the U.S. to invest <a href="/wiki/United_States_dollar" title="United States dollar">$</a>1 trillion to build an additional 200,000 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Megawatt" title="Megawatt">MW</a> of <a href="/wiki/Wind_power" title="Wind power">wind power</a> <a href="/wiki/Intermittent_energy_source#Terminology" title="Intermittent energy source">nameplate capacity</a> in the Plains, as part of his <a href="/wiki/Pickens_Plan" title="Pickens Plan">Pickens Plan</a>. Pickens cited <a href="/wiki/Sweetwater,_Texas" title="Sweetwater, Texas">Sweetwater, Texas</a> as an example of economic revitalization driven by wind power development.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-oilman_wind_power_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-oilman_wind_power-26">[26]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-oil_addiction_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-oil_addiction-27">[27]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pickens_order_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-pickens_order-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<p>Sweetwater was a struggling town typical of the Plains, steadily losing businesses and population, until <a href="/wiki/Wind_turbine" title="Wind turbine">wind turbines</a> came to the surrounding <a href="/wiki/Nolan_County,_Texas" title="Nolan County, Texas">Nolan County</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-block_2008_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-block_2008-29">[29]</a></sup> Wind power brought jobs to local residents, along with royalty payments to landowners who leased sites for turbines, reversing the town's <a href="/wiki/Population_decline" title="Population decline">population decline</a>. Pickens claims the same economic benefits are possible throughout the Plains, which he refers to as North America's "wind corridor."</p>
