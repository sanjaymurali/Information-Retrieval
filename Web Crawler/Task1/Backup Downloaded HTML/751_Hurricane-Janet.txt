<p><b>Hurricane Janet</b> was the most powerful <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone" title="Tropical cyclone">tropical cyclone</a> of the <a href="/wiki/1955_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="1955 Atlantic hurricane season">1955 Atlantic hurricane season</a> and one of the strongest <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/North_Atlantic_tropical_cyclone" title="North Atlantic tropical cyclone">Atlantic hurricanes</a> on record. The eleventh tropical storm, ninth hurricane, and fifth <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Category_3_hurricane" title="Category 3 hurricane">major hurricane</a> of the year,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[nb 1]</a></sup> Janet formed from a <a href="/wiki/Tropical_wave" title="Tropical wave">tropical wave</a> east of the <a href="/wiki/Lesser_Antilles" title="Lesser Antilles">Lesser Antilles</a> on September 21. Moving westward across the <a href="/wiki/Caribbean_Sea" title="Caribbean Sea">Caribbean Sea</a>, Janet fluctuated in intensity, but generally strengthened before reaching its peak intensity as a <a href="/wiki/List_of_Category_5_Atlantic_hurricanes" title="List of Category 5 Atlantic hurricanes">Category 5 hurricane</a> with winds of 175 mph (280 km/h). The intense hurricane later made <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Landfall_(meteorology)" title="Landfall (meteorology)">landfall</a> at that intensity near <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chetumal,_Mexico" title="Chetumal, Mexico">Chetumal, Mexico</a> on September 28. After weakening over the <a href="/wiki/Yucat%C3%A1n_Peninsula" title="Yucatán Peninsula">Yucatán Peninsula</a>, it moved into the <a href="/wiki/Bay_of_Campeche" title="Bay of Campeche">Bay of Campeche</a>, where it slightly strengthened before making its final landfall near <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Veracruz,_Veracruz" title="Veracruz, Veracruz">Veracruz</a> on September 29. Janet quickly weakened over Mexico's mountainous terrain before dissipating on September 30.</p>
<p>In its developmental stages, Janet caused $7.8 million in damage to the Lesser Antilles and 189 deaths in the <a href="/wiki/Grenadines" title="Grenadines">Grenadines</a> and <a href="/wiki/Barbados" title="Barbados">Barbados</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[nb 2]</a></sup> While Janet was in the central Caribbean Sea, a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hurricane_Hunters" title="Hurricane Hunters">reconnaissance aircraft</a> flew into the storm and was lost, with all eleven crew members believed to be perished. This remains the only such loss which has occurred in association with an Atlantic hurricane. A Category 5 upon landfall on the Yucatán Peninsula, Janet caused severe devastation in areas of <a href="/wiki/Quintana_Roo" title="Quintana Roo">Quintana Roo</a> and <a href="/wiki/British_Honduras" title="British Honduras">British Honduras</a>. Only five buildings in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chetumal,_Mexico" title="Chetumal, Mexico">Chetumal, Mexico</a> remained intact after the storm, and an estimated 500 deaths occurred in the <a href="/wiki/Administrative_divisions_of_Mexico" title="Administrative divisions of Mexico">Mexican state</a> of Quintana Roo. At Janet's second landfall near Veracruz, significant river <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Flooding" title="Flooding">flooding</a> ensued, worsening effects caused by Hurricanes <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hurricane_Gladys_(1955)" title="Hurricane Gladys (1955)">Gladys</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Hilda_(1955)" title="Hurricane Hilda (1955)">Hilda</a> earlier in the month. The floods left thousands of people stranded and killed at least 326 people in the Tampico area, leading to the largest Mexican relief operation ever executed by the United States.</p>
<p>Janet's landfall as a Category 5 hurricane on the Yucatán Peninsula was the first recorded instance that a storm of such intensity in the Atlantic made landfall on a continental <a href="/wiki/Mainland" title="Mainland">mainland</a>; prior to Janet, landfalls of Category 5 intensity were only known to have taken place on islands.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Monsters_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Monsters-4">[2]</a></sup> Janet's minimum <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Barometric_pressure" title="Barometric pressure">barometric pressure</a>, recorded in Chetumal, was at the time the second lowest recorded pressure on land associated with a tropical cyclone in the Atlantic, behind the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/1935_Labor_Day_Hurricane" title="1935 Labor Day Hurricane">1935 Labor Day Hurricane</a>. At least 1,023 deaths were attributed to Hurricane Janet, as well as $65.8 million in damages. The name Janet was later <a href="/wiki/List_of_retired_Atlantic_hurricane_names" title="List of retired Atlantic hurricane names">retired</a> from the Atlantic list of tropical cyclone names due to its significance to the scientific research community.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Meteorological_history">Meteorological history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hurricane_Janet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Meteorological history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Janet_1955_track.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1669" data-file-width="2700" height="136" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/37/Janet_1955_track.png/220px-Janet_1955_track.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/37/Janet_1955_track.png/330px-Janet_1955_track.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/37/Janet_1955_track.png/440px-Janet_1955_track.png 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Janet_1955_track.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Map plotting the track and intensity of the storm, according to the <a href="/wiki/Saffir%E2%80%93Simpson_scale" title="Saffir–Simpson scale">Saffir–Simpson scale</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>A weak tropical disturbance was first reported by the <a href="/wiki/Air_France" title="Air France">Air France</a> and <a href="/wiki/Iberia_(airline)" title="Iberia (airline)">Iberia</a> airlines east of the <a href="/wiki/Lesser_Antilles" title="Lesser Antilles">Lesser Antilles</a> early on September 21. Although it was speculated that the disturbance originated from a <a href="/wiki/Tropical_wave" title="Tropical wave">tropical wave</a> near <a href="/wiki/Cape_Verde" title="Cape Verde">Cape Verde</a>, the <a href="/wiki/National_Hurricane_Center" title="National Hurricane Center">Weather Bureau</a> considered the system too weak to be detected due to a lack of <a href="/wiki/Weather_forecasting" title="Weather forecasting">reports</a> from the islands.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-5">[3]</a></sup> At 1800 <a href="/wiki/Coordinated_Universal_Time" title="Coordinated Universal Time">UTC</a> on September 21, while it was located 350 mi (565 km) east-southeast of <a href="/wiki/Martinique" title="Martinique">Martinique</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HURDAT_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-HURDAT-6">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Janet10_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Janet10-7">[5]</a></sup> the disturbance became sufficiently organized for the Weather Bureau to classify it as Tropical Storm Janet, the tenth named storm of the season.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Janet10_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-Janet10-7">[5]</a></sup> Upon classification, Janet quickly intensified as it moved to the west.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HURDAT_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-HURDAT-6">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-adv_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-adv-8">[6]</a></sup> On September 22, Janet attained hurricane strength, and proceeded to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rapid_deepening" title="Rapid deepening">intensify rapidly</a> as it moved westward across the <a href="/wiki/Windward_Islands" title="Windward Islands">Windward Islands</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-5">[3]</a></sup> By 1200 UTC that day, Janet already attained <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Category_3_hurricane" title="Category 3 hurricane">Category 3 hurricane</a> strength with <a href="/wiki/Maximum_sustained_wind" title="Maximum sustained wind">maximum sustained winds</a> of 120 mph (195 km/h), before stalling in intensification.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HURDAT_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-HURDAT-6">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>Shortly after 1700 UTC on September 22, the <a href="/wiki/Eye_(cyclone)" title="Eye (cyclone)">eye</a> of Janet passed over <a href="/wiki/Barbados" title="Barbados">Barbados</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[7]</a></sup> A <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hurricane_Hunters" title="Hurricane Hunters">reconnaissance flight</a> into the hurricane discovered that the Hurricane Janet's eye measured only 20 mi (32 km) in diameter,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-5">[3]</a></sup> with gale force winds extending 120 mi (190 km) away from the center of circulation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-adv_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-adv-8">[6]</a></sup> The flight also reported a minimum <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Barometric_pressure" title="Barometric pressure">barometric pressure</a> of 979 <a href="/wiki/Bar_(unit)" title="Bar (unit)">mbar</a> (<a href="/wiki/Pascal_(unit)" title="Pascal (unit)">hPa</a>; 28.91 <a href="/wiki/Inch_of_mercury" title="Inch of mercury">inHg</a>). After passing between the islands of <a href="/wiki/Grenada" title="Grenada">Grenada</a> and <a href="/wiki/Carriacou_and_Petite_Martinique" title="Carriacou and Petite Martinique">Carriacou</a> in the morning hours of September 23, Janet entered an area of unfavorable conditions in the eastern <a href="/wiki/Caribbean_Sea" title="Caribbean Sea">Caribbean Sea</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_5-3"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-5">[3]</a></sup> As a result, the hurricane became disorganized, with winds weakening to 90 mph (145 km/h) by 1200 UTC on September 23.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HURDAT_6-3"><a href="#cite_note-HURDAT-6">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_5-4"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-5">[3]</a></sup> A <a href="/wiki/United_States_Navy" title="United States Navy">U.S. Navy</a> reconnaissance plane entered the hurricane early on September 24, reporting a lack of organization, and noting an indiscernible center of circulation with weak <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rainbands" title="Rainbands">rainbands</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_5-5"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-5">[3]</a></sup> However, the hurricane began to reintensify in favorable conditions, regaining major hurricane strength by 1200 UTC on September 24 and subsequently <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Category_4_hurricane" title="Category 4 hurricane">Category 4 hurricane</a> intensity by the next day.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-adv_8-2"><a href="#cite_note-adv-8">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HURDAT_6-4"><a href="#cite_note-HURDAT-6">[4]</a></sup></p>

<p>While trekking across the central Caribbean Sea, Janet was only slightly larger than while it was moving over the Windward Islands, with gale force winds extending 125 mi (200 km) out from the center by September 25.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-adv_8-3"><a href="#cite_note-adv-8">[6]</a></sup> Remaining a Category 4 hurricane as it moved erratically westward across the Caribbean, a reconnaissance flight mission during the night of September 25–26 indicated strong rainbands with frequent <a href="/wiki/Lightning" title="Lightning">lightning</a> strikes and a well-defined eye, evidence that the storm was once again rapidly intensifying.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_5-6"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-5">[3]</a></sup> As it neared the <a href="/wiki/Yucat%C3%A1n_Peninsula" title="Yucatán Peninsula">Yucatán Peninsula</a> on September 26, Janet began accelerating in forward speed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-adv_8-4"><a href="#cite_note-adv-8">[6]</a></sup> After the reconnaissance flight <i>Snowcloud Five</i> was lost while making a penetration into the hurricane's eye, another flight early on September 27 reported a minimum pressure of 938 mbar (hPa; 27.70 inHg), with winds in excess of 115 mph (185 km/h) "by a large and incalculable amount."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_5-7"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-5">[3]</a></sup> The hurricane was estimated to have intensified to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Category_5_hurricane" title="Category 5 hurricane">Category 5 hurricane</a> intensity—the highest rating on the modern day <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saffir%E2%80%93Simpson_hurricane_scale" title="Saffir–Simpson hurricane scale">Saffir–Simpson hurricane scale</a>—at 1200 UTC on September 27, while in the western Caribbean Sea. Janet continued to intensify afterwards, eventually reaching its peak intensity with maximum sustained winds of 175 mph (280 km/h),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HURDAT_6-5"><a href="#cite_note-HURDAT-6">[4]</a></sup> with gale force winds having expanded to at least 250 mi (400 km) away from the center of the hurricane.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-adv_8-5"><a href="#cite_note-adv-8">[6]</a></sup> Janet maintained peak intensity as it passed over the <a href="/wiki/Swan_Islands,_Honduras" title="Swan Islands, Honduras">Swan Islands</a> before making <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Landfall_(meteorology)" title="Landfall (meteorology)">landfall</a> in extreme southern Quintana Roo, Mexico, just east of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chetumal,_Mexico" title="Chetumal, Mexico">Chetumal, Mexico</a>. In that city, a <a href="/wiki/Barometer" title="Barometer">barometer</a> indicated a minimum barometric pressure of 914 mbar (hPa; 27.00 inHg) in the eye of Janet.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_5-8"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-5">[3]</a></sup> At the time, this was the second lowest pressure ever recorded on land in a landfalling <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_hurricane" title="Atlantic hurricane">Atlantic hurricane</a>, behind the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/1935_Labor_Day_Hurricane" title="1935 Labor Day Hurricane">1935 Labor Day Hurricane</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_5-9"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-5">[3]</a></sup> though Janet was later surpassed by hurricanes <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Dean" title="Hurricane Dean">Dean</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Gilbert" title="Hurricane Gilbert">Gilbert</a>, which also made landfall on the Yucatán Peninsula as Category 5 hurricanes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dean_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Dean-10">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>Once over land, Janet considerably weakened to a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Category_2_hurricane" title="Category 2 hurricane">Category 2 hurricane</a> with winds of 105 mph (170 km/h). After crossing the Yucatán Peninsula with a forward speed of 21 mph (34 km/h), the hurricane emerged into the <a href="/wiki/Bay_of_Campeche" title="Bay of Campeche">Bay of Campeche</a> by 1600 UTC on September 28.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HURDAT_6-6"><a href="#cite_note-HURDAT-6">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-adv_8-6"><a href="#cite_note-adv-8">[6]</a></sup> The weakened hurricane marginally intensified as it crossed the Bay of Campeche to a secondary peak intensity of 110 mph (175 km/h) with a minimum central pressure of 950 mbar (hPa; 28.06 inHg).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HURDAT_6-7"><a href="#cite_note-HURDAT-6">[4]</a></sup> Janet eventually made its final landfall 50 mi (80 km) north of the city of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Veracruz,_Veracruz" title="Veracruz, Veracruz">Veracruz</a> by 2200 UTC on September 29 as a Category 2 hurricane. After moving inland, the hurricane became quickly disorganized due to the highly mountainous terrain of <a href="/wiki/Mexico" title="Mexico">Mexico</a>, and as a result the Weather Bureau issued its last advisory on Janet.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-adv_8-7"><a href="#cite_note-adv-8">[6]</a></sup> The weakening system degenerated to tropical storm strength by September 30, and later dissipated over central Mexico by 0600 UTC that day.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HURDAT_6-8"><a href="#cite_note-HURDAT-6">[4]</a></sup> Janet's remnant circulation contributed to the development of a disturbed area of weather off the western coast of Mexico that would subsequently develop into a <a href="/wiki/1955_Pacific_hurricane_season#Tropical_Storm_Five" title="1955 Pacific hurricane season">tropical storm</a> on October 1.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-adv_8-8"><a href="#cite_note-adv-8">[6]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Preparations">Preparations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hurricane_Janet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Preparations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Caribbean_Islands">Caribbean Islands</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hurricane_Janet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Caribbean Islands">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Upon Janet's formation east of the Windward Islands, the Weather Bureau office in <a href="/wiki/San_Juan,_Puerto_Rico" title="San Juan, Puerto Rico">San Juan, Puerto Rico</a> advised <a href="/wiki/Small_craft_advisory" title="Small craft advisory">small craft</a> to remain in port and vessels in the path of the developing storm to exercise caution. After a reconnaissance flight reported much stronger winds than initially suggested, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hurricane_warning" title="Hurricane warning">hurricane warnings</a> were issued at 1600 UTC on September 22 for the entirety of the Lesser Antilles from <a href="/wiki/Saint_Lucia" title="Saint Lucia">Saint Lucia</a> to Grenada, including Barbados. <a href="/wiki/Storm_warning" title="Storm warning">Storm warnings</a> were issued for all islands in the Windward Islands from Saint Lucia south to <a href="/wiki/Tobago" title="Tobago">Tobago</a>, excluding <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/St._Vincent_and_the_Grenadines" title="St. Vincent and the Grenadines">St. Vincent and the Grenadines</a>. All warnings in the Windward Antilles remained in effect until 1000 UTC on September 22. Shortly after warnings were lowered in the Windward Islands, storm warnings were issued for the <a href="/wiki/ABC_islands_(Lesser_Antilles)" title="ABC islands (Lesser Antilles)">ABC islands</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Paraguan%C3%A1_Peninsula" title="Paraguaná Peninsula">Paraguaná Peninsula</a> of <a href="/wiki/Venezuela" title="Venezuela">Venezuela</a> at 0200 UTC on September 23. After Janet passed north of the islands, all storm warnings were lifted on the morning of September 25. The Weather Bureau later warned interests in Jamaica, but did not issue any hurricane-related warnings for the island.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-adv_8-9"><a href="#cite_note-adv-8">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span id="Yucatán_Peninsula_and_Mexico"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Yucat.C3.A1n_Peninsula_and_Mexico">Yucatán Peninsula and Mexico</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hurricane_Janet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Yucatán Peninsula and Mexico">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Weather Bureau first began cautioning areas of <a href="/wiki/Central_America" title="Central America">Central America</a> at 1600 UTC on September 26, advising people in areas of northeast <a href="/wiki/Nicaragua" title="Nicaragua">Nicaragua</a> and <a href="/wiki/Honduras" title="Honduras">Honduras</a> to take precautionary measures, along with all vessels and small craft in the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_of_Honduras" title="Gulf of Honduras">Gulf of Honduras</a> south to <a href="/wiki/Cabo_Gracias_a_Dios" title="Cabo Gracias a Dios">Cabo Gracias a Dios</a>. After Janet began accelerating towards the west-northwest, advisories warned areas in <a href="/wiki/British_Honduras" title="British Honduras">British Honduras</a> and <a href="/wiki/Quintana_Roo" title="Quintana Roo">Quintana Roo</a> of hurricane-related impacts. Though no official warnings were issued for areas of Central America, advisories published by the Weather Bureau cautioned interests in the hurricane's path up until landfall.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-adv_8-10"><a href="#cite_note-adv-8">[6]</a></sup> Evacuations took place in several coastal towns in Quintana Roo upon notification of the oncoming storm. People were evacuated to inland hillside shelters.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-encyclopedia_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-encyclopedia-11">[9]</a></sup> After the hurricane moved through the Yucatán Peninsula and entered the Bay of Campeche, the Weather Bureau alerted areas in the storm's path on the western side of Mexico's Gulf Coast. At the time, Janet was expected to make landfall between Veracruz and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tuxpan,_Veracruz" title="Tuxpan, Veracruz">Tuxpan</a>. Areas were warned of the storm's potential flooding and coastal impacts. The Weather Bureau advised all small craft south of <a href="/wiki/Port_O%27Connor,_Texas" title="Port O'Connor, Texas">Port O'Connor, Texas</a> to remain in port. Notifications of Janet to potentially affected areas were stopped after the hurricane made landfall.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-adv_8-11"><a href="#cite_note-adv-8">[6]</a></sup></p>

<p>During its existence, areas of the <a href="/wiki/Lesser_Antilles" title="Lesser Antilles">Lesser Antilles</a>, ABC islands, and Central America were affected by Janet.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-adv_8-12"><a href="#cite_note-adv-8">[6]</a></sup> At least $65.8 million in damages and 1,023 deaths were caused by the hurricane, mostly in Quintana Roo.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_5-17"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-5">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sakatoon_Smash_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-Sakatoon_Smash-14">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EMDAT_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-EMDAT-15">[13]</a></sup> The large number of deaths and damage caused by Janet helped make the 1955 Atlantic hurricane season the deadliest and costliest hurricane season documented since comprehensive record of such statistics began in 1942.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_5-18"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-5">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Lesser_Antilles">Lesser Antilles</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hurricane_Janet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Lesser Antilles">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>As a small but rapidly intensifying hurricane, Janet passed just south of Barbados on September 22, becoming the first hurricane to strike the island in 57 years, since the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/1898_Windward_Islands_Hurricane" title="1898 Windward Islands Hurricane">1898 Windward Islands Hurricane</a>. Maximum sustained winds on the south side of the island were estimated to be between 110–120 mph (180–190 km/h).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_5-19"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-5">[3]</a></sup> The strong winds knocked out communication lines, preventing officials from accurately enumerating casualties on the island,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Smash_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-Smash-16">[14]</a></sup> though communications were still maintained by commercial cable companies and <a href="/wiki/Amateur_radio" title="Amateur radio">amateur radio</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-High_Damage_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-High_Damage-17">[15]</a></sup> Electric power was later restored, allowing newspapers to publish information on the storm.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Heavy_Loss_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-Heavy_Loss-18">[16]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sugar_cane" title="Sugar cane">Sugar cane</a>, the island country's main source of revenue, was severely damaged, with losses estimated to be in excess of $1 million.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Smash_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-Smash-16">[14]</a></sup> Poorly constructed dwellings were blown apart by the strong winds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Whirling_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-Whirling-19">[17]</a></sup> An estimated 8,100 homes were damaged by the hurricane.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CDMA_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-CDMA-20">[18]</a></sup> The destroyed buildings and trees blocked <a href="/wiki/Highway" title="Highway">highways</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Whirling_19-1"><a href="#cite_note-Whirling-19">[17]</a></sup> and the <a href="/wiki/Grantley_Adams_International_Airport" title="Grantley Adams International Airport">Seawell Airport</a> was forced to close.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Homeless_Sentinel_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-Homeless_Sentinel-21">[19]</a></sup> Heavy rains peaked at 4 in (100 mm) and a <a href="/wiki/Storm_surge" title="Storm surge">storm surge</a> of 7 ft (2.1 m) swept away low-lying houses along the coast.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-encyclopedia_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-encyclopedia-11">[9]</a></sup> An estimated 2,000 people were left homeless due to the hurricane.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2000_Homeless_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-2000_Homeless-12">[10]</a></sup> Property damage on Barbados was estimated to be $5 million,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2000_Homeless_12-2"><a href="#cite_note-2000_Homeless-12">[10]</a></sup> and at least 57 people were killed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EMDAT_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-EMDAT-15">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>On September 23 Janet passed directly between Grenada and Carriacou in the <a href="/wiki/Grenadines" title="Grenadines">Grenadines</a>, killing 122 people in the <a href="/wiki/Archipelago" title="Archipelago">island chain</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_5-20"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-5">[3]</a></sup> An <a href="/wiki/Airport" title="Airport">airport</a> on Grenada was covered in debris strewn by the strong winds. Eight people were killed in a small town adjacent to the airport. Strong winds were also reported to have destroyed docks and warehouses, and unroofed a hotel in <a href="/wiki/St._George%27s,_Grenada" title="St. George's, Grenada">St. George's, Grenada</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Death_Path_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-Death_Path-22">[20]</a></sup> Houses were also unroofed, and <a href="/wiki/Balcony" title="Balcony">balconies</a> of government offices in St. George's were torn off.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Palm_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-Palm-23">[21]</a></sup> All bridges in the island's interior regions collapsed,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-108_Dead_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-108_Dead-24">[22]</a></sup> and spice crops sustained heavy damage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Glasglow_Havoc_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-Glasglow_Havoc-25">[23]</a></sup> An estimated 75% of <a href="/wiki/Nutmeg" title="Nutmeg">nutmeg</a> plantations were destroyed,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Adventure_Guide_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-Adventure_Guide-26">[24]</a></sup> along with nearly all of the island's banana and <a href="/wiki/Cocoa_bean" title="Cocoa bean">cocoa</a> crops.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BWI_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-BWI-27">[25]</a></sup> Three ships were also grounded in the local harbor.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Glasglow_Havoc_25-1"><a href="#cite_note-Glasglow_Havoc-25">[23]</a></sup> In The Carenage, the waterfront region of St. George's, debris was scattered and an 820 ft (250 m) <a href="/wiki/Pier" title="Pier">pier</a> collapsed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Adventure_Guide_26-1"><a href="#cite_note-Adventure_Guide-26">[24]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saint_Vincent_(island)" title="Saint Vincent (island)">St. Vincent</a> was mostly destroyed,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-St._Vincent_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-St._Vincent-28">[26]</a></sup> and St. Lucia suffered significant coastal damage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NEMO_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-NEMO-29">[27]</a></sup> Over $2.8 million in damages were estimated throughout the Grenadines.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_5-21"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-5">[3]</a></sup> Farther south, in <a href="/wiki/Port_of_Spain" title="Port of Spain">Port of Spain</a>, a church being used as a storm shelter collapsed, killing ten people.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sakatoon_Smash_14-2"><a href="#cite_note-Sakatoon_Smash-14">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>Hurricane Janet skirted the <a href="/wiki/ABC_islands_(Lesser_Antilles)" title="ABC islands (Lesser Antilles)">ABC islands</a> with strong winds as it passed to the north from September 24–25.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HURDAT_6-9"><a href="#cite_note-HURDAT-6">[4]</a></sup> In <a href="/wiki/Aruba" title="Aruba">Aruba</a>, gusts peaked at 50 mph (80 km/h), uprooting trees. However, damage outside of trees was primarily insignificant. In <a href="/wiki/Bonaire" title="Bonaire">Bonaire</a>, piers were destroyed, and the island's coastal boulevard was damaged. Beach facilities at <a href="/wiki/Cura%C3%A7ao" title="Curaçao">Curaçao</a>'s Piscadera and Vaersen Bays suffered considerable damage. Quays along the harbor entrance were also damaged.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[28]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Snowcloud_Five">Snowcloud Five</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hurricane_Janet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Snowcloud Five">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>On September 26, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hurricane_Hunter" title="Hurricane Hunter">Hurricane Hunter</a> <a href="/wiki/Lockheed_P-2_Neptune#Variants" title="Lockheed P-2 Neptune">P2V-3W Neptune</a> aircraft Snowcloud Five, led by U.S. Navy <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lieutenant_Commander" title="Lieutenant Commander">Lieutenant Commander</a> Grover B. Windham, was sent from <a href="/wiki/Guant%C3%A1namo_Bay" title="Guantánamo Bay">Guantánamo Bay</a> to investigate Janet, which was at the time a Category 4 hurricane south of <a href="/wiki/Jamaica" title="Jamaica">Jamaica</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hurricane_Hunters_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hurricane_Hunters-31">[29]</a></sup> Upon penetration of the hurricane's eyewall at an altitude of 700 ft (200 m), a final transmission was received from the reconnaissance flight before it presumably crashed in the Caribbean Sea.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bill_Blog_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bill_Blog-32">[30]</a></sup> All 11 people on board, including nine crew members and two journalists, were killed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bill_Blog_32-1"><a href="#cite_note-Bill_Blog-32">[30]</a></sup> Following the loss of the plane, an extensive <a href="/wiki/Search_and_rescue" title="Search and rescue">search and rescue</a> operation took place over a large area of the Caribbean Sea, with 3,000 personnel involved with the search, returning no results.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hurricane_Hunters_31-1"><a href="#cite_note-Hurricane_Hunters-31">[29]</a></sup> The exact cause of the crash remains unknown, though it is speculated that the plane's <a href="/wiki/Altimeter" title="Altimeter">altimeter</a> gave an incorrect reading due to the low surrounding barometric pressure.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bill_Blog_32-2"><a href="#cite_note-Bill_Blog-32">[30]</a></sup> Although four hurricane reconnaissance flights have been lost since operations began in 1943, Snowcloud Five remains the only known loss of a reconnaissance aircraft as a result of a hurricane in the Atlantic basin, with the other three occurring as a result of <a href="/wiki/Typhoon" title="Typhoon">Pacific typhoons</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Q.26A_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-Q.26A-33">[31]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span id="Yucatán_Peninsula"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Yucat.C3.A1n_Peninsula">Yucatán Peninsula</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hurricane_Janet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Yucatán Peninsula">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">

<p>As it approached the Yucatán Peninsula, Janet passed over the Swan Islands north of Honduras as a Category 5 hurricane on September 27.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HURDAT_6-10"><a href="#cite_note-HURDAT-6">[4]</a></sup> The hurricane caused rough seas, and strong winds uprooted trees and <a href="/wiki/Antenna_(radio)" title="Antenna (radio)">antennas</a>. After gusts exceeded an estimated 100 mph (160 km/h), officials on the islands took shelter in a Navy <a href="/wiki/Seismometer" title="Seismometer">seismograph building</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_5-22"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-5">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Blasts_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-Blasts-34">[32]</a></sup> Though no deaths were reported, Janet destroyed almost all buildings on the islands. The U.S. Weather Bureau and <a href="/wiki/Civil_Aeronautics_Board#Civil_Aeronautics_Authority" title="Civil Aeronautics Board">Civil Aeronautics Authority</a> requested food and supplies for 82 people on the island after the storm passed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lash_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-Lash-35">[33]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Quintana_Roo">Quintana Roo</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hurricane_Janet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Quintana Roo">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>After passing the Swan Islands, Janet made landfall on the Yucatán Peninsula between Corozal Town, British Honduras, and Chetumal, Mexico, at 1700 UTC on September 28.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_5-23"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-5">[3]</a></sup> At the time, it had winds of 175 mph (280 km/h).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HURDAT_6-11"><a href="#cite_note-HURDAT-6">[4]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hurricane_force" title="Hurricane force">Hurricane force</a> winds were reported for two hours preceding the hurricane's landfall, with numerous pressure readings below 948 mbar (hPa; 28.00 inHg).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_5-24"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-5">[3]</a></sup> Roads and crops on the peninsula sustained heavy damage due to Janet.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rip_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-Rip-36">[34]</a></sup> In <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Xcalak,_Quintana_Roo" title="Xcalak, Quintana Roo">Xcalak, Quintana Roo</a>, the strong winds from Janet flattened the port's infrastructure, and only one house remained intact after the storm's passage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Xcalak_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-Xcalak-37">[35]</a></sup> 97 people were killed in Xcalak, constituting more than a third of the port's population.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dying_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-Dying-38">[36]</a></sup> Chetumal, Mexico was devastated, with only four buildings left standing. Storm surge pushed water to a depth of 6.5 feet (2 m), 1600 ft (490 m) inland, despite the peninsula protecting the city from the open ocean. The death toll in Chetumal remains uncertain; 120 bodies were recovered in the city but it is unknown how many were ever found.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_5-25"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-5">[3]</a></sup> An additional 10,000 people in the city were left homeless after the hurricane, and were forced to sleep in the open overnight.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bears_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bears-39">[37]</a></sup> Federal <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Relief_agency" title="Relief agency">relief agencies</a> previously providing service for areas affected by Hurricanes Gladys and Hilda earlier in the year in western areas of the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_Coast_of_Mexico" title="Gulf Coast of Mexico">Gulf Coast of Mexico</a> were ordered to extend relief operations to the Yucatán Peninsula.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SP_40-0"><a href="#cite_note-SP-40">[38]</a></sup> In Quintana Roo, Hurricane Janet was estimated to have caused $40 million in damages and at least 500 deaths, the most of any region affected by Janet.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_5-26"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-5">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="British_Honduras">British Honduras</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hurricane_Janet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: British Honduras">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Making landfall near the border between Mexico and British Honduras, the latter colony's <a href="/wiki/Corozal_District" title="Corozal District">Corozal</a> and <a href="/wiki/Orange_Walk_District" title="Orange Walk District">Orange Walk</a> <a href="/wiki/Districts_of_Belize" title="Districts of Belize">districts</a>, containing a population of 15,500 at the time, experienced severe devastation from Janet. Strong hurricane winds knocked down and damaged numerous trees across British Honduras, particularly in the Freshwater Creek Forest Preserve. There, an estimated 30% of all trees had been damaged, especially <a href="/wiki/Mahogany" title="Mahogany">mahogany</a> and <a href="/wiki/Manilkara_zapota" title="Manilkara zapota">sapodilla</a> trees. Hurricane Janet also caused extensive damage to crops, with an estimated $2.631 million in damages. <a href="/wiki/Fruit_tree" title="Fruit tree">Fruit trees</a>, <a href="/wiki/Maize" title="Maize">maize</a>, and sugar cane crops suffered the most damage. Sugar cane was expected to have a 20% decrease in yield due to the damage sustained after the storm. <a href="/wiki/Pineapple" title="Pineapple">Pineapple</a> crops and <a href="/wiki/Livestock" title="Livestock">livestock</a> experienced less significant losses. To a lesser extent, the hurricane also affected the <a href="/wiki/Belize_District" title="Belize District">Belize District</a> and several of British Honduras' offshore <a href="/wiki/Cay" title="Cay">cayes</a>. Similar to Corozal and Orange Walk Districts, corn, trees, and coconut experienced the most severe damage of any crop.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Belize-Report_41-0"><a href="#cite_note-Belize-Report-41">[39]</a></sup></p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Corozal_Town" title="Corozal Town">Corozal Town, British Honduras</a>, south of where Janet made landfall, 500 people were made homeless, and six people were killed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Slap_42-0"><a href="#cite_note-Slap-42">[40]</a></sup> About 90% of all buildings in the town were destroyed,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Razed_43-0"><a href="#cite_note-Razed-43">[41]</a></sup> and communications were knocked out by the strong winds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Slap_42-1"><a href="#cite_note-Slap-42">[40]</a></sup> Based on an average home cost of $2,000, it was estimated that property damage to homes in Corozal Town totaled $800,000.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Belize-Report_41-1"><a href="#cite_note-Belize-Report-41">[39]</a></sup> Much of <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Santa_Elena,_British_Honduras&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Santa Elena, British Honduras (page does not exist)">Santa Elena, British Honduras</a> was also flattened by the strong winds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bears_39-1"><a href="#cite_note-Bears-39">[37]</a></sup> Farther south in <a href="/wiki/Belize_City" title="Belize City">Belize City</a>, winds peaked at 60 mph (100 km/h), though no damage was reported.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Roar_44-0"><a href="#cite_note-Roar-44">[42]</a></sup> In British Honduras, the hurricane's effects were less deadly than in Quintana Roo,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ellensburg_45-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ellensburg-45">[43]</a></sup> but in northern portions of the colony the storm killed 16 people and caused $5 million in damages.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_5-27"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-5">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mainland_Mexico">Mainland Mexico</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hurricane_Janet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Mainland Mexico">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Tampico_Flood_Damage_Hurricane_Janet.jpg"><img alt="Sepia-toned helicopter view of a city with roads flooded by water and buildings damaged. <div class="thumbcaption">

<p>While in the Bay of Campeche, the <a href="/wiki/Fishing_vessel" title="Fishing vessel">shrimp boat</a> <i>Celestino Arias</i> was sunken by strong waves generated by Janet after it suffered <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Engine_failure" title="Engine failure">engine failures</a>. The stranded ship was heavily damaged by the hurricane's strong winds in the bay, which broke towing lines. All of the ship's crewmen, which had evacuated the boat after it sunk, were later rescued and brought back to <a href="/wiki/Tampa,_Florida" title="Tampa, Florida">Tampa, Florida</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tampa_Boat_46-0"><a href="#cite_note-Tampa_Boat-46">[44]</a></sup> Along the coast, tides were reported to be 3–4 ft (0.91–1.2 m) above average. Farther north along the Texas coast, tides were 2 ft (0.61 m) above average, blocking beach roads near <a href="/wiki/Corpus_Christi,_Texas" title="Corpus Christi, Texas">Corpus Christi</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-clim_47-0"><a href="#cite_note-clim-47">[45]</a></sup></p>
<p>Janet made its final landfall in the Mexican state of <a href="/wiki/Veracruz" title="Veracruz">Veracruz</a> between the cities of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nautla" title="Nautla">Nautla</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Veracruz,_Veracruz" title="Veracruz, Veracruz">Veracruz</a> as a Category 2 hurricane at 2200 UTC on September 29.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HURDAT_6-12"><a href="#cite_note-HURDAT-6">[4]</a></sup> In Nautla, communications were cut by strong winds from the hurricane.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Repulse_48-0"><a href="#cite_note-Repulse-48">[46]</a></sup> The strong winds also caused a relief plane to crash, causing five deaths.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17_More_49-0"><a href="#cite_note-17_More-49">[47]</a></sup> Heavy rains in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tampico,_Tamaulipas" title="Tampico, Tamaulipas">Tampico</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tamaulipas" title="Tamaulipas">Tamaulipas</a> area added to floods caused by hurricanes Gladys and Hilda earlier in the year. The resulting flood was reported by the Weather Bureau office in New Orleans to be one of the worst <a href="/wiki/Natural_disaster" title="Natural disaster">natural disasters</a> in Mexican history.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_5-28"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-5">[3]</a></sup> In Tampico, 16 in (410 mm) of rain was reported.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Deluge_Floods_50-0"><a href="#cite_note-Deluge_Floods-50">[48]</a></sup> The floods contributed to a localized <a href="/wiki/Typhoid_fever" title="Typhoid fever">typhoid fever</a> and <a href="/wiki/Dysentery" title="Dysentery">dysentery</a> outbreak,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-blowout_51-0"><a href="#cite_note-blowout-51">[49]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-New_Flood_52-0"><a href="#cite_note-New_Flood-52">[50]</a></sup> causing over 1,000 people to evacuate out of the city to prevent further spreading of the diseases. An additional 36,000 people were being cared for in concentration centers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-New_Flood_52-1"><a href="#cite_note-New_Flood-52">[50]</a></sup> Although located south of where Janet made landfall, areas of Veracruz were inundated by strong <a href="/wiki/Storm_surge" title="Storm surge">storm surge</a>, including the city's main streets and port.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Repulse_48-1"><a href="#cite_note-Repulse-48">[46]</a></sup> Operations along a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Railroad" title="Railroad">railroad</a> stretching from <a href="/wiki/Laredo,_Texas" title="Laredo, Texas">Laredo, Texas</a> to <a href="/wiki/Mexico_City" title="Mexico City">Mexico City</a> were stopped, after having just reopened due to Hurricane Hilda.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Delay_53-0"><a href="#cite_note-Delay-53">[51]</a></sup></p>
<p>Farther inland, as Janet dissipated over the mountainous central Mexico, the storm dropped torrential rainfall over the Tamisi and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Panuco_River" title="Panuco River">Panuco River</a> basins.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mpm_54-0"><a href="#cite_note-mpm-54">[52]</a></sup> The water levels in the Panuco River remained above <a href="/wiki/Flood_stage" title="Flood stage">flood stage</a> for four weeks.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Terrible_Toll_55-0"><a href="#cite_note-Terrible_Toll-55">[53]</a></sup> In Tampico, the river flooded neighborhoods, forcing cables to be stretched along roads to prevent people from being swept away. Small boats were also sent to monitor streets.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Record_Floods_56-0"><a href="#cite_note-Record_Floods-56">[54]</a></sup> According to some sources, 800 people died from the floods, with thousands more being stranded in the city.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mpm_54-1"><a href="#cite_note-mpm-54">[52]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Tamazunchale" title="Tamazunchale">Tamazunchale</a> was inundated by an overflowing <a href="/wiki/Moctezuma_River" title="Moctezuma River">Moctezuma River</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Grow_57-0"><a href="#cite_note-Grow-57">[55]</a></sup> The rains caused a <a href="/wiki/Landslide" title="Landslide">landslide</a> in <a href="/wiki/Colonia_San_Rafael" title="Colonia San Rafael">Colonia San Rafael</a>, killing 12 people.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17_More_49-1"><a href="#cite_note-17_More-49">[47]</a></sup> Heavy rains also impacted <a href="/wiki/Guadalajara" title="Guadalajara">Guadalajara</a>, which had previously not been affected by any tropical cyclones earlier in the year. In Maltarana, <a href="/wiki/Jalisco" title="Jalisco">Jalisco</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Lerma_River" title="Lerma River">Lerma</a> and <a href="/wiki/Duero_River_(Mexico)" title="Duero River (Mexico)">Duero Rivers</a> overflowed, forcing 800 people in the town to evacuate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Record_Floods_56-1"><a href="#cite_note-Record_Floods-56">[54]</a></sup> Farther north, in <a href="/wiki/Sonora" title="Sonora">Sonora</a>, cotton crops damaged by Janet were estimated to amount to $12 million in damages.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rains_Continue_58-0"><a href="#cite_note-Rains_Continue-58">[56]</a></sup> Combined property damage in Mexico from Hurricanes Gladys, Hilda, and Janet was estimated to total $200 million, nearly half of the government's 1955 <a href="/wiki/Government_budget" title="Government budget">national budget</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-New_Floods_59-0"><a href="#cite_note-New_Floods-59">[57]</a></sup> Although there were no official damage or fatality estimates,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_5-29"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-5">[3]</a></sup> at least 1,000 people were injured and another 100,000 people were made homeless on the Mexican mainland due to Janet.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rising_Fast_60-0"><a href="#cite_note-Rising_Fast-60">[58]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Lesser_Antilles_2">Lesser Antilles</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hurricane_Janet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Lesser Antilles">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>After Janet passed Barbados, the island was declared a <a href="/wiki/State_of_emergency" title="State of emergency">state of emergency</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Heavy_Loss_18-1"><a href="#cite_note-Heavy_Loss-18">[16]</a></sup> Relief workers on Barbados helped to clean up debris scattered by the strong winds on highways, and the local government made requests for food and construction materials.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Venezuela_61-0"><a href="#cite_note-Venezuela-61">[59]</a></sup> The extensive damaged caused by Janet on the island to low-income households led the passage of the 1955 Housing Act, which created the National Housing Authority (NHA) in 1956. The NHA was responsible for the acquisition of lands on which houses could be built with stronger and more permanent materials, which was thought to minimize maintenance costs and hurricane-related damage. The newly founded organization quickly worked to rebuild homes after the hurricane struck.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Low-cost_Barbados_62-0"><a href="#cite_note-Low-cost_Barbados-62">[60]</a></sup></p>
<p>In Grenada, the island's governor issued an emergency order against the <a href="/wiki/Hoarding" title="Hoarding">hoarding</a> of food in the aftermath of Janet, due to the food and water shortage that resulted from Janet. A United States <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Destroyer_(ship)" title="Destroyer (ship)">destroyer</a> and a British <a href="/wiki/Frigate" title="Frigate">frigate</a> were sent to bring relief supplies to Grenada, and ships departing from Jamaica and Trinidad were redirected to the island to help send supplies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Aid_Rush_63-0"><a href="#cite_note-Aid_Rush-63">[61]</a></sup> The British government later announced that it would send <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/British_Pound" title="British Pound">£</a>50,000 (US$155,000) in relief to the Grenadines.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-65"><a href="#cite_note-65">[nb 3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kills350_66-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kills350-66">[63]</a></sup> The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Crown_Agents_for_Oversea_Governments_and_Administrations_Ltd" title="Crown Agents for Oversea Governments and Administrations Ltd">Crown Agents</a> and <a href="/wiki/International_Red_Cross_and_Red_Crescent_Movement" title="International Red Cross and Red Crescent Movement">Red Cross</a> delivered relief supplies to Barbados and the Grenadines, which included aluminum roofing sheets and portable <a href="/wiki/Electric_generator" title="Electric generator">electric generators</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Glasgow_Herald_67-0"><a href="#cite_note-Glasgow_Herald-67">[64]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="British_Honduras_and_Mexico">British Honduras and Mexico</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hurricane_Janet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: British Honduras and Mexico">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Relief and reconstruction measures were enacted in the British Honduras beginning on September 30.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Belize-Report_41-2"><a href="#cite_note-Belize-Report-41">[39]</a></sup> A large-scale reconstruction program was initiated by the government to help rebuild 48 villages.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-torebuild_68-0"><a href="#cite_note-torebuild-68">[65]</a></sup> The government also declared a state of emergency for Corozal, Orange Walk, and Belize administrative districts, including a ban on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Liquor" title="Liquor">liquor</a> sales. Temporary communication lines were rebuilt, which initially only allowed official communications with affected areas. Due to the severity of the damage in Corozal, an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Airstrip" title="Airstrip">airstrip</a> was built to help deliver relief to the city more efficiently. Food depots in Corozal, <a href="/wiki/Louisville,_Belize" title="Louisville, Belize">Louisville</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Orange_Walk_Town" title="Orange Walk Town">Orange Walk Town</a> were tasked with distributing food. The potential for widespread disease following the devastation wrought by Janet forced a widespread <a href="/wiki/Vaccine" title="Vaccine">vaccination</a> initiative against typhoid fever in affected areas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Belize-Report_41-3"><a href="#cite_note-Belize-Report-41">[39]</a></sup> The Jamaican government sent £20,000 (US$55,000) to the colony in relief funds,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Glasgow_Herald_67-1"><a href="#cite_note-Glasgow_Herald-67">[64]</a></sup> while the British government sent £40,000 (US$110,000) to affected areas in the British Honduras and other affected islands in the Caribbean. The United States sent the <a href="/wiki/Cargo_ship" title="Cargo ship">cargo ship</a> <i><a href="/wiki/USS_Antares_(AK-258)" title="USS Antares (AK-258)">USS Antares</a></i>, which supplied the colony with various relief materials.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Belize-Report_41-4"><a href="#cite_note-Belize-Report-41">[39]</a></sup> In Corozal Town, a $3.5 million grant was given to <a href="/wiki/Surveying#Land_surveyor" title="Surveying">land surveyor</a> H.C. Fairweather to plan and reconstruct the township.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CorozalJanet_69-0"><a href="#cite_note-CorozalJanet-69">[66]</a></sup></p>
<p>A U.S. Navy relief plane was sent to Chetumal, Mexico, to deliver food and other relief supplies after Janet struck the region.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Been_Here_70-0"><a href="#cite_note-Been_Here-70">[67]</a></sup> In areas previously affected by hurricanes Gladys and Hilda earlier in the year, federal <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Relief_agency" title="Relief agency">relief agencies</a> were ordered to extend relief operations to the Yucatán Peninsula.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SP_40-1"><a href="#cite_note-SP-40">[38]</a></sup> However, after crossing the Bay of Campeche and making landfall near Veracruz, relief measures were once again implemented in the western Gulf Coast of Mexico. The <a href="/wiki/Mexican_Air_Force" title="Mexican Air Force">Mexican Air Force</a>, commercial and private planes, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Helicopters" title="Helicopters">helicopters</a> were sent to bring relief supplies and <a href="/wiki/Airlift" title="Airlift">airlift</a> refugees.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fury_Janet_71-0"><a href="#cite_note-Fury_Janet-71">[68]</a></sup> The United States initiated the largest relief campaign ever staged in Mexico at the time. Various government and relief agencies, including the <a href="/wiki/American_Red_Cross" title="American Red Cross">American Red Cross</a> and <a href="/wiki/The_Salvation_Army" title="The Salvation Army">The Salvation Army</a>, participated in the relief efforts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Toll_72-0"><a href="#cite_note-Toll-72">[69]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/United_States_Air_Force" title="United States Air Force">United States Air Force</a> and <a href="/wiki/United_States_Army" title="United States Army">United States Army</a> began a joint-relief operation to send relief supplies to the Tampico area, beginning with the deployment of a <a href="/wiki/Curtiss_C-46_Commando" title="Curtiss C-46 Commando">Curtiss C-46 Commando</a> from the <a href="/wiki/Harlingen_Air_Force_Base" title="Harlingen Air Force Base">Harlingen Air Force Base</a> on October 1.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17_More_49-2"><a href="#cite_note-17_More-49">[47]</a></sup> Ten other <a href="/wiki/Military_transport_aircraft" title="Military transport aircraft">military transport aircraft</a> from the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Air_Force" title="United States Air Force">United States Air Force</a> were sent from Texas to provide food and clothing. <a href="/wiki/Pan_American_World_Airways" title="Pan American World Airways">Pan American World Airways</a> sent a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Boeing_314" title="Boeing 314">Boeing 314</a> from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Miami,_Florida" title="Miami, Florida">Miami, Florida</a> to evacuate victims.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rains_Continue_58-1"><a href="#cite_note-Rains_Continue-58">[56]</a></sup> Helicopters were sent from the <a href="/wiki/USS_Saipan_(CVL-48)" title="USS Saipan (CVL-48)">USS Saipan</a> to transfer food and medical supplies from the <a href="/wiki/Light_aircraft_carrier" title="Light aircraft carrier">light aircraft carrier</a>. Other ships also aided in delivering supplies,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Battle_73-0"><a href="#cite_note-Battle-73">[70]</a></sup> including the <a href="/wiki/USS_Siboney_(CVE-112)" title="USS Siboney (CVE-112)">USS Siboney</a>, which also deployed helicopters to assist in relief efforts. By October 6, 1,500 people were estimated to have been rescued from the Tampico area, and at least 280,000 lb (130,000 kg) of food and clothing materials were estimated to have been delivered by the US Air Force to the city.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rains_Continue_58-2"><a href="#cite_note-Rains_Continue-58">[56]</a></sup> There, typhoid <a href="/wiki/Serum_(blood)" title="Serum (blood)">serum</a> shots were administered as a precautionary measure.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-326_Deaths_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-326_Deaths-13">[11]</a></sup> Former <a href="/wiki/President_of_Mexico" title="President of Mexico">president</a> <a href="/wiki/Manuel_%C3%81vila_Camacho" title="Manuel Ávila Camacho">Manuel Ávila Camacho</a> died of a heart ailment, in part due to over-exertion in helping in the relief efforts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ex-president_74-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ex-president-74">[71]</a></sup></p>
<p>Due to its large swath of destruction, the name <i>Janet</i> was <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_retired_Atlantic_hurricanes" title="List of retired Atlantic hurricanes">retired</a> by the Weather Bureau, becoming just the seventh Atlantic tropical cyclone to have its name retired, and the fourth to be retired in 1955.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Retired_75-0"><a href="#cite_note-Retired-75">[72]</a></sup></p>
