<p><i><b>The Hurricane</b></i> is a 1999 American <a href="/wiki/Biographical_film" title="Biographical film">biographical</a> <a href="/wiki/List_of_sports_films" title="List of sports films">sports</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Drama_(genre)" title="Drama (genre)">drama film</a> directed and produced by <a href="/wiki/Norman_Jewison" title="Norman Jewison">Norman Jewison</a>. The film stars <a href="/wiki/Denzel_Washington" title="Denzel Washington">Denzel Washington</a> as <a href="/wiki/Rubin_Carter" title="Rubin Carter">Rubin "The Hurricane" Carter</a>, a former middleweight boxer who was wrongly convicted for a triple murder in a bar in <a href="/wiki/Paterson,_New_Jersey" title="Paterson, New Jersey">Paterson, New Jersey</a>. The script was adapted by <a href="/wiki/Armyan_Bernstein" title="Armyan Bernstein">Armyan Bernstein</a> and <a href="/wiki/Dan_Gordon_(screenwriter)" title="Dan Gordon (screenwriter)">Dan Gordon</a> from Carter's autobiography <i>The Sixteenth Round: From Number 1 Contender To 45472</i> and the non-fiction work <i>Lazarus and the Hurricane: The Freeing of Rubin "The Hurricane" Carter</i> by Sam Chaiton and Terry Swinton.</p>
<p>The film depicts Carter's arrest, his life in prison and how he was freed by the love and compassion of a teenager from Brooklyn named <a href="/wiki/Lesra_Martin" title="Lesra Martin">Lesra Martin</a> and his <a href="/wiki/Canadians" title="Canadians">Canadian</a> foster family.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-answers_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-answers-2">[2]</a></sup> The film received positive reviews, but has been criticized for inaccuracies by some media outlets and participants in Carter's trials.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Plot">Plot</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Hurricane_(1999_film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Plot">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The film tells the story of <a href="/wiki/Middleweight" title="Middleweight">middleweight</a> boxer <a href="/wiki/Rubin_Carter" title="Rubin Carter">Rubin "The Hurricane" Carter</a>, who was convicted of committing a triple murder in a bar in Paterson, New Jersey. His sentence was set aside after he had spent nearly 20 years in prison. The film concentrates on Rubin Carter's life between 1966 and 1985. It describes his fight against the conviction for triple murder and how he copes with nearly 20 years in prison.</p>
<p>A parallel plot follows <a href="/wiki/Lesra_Martin" title="Lesra Martin">Lesra Martin</a>, an underprivileged Afro-American youth from Brooklyn, now living in <a href="/wiki/Toronto" title="Toronto">Toronto</a>. In the 1980s, the child becomes interested in Carter's life and circumstances after reading Carter's autobiography. He convinces his Canadian foster family to commit themselves to Carter's case. The story culminates with Carter's legal team's successful pleas to Judge <a href="/wiki/H._Lee_Sarokin" title="H. Lee Sarokin">H. Lee Sarokin</a> of the <a href="/wiki/United_States_District_Court_for_the_District_of_New_Jersey" title="United States District Court for the District of New Jersey">United States District Court for the District of New Jersey</a>.</p>
<p>In 1966, Rubin "The Hurricane" Carter was a top-ranked middleweight boxer, expected by many fans to become the world's greatest boxing champion. When three victims, specifically the club's bartender and a male and a female customer, were shot to death in a bar in Paterson, New Jersey, Carter and his friend John Artis, driving home from another club in Paterson, were stopped and interrogated by the police.</p>
<p>Although the police asserted that Carter and Artis were innocent and thus, "were never suspects," a man named Alfred Bello, a suspect himself in the killings, claimed that Carter and Artis were present at the time of the murders. On the basis of Bello's testimony, Carter and Artis were convicted of the triple homicide in the club, Carter was given three consecutive life sentences.</p>
<p>Throughout the trial, Carter proclaimed his innocence, claiming that his race, his boxing career and status and his work as a civil rights activist were the real reasons for his conviction. Eight years later, Bello and a co-suspect, Arthur Bradley, who also claimed that Carter was present at the scene of the crimes, renounced and recanted their testimony. However, Carter and Artis were convicted once again.</p>
<p>Afterwards, the plot goes back to Lesra Martin, who works with a trio of Canadian activists to push the State of New Jersey to reexamine Carter's case.</p>
<p>In 1985, a Federal District Court ruled that the prosecution in Carter's second trial committed "grave constitutional violations" and that his conviction was based on racism rather than facts. As a result, Carter was finally freed, and he summed up his story by saying, "Hate got me into this place, love got me out."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-answers_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-answers-2">[2]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Production">Production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Hurricane_(1999_film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Production">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Background">Background</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Hurricane_(1999_film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Background">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Norman_Jewison" title="Norman Jewison">Norman Jewison</a> got interested in a "Hurricane" Carter biopic in 1992.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nj_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-nj-3">[3]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Armyan_Bernstein" title="Armyan Bernstein">Armyan Bernstein</a> purchased the filming rights through <a href="/wiki/Beacon_Pictures" title="Beacon Pictures">Beacon Pictures</a>, and went on to write the first scripts while establishing a financing partnership with <a href="/wiki/Irving_Azoff" title="Irving Azoff">Irving Azoff</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> At first, Jewison felt the story was so extensive that it would fit better as a television miniseries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nj_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-nj-3">[3]</a></sup> Once Denzel Washington signed to play the title character, he went through long boxing training, and worked closely with Rubin Carter. Washington said, "He went through pots and pots of coffee and packs of cigarettes. I'd drink a little coffee. It's interesting and challenging when the person is there, alive and in the room."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Filming began in November 1998, with locations in both <a href="/wiki/New_Jersey" title="New Jersey">New Jersey</a> – <a href="/wiki/East_Jersey_State_Prison" title="East Jersey State Prison">East Jersey State Prison</a> in <a href="/wiki/Trenton,_New_Jersey" title="Trenton, New Jersey">Trenton</a> and the cities of <a href="/wiki/Avenel,_New_Jersey" title="Avenel, New Jersey">Avenel</a> and Paterson – and <a href="/wiki/Toronto" title="Toronto">Toronto</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Release">Release</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Hurricane_(1999_film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Release">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Premiere">Premiere</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Hurricane_(1999_film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Premiere">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><i>The Hurricane</i> premiered on September 17, 1999, at the <a href="/wiki/Toronto_International_Film_Festival" title="Toronto International Film Festival">Toronto International Film Festival</a>. It also was featured at the <a href="/wiki/Berlin_International_Film_Festival" title="Berlin International Film Festival">Berlin International Film Festival</a> on February 17, 2000.</p>
<p>Two weeks prior to its opening in North America, a premiere for <i>The Hurricane</i> was held at the <a href="/wiki/Fox_Theater,_Westwood_Village" title="Fox Theater, Westwood Village">Mann Village Theater</a> in Los Angeles. Many of the depicted people were in attendance. When asked about being portrayed by Denzel Washington, Rubin Carter replied that “I didn’t know I was that good-looking." The film was praised by Lesra Martin, who described it as "a stupendous depiction of accurate events", and John Artis, Carter's friend who was convicted with him, said he “was in awe to see what unfolded and not have to feel the pressure I felt at the time.” An objection was held by H. Lee Sarokin, the federal judge who freed Carter, saying that unlike his portrayal by Rod Steiger “I’m a lower-key guy.”<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Box_office">Box office</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Hurricane_(1999_film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Box office">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The film opened in North American limited release on December 29, 1999. The first week's gross was $384,640 (11 screens) and the total receipts for the run were $50,668,906. In its widest release the film was featured in 2,148 theaters. It closed the week of April 14, 2000. The motion picture was in circulation sixteen weeks.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Critical_response">Critical response</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Hurricane_(1999_film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Critical response">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Reviews were positive, with much praise to Denzel Washington's performance. The film has an 83% approval rating at <a href="/wiki/Rotten_Tomatoes" title="Rotten Tomatoes">Rotten Tomatoes</a> based on 113 reviews. The consensus reads: "Thanks in large part to one of Denzel Washington's most powerful on-screen performances, <i>The Hurricane</i> is a moving, inspirational sports drama, even if it takes few risks in telling its story."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Roger_Ebert" title="Roger Ebert">Roger Ebert</a>, film critic for the <i>Chicago Sun Times</i>, liked the film and the acting, and wrote, "This is one of Denzel Washington's great performances, on a par with his work in <i><a href="/wiki/Malcolm_X_(1992_film)" title="Malcolm X (1992 film)">Malcolm X</a>.</i>...Washington as Hurricane Carter is spare, focused, filled with anger and pride.... This is strong stuff, and I was amazed, after feeling some impatience in the earlier reaches of the film, to find myself so deeply absorbed in its second and third acts, until at the end I was blinking at tears. What affects me emotionally at the movies is never sadness, but goodness."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ebert_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-ebert-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>Regarding the "fictionalized" aspects of the film, Ebert discussed why that is often the case in films: "Several people have told me dubiously that they heard the movie was 'fictionalized'. Well, of course it was. Those who seek the truth about a man from the film of his life might as well seek it from his loving grandmother. Most biopics, like most grandmothers, see the good in a man and demonize his enemies. They pass silently over his imprudent romances. In dramatizing his victories, they simplify them. And they provide the best roles to the most interesting characters. If they didn't, we wouldn't pay to see them." He added, "<i>The Hurricane</i> is not a documentary but a parable, in which two lives are saved by the power of the written word."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ebert_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-ebert-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>Film critic <a href="/wiki/Stephen_Holden" title="Stephen Holden">Stephen Holden</a>, writing for <i>The New York Times</i>, had mixed views of the film but did like the acting. He wrote, "In telling the story of Mr. Carter's protracted and ultimately successful fight for freedom and justice, <i>The Hurricane</i> rides to glory on an astonishing performance by Denzel Washington....That is to say, Mr. Washington leans into an otherwise schlocky movie and slams it out of the ballpark. If his Hurricane is an inspiring portrait of nobility, it is because the actor never conceals the demons of fury and despair gnawing beneath his character's forcefully articulate surface."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nyt_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-nyt-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>Holden was forthright about the veracity of the film, writing, "The film is so eager to stir us up that it thinks little of bending the facts for dramatic effect. Among its most egregious distortions is its depiction of Mr. Carter's 1964 middleweight title match with Joey Giardello. The movie (which has fine, naturalistic boxing sequences) inaccurately portrays the fight as lost by Carter solely because of the judges' racism. The taking of such license, of course, adds an extra jolt of drama. But when these and other distortions and exaggerations are added up, it's worth wondering if that self-congratulatory glow the movie leaves us with has been gotten far too easily and at what cost."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nyt_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-nyt-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>Jewison considers <i>The Hurricane</i> his best work.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Controversy">Controversy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Hurricane_(1999_film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Controversy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Film_accuracy">Film accuracy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Hurricane_(1999_film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Film accuracy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The film was criticized for misrepresenting many of the facts of Carter's life and the case itself, as documented in both his criminal and military records, and police reports and court documentation. Such critics include: <i>Herald-News</i> reporter Cal Deal;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Larry_Elder" title="Larry Elder">Larry Elder</a>;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> Thomas Clough; Barbara Burns, the daughter of victim Hazel Tanis; George Kimball of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Irish_Times" title="The Irish Times">The Irish Times</a></i>;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> Milan Simonich of the <i><a href="/wiki/Pittsburgh_Post-Gazette" title="Pittsburgh Post-Gazette">Pittsburgh Post-Gazette</a></i>; Lona Manning;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/The_New_York_Times" title="The New York Times">The New York Times</a></i> reporter Robert Lipsyte;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> Paul Mulshine of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Star-Ledger" title="The Star-Ledger">The Star-Ledger</a></i>; and Jack Newfield of the <i><a href="/wiki/New_York_Post" title="New York Post">New York Post</a></i>, who stated, "I knew Rubin Carter, attended his fights, covered his retrial and I didn't see much reality on the screen."<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> <i><a href="/wiki/The_New_Yorker" title="The New Yorker">The New Yorker</a></i> film critic David Denby called it: "False, evasive and factually very thin – a liberal fairytale."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Lawsuit">Lawsuit</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Hurricane_(1999_film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Lawsuit">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Former <a href="/wiki/Middleweight" title="Middleweight">middleweight</a> World Champion <a href="/wiki/Joey_Giardello" title="Joey Giardello">Joey Giardello</a> sued the film's producers for <a href="/wiki/Defamation" title="Defamation">libel</a> over the depiction of his fight with Carter as a "racist fix." "This is a joke, [he told the <i><a href="/wiki/New_York_Daily_News" title="New York Daily News">New York Daily News</a></i>] he never hit me that much in 15 rounds. Virtually every boxing expert then and now will tell you I won the fight." Referee Robert Polis who scored the fight 72–66 in Giardello's favor stated: "They portrayed Joey Giardello as an incompetent fighter. I thought it was ludicrous."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> Even Carter himself agreed that Giardello was the rightful victor.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p>Eventually, the case was settled out of court, with the producers paying the retired champion damages and with Jewison agreeing to make a statement on the DVD version that "Giardello no doubt was a great fighter."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
