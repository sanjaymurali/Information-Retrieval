<p><b>Three-dimensional space</b> (also: <b>3-space</b> or, rarely, <b>tri-dimensional space</b>) is a geometric setting in which three values (called <a href="/wiki/Parameter" title="Parameter">parameters</a>) are required to determine the position of an element (i.e., <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Point_(mathematics)" title="Point (mathematics)">point</a>). This is the informal meaning of the term <a href="/wiki/Dimension" title="Dimension">dimension</a>.</p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Physics" title="Physics">physics</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mathematics" title="Mathematics">mathematics</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Euclidean_vector" title="Euclidean vector">sequence</a> of <span class="texhtml"><i>n</i></span> <a href="/wiki/Real_number" title="Real number">numbers</a> can be understood as a location in <span class="texhtml"><i>n</i></span>-dimensional space. When <span class="texhtml"><i>n</i> = 3</span>, the set of all such locations is called <span id="3D_EUCLIDEAN_SPACE"></span><b>three-dimensional <a href="/wiki/Euclidean_space" title="Euclidean space">Euclidean space</a></b>. It is commonly represented by the symbol <span class="texhtml">ℝ<sup>3</sup></span>. This serves as a three-parameter model of the physical <a href="/wiki/Universe" title="Universe">universe</a> (that is, the spatial part, without considering time) in which all known <a href="/wiki/Matter" title="Matter">matter</a> exists. However, this space is only one example of a large variety of spaces in three dimensions called <a href="/wiki/3-manifold" title="3-manifold">3-manifolds</a>. In this classical example, when the three values refer to measurements in different directions (<a href="/wiki/Coordinate_system" title="Coordinate system">coordinates</a>), any three directions can be chosen, provided that <a href="/wiki/Vector_space" title="Vector space">vectors</a> in these directions do not all lie in the same <a href="/wiki/Two-dimensional_space" title="Two-dimensional space">2-space</a> (<a href="/wiki/Plane_(geometry)" title="Plane (geometry)">plane</a>). Furthermore, in this case, these three values can be labeled by any combination of three chosen from the terms <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Width" title="Width">width</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Height" title="Height">height</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Elevation" title="Elevation">depth</a></i>, and <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Breadth" title="Breadth">breadth</a></i>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_euclidean_geometry">In euclidean geometry</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Three-dimensional_space&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: In euclidean geometry">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Coordinate_systems">Coordinate systems</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Three-dimensional_space&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Coordinate systems">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Coordinate_system" title="Coordinate system">Coordinate system</a></div>
<p>In mathematics, <a href="/wiki/Analytic_geometry" title="Analytic geometry">analytic geometry</a> (also called Cartesian geometry) describes every point in three-dimensional space by means of three coordinates. Three <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coordinate_axis" title="Coordinate axis">coordinate axes</a> are given, each perpendicular to the other two at the <a href="/wiki/Origin_(mathematics)" title="Origin (mathematics)">origin</a>, the point at which they cross. They are usually labeled <span class="texhtml"><i>x</i>, <i>y</i></span>, and <span class="texhtml"><i>z</i></span>. Relative to these axes, the position of any point in three-dimensional space is given by an ordered triple of <a href="/wiki/Real_number" title="Real number">real numbers</a>, each number giving the distance of that point from the <a href="/wiki/Origin_(mathematics)" title="Origin (mathematics)">origin</a> measured along the given axis, which is equal to the distance of that point from the plane determined by the other two axes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hughes_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hughes-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Other popular methods of describing the location of a point in three-dimensional space include <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cylindrical_coordinates" title="Cylindrical coordinates">cylindrical coordinates</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spherical_coordinates" title="Spherical coordinates">spherical coordinates</a>, though there are an infinite number of possible methods. See <a href="/wiki/Euclidean_space" title="Euclidean space">Euclidean space</a>.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Lines_and_planes">Lines and planes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Three-dimensional_space&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Lines and planes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Two distinct points always determine a (straight) <a href="/wiki/Line_(geometry)" title="Line (geometry)">line</a>. Three distinct points are either <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Collinear" title="Collinear">collinear</a> or determine a unique plane. Four distinct points can either be collinear, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coplanar" title="Coplanar">coplanar</a> or determine the entire space.</p>
<p>Two distinct lines can either intersect, be <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Parallel_line" title="Parallel line">parallel</a> or be <a href="/wiki/Skew_lines" title="Skew lines">skew</a>. Two parallel lines, or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Two_intersecting_lines" title="Two intersecting lines">two intersecting lines</a>, lie in a unique plane, so skew lines are lines that do not meet and do not lie in a common plane.</p>
<p>Two distinct planes can either meet in a common line or are parallel (do not meet). Three distinct planes, no pair of which are parallel, can either meet in a common line, meet in a unique common point or have no point in common. In the last case, the three lines of intersection of each pair of planes are mutually parallel.</p>
<p>A line can lie in a given plane, intersect that plane in a unique point or be parallel to the plane. In the last case, there will be lines in the plane that are parallel to the given line.</p>
<p>A <a href="/wiki/Hyperplane" title="Hyperplane">hyperplane</a> is a subspace of one dimension less than the dimension of the full space. The hyperplanes of a three-dimensional space are the two-dimensional subspaces, that is, the planes. In terms of cartesian coordinates, the points of a hyperplane satisfy a single <a href="/wiki/Linear_equation" title="Linear equation">linear equation</a>, so planes in this 3-space are described by linear equations. A line can be described by a pair of independent linear equations, each representing a plane having this line as a common intersection.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Varignon%27s_theorem" title="Varignon's theorem">Varignon's theorem</a> states that the midpoints of any quadrilateral in ℝ<sup>3</sup> form a <a href="/wiki/Parallelogram" title="Parallelogram">parallelogram</a>, and so, are coplanar.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Surfaces_of_revolution">Surfaces of revolution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Three-dimensional_space&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Surfaces of revolution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Surface_of_revolution" title="Surface of revolution">Surface of revolution</a></div>
<p>A <a href="/wiki/Surface_(topology)" title="Surface (topology)">surface</a> generated by revolving a plane <a href="/wiki/Curve" title="Curve">curve</a> about a fixed line in its plane as an axis is called a <a href="/wiki/Surface_of_revolution" title="Surface of revolution">surface of revolution</a>. The plane curve is called the <i>generatrix</i> of the surface. A section of the surface, made by intersecting the surface with a plane that is perpendicular (orthogonal) to the axis, is a circle.</p>
<p>Simple examples occur when the generatrix is a line. If the generatrix line intersects the axis line, the surface of revolution is a right circular <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cone_(mathematics)" title="Cone (mathematics)">cone</a> with vertex (apex) the point of intersection. However, if the generatrix and axis are parallel, the surface of revolution is a circular <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cylinder_(geometry)" title="Cylinder (geometry)">cylinder</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_linear_algebra">In linear algebra</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Three-dimensional_space&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: In linear algebra">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Another way of viewing three-dimensional space is found in <a href="/wiki/Linear_algebra" title="Linear algebra">linear algebra</a>, where the idea of independence is crucial. Space has three dimensions because the length of a <a href="/wiki/Cuboid" title="Cuboid">box</a> is independent of its width or breadth. In the technical language of linear algebra, space is three-dimensional because every point in space can be described by a linear combination of three independent <a href="/wiki/Coordinate_vector" title="Coordinate vector">vectors</a>.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cross_product">Cross product</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Three-dimensional_space&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Cross product">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Cross_product" title="Cross product">Cross product</a></div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Cross_product" title="Cross product">cross product</a> or <b>vector product</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Binary_operation" title="Binary operation">binary operation</a> on two <a href="/wiki/Euclidean_vector" title="Euclidean vector">vectors</a> in three-dimensional <a href="/wiki/Euclidean_space" title="Euclidean space">space</a> and is denoted by the symbol ×. The cross product <b>a</b> × <b>b</b> of the vectors <b>a</b> and <b>b</b> is a vector that is <a href="/wiki/Perpendicular" title="Perpendicular">perpendicular</a> to both and therefore <a href="/wiki/Normal_(geometry)" title="Normal (geometry)">normal</a> to the plane containing them. It has many applications in mathematics, <a href="/wiki/Physics" title="Physics">physics</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Engineering" title="Engineering">engineering</a>.</p>
<p>The space and product form an <a href="/wiki/Algebra_over_a_field" title="Algebra over a field">algebra over a field</a>, which is neither <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Commutative" title="Commutative">commutative</a> nor <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Associative" title="Associative">associative</a>, but is a <a href="/wiki/Lie_algebra" title="Lie algebra">Lie algebra</a> with the cross product being the Lie bracket.</p>
<p>One can in <i>n</i> dimensions take the product of <span class="nowrap"><i>n</i> − 1</span> vectors to produce a vector perpendicular to all of them. But if the product is limited to non-trivial binary products with vector results, it exists only in three and <a href="/wiki/Seven-dimensional_cross_product" title="Seven-dimensional cross product">seven dimensions</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Massey2_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Massey2-6">[6]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_topology">In topology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Three-dimensional_space&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: In topology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Three-dimensional space has a number of topological properties that distinguish it from spaces of other dimension numbers. For example, at least three dimensions are required to tie a <a href="/wiki/Knot_theory" title="Knot theory">knot</a> in a piece of string.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
