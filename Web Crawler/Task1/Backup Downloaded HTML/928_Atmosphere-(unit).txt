<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Atmosphere (unit)</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">
					Jump to:					<a href="#mw-head">navigation</a>, 					<a href="#p-search">search</a>
</div>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><p>The <b>standard atmosphere</b> (symbol: atm) is a unit of <a href="/wiki/Pressure" title="Pressure">pressure</a> defined as <span style="white-space: nowrap">101<span style="margin-left: 0.25em">325</span></span> <a href="/wiki/Pascal_(unit)" title="Pascal (unit)">Pa</a> (<span style="white-space: nowrap">1.013<span style="margin-left: 0.25em">25</span></span> bar). It is sometimes used as a <i>reference</i> or <i>standard pressure</i>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Atmosphere_(unit)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 1954 the 10th Conférence Générale des Poids et Mesures (<a href="/wiki/General_Conference_on_Weights_and_Measures" title="General Conference on Weights and Measures">CGPM</a>) adopted <i>standard atmosphere</i> for general use and affirmed its definition of being precisely equal to 1,013,250 <a href="/wiki/Dyne" title="Dyne">dynes</a> per <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Square_centimetre" title="Square centimetre">square centimetre</a> (101325 <a href="/wiki/Pascal_(unit)" title="Pascal (unit)">Pa</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BIPM_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-BIPM-1">[1]</a></sup> This value was intended to represent the mean atmospheric pressure at mean sea level at the latitude of <a href="/wiki/Paris" title="Paris">Paris, France</a>,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> and does reflect the mean sea level pressure for many industrialized nations that are at broadly similar latitudes.</p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Chemistry" title="Chemistry">chemistry</a> and in various industries, the reference pressure referred to in “<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Standard_Temperature_and_Pressure" title="Standard Temperature and Pressure">Standard Temperature and Pressure</a>” (STP) was commonly 1 atm (101.325 kPa) but standards have since diverged; in 1982, the <a href="/wiki/International_Union_of_Pure_and_Applied_Chemistry" title="International Union of Pure and Applied Chemistry">International Union of Pure and Applied Chemistry</a> (IUPAC) recommended that for the purposes of specifying the physical properties of substances, “<i>standard pressure</i>” should be precisely 100 <a href="/wiki/Pascal_(unit)" title="Pascal (unit)">kPa</a> (1 <a href="/wiki/Bar_(unit)" title="Bar (unit)">bar</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>

<p>A pressure of 1 atm can also be stated as:</p>
<dl>
<dd>≡ 1.01325 <a href="/wiki/Bar_(unit)" title="Bar (unit)">bar</a></dd>
<dd>≡ 101325 <a href="/wiki/Pascal_(unit)" title="Pascal (unit)">pascal</a> (Pa) or 101.325 kilopascal (kPa)</dd>
<dd>≡ 1013.25 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Millibar" title="Millibar">millibars</a> (mbar, also mb)</dd>
<dd>≡ 760 <a href="/wiki/Torr" title="Torr">torr</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[n 1]</a></sup></dd>
<dd>≈ 760.001 mm-Hg, 0 °C, subject to revision as more precise measurements of mercury’s density become available<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-n2_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-n2-4">[n 2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-n3_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-n3-5">[n 3]</a></sup></dd>
<dd>≈ 29.9213 in-Hg, 0 °C, subject to revision as more precise measurements of mercury’s density become available<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-n3_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-n3-5">[n 3]</a></sup></dd>
<dd>≈ 1.033 227 452 799 886 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kgf" title="Kgf">kgf</a>/cm²</dd>
<dd>≈ 1.033 227 452 799 886 <a href="/wiki/Technical_atmosphere" title="Technical atmosphere">technical atmosphere</a></dd>
<dd>≈ 1033.227 452 799 886 cm–H<sub>2</sub>O, 4 °C<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-n2_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-n2-4">[n 2]</a></sup></dd>
<dd>≈ 406.782 461 732 2385 in–H<sub>2</sub>O, 4 °C<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-n2_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-n2-4">[n 2]</a></sup></dd>
<dd>≈ 14.695 948 775 5134 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pound-force_per_square_inch" title="Pound-force per square inch">pounds-force per square inch</a> (psi)</dd>
<dd>≈ 2116.216 623 673 94 pounds-force per square foot (psf)</dd>
<dd>= 1 ata (atmosphere absolute). The ata unit is used in place of atm to indicate that the pressure shown is the total ambient pressure, compared to vacuum, of the system being calculated or measured.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[3]</a></sup> For example, for underwater pressures, a pressure of 3.1 ata would mean that the 1 atm of the air above water is included in this value and the pressure due to water would total 2.1 atm.</dd>
</dl>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_applications">Other applications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Atmosphere_(unit)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Other applications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Scuba_diver" title="Scuba diver">Scuba divers</a> and others use the word <i>atmosphere</i> and "atm" in relation to pressures that are relative to mean atmospheric pressure at sea level (1.013 bar).<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> For example, a partial pressure of oxygen is calibrated typically using air at sea level, so is expressed in units of atm.</p>
<p>The old European unit <a href="/wiki/Technical_atmosphere" title="Technical atmosphere">technical atmosphere</a> (at) is equal to 1 <a href="/wiki/Kilogram-force_per_square_centimetre" title="Kilogram-force per square centimetre">kilogram-force per square centimetre</a> (kgf/cm<sup>2</sup>), <span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">7004980665000000000♠</span>98<span style="margin-left:.25em;">066</span>.5</span> Pa.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
