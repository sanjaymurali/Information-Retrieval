<p><i><b>Hurricane</b></i> is the third <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Studio_album" title="Studio album">studio album</a> by American <a href="/wiki/Contemporary_R%26B" title="Contemporary R&amp;B">R&amp;B</a> recording artist <a href="/wiki/Eric_Ben%C3%A9t" title="Eric Benét">Eric Benét</a>, released June 21, 2005, on Friday Records, with distributing by <a href="/wiki/Reprise_Records" title="Reprise Records">Reprise Records</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> It is the follow-up to his second album <i><a href="/wiki/A_Day_in_the_Life_(Eric_Ben%C3%A9t_album)" title="A Day in the Life (Eric Benét album)">A Day in the Life</a></i> (1999) and follows the shelving of his <i><a href="/wiki/Better_and_Better" title="Better and Better">Better and Better</a></i> project for <a href="/wiki/Warner_Bros._Records" title="Warner Bros. Records">Warner Bros. Records</a>. Recorded at various recording studios, the album was written and produced by Benét with several other producers, including Walter Afanasieff, Michael Carney, Hod David, David Foster, George Nash, Jr., and Demonté Posey.</p>

<p>Recording sessions for the album took place at various recording locations, including the Hit Factory in New York, New York, Bowery Digital, Chalice Studios, Ocean Way Studio, The Studio, Warner Bros. Sound Stage, and Westlake Audio in Los Angeles, California, Chartmaker Studios, Drew's Groove Studios, Panic Room, and Soundstage Studio in Nashville, Tennessee, Jade Studios and Jupiter Studios in Franklin, Tennessee, and Eastman Scoring Stage in Burbank, California.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-notes_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-notes-2">[2]</a></sup> Production was handled by Benét and several other record producers, including Walter Afanasieff, Michael Carney, Hod David, Dave Foster, George Nash, Jr., and Demonté Posey.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-credits_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-credits-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Composition">Composition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hurricane_(Eric_Ben%C3%A9t_album)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Composition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><i>Hurricane</i> incorporates musical elements of <a href="/wiki/Gospel_music" title="Gospel music">gospel</a> and contributions from <a href="/wiki/Jazz" title="Jazz">jazz</a> musicians <a href="/wiki/Roy_Hargrove" title="Roy Hargrove">Roy Hargrove</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chris_Botti" title="Chris Botti">Chris Botti</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Viktor_Krauss" title="Viktor Krauss">Viktor Krauss</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Post_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-Post-4">[4]</a></sup> A departure from the jazzy, <a href="/wiki/Groove_(music)" title="Groove (music)">groove</a>-based <a href="/wiki/Urban_contemporary" title="Urban contemporary">urban</a> sound of Benét's previous work, the album features an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Adult_contemporary" title="Adult contemporary">adult contemporary</a> radio format style, with sweeping strings, layered vocals, and nimble acoustic guitars.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ollison_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ollison-7">[7]</a></sup> According to Rashod D. Ollison of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Washington_Post" title="The Washington Post">The Washington Post</a></i>, "<i>Hurricane</i> is lush in spots, the overall sound more in the adult contemporary vein. More <a href="/wiki/Christopher_Cross" title="Christopher Cross">Christopher Cross</a>, less <a href="/wiki/R._Kelly" title="R. Kelly">R. Kelly</a>".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ollison_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-Ollison-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reception">Reception</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hurricane_(Eric_Ben%C3%A9t_album)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Reception">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Allmusic" title="Allmusic">Allmusic</a> writer <a href="/wiki/Stephen_Thomas_Erlewine" title="Stephen Thomas Erlewine">Stephen Thomas Erlewine</a> gave it three-and-a-half out of four stars and commented that "The <a href="/wiki/Quiet_storm" title="Quiet storm">quiet storm</a> is well performed but dull, yet the <a href="/wiki/Prince_(musician)" title="Prince (musician)">Prince</a>-styled numbers are engaging, melodic, and nimble, strong showcases for Benet's mellow skills".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Erlewine_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Erlewine-1">[1]</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/Mojo_(magazine)" title="Mojo (magazine)">Mojo</a></i> gave the album three out of five stars and called it "a very palatable collection of self-penned confessional songs".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-notes_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-notes-2">[2]</a></sup> Laura Checkoway of <i><a href="/wiki/Vibe_(magazine)" title="Vibe (magazine)">Vibe</a></i> called it "an uneven mix of simplistic sentimentality", stating "Reflective and repentant at times, <i>Hurricane</i> is more likely to sing you to sleep than knock you off your feet".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Checkoway_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-Checkoway-3">[3]</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/The_Washington_Post" title="The Washington Post">The Washington Post</a></i> commented favorably on the album's themes of "healing and redemption", and stated "The spiritual tone often brings out the best in [Eric Benet]'s supple tenor on 'In The End' and other tracks".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Post_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-Post-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
