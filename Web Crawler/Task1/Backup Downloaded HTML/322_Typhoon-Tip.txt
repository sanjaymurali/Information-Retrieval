<p><b>Typhoon Tip</b>, known in the Philippines as <b>Typhoon Warling</b>, was the largest and most intense <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone" title="Tropical cyclone">tropical cyclone</a> ever recorded. The nineteenth storm and twelfth typhoon of the <a href="/wiki/1979_Pacific_typhoon_season" title="1979 Pacific typhoon season">1979 Pacific typhoon season</a>, Tip developed out of a disturbance from the <a href="/wiki/Monsoon_trough" title="Monsoon trough">monsoon trough</a> on October 4 near <a href="/wiki/Pohnpei" title="Pohnpei">Pohnpei</a>. Initially, a tropical storm to the northwest hindered the development and motion of Tip, though after it tracked farther north Tip was able to intensify. After passing <a href="/wiki/Guam" title="Guam">Guam</a>, Tip <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rapid_deepening" title="Rapid deepening">rapidly intensified</a> and reached peak winds of 305 km/h (190 mph)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[nb 1]</a></sup> and a worldwide record-low <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_pressure" title="Atmospheric pressure">sea-level pressure</a> of 870 <a href="/wiki/Bar_(unit)" title="Bar (unit)">mbar</a> (870.0 <a href="/wiki/Pascal_(unit)" title="Pascal (unit)">hPa</a>; 25.69 <a href="/wiki/Inch_of_mercury" title="Inch of mercury">inHg</a>) on October 12. At its peak strength, it was also the largest tropical cyclone on record with a wind diameter of 2,220 km (1,380 mi). Tip slowly weakened as it continued west-northwestward and later turned to the northeast in response to an approaching <a href="/wiki/Trough_(meteorology)" title="Trough (meteorology)">trough</a>. The typhoon made <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Landfall_(meteorology)" title="Landfall (meteorology)">landfall</a> in southern Japan on October 19, and became an <a href="/wiki/Extratropical_cyclone" title="Extratropical cyclone">extratropical cyclone</a> shortly thereafter.</p>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Air_Force_Weather_Agency" title="Air Force Weather Agency">U.S. Air Force aircraft</a> flew 60 <a href="/wiki/Weather_reconnaissance" title="Weather reconnaissance">weather reconnaissance</a> missions into the typhoon, making Tip one of the most closely observed tropical cyclones.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jtwc_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-jtwc-2">[1]</a></sup> Rainfall from Tip indirectly led to a fire that killed 13 <a href="/wiki/United_States_Marine_Corps" title="United States Marine Corps">Marines</a> and injured 68 at <a href="/wiki/Camp_Fuji" title="Camp Fuji">Combined Arms Training Center, Camp Fuji</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Shizuoka_Prefecture" title="Shizuoka Prefecture">Shizuoka Prefecture</a> of Japan.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[2]</a></sup> Elsewhere in the country, the typhoon caused widespread flooding and 42 deaths; offshore shipwrecks left 44 people killed or missing.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Meteorological_history">Meteorological history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Typhoon_Tip&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Meteorological history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Tip_1979_track.png"><img alt="Satellite image of the path of the typhoon. It starts in the Pacific Ocean east of the Philippines, arcs through Japan, and ends near the Aleutian Islands." class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2280" data-file-width="2700" height="186" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d0/Tip_1979_track.png/220px-Tip_1979_track.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d0/Tip_1979_track.png/330px-Tip_1979_track.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d0/Tip_1979_track.png/440px-Tip_1979_track.png 2x" width="220"/></a>

<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Tip_1979_track.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>

<p>Three circulations developed within the <a href="/wiki/Monsoon_trough" title="Monsoon trough">monsoon trough</a> that extended from the <a href="/wiki/Philippines" title="Philippines">Philippines</a> to the <a href="/wiki/Marshall_Islands" title="Marshall Islands">Marshall Islands</a>, at the beginning of October 1979. The westernmost disturbance developed into <a href="/wiki/1979_Pacific_typhoon_season#Typhoon_Sarah_.28Trining-Uring.29" title="1979 Pacific typhoon season">Typhoon Sarah</a> west of <a href="/wiki/Luzon" title="Luzon">Luzon</a>, on October 1. On October 3, a disturbance southwest of <a href="/wiki/Guam" title="Guam">Guam</a> developed into <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tropical_Storm_Roger_(Sisang)" title="Tropical Storm Roger (Sisang)">Tropical Storm Roger</a>, and later on the same day, a third tropical disturbance that would later become Typhoon Tip formed south of <a href="/wiki/Pohnpei" title="Pohnpei">Pohnpei</a>. Strong flow from across the <a href="/wiki/Equator" title="Equator">equator</a> was drawn into Roger's wind circulation, initially preventing significant <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclogenesis" title="Tropical cyclogenesis">development</a> of the precursor disturbance to Tip. Despite the unfavorable air pattern, the tropical disturbance gradually organized as it moved westward. Due to the large-scale circulation pattern of Tropical Storm Roger, Tip's precursor moved erratically and slowly executed a cyclonic loop to the southeast of <a href="/wiki/Chuuk_State" title="Chuuk State">Chuuk</a>. A <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hurricane_Hunters" title="Hurricane Hunters">reconnaissance aircraft</a> flight into the system late on October 4 confirmed the existence of a closed low-level circulation, and early on October 5, the <a href="/wiki/Joint_Typhoon_Warning_Center" title="Joint Typhoon Warning Center">Joint Typhoon Warning Center</a> (JTWC) issued its first warning on Tropical Depression Twenty-Three.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jtwc_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-jtwc-2">[1]</a></sup></p>

<p>While executing a loop near Chuuk, the tropical depression intensified into Tropical Storm Tip, though the storm failed to organize significantly due to the influence of Tropical Storm Roger. Reconnaissance aircraft provided the track of the surface circulation, since satellite imagery estimated the center was about 60 km (37 mi) from its true position. After drifting erratically for several days, Tip began a steady northwest motion on October 8. By that time, Tropical Storm Roger had become an <a href="/wiki/Extratropical_cyclone" title="Extratropical cyclone">extratropical cyclone</a>, resulting in the southerly flow to be entrained into Tip. An area of a <a href="/wiki/Tropical_upper_tropospheric_trough" title="Tropical upper tropospheric trough">tropical upper tropospheric trough</a> moved north of Guam at the time, providing an excellent <a href="/wiki/Outflow_(meteorology)" title="Outflow (meteorology)">outflow channel</a> north of Tip. Initially, Tip was predicted to continue northwestward and make <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Landfall_(meteorology)" title="Landfall (meteorology)">landfall</a> on Guam, though it turned to the west early on October 9, passing about 45 km (28 mi) south of Guam. Later that day, Tip intensified to attain typhoon status.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jtwc_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-jtwc-2">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Owing to very favorable conditions for development, Typhoon Tip <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rapid_deepening" title="Rapid deepening">rapidly intensified</a> over the open waters of the western <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Ocean" title="Pacific Ocean">Pacific Ocean</a>. Late on October 10, Tip attained wind speeds equal to Category 4 strength on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saffir%E2%80%93Simpson_Hurricane_Scale" title="Saffir–Simpson Hurricane Scale">Saffir–Simpson Hurricane Scale</a>, and it became a <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone_scales" title="Tropical cyclone scales">super typhoon</a> the next day. The central <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_pressure" title="Atmospheric pressure">pressure</a> dropped by 92 <a href="/wiki/Bar_(unit)" title="Bar (unit)">mbar</a> (92.0 hPa; 2.72 inHg) from October 9 to 11, during which the circulation pattern of Typhoon Tip expanded to a record diameter of 2,220 km (1,380 mi). Tip continued to intensify further, and early on October 12 reconnaissance aircraft recorded a worldwide record-low pressure of 870 mbar (870.0 hPa; 25.69 inHg) with winds of 305 km/h (190 mph), when Tip was located about 840 km (520 mi) west-northwest of Guam.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jtwc_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-jtwc-2">[1]</a></sup> In its best track, the <a href="/wiki/Japan_Meteorological_Agency" title="Japan Meteorological Agency">Japan Meteorological Agency</a> listed Tip as peaking with 10-minute sustained winds of 160 mph (260 km/h).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jma_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-jma-4">[3]</a></sup> At the time of its peak strength, its <a href="/wiki/Eye_(cyclone)" title="Eye (cyclone)">eye</a> was 15 km (9.3 mi) wide.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jtwc_2-4"><a href="#cite_note-jtwc-2">[1]</a></sup> Tip crossed the <a href="/wiki/135th_meridian_east" title="135th meridian east">135th meridian east</a> on the afternoon of October 13, prompting the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Philippine_Atmospheric,_Geophysical_and_Astronomical_Services_Administration" title="Philippine Atmospheric, Geophysical and Astronomical Services Administration">Philippine Atmospheric, Geophysical and Astronomical Services Administration</a> to issue warnings on Typhoon Tip, assigning it the local name <i>Warling</i>.</p>
<p>After peaking intensity, Tip weakened to 230 km/h (140 mph) and remained at that intensity for several days as it continued west-northwestward. For five days after its peak strength, the average radius of winds stronger than 55 km/h (34 mph) extended over 1,100 km (684 mi). On October 17, Tip began to weaken steadily and decrease in size, recurving northeastward under the influence of a mid-level <a href="/wiki/Trough_(meteorology)" title="Trough (meteorology)">trough</a> the next day. After passing about 65 km (40 mi) east of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Okinawa" title="Okinawa">Okinawa</a>, the typhoon accelerated to 75 km/h (47 mph). Tip made landfall on the Japanese island of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Honsh%C5%AB" title="Honshū">Honshū</a> with winds of about 130 km/h (81 mph) on October 19. It continued rapidly northeastward through the country and became an extratropical cyclone over northern Honshū a few hours after moving ashore.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jtwc_2-5"><a href="#cite_note-jtwc-2">[1]</a></sup> The extratropical remnant of Tip proceeded northeastward and gradually weakened, crossing the <a href="/wiki/International_Date_Line" title="International Date Line">International Date Line</a> on October 22. The storm was last observed near the <a href="/wiki/Aleutian_Islands" title="Aleutian Islands">Aleutian Islands</a> of <a href="/wiki/Alaska" title="Alaska">Alaska</a> on October 24.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jma_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-jma-4">[3]</a></sup></p>

<p>The typhoon produced heavy rainfall early in its lifetime while passing near Guam, including a total of 23.1 cm (9.09 in) at <a href="/wiki/Andersen_Air_Force_Base" title="Andersen Air Force Base">Andersen Air Force Base</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jtwc_2-6"><a href="#cite_note-jtwc-2">[1]</a></sup> The outer <a href="/wiki/Rainband" title="Rainband">rainbands</a> of the large circulation of Tip produced moderate rainfall in the mountainous regions of the Philippine island of <a href="/wiki/Luzon" title="Luzon">Luzon</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwl_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-mwl-6">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>Heavy rainfall from the typhoon breached a flood-retaining wall at <a href="/wiki/Camp_Fuji" title="Camp Fuji">Camp Fuji</a>, a training facility for the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Marine_Corps" title="United States Marine Corps">United States Marine Corps</a> near <a href="/wiki/Yokosuka,_Kanagawa" title="Yokosuka, Kanagawa">Yokosuka</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-usn_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-usn-7">[6]</a></sup> Marines inside the camp weathered the storm inside huts situated at the base of a hill which housed a fuel farm. The breach led to hoses being dislodged from two rubber storage bladders, releasing large quantities of fuel. The fuel flowed down the hill and was ignited by a heater used to warm one of the huts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mcb_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-mcb-8">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[9]</a></sup> The resultant fire killed 13 Marines, injured 68,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jtwc_2-7"><a href="#cite_note-jtwc-2">[1]</a></sup> and caused moderate damage to the facility. The facility's barracks were destroyed,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-usn_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-usn-7">[6]</a></sup> along with fifteen huts and several other structures.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mcb_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-mcb-8">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[10]</a></sup> The barracks were rebuilt,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-usn_7-2"><a href="#cite_note-usn-7">[6]</a></sup> and a memorial was established for those who lost their lives in the fire.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mcb_8-2"><a href="#cite_note-mcb-8">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>During recurvature, Typhoon Tip passed about 65 km (40 mi) east of Okinawa. Sustained winds reached 72 km/h (44 mph), with gusts to 112 km/h (69 mph). Sustained wind velocities in Japan are not known, though they were estimated at minimal typhoon strength. The passage of the typhoon through the region resulted in millions of dollars in damage to the agricultural and fishing industries of the country.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jtwc_2-8"><a href="#cite_note-jtwc-2">[1]</a></sup> Eight ships were grounded or sunk by Tip, leaving 44 fishermen dead or unaccounted for. A Chinese freighter broke in half as a result of the typhoon, though its crew of 46 were rescued.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwl_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-mwl-6">[5]</a></sup> The rainfall led to over 600 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mudslide" title="Mudslide">mudslides</a> throughout the mountainous regions of Japan and flooded more than 22,000 homes; 42 people died throughout the country, with another 71 missing and 283 injured.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwl_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-mwl-6">[5]</a></sup> River embankments broke in 70 places, destroying 27 bridges, while about 105 dikes were destroyed. Following the storm, at least 11,000 people were left homeless. Tip destroyed apple, rice, peach and other crops. Five ships sank in heavy seas off the coast and 50-story buildings swayed in the capital, <a href="/wiki/Tokyo" title="Tokyo">Tokyo</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[12]</a></sup> Transportation in the country was disrupted; 200 trains and 160 domestic flights were canceled.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[13]</a></sup> Tip was described as the most severe storm to strike Japan in 13 years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[14]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Records_and_meteorological_statistics">Records and meteorological statistics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Typhoon_Tip&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Records and meteorological statistics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:290px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Typhoonsizes.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="180" data-file-width="288" height="180" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/4/4e/Typhoonsizes.jpg" width="288"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">Depictions of Typhoon Tip and <a href="/wiki/Cyclone_Tracy" title="Cyclone Tracy">Cyclone Tracy</a> (one of the smallest tropical cyclones ever recorded) superimposed to-scale on a map of the United States.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Typhoon Tip was the largest tropical cyclone on record, with a diameter of 1,380 mi (2,220 km)—almost double the previous record of 700 mi (1,130 km) set by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/1950%E2%80%9359_Pacific_typhoon_seasons#1951_Pacific_typhoon_season" title="1950–59 Pacific typhoon seasons">Typhoon Marge</a> in August 1951.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[17]</a></sup> At its largest, Tip was nearly half the size of the <a href="/wiki/Contiguous_United_States" title="Contiguous United States">contiguous United States</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[18]</a></sup> The temperature inside the <a href="/wiki/Eye_(cyclone)" title="Eye (cyclone)">eye</a> of Typhoon Tip at peak intensity was 30 <a href="/wiki/Celsius" title="Celsius">°C</a> (86 <a href="/wiki/Fahrenheit" title="Fahrenheit">°F</a>) and described as exceptionally high.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jtwc_2-9"><a href="#cite_note-jtwc-2">[1]</a></sup> With 10-minute sustained winds of 160 mph (260 km/h), Typhoon Tip is the strongest cyclone in the complete tropical cyclone listing by the Japan Meteorological Agency.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jma_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-jma-4">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>The typhoon was also the most intense tropical cyclone on record, with a pressure of 870 mbar (25.69 inHg), 6 mbar (0.18 inHg) lower than the previous record set by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Typhoon_June_(1975)" title="Typhoon June (1975)">Super Typhoon June</a> in <a href="/wiki/1975_Pacific_typhoon_season" title="1975 Pacific typhoon season">1975</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jtwc_2-10"><a href="#cite_note-jtwc-2">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[19]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[20]</a></sup> The records set by Tip still technically stand, though with the end of routine <a href="/wiki/Reconnaissance_aircraft" title="Reconnaissance aircraft">reconnaissance aircraft</a> flights in the western Pacific Ocean in August 1987, modern researchers have questioned whether Tip indeed remains the strongest. After a detailed study, three researchers determined that two typhoons, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Typhoon_Angela_(1995)" title="Typhoon Angela (1995)">Angela</a> in 1995 and <a href="/wiki/Typhoon_Gay_(1992)" title="Typhoon Gay (1992)">Gay</a> in 1992, registered higher <a href="/wiki/Dvorak_technique" title="Dvorak technique">Dvorak numbers</a> than Tip, and concluded that one or both of the two may have therefore been more intense.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ams_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-ams-22">[21]</a></sup> Other recent storms may have also been deeper than Tip at its peak; for instance, satellite-derived intensity estimates for <a href="/wiki/Typhoon_Haiyan" title="Typhoon Haiyan">Typhoon Haiyan</a> of <a href="/wiki/2013_Pacific_typhoon_season" title="2013 Pacific typhoon season">2013</a> indicated that its core pressure may have been as low as 858 mbar (25.34 inHg).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[22]</a></sup> Due to the dearth of <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone_observation" title="Tropical cyclone observation">direct observations</a> into these cyclones, conclusive data are lacking.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ams_22-1"><a href="#cite_note-ams-22">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p>Despite the typhoon's intensity and damage, the <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone_naming" title="Tropical cyclone naming">name</a> <i>Tip</i> was not <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_retired_tropical_cyclone_names" title="List of retired tropical cyclone names">retired</a> and was reused in <a href="/wiki/1983_Pacific_typhoon_season" title="1983 Pacific typhoon season">1983</a>, <a href="/wiki/1986_Pacific_typhoon_season" title="1986 Pacific typhoon season">1986</a>, and <a href="/wiki/1989_Pacific_typhoon_season" title="1989 Pacific typhoon season">1989</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jma_4-3"><a href="#cite_note-jma-4">[3]</a></sup></p>
