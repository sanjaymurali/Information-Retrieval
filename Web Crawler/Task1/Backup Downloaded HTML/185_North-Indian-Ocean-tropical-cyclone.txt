<p>In the <a href="/wiki/Indian_Ocean" title="Indian Ocean">Indian Ocean</a> north of the <a href="/wiki/Equator" title="Equator">equator</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone" title="Tropical cyclone">tropical cyclones</a> can form throughout the year on either side of <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a>. On the east side is the <a href="/wiki/Bay_of_Bengal" title="Bay of Bengal">Bay of Bengal</a>, and on the west side is the <a href="/wiki/Arabian_Sea" title="Arabian Sea">Arabian Sea</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Sub-basins">Sub-basins</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=North_Indian_Ocean_tropical_cyclone&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Sub-basins">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Bay of Bengal, located in the northeast of the Indian Ocean, is responsible for the formation of some of the strongest and deadliest tropical cyclones in the world. The basin is abbreviated <i>BOB</i> by the <a href="/wiki/India_Meteorological_Department" title="India Meteorological Department">India Meteorological Department</a> (IMD), the official <a href="/wiki/Regional_Specialized_Meteorological_Center" title="Regional Specialized Meteorological Center">Regional Specialized Meteorological Center</a> of the basin. The Bay of Bengal's coast is shared among <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bangladesh" title="Bangladesh">Bangladesh</a>, <a href="/wiki/Myanmar" title="Myanmar">Myanmar</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sri_Lanka" title="Sri Lanka">Sri Lanka</a> and western part of <a href="/wiki/Thailand" title="Thailand">Thailand</a>. The strongest storm in the bay was the <a href="/wiki/1999_Odisha_cyclone" title="1999 Odisha cyclone">1999 Odisha cyclone</a>. As the remnants impact <a href="/wiki/Tibet" title="Tibet">Tibet</a> and <a href="/wiki/Yunnan" title="Yunnan">Yunnan</a> sometimes, the <a href="/wiki/National_Meteorological_Center_of_CMA" title="National Meteorological Center of CMA">National Meteorological Center of CMA</a> also issues full advisories to cyclonic storms in this basin.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Arabian Sea is a sea located in the northwest of the Indian Ocean. Tropical cyclones in the basin are abbreviated <i>ARB</i> by the <a href="/wiki/India_Meteorological_Department" title="India Meteorological Department">India Meteorological Department</a> (IMD), the official <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Regional_Specialized_Meteorological_Centre" title="Regional Specialized Meteorological Centre">Regional Specialized Meteorological Centre</a> (RSMC) of the basin. The Arabian Sea's coast is shared among <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a>, <a href="/wiki/Yemen" title="Yemen">Yemen</a>, <a href="/wiki/Oman" title="Oman">Oman</a>, <a href="/wiki/Iran" title="Iran">Iran</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pakistan" title="Pakistan">Pakistan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sri_Lanka" title="Sri Lanka">Sri Lanka</a>, <a href="/wiki/Maldives" title="Maldives">Maldives</a> and <a href="/wiki/Somalia" title="Somalia">Somalia</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mahalo.com_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-mahalo.com-2">[2]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Monsoons" title="Monsoons">Monsoons</a> are characteristic of the Arabian Sea and responsible for the yearly cycling of its waters. In summer, strong winds blow from the southwest to the northeast, bringing rain to the <a href="/wiki/Indian_subcontinent" title="Indian subcontinent">Indian subcontinent</a>. During the winter, the winds are milder and blow in the opposite direction, from the northeast to the southwest.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mahalo.com_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-mahalo.com-2">[2]</a></sup> Cyclones are rare in the Arabian Sea, but the basin can produce strong tropical cyclones. <a href="/wiki/Cyclone_Gonu" title="Cyclone Gonu">Super Cyclonic Storm Gonu</a> was the strongest recorded tropical cyclone in the basin.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> However, storms typically do not reach a high intensity in the Arabian Sea due to the small amount of water, dry air coming from the desert of the <a href="/wiki/Arabian_Peninsula" title="Arabian Peninsula">Arabian Peninsula</a>, and unfavorable wind shear from the monsoon.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History_of_the_basin">History of the basin</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=North_Indian_Ocean_tropical_cyclone&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History of the basin">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The systematic scientific studies of tropical systems in the <a href="/wiki/Bay_of_Bengal" title="Bay of Bengal">Bay of Bengal</a> and <a href="/wiki/Arabian_Sea" title="Arabian Sea">Arabian Sea</a> was started during the 19th century by <a href="/wiki/Henry_Piddington" title="Henry Piddington">Henry Piddington</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IMD_BT_Report_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-IMD_BT_Report-7">[7]</a></sup> Piddington utilised meteorological logs of vessels that navigated the seas and published a series of memoirs, in the “Journal of the Asiatic Society of Bengal” between 1839 and 1858.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IMD_BT_Report_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-IMD_BT_Report-7">[7]</a></sup> These memoirs gave accounts and tracks of individual storms in the Bay of Bengal and the Arabian Sea.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IMD_BT_Report_7-2"><a href="#cite_note-IMD_BT_Report-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>During the 2004 post monsoon season the IMD started to name tropical cyclones within the basin, with the first one named <a href="/wiki/Cyclone_Onil" title="Cyclone Onil">Cyclone Onil</a> during September 2004.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IMD_2004_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-IMD_2004-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>During 2015 a modification to the intensity scale took place, with the IMD and WMO calling a system with 3-minute maximum sustained wind speeds between 90 knots (165 km/h; 105 mph) and 120 knots (220 km/h; 140 mph) an extremely severe cyclonic storm.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>Water temperatures in the Arabian Sea are typically warm enough to allow for <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclogenesis" title="Tropical cyclogenesis">tropical cyclogenesis</a> year round, although strong <a href="/wiki/Wind_shear" title="Wind shear">wind shear</a> from the <a href="/wiki/Monsoon_trough" title="Monsoon trough">monsoon trough</a> prevents formation in the summer months and limits intensity other times of the year. An increase in air pollution since the 1930s caused a decrease in the wind shear, allowing storms to have become stronger since 1979.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Notes">Notes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=North_Indian_Ocean_tropical_cyclone&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Notes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A study analyzing the spring season of tropical cyclones in the Bay of Bengal<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[15]</a></sup> found increases in both premonsoon precipitation and tropical cyclone intensity as a result of enhanced large-scale monsoon circulation after 1979. The deepened monsoon trough in the Bay of Bengal not only affects cyclone frequency and timing, but also acts to direct more cyclones towards Myanmar. Increased <a class="external text" href="http://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1002/jgrd.50396/abstract" rel="nofollow">anthropogenic aerosols</a> likely contributed to such a regional climate change.</p>
