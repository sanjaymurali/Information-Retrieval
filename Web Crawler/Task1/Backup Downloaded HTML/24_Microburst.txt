<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Microburst</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">

<p>A <b>microburst</b> is a small <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Downdraft" title="Downdraft">downdraft</a> that moves in a way opposite to a <a href="/wiki/Tornado" title="Tornado">tornado</a>. Microbursts are found in strong thunderstorms.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> There are two types of microbursts within a <a href="/wiki/Thunderstorm" title="Thunderstorm">thunderstorm</a>: wet microbursts and dry microbursts. They go through three stages in their cycle, the downburst, outburst, and cushion stages. A microburst can be particularly dangerous to <a href="/wiki/Aircraft" title="Aircraft">aircraft</a>, especially during landing, due to the <a href="/wiki/Wind_shear" title="Wind shear">wind shear</a> caused by its <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gust_front" title="Gust front">gust front</a>. Several fatal and historic crashes have been attributed to the phenomenon over the past several decades, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Flight_crew" title="Flight crew">flight crew</a> training goes to great lengths on how to properly recover from a microburst/wind shear event.</p>
<p>A microburst often has high <a href="/wiki/Wind" title="Wind">winds</a> that can knock over fully grown trees. They usually last from a couple of seconds to several minutes.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History_of_term">History of term</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Microburst&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History of term">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The term was defined by <a href="/wiki/Mesoscale_meteorology" title="Mesoscale meteorology">mesoscale meteorology</a> expert <a href="/wiki/Ted_Fujita" title="Ted Fujita">Ted Fujita</a> as affecting an area 4 km (2.5 mi) in diameter or less, distinguishing them as a type of <b><a href="/wiki/Downburst" title="Downburst">downburst</a></b> and apart from common wind shear which can encompass greater areas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> Fujita also coined the term <b>macroburst</b> for downbursts larger than 4 km (2.5 mi).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>A distinction can be made between a <b>wet microburst</b> which consists of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Precipitation_(meteorology)" title="Precipitation (meteorology)">precipitation</a> and a <b>dry microburst</b> which typically consists of <a href="/wiki/Virga" title="Virga">virga</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-micro_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-micro-4">[4]</a></sup> They generally are formed by precipitation-cooled air rushing to the surface, but they perhaps also could be powered from the high speed winds of the <a href="/wiki/Jet_stream" title="Jet stream">jet stream</a> deflected toward the surface by a thunderstorm or by dynamical processes (see <a href="/wiki/Rear_flank_downdraft" title="Rear flank downdraft">rear flank downdraft</a>).</p>
<p>Microbursts are recognized as capable of generating wind speeds higher than 75 <a href="/wiki/Metre_per_second" title="Metre per second">m/s</a> (170 <a href="/wiki/Miles_per_hour" title="Miles per hour">mph</a>; 270 <a href="/wiki/Kilometres_per_hour" title="Kilometres per hour">km/h</a>).</p>
<p>Microbursts have also been called <i>air bombs.</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Today20161021_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Today20161021-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Dry_microbursts">Dry microbursts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Microburst&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Dry microbursts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Drymicroburst.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="384" data-file-width="512" height="188" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/21/Drymicroburst.jpg/250px-Drymicroburst.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/21/Drymicroburst.jpg/375px-Drymicroburst.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/21/Drymicroburst.jpg/500px-Drymicroburst.jpg 2x" width="250"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Drymicroburst.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Dry microburst schematic</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>When rain falls below the <a href="/wiki/Cloud_base" title="Cloud base">cloud base</a> or is mixed with dry air, it begins to evaporate and this evaporation process cools the air. The cool air descends and accelerates as it approaches the ground. When the cool air approaches the ground, it spreads out in all directions and this divergence of the wind is the signature of the microburst. High winds spread out in this type of pattern showing little or no curvature are known as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Straight-line_wind" title="Straight-line wind">straight-line winds</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>Dry <b>microbursts</b>, produced by high based thunderstorms that generate little to no surface rainfall, occur in environments characterized by a thermodynamic profile exhibiting an inverted-V at thermal and moisture profile, as viewed on a <a href="/wiki/Skew-T_log-P_diagram" title="Skew-T log-P diagram">Skew-T log-P thermodynamic diagram</a>. Wakimoto (1985) developed a conceptual model (over the <a href="/wiki/High_Plains_(United_States)" title="High Plains (United States)">High Plains</a> of the United States) of a dry microburst environment that comprised three important variables: mid-level moisture, a deep and dry adiabatic <a href="/wiki/Lapse_rate" title="Lapse rate">lapse rate</a> in the sub-cloud layer, and low surface <a href="/wiki/Relative_humidity" title="Relative humidity">relative humidity</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Wet_microbursts">Wet microbursts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Microburst&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Wet microbursts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Wetmicroburst.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="384" data-file-width="512" height="188" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1f/Wetmicroburst.jpg/250px-Wetmicroburst.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1f/Wetmicroburst.jpg/375px-Wetmicroburst.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1f/Wetmicroburst.jpg/500px-Wetmicroburst.jpg 2x" width="250"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Wetmicroburst.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Wet microburst schematic</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Wet microbursts are downbursts accompanied by significant precipitation at the surface which are warmer<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Accuracy_dispute#Disputed_statement" title="Wikipedia:Accuracy dispute"><span title="The material near this tag is possibly inaccurate or nonfactual. (July 2016)">dubious</span></a> <span class="metadata">– <a href="/wiki/Talk:Microburst#Dubious" title="Talk:Microburst">discuss</a></span></i>]</sup> than their environment (Wakimoto, 1998).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fuj85_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-fuj85-7">[7]</a></sup> These downbursts rely more on the drag of precipitation for downward acceleration of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Air_parcel" title="Air parcel">parcels</a> than negative buoyancy which tend to drive "dry" microbursts. As a result, higher <a href="/wiki/Mixing_ratio" title="Mixing ratio">mixing ratios</a> are necessary for these downbursts to form (hence the name "wet" microbursts). Melting of ice, particularly <a href="/wiki/Hail" title="Hail">hail</a>, appears to play an important role in downburst formation (Wakimoto and Bringi, 1988), especially in the lowest 1 km (0.62 mi) above ground level (Proctor, 1989). These factors, among others, make <a href="/wiki/Weather_forecasting" title="Weather forecasting">forecasting</a> wet microbursts difficult.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Development_stages_of_microbursts">Development stages of microbursts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Microburst&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Development stages of microbursts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>The first term is the effect of perturbation pressure gradients on vertical motion. In some storms this term has a large effect on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Updraft" title="Updraft">updrafts</a> (Rotunno and Klemp, 1982) but there is not much reason to believe it has much of an impact on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Downdraft" title="Downdraft">downdrafts</a> (at least to a first approximation) and therefore will be ignored.</p>
<p>The second term is the effect of buoyancy on vertical motion. Clearly, in the case of microbursts, one expects to find that B is negative meaning the parcel is cooler than its environment. This cooling typically takes place as a result of <a href="/wiki/Phase_transition" title="Phase transition">phase changes</a> (<a href="/wiki/Evaporation" title="Evaporation">evaporation</a>, <a href="/wiki/Melting" title="Melting">melting</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Sublimation_(phase_transition)" title="Sublimation (phase transition)">sublimation</a>). Precipitation particles that are small, but are in great quantity, promote a maximum contribution to cooling and, hence, to creation of negative buoyancy. The major contribution to this process is from evaporation.</p>
<p>The last term is the effect of water loading. Whereas evaporation is promoted by large numbers of small droplets, it only requires a few large <a href="/wiki/Drop_(liquid)" title="Drop (liquid)">drops</a> to contribute substantially to the downward acceleration of air parcels. This term is associated with storms having high precipitation rates. Comparing the effects of water loading to those associated with buoyancy, if a parcel has a liquid water mixing ratio of 1.0 <a href="/wiki/Gram" title="Gram">g</a> <a href="/wiki/Kilogram" title="Kilogram">kg</a><sup>−1</sup>, this is roughly equivalent to about 0.3 <a href="/wiki/Kelvin" title="Kelvin">K</a> of negative buoyancy; the latter is a large (but not extreme) value. Therefore, in general terms, negative buoyancy is typically the major contributor to downdrafts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chuck_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Chuck-9">[9]</a></sup></p>

<p>A microburst often causes aircraft to crash when they are attempting to land (the above-mentioned BOAC and Pan Am flights are notable exceptions). The microburst is an extremely powerful gust of air that, once hitting the ground, spreads in all directions. As the aircraft is coming in to land, the pilots try to slow the plane to an appropriate speed. When the microburst hits, the pilots will see a large spike in their airspeed, caused by the force of the headwind created by the microburst. A pilot inexperienced with microbursts would try to decrease the speed. The plane would then travel through the microburst, and fly into the tailwind, causing a sudden decrease in the amount of air flowing across the wings. The decrease in airflow over the wings of the aircraft causes a drop in the amount of lift produced. This decrease in lift combined with a strong downward flow of air can cause the thrust required to remain at altitude to exceed what is available, thus causing the aircraft to stall.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NASA_10-3"><a href="#cite_note-NASA-10">[10]</a></sup> If the plane is at a low altitude shortly after takeoff or during landing, it will not have sufficient altitude to recover.</p>
<p>The strongest microburst recorded thus far occurred at Andrews Field, Maryland on August 1st 1983, with wind speeds reaching 240.5 km/h (149.5 mi/h).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Danger_to_buildings">Danger to buildings</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Microburst&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Danger to buildings">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<ul>
<li>On August 9, 2016, a wet microburst struck the city of <a href="/wiki/Cleveland_Heights,_Ohio" title="Cleveland Heights, Ohio">Cleveland Heights, Ohio</a>, an eastern suburb of <a href="/wiki/Cleveland" title="Cleveland">Cleveland</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-steerwright_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-steerwright-14">[14]</a></sup> The storm developed very quickly. Thunderstorms developed west of Cleveland at 9 PM, and the <a href="/wiki/National_Weather_Service" title="National Weather Service">National Weather Service</a> issued a severe thunderstorm warning at 9:55 PM. The storm had passed over <a href="/wiki/Cuyahoga_County,_Ohio" title="Cuyahoga County, Ohio">Cuyahoga County</a> by 10:20 PM.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-reardonCH_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-reardonCH-15">[15]</a></sup> Lightning struck 10 times per minute over Cleveland Heights.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-reardonCH_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-reardonCH-15">[15]</a></sup> and 80 miles per hour (130 km/h) winds knocked down hundreds of trees and utility poles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-steerwright_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-steerwright-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-higgsCH_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-higgsCH-16">[16]</a></sup> More than 45,000 people lost power, with damage so severe that nearly 6,000 homes remained without power two days later.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-higgsCH_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-higgsCH-16">[16]</a></sup></li>
<li>On July 22, 2016 a wet microburst hit portions of Kent and Providence Counties in Rhode Island, causing wind damage in the cities of <a href="/wiki/Cranston,_Rhode_Island" title="Cranston, Rhode Island">Cranston, Rhode Island</a> and <a href="/wiki/West_Warwick,_Rhode_Island" title="West Warwick, Rhode Island">West Warwick, Rhode Island</a>. Numerous fallen trees were reported, as well as downed powerlines and minimal property damage. Thousands of people were without power for several days, even as long as over 4 days. The storm occurred late at night, and no injuries were reported.</li>
<li>On June 23, 2015 a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Macroburst" title="Macroburst">macroburst</a> hit portions of Gloucester and Camden Counties in New Jersey causing widespread damage mostly due to falling trees. Electrical utilities were affected for several days causing protracted traffic signal disruption and closed businesses.</li>
<li>On August 23, 2014, a dry microburst hit <a href="/wiki/Mesa,_Arizona" title="Mesa, Arizona">Mesa, Arizona</a>. It ripped the roof off of half a building and a shed, nearly damaging the surrounding buildings. No serious injuries were reported.</li>
<li>On December 21, 2013 a wet microburst hit <a href="/wiki/Brunswick,_Ohio" title="Brunswick, Ohio">Brunswick, Ohio</a>. The roof was ripped off of a local business; the debris damaged several houses and cars near the business. Due to the time, between 1 am and 2 am, there were no injuries.</li>
<li>On July 9, 2012, a wet microburst hit an area of <a href="/wiki/Spotsylvania_County,_Virginia" title="Spotsylvania County, Virginia">Spotsylvania County, Virginia</a> near the border of the city of <a href="/wiki/Fredericksburg,_Virginia" title="Fredericksburg, Virginia">Fredericksburg</a>, causing severe damage to two buildings. One of the buildings was a children's cheerleading center. Two serious injuries were reported.</li>
<li>On July 1, 2012, a wet microburst hit <a href="/wiki/DuPage_County,_Illinois" title="DuPage County, Illinois">DuPage County, Illinois</a>, a county 15 to 30 mi (24 to 48 km) west of <a href="/wiki/Chicago" title="Chicago">Chicago</a>. The microburst left 250,000 <a href="/wiki/Commonwealth_Edison" title="Commonwealth Edison">Commonwealth Edison</a> users without power. Many homes did not recover power for one week. Several roads were closed due to 200 reported fallen trees.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></li>
<li>On June 22, 2012, a wet microburst hit the town of <a href="/wiki/Bladensburg,_Maryland" title="Bladensburg, Maryland">Bladensburg, Maryland</a>, causing severe damage to trees, apartment buildings, and local roads. The storm caused an outage in which 40,000 customers lost power.</li>
<li>On September 8, 2011, at 5:01 PM, a dry microburst hit <a href="/wiki/Nellis_Air_Force_Base" title="Nellis Air Force Base">Nellis Air Force Base</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nevada" title="Nevada">Nevada</a> causing several aircraft shelters to collapse. Multiple aircraft were damaged and eight people were injured.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></li>
<li>On September 22, 2010 in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hegewisch" title="Hegewisch">Hegewisch</a> neighborhood of <a href="/wiki/Chicago" title="Chicago">Chicago</a>, a wet microburst hit, causing severe localized damage and localized power outages, including fallen-tree impacts into at least four homes. No fatalities were reported.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></li>
<li>On September 16, 2010, just after 5:30 PM, a wet <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Macroburst" title="Macroburst">macroburst</a> with winds of 125 mph (201 km/h) hit parts of Central <a href="/wiki/Queens" title="Queens">Queens</a> in <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York City</a>, causing extensive damage to trees, buildings, and vehicles in an area 8 miles long and 5 miles wide. Approximately 3,000 trees were knocked down by some reports. There was one fatality when a tree fell onto a car on the <a href="/wiki/Grand_Central_Parkway" title="Grand Central Parkway">Grand Central Parkway</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></li>
<li>On June 24, 2010, shortly after 4:30 PM, a wet microburst hit the city of <a href="/wiki/Charlottesville,_Virginia" title="Charlottesville, Virginia">Charlottesville, Virginia</a>. Field reports and damage assessments show that Charlottesville experienced numerous downbursts during the storm, with wind estimates at over 75 mph (121 km/h). In a matter of minutes, trees and downed power lines littered the roadways. A number of houses were hit by trees. Immediately after the storm, up to 60,000 Dominion Power customers in Charlottesville and surrounding <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Albemarle_County" title="Albemarle County">Albemarle County</a> were without power.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></li>
<li>On June 11, 2010, around 3:00 AM, a wet microburst hit a neighborhood in southwestern <a href="/wiki/Sioux_Falls,_South_Dakota" title="Sioux Falls, South Dakota">Sioux Falls, South Dakota</a>. It caused major damage to four homes, all of which were occupied. No injuries were reported. Roofs were blown off of garages and walls were flattened by the estimated 100 mph (160 km/h) winds. The cost of repairs was thought to be $500,000 or more.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></li>
<li>On May 2, 2009, the lightweight steel and mesh building in <a href="/wiki/Irving,_Texas" title="Irving, Texas">Irving, Texas</a> used for practice by the <a href="/wiki/Dallas_Cowboys" title="Dallas Cowboys">Dallas Cowboys</a> football team was flattened by a microburst, according to the National Weather Service.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NWS_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-NWS-24">[24]</a></sup></li>
<li>On March 12, 2006, a microburst hit <a href="/wiki/Lawrence,_Kansas" title="Lawrence, Kansas">Lawrence, Kansas</a>. 60 percent of the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Kansas" title="University of Kansas">University of Kansas</a> campus buildings sustained some form of damage from the storm. Preliminary estimates put the cost of repairs at between $6 million and $7 million.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
