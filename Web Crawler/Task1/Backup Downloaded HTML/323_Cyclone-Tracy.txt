<p><b>Cyclone Tracy</b> was a <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone" title="Tropical cyclone">tropical cyclone</a> that from <a href="/wiki/Christmas_Eve" title="Christmas Eve">Christmas Eve</a> to <a href="/wiki/Christmas" title="Christmas">Christmas Day</a>, 1974, devastated the city of <a href="/wiki/Darwin,_Northern_Territory" title="Darwin, Northern Territory">Darwin, Northern Territory</a>, <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a>. It is the most compact cyclone or equivalent-strength hurricane on record in the Australian basin and Southern Hemisphere, with gale-force winds extending only 48 kilometres (30 mi) from the centre and was the most compact system worldwide until 2008 when <a href="/wiki/Tropical_Storm_Marco_(2008)" title="Tropical Storm Marco (2008)">Tropical Storm Marco</a> of the <a href="/wiki/2008_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="2008 Atlantic hurricane season">2008 Atlantic hurricane season</a> broke the record, with gale-force winds extending only 19 kilometres (12 mi) from the centre.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> After forming over the <a href="/wiki/Arafura_Sea" title="Arafura Sea">Arafura Sea</a>, the storm moved southwards and affected the city with Category 4 winds on the <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone_scales" title="Tropical cyclone scales">Australian cyclone intensity scale</a>, while there is evidence to suggest that it had reached Category 3 on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saffir-Simpson_Hurricane_Scale" title="Saffir-Simpson Hurricane Scale">Saffir-Simpson Hurricane Scale</a> when it made <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Landfall_(meteorology)" title="Landfall (meteorology)">landfall</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IBTrACS_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-IBTrACS-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Tracy killed 71 people, caused <span style="white-space: nowrap">A$</span>837 million in damage (1974 dollars), or approximately <span style="white-space: nowrap">A$</span>6.41 billion (2014 dollars). It destroyed more than 70 percent of Darwin's buildings, including 80 percent of houses.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Deathtoll_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Deathtoll-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bom-ext_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-bom-ext-5">[5]</a></sup> Tracy left more than 41,000 out of the 47,000 inhabitants of the city homeless prior to landfall and required the evacuation of over 30,000 people.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EMAtctracy_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-EMAtctracy-6">[6]</a></sup> Most of Darwin's population was evacuated to <a href="/wiki/Adelaide" title="Adelaide">Adelaide</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Whyalla,_South_Australia" title="Whyalla, South Australia">Whyalla</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alice_Springs,_Northern_Territory" title="Alice Springs, Northern Territory">Alice Springs</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sydney" title="Sydney">Sydney</a>, and many never returned to the city. After the storm passed, the city was rebuilt using more stringent standards "to cyclone code". Bruce Stannard of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Age" title="The Age">The Age</a></i> stated that Cyclone Tracy was a "disaster of the first magnitude ... without parallel in <a href="/wiki/History_of_Australia" title="History of Australia">Australia's history</a>."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Meteorological_history">Meteorological history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cyclone_Tracy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Meteorological history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Tracy_1974_track.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1669" data-file-width="2700" height="136" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a7/Tracy_1974_track.png/220px-Tracy_1974_track.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a7/Tracy_1974_track.png/330px-Tracy_1974_track.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a7/Tracy_1974_track.png/440px-Tracy_1974_track.png 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Tracy_1974_track.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Map plotting the track and intensity of the storm, according to the <a href="/wiki/Saffir%E2%80%93Simpson_scale" title="Saffir–Simpson scale">Saffir–Simpson scale</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>On 20 December 1974, the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>' <a href="/wiki/ESSA-8" title="ESSA-8">ESSA-8</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Environmental_satellite" title="Environmental satellite">environmental satellite</a> recorded a large cloud mass centred over the <a href="/wiki/Arafura_Sea" title="Arafura Sea">Arafura Sea</a> about 370 kilometres (230 mi) northeast of Darwin. This disturbance was tracked by the Darwin Weather Bureau's regional director Ray Wilkie, and by senior <a href="/wiki/Meteorology" title="Meteorology">meteorologist</a> Geoff Crane. On 21 December 1974, the <a href="/wiki/ESSA-8" title="ESSA-8">ESSA-8</a> <a href="/wiki/Satellite" title="Satellite">satellite</a> showed evidence of a newly formed circular centre near <a href="/wiki/Latitude" title="Latitude">latitude</a> <a href="/wiki/8th_parallel_south" title="8th parallel south">8° south</a> and <a href="/wiki/Longitude" title="Longitude">longitude</a> <a href="/wiki/135th_meridian_east" title="135th meridian east">135° east</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ntl-countdown_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-ntl-countdown-8">[8]</a></sup> Crane - the meteorological duty officer at the time - issued the initial tropical cyclone alert describing the storm as a tropical low that could develop into a tropical cyclone.</p>
<p>Later in the evening, the Darwin meteorological office received an infrared satellite image from the <a href="/wiki/National_Oceanic_and_Atmospheric_Administration" title="National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration">National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration</a>'s satellite, <a href="/wiki/NOAA-4" title="NOAA-4">NOAA-4</a>, showing that the low pressure had developed further and that spiralling clouds could be observed. The storm was officially pronounced a tropical cyclone at around 10 p.m. on 21 December, when it was around 200 kilometres (120 mi) to the north-northeast of <a href="/wiki/Cape_Don_Light" title="Cape Don Light">Cape Don</a> (700 km or 435 mi northeast of Darwin).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ntl-warnings_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-ntl-warnings-9">[9]</a></sup> Cyclone Tracy was first observed on the Darwin radar on the morning of 22 December.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TracyReport1_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-TracyReport1-10">[10]</a></sup> Over the next few days, the cyclone moved in a southwesterly direction, passing north of Darwin on 22 December. A broadcast on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/ABC_Radio_(Australia)" title="ABC Radio (Australia)">ABC Radio</a> that day stated that Cyclone Tracy posed no immediate threat to Darwin. However, early in the morning of 24 December, Tracy rounded <a href="/wiki/Cape_Fourcroy" title="Cape Fourcroy">Cape Fourcroy</a> on the western tip of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bathurst_Island,_Northern_Territory" title="Bathurst Island, Northern Territory">Bathurst Island</a>, and moved in a southeasterly direction, straight towards Darwin.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-naaTCtracy_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-naaTCtracy-11">[11]</a></sup> The bureau's weather station at Cape Fourcroy measured a mean wind speed of 120 kilometres per hour (75 mph) at 9:00 that morning.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bigblow1_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-bigblow1-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>By late afternoon on 24 December, the sky over the city was heavily <a href="/wiki/Overcast" title="Overcast">overcast</a>, with low clouds, and was experiencing strong rain.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NTLcycloneTracy1_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-NTLcycloneTracy1-13">[13]</a></sup> Wind gusts increased in strength; between 10 p.m (local time) and midnight, the damage became serious, and residents began to realise that the cyclone would not just pass by the city, but rather over it. On 25 December at around 3:30 a.m, Tracy's centre crossed the coast near <a href="/wiki/Fannie_Bay,_Northern_Territory" title="Fannie Bay, Northern Territory">Fannie Bay</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TracyReport1_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-TracyReport1-10">[10]</a></sup> The highest recorded wind gust from the cyclone was 217 kilometres per hour (135 mph), which was recorded around 3:05 a.m at <a href="/wiki/Darwin_International_Airport" title="Darwin International Airport">Darwin Airport</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TracyReport1_10-2"><a href="#cite_note-TracyReport1-10">[10]</a></sup> The anemometer (wind speed instrument) failed at around 3:10 a.m, with the wind vane (wind direction) destroyed after the cyclone's eye.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TracyReport1_10-3"><a href="#cite_note-TracyReport1-10">[10]</a></sup> The Bureau of Meteorology's official estimates suggested that Tracy's <a href="/wiki/Wind#Measurement" title="Wind">gusts</a> had reached 240 km/h (150 mph).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> The lowest air pressure reading during Tracy was 950 hectopascals (28 inHg), which was taken at around 4 a.m, by a Bureau staff member at Darwin Airport.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TracyReport1_10-4"><a href="#cite_note-TracyReport1-10">[10]</a></sup> This was recorded during the eye of the cyclone.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TracyReport1_10-5"><a href="#cite_note-TracyReport1-10">[10]</a></sup> From around 6:30 a.m, the winds began to ease, with the rainfall ceasing at around 8:30 a.m.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TracyReport1_10-6"><a href="#cite_note-TracyReport1-10">[10]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Preparations">Preparations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cyclone_Tracy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Preparations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Darwin had been severely battered by cyclones before; in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/1897_cyclone" title="1897 cyclone">January 1897</a> and again in March 1937.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ntl-history_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-ntl-history-15">[15]</a></sup> However, in the 20 years leading up to Cyclone Tracy, the city had undergone a period of rapid expansion. E.P. Milliken estimated that on the eve of the cyclone there were 43,500 people living in 12,000 dwellings in the Darwin area. Though building standards required that some attention be given to the possibility of cyclones, most buildings were not capable of withstanding the force of a cyclone's direct hit.</p>
<p>On the day of the cyclone, most residents of Darwin believed that the cyclone would not cause any damage to the city. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cyclone_Selma" title="Cyclone Selma">Cyclone Selma</a> had been predicted to hit Darwin earlier in the month, but it instead went north and dissipated without affecting Darwin in any way. As a result, Cyclone Tracy took most Darwin residents by surprise. Despite several warnings the people of Darwin did not evacuate or prepare for the cyclone. Many residents continued to prepare for Christmas, and many attended Christmas parties, despite the increasing winds and heavy rain. Journalist Bill Bunbury interviewed the residents of Darwin some time later and recorded the experiences of the survivors of the cyclone in his book <i>Cyclone Tracy, picking up the pieces</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bunbury_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bunbury-16">[16]</a></sup> Resident Dawn Lawrie, a 1971 independent candidate for the electorate of <a href="/wiki/Nightcliff,_Northern_Territory" title="Nightcliff, Northern Territory">Nightcliff</a>, told him:</p>

<p>Cyclone Tracy killed 71 people,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Deathtoll_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-Deathtoll-4">[4]</a></sup> 49 of whom were on land and 22 at sea. Two Australian Navy <a href="/wiki/Royal_Australian_Navy" title="Royal Australian Navy">RAN</a> sailors died when <a href="/wiki/HMAS_Arrow_(P_88)" title="HMAS Arrow (P 88)">HMAS <i>Arrow</i></a>, an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Attack_class_patrol_boat" title="Attack class patrol boat"><i>Attack</i>-class</a> <a href="/wiki/Patrol_boat" title="Patrol boat">patrol boat</a>, sank at <a href="/wiki/Stokes_Hill_Wharf" title="Stokes Hill Wharf">Stokes Hill Wharf</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> The storm also caused the substantial destruction of the city of Darwin. At Darwin Airport, thirty-one aircraft were destroyed and another twenty-five badly damaged.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bigblow2_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-bigblow2-18">[18]</a></sup> The initial estimate put the reported death toll at 65, but it was revised upwards in March 2005, when the <a href="/wiki/Northern_Territory" title="Northern Territory">Northern Territory</a> Coroner proclaimed that those who still remained listed as missing had "perished at sea".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Deathtoll_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-Deathtoll-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EMAtctracy_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-EMAtctracy-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>Several factors delayed the dissemination of the news of the cyclone's impact. The destruction of transportation infrastructure and the distance between Darwin and the rest of the Australian population played a role, as did the fact the storm made landfall on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Christmas_Day" title="Christmas Day">Christmas Day</a> and most media outlets had only a skeleton crew rostered on at best. Most Australians were not aware of the cyclone until late in the afternoon. Dick Muddimer, a reporter for the local <a href="/wiki/Australian_Broadcasting_Corporation" title="Australian Broadcasting Corporation">ABC</a> station, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/105.7_ABC_Darwin" title="105.7 ABC Darwin">5DR</a>, was able to travel through the wreckage to local television station <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/NTD_(television_station)" title="NTD (television station)">NTD</a> and had the ABC station in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mount_Isa,_Queensland" title="Mount Isa, Queensland">Mount Isa, Queensland</a> notify ABC headquarters in <a href="/wiki/Sydney" title="Sydney">Sydney</a> that Darwin had been hit by a cyclone.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ABCHistory_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-ABCHistory-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>In order to provide the initial emergency response, a committee was created. The committee, composed of several high-level public servants and police, stated that, "Darwin had, for the time being, ceased to exist as a city". <a href="/wiki/Gough_Whitlam" title="Gough Whitlam">Gough Whitlam</a>, the Australian Prime Minister, was touring <a href="/wiki/Syracuse,_Sicily" title="Syracuse, Sicily">Syracuse, Sicily</a> at the time and flew to Darwin upon hearing of the disaster. Additionally, the Australian government began a mass evacuation by road and air; all of the <a href="/wiki/Australian_Defence_Force" title="Australian Defence Force">Defence Force</a> personnel throughout Australia, along with the entire <a href="/wiki/Royal_Australian_Air_Force" title="Royal Australian Air Force">Royal Australian Air Force</a>'s fleet of transport planes, were recalled from holiday leave and deployed to evacuate civilians from Darwin, as well as to bring essential relief supplies to the area. Thirteen ships of the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Australian_Navy" title="Royal Australian Navy">Royal Australian Navy</a> were used to transport supplies to the area as part of <a href="/wiki/Operation_Navy_Help_Darwin" title="Operation Navy Help Darwin">Operation Navy Help Darwin</a>; the largest humanitarian or disaster relief operation ever performed by the Navy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Semaphore_14_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-Semaphore_14-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Health_and_essential_services_crisis">Health and essential services crisis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cyclone_Tracy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Health and essential services crisis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>As soon as the worst of the storm had passed, Darwin faced several immediate health crises. On Christmas Day, the Darwin Hospital treated well over five hundred patients, with 112 of these being admitted into the hospital, and both of the facility's operating theatres being utilised. The first casualties did not arrive till 7 a.m. because of high winds and severe road conditions in and around the Darwin area. Operating continued throughout the night and into the early morning. Local teams worked without relief until the arrival of a surgical team from Canberra late that day. Those who were considered unable to return to work within two weeks were evacuated by air to safer locations.</p>
<p>All official communications out of Darwin were no longer operational. The antennas at the OTC Coastal Radio Service station (callsign VID) were destroyed during the storm. Station manager Bob Hooper, who was an <a href="/wiki/Amateur_radio_operator" title="Amateur radio operator">amateur radio operator</a>, helped to establish communications using his own amateur radio equipment, By 10 a.m. Gary Gibson another amateur operator was able to establish a station at the Darwin Community College, within a short period of time a network of stations was established across the country. This network, coordinated by Melbourne D24 police, provided message services to the cities of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Perth,_Western_Australia" title="Perth, Western Australia">Perth</a>, <a href="/wiki/Melbourne" title="Melbourne">Melbourne</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sydney" title="Sydney">Sydney</a>, <a href="/wiki/Canberra" title="Canberra">Canberra</a>, <a href="/wiki/Townsville" title="Townsville">Townsville</a>, <a href="/wiki/Brisbane" title="Brisbane">Brisbane</a>, <a href="/wiki/Adelaide" title="Adelaide">Adelaide</a>, <a href="/wiki/Alice_Springs" title="Alice Springs">Alice Springs</a>, Gove, Mt Isa, Cairns, Rockhampton, MacKay, Lismore, and Cooma.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> By 10:40 a.m. VID operators had established VID2 on board the MV <i>Nyanda</i> in Darwin Harbour, and then for five days official communications traffic in and out of Darwin was handled via <a href="/wiki/Continuous_wave" title="Continuous wave">continuous wave</a> radio (<a href="/wiki/Morse_code" title="Morse code">Morse code</a>). The only local radio station that wasn't completely disabled was the ABC's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/5DR" title="5DR">5DR</a>; for the next two days, it was Darwin's only link to the outside world.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ABCHistory_19-1"><a href="#cite_note-ABCHistory-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>Those who remained in Darwin faced the threat of several diseases due to much of the city being without water, electricity or basic sanitation. An initial response was to vaccinate residents for typhoid and cholera. Approximately 30,000 people were homeless, and were forced to seek shelter in several makeshift housing and emergency centres that lacked proper hygienic conditions. Volunteers came in from across the country to assist with the emergency relief efforts. Trench <a href="/wiki/Latrine" title="Latrine">latrines</a> were dug; water supplies delivered by tankers, and mass immunisation programs begun. The army was given the task of searching houses for bodies of people and animals, as well as locating other health risks; for example, cleaning out rotting contents from fridges and freezers across the city. This was completed within a week. Houses which had been 'searched and cleared' had S&amp;C painted on an external wall. The city itself was sprayed with <a href="/wiki/Malathion" title="Malathion">malathion</a> to control mosquitoes and other similar pests.</p>
<p>Attempts to reconnect the essential services to the city began on Christmas Day. Local officers from the Commonwealth Department of Housing and Construction began clearing debris and working to restore power. They sealed off damaged water hydrants and activated pumps to reactivate the city's water and sewerage systems.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Evacuation_and_the_public_response">Evacuation and the public response</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cyclone_Tracy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Evacuation and the public response">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Major-General <a href="/wiki/Alan_Stretton" title="Alan Stretton">Alan Stretton</a>, Director-General of the Natural Disasters Organisation,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-naaTCtracy_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-naaTCtracy-11">[11]</a></sup> and the Commonwealth Minister for the Northern Territory, <a href="/wiki/Rex_Patterson" title="Rex Patterson">Rex Patterson</a>, arrived at <a href="/wiki/Darwin_International_Airport" title="Darwin International Airport">Darwin Airport</a> late on Christmas Day and took charge of the relief efforts. After an assessment of the situation and meetings with the Department of the Northern Territory and the relevant minister, it was concluded that Darwin's population needed to be reduced to a "safe level" of 10,500 people. This decision was made on the advice of Dr. Charles Gurd, the Director of Health in the Northern Territory. Around 10,000 people left Darwin and the surrounding area within the first two days, but the rate of departures then began to slow down. The government then gave support to his position, offering full reimbursement of personal costs, as long as the evacuation took place.</p>
<p>The population was evacuated by air and ground; because of communications difficulties with Darwin airport, landing was limited to one plane every ninety minutes. At major airports, teams of federal and territory department officials as well as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Salvation_Army" title="Salvation Army">Salvation Army</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Red_Cross" title="Red Cross">Red Cross</a> workers met refugees, with the Red Cross taking responsibility for keeping track of the names and temporary addresses of the refugees. Evacuations were prioritised according to need; women, children, and the elderly and sick were evacuated first. There were reports of men dressing up as women to escape with the early evacuations. Between 26 and 31 December, a total of 35,362 people were evacuated from Darwin.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EMAtctracy_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-EMAtctracy-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bigblow2_18-1"><a href="#cite_note-bigblow2-18">[18]</a></sup> Of those, 25,628 were evacuated by air, the remainder by road.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EMAtctracy_6-3"><a href="#cite_note-EMAtctracy-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bigblow2_18-2"><a href="#cite_note-bigblow2-18">[18]</a></sup> By 31 December, only 10,638 people (mostly men who were required to help clean up the city) remained in Darwin. Stretton also regulated access to the city by means of a permit system. Permits were only issued to those who were involved in either the relief or reconstruction efforts, and were used to prevent the early return of those who had been evacuated.</p>
<p>Upon receiving news of the damage, several community groups across Australia began fundraising and relief efforts to assist the survivors. Major reception centres were set up in cities such as <a href="/wiki/Katherine" title="Katherine">Katherine</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tennant_Creek" title="Tennant Creek">Tennant Creek</a> and <a href="/wiki/Alice_Springs" title="Alice Springs">Alice Springs</a>. Several of the small towns along the Stuart Highway made efforts to assist people who were fleeing by road, supplying them with food, fuel, rest and mechanical aid. At <a href="/wiki/Adelaide_River" title="Adelaide River">Adelaide River</a>, the small local population provided hot meals to the refugees who stopped there. Approximately twenty-four hours after the storm hit Darwin, the population of <a href="/wiki/Alice_Springs" title="Alice Springs">Alice Springs</a> had raised over $105,000 to assist the victims of Tracy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> In <a href="/wiki/Melbourne" title="Melbourne">Melbourne</a> at the <a href="/wiki/Boxing_Day_Test" title="Boxing Day Test">Boxing Day Test</a> <a href="/wiki/Cricket" title="Cricket">cricket</a> match between <a href="/wiki/Australia_national_cricket_team" title="Australia national cricket team">Australia</a> and <a href="/wiki/England_cricket_team" title="England cricket team">England</a>, members of both teams moved around the boundaries carrying buckets which the crowd threw cash into for the relief funds. Darwin families were also given priority on public housing waiting lists. On 31 December 1974, Stretton recommended that full civilian control should resume in Darwin, and handed over control of the city to its elected officials.</p>

<p>In February 1975, Prime Minister <a href="/wiki/Gough_Whitlam" title="Gough Whitlam">Gough Whitlam</a> announced the creation of the Darwin Reconstruction Commission, which was given the task of rebuilding the city "within five years", focusing primarily on building houses.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> The Commission was headed by Tony Powell.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> The damage to the city was so severe that some advocated moving the entire city. However, the government insisted that it be rebuilt in the same location. By May 1975, Darwin's population had recovered somewhat, with 30,000 residing in the city. Temporary housing, caravans, hotels and an <a href="/wiki/Ocean_liner" title="Ocean liner">ocean liner</a>, <a href="/wiki/MV_Bloemfontein_Castle" title="MV Bloemfontein Castle">MV <i>Patris</i></a>, were used to house people, as reconstruction of permanent housing had not yet begun by September that year. <a href="/wiki/Ella_Stack" title="Ella Stack">Ella Stack</a> became Mayor of Darwin in May 1975 and was heavily involved in its reconstruction.</p>
<p>However, by the following April, and after receiving criticism for the slow speed of reconstruction, the Commission had built 3,000 new homes in the nearly destroyed northern suburbs, and completed repairs to those that had survived the storm. Several new <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Building_codes" title="Building codes">building codes</a> were drawn up, trying to achieve the competing goals of the speedy recovery of the area and ensuring that there would be no repeat of the damage that Darwin took in 1974. By 1978, much of the city had recovered and was able to house almost the same number of people as it had before the cyclone hit. However, by the 1980s, as many as sixty percent of Darwin's 1974 population had left, never to return. In the years that followed, Darwin was almost entirely rebuilt and now shows almost no resemblance to the pre-Tracy Darwin of December 1974.</p>
<p>Although a <a href="/wiki/Northern_Territory_Legislative_Assembly" title="Northern Territory Legislative Assembly">Legislative Assembly</a> had been set up earlier in the year, the Northern Territory had only minimal self-government, with a federal minister being responsible for the Territory from <a href="/wiki/Canberra" title="Canberra">Canberra</a>. However, the cyclone and subsequent responses highlighted several problems with the way the regional government was set up. This led <a href="/wiki/Malcolm_Fraser" title="Malcolm Fraser">Malcolm Fraser</a>, Whitlam's successor as Prime Minister, to give self-government to the Territory in 1978.</p>
<p>Many of the government records associated with Cyclone Tracy became publicly available on 1 January 2005 under the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/30_year_rule" title="30 year rule">30 year rule</a>.</p>
<p><span id="Cyclone_Tracy_in_popular_culture"></span></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="In_popular_culture">In popular culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cyclone_Tracy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: In popular culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Arguably, the most famous work that Cyclone Tracy has inspired is the song "Santa Never made it into Darwin" composed by Bill Cate and performed by <a href="/wiki/Bill_and_Boyd" title="Bill and Boyd">Bill (Cate) and Boyd (Robinson)</a> in 1975 to raise money for the relief and reconstruction efforts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> Subsequently, the song became so well known that in 1983 <a href="/wiki/Hoodoo_Gurus" title="Hoodoo Gurus">Hoodoo Gurus</a> released "<a href="/wiki/Tojo_(song)" title="Tojo (song)">Tojo</a>", a song comparing the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Imperial_Japan" title="Imperial Japan">Japanese</a> bombing of Darwin under the command of <a href="/wiki/Hideki_Tojo" title="Hideki Tojo">Hideki Tojo</a> during <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a> to the damage done by Cyclone Tracy. The much feared Japanese invasion never happened, but the cyclone was virtually ignored and ended up destroying the city.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1986, the <a href="/wiki/Nine_Network" title="Nine Network">Nine Network</a> and <a href="/wiki/Publishing_and_Broadcasting_Limited" title="Publishing and Broadcasting Limited">PBL</a> created <i><a href="/wiki/Cyclone_Tracy_(mini-series)" title="Cyclone Tracy (mini-series)">Cyclone Tracy</a></i>, a period drama <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mini-series" title="Mini-series">mini-series</a> based on the events during the cyclone. Michael Fisher, Ted Roberts and Leon Saunders wrote the series, and it starred <a href="/wiki/Chris_Haywood" title="Chris Haywood">Chris Haywood</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tracy_Mann" title="Tracy Mann">Tracy Mann</a>, who played the lead characters of Steve and Connie.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> The mini-series was released on DVD by <a href="/wiki/Umbrella_Entertainment" title="Umbrella Entertainment">Umbrella Entertainment</a> in December 2005. The DVD is compatible with all region codes and includes special features such as newsreel footage of the devastation and a documentary titled <i>On A Wind And A Prayer</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Umbrella_Entertainment_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-Umbrella_Entertainment-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
