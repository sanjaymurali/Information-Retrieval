<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Hurricane Diana</h1>

<p><b>Hurricane Diana</b> was the deadliest <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone" title="Tropical cyclone">tropical cyclone</a> during the <a href="/wiki/1990_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="1990 Atlantic hurricane season">1990 Atlantic hurricane season</a>, killing 139 people in <a href="/wiki/Mexico" title="Mexico">Mexico</a>. Forming out of a <a href="/wiki/Tropical_wave" title="Tropical wave">tropical wave</a> on August 4, the system brushed <a href="/wiki/Honduras" title="Honduras">Honduras</a> before intensifying into a tropical storm the following day. Gradually gaining strength, Diana struck the <a href="/wiki/Yucat%C3%A1n_Peninsula" title="Yucatán Peninsula">Yucatán Peninsula</a> with winds of 65 mph (100 km/h). Due land interaction, the cyclone weakened slightly before moving over the warm waters of the <a href="/wiki/Bay_of_Campeche" title="Bay of Campeche">Bay of Campeche</a> on August 6. Once over water, the Diana quickly became a hurricane and later reached its peak intensity on August 7 as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/SSHS#Category_2" title="SSHS">Category 2</a> on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saffir%E2%80%93Simpson_Hurricane_Scale" title="Saffir–Simpson Hurricane Scale">Saffir–Simpson Hurricane Scale</a>. Shortly thereafter, the storm made <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Landfall_(meteorology)" title="Landfall (meteorology)">landfall</a> near <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tampico,_Tamaulipas" title="Tampico, Tamaulipas">Tampico, Tamaulipas</a> with winds of 100 mph (165 km/h). Rapid weakening ensued once the storm moved over the high terrain of Mexico and Diana diminished to a tropical depression roughly 24 hours after moving onshore. The cyclone later emerged into the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_of_California" title="Gulf of California">Gulf of California</a> on August 9 before dissipating, though its remnants were monitored until August 14 when the system lost its identity over <a href="/wiki/Arizona" title="Arizona">Arizona</a>.</p>
<p>Throughout Mexico, Hurricane Diana produced torrential rains that triggered deadly flooding and landslides. Roughly 155 mi² (400 km²) of farmland was destroyed and 30,000 people were left homeless by the storm. Numerous roads and railways were either washed out or blocked by debris, cutting communication with several communities. In all, 139 people were killed in the country and $90 million (1990 <a href="/wiki/United_States_dollar" title="United States dollar">USD</a>) was wrought in damage. Due to the extensive loss of life and damage caused by the storm, the name <i>Diana</i> was <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone_naming#Retirement" title="Tropical cyclone naming">retired</a> the following year and replaced with <i>Dolly</i>, which was <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Dolly_(1996)" title="Hurricane Dolly (1996)">first used</a> in the <a href="/wiki/1996_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="1996 Atlantic hurricane season">1996 season</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Meteorological_history">Meteorological history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hurricane_Diana&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Meteorological history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Diana_1990_track.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="633" data-file-width="1024" height="136" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/34/Diana_1990_track.png/220px-Diana_1990_track.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/34/Diana_1990_track.png/330px-Diana_1990_track.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/34/Diana_1990_track.png/440px-Diana_1990_track.png 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Diana_1990_track.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Map plotting the track and intensity of the storm, according to the <a href="/wiki/Saffir%E2%80%93Simpson_scale" title="Saffir–Simpson scale">Saffir–Simpson scale</a></div>

<p>The origins of Hurricane Diana were from a tropical wave that existed western Africa into the <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Ocean" title="Atlantic Ocean">Atlantic</a> on July 27. Conditions were unfavorable for development of a tropical cyclone, and the tropical wave remained disorganized until reaching the eastern Caribbean Sea. The system entered the Caribbean Sea through the southern <a href="/wiki/Windward_Islands" title="Windward Islands">Windward Islands</a>, where falling barometric pressures were observed, decreasing by 3.5 <a href="/wiki/Bar_(unit)" title="Bar (unit)">mbar</a> (<a href="/wiki/Pascal_(unit)" title="Pascal (unit)">hPa</a>; 0.1 <a href="/wiki/Inch_of_mercury" title="Inch of mercury">inHg</a>) in 24 hours. Upper-level air data from the <a href="/wiki/Lesser_Antilles" title="Lesser Antilles">Lesser Antilles</a> indicated that the tropical wave was associated with an upper-level anticyclone. With increasing convection, the first Air Force reconnaissance plane flight indicated no low-level circulation, but a relatively large amount of thunderstorm activity. The system continued to have a large amount of associated convection while passing over the <a href="/wiki/Netherlands_Antilles" title="Netherlands Antilles">Netherlands Antilles</a>, as indicated by satellite images and surface observations. <a href="/wiki/Cyclonic_rotation" title="Cyclonic rotation">Cyclonic rotation</a> was observed in the low-level convection by satellite images, and the fifth tropical depression of the season had developed at 0000 UTC on August 4, situated in the southwestern Caribbean Sea. However, this was based on surface observations, and it was not confirmed that tropical cyclone formation occurred until later that day.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-page1_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-page1-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>The new tropical depression moved to the northwest under the influence of a mid-level trough, and rapidly intensified to a tropical storm the following day offshore of eastern Honduras; the <a href="/wiki/National_Hurricane_Center" title="National Hurricane Center">National Hurricane Center</a> assigned the name Diana to the system. Becoming a tropical storm, Diana further intensified, and winds reached 65 mph (105 km/h) before landfall in <a href="/wiki/Felipe_Carrillo_Puerto,_Quintana_Roo" title="Felipe Carrillo Puerto, Quintana Roo">Felipe Carrillo Puerto</a> in <a href="/wiki/Quintana_Roo" title="Quintana Roo">Quintana Roo</a> occurred. The storm initially rapidly weakened over land, although it retained winds of 50 mph (80 km/h) until moving over open waters. When Diana entered the southern Gulf of Mexico, the trough of low pressure in that vicinity weakened and steering currents caused the storm to head westward. Conditions became more favorable in the Gulf of Mexico, and Diana quickly intensified, becoming a hurricane late on August 7. Hurricane Diana rapidly intensified, and briefly reached Category 2 hurricane status, attaining peak intensity with winds of 100 mph (165 km/h) and a minimum barometric pressure of 980 mbar (hPa; 28.94 inHg). Only two hours after attaining peak intensity on August 7, Hurricane Diana made landfall near <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tampico,_Tamaulipas" title="Tampico, Tamaulipas">Tampico, Tamaulipas</a> at the same intensity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-page1_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-page1-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Moving inland, Diana rapidly weakened over the high terrain of Mexico, quickly deteriorating from a low-end Category 2 hurricane to a strong tropical storm within four hours. Diana continued westward over Mexico, and weakened to a tropical depression on August 8, while centered near <a href="/wiki/Mexico_City" title="Mexico City">Mexico City</a>. The weakening tropical depression turned west-northwestward over central Mexico, and emerged into the Pacific Ocean on August 9, before dissipating in the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_of_California" title="Gulf of California">Gulf of California</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-page1_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-page1-1">[1]</a></sup> The remnants of Hurricane Diana curved northward and tracked through the Gulf of California, and eventually came ashore in northwestern <a href="/wiki/Sonora" title="Sonora">Sonora</a>. Crossing northwestern Mexico, the remnants of Diana entered <a href="/wiki/Arizona" title="Arizona">Arizona</a> and quickly dissipated.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rain_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-rain-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Preparations">Preparations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hurricane_Diana&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Preparations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone_warnings_and_watches#North_Atlantic_systems" title="Tropical cyclone warnings and watches">Tropical cyclone warnings and watches § North Atlantic systems</a></div>
<p>In anticipation of Diana's first landfall, a tropical storm warning was issued on August 5 for <a href="/wiki/Canc%C3%BAn" title="Cancún">Cancún</a>, Mexico, southward to <a href="/wiki/Belize_City" title="Belize City">Belize City</a>, Belize, on August 5, including the offshore islands from both nations; a tropical storm warning indicates that tropical storm force winds, 39 to 73 mph (39 to 118 km/h), are expected within 24 hours. Eleven hours later, the tropical storm warning was extended as far as <a href="/wiki/Carmen_Municipality" title="Carmen Municipality">Carmen</a>, but was discontinued for the entire east coast of Belize.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-page5_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-page5-3">[3]</a></sup> Residents along coastal areas of <a href="/wiki/Quintana_Roo" title="Quintana Roo">Quintana Roo</a> were advised to evacuate inland,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8.2F5ap_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-8.2F5ap-4">[4]</a></sup> as tides were expected to range from 3 to 5 ft (0.91 to 1.52 m) above normal.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-diana45mph_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-diana45mph-5">[5]</a></sup> Emergency officials set up shelters were set up in Xcalak, Punta Herrero, and Punta Allen.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8.2F5ap_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-8.2F5ap-4">[4]</a></sup> The Mexican Coast Guard was placed on alert and shipping across Quantana Roo was suspended during the evening of August 5. Additionally, there were concerns about flash flooding due to the possibility of 5 to 8 in (130 to 200 mm) of rainfall in areas near the storm's path.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-diana45mph_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-diana45mph-5">[5]</a></sup> After Diana made landfall on the Yucatán Peninsula on August 6, all of the tropical storm warnings in place were discontinued.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-page5_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-page5-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Diana then threatened the mainland east coast of Mexico. A hurricane watch was issued for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tuxpan,_Veracruz" title="Tuxpan, Veracruz">Tuxpan</a> to Boca de Jesus Maria at 21:00 UTC on August 6; a hurricane watch notes the possibility of hurricane conditions within 48 hours, such as winds of at least 74 mph (119 km/h). Early on August 7, the National Hurricane Center anticipated hurricane conditions within 24 hours, and as a result, a hurricane warning was issued for <a href="/wiki/Nautla,_Veracruz" title="Nautla, Veracruz">Nautla</a> to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/La_Pesca,_Tamaulipas" title="La Pesca, Tamaulipas">Le Pesca</a>. Simultaneously, a tropical storm warning was issued for La Pesca to Boca de Jesus Maria. Later that day, a hurricane warning had been issued for a similar area, stretching from Nautla to Lerdo de Tejada.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-page5_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-page5-3">[3]</a></sup> The National Hurricane Center also predicted precipitation totals of 8 in (200 mm) and emphasized that flash floods and mudslides would likely occurred in the vicinity of where Diana was projected to make landfall.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> According to Miguelangel Rebolledo, the captain of the port of Tuxpan, about 300 people fled low-lying areas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OR_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-OR-7">[7]</a></sup> State and federal officials prepared 10,000 packages of emergency supplies, food, and medicine and moved them to several locations along the coast. Police and <a href="/wiki/Mexican_Armed_Forces" title="Mexican Armed Forces">Armed Forces</a> were placed on alert.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> By 00:00 UTC on August 8, all of the watches and warnings in place were officially discontinued.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-page5_3-3"><a href="#cite_note-page5-3">[3]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mexico">Mexico</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hurricane_Diana&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Mexico">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Offshore Quintana Roo, the island <a href="/wiki/Cozumel" title="Cozumel">Cozumel</a> of reported sustained winds of 45 mph (75 km/h).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> While crossing over the Yucatán Peninsula, sustained winds of 34 mph (54 km/h) and gusts to 40 mph (65 km/h) were observed in <a href="/wiki/M%C3%A9rida,_Yucat%C3%A1n" title="Mérida, Yucatán">Mérida</a>. In addition, wind gusts of 37 and 45 mph (60 and 73 km/h) were reported in the towns of Felipe Carrillo Puerto and José María Morelos, respectively.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Diana also caused heavy rain across the area, though damage is unknown.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-page2_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-page2-11">[11]</a></sup> Due to heavy rainfall, minor street flooding occurred in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chetumal,_Quintana_Roo" title="Chetumal, Quintana Roo">Chetumal, Quintana Roo</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>Rough seas were reported mainly between <a href="/wiki/Coatzacoalcos" title="Coatzacoalcos">Coatzacoalcos</a> and Tampico; the ports of those two cities were closed during the passage of Diana, according to the <a href="/wiki/Servicio_Meteorol%C3%B3gico_Nacional_(Mexico)" title="Servicio Meteorológico Nacional (Mexico)">Servicio Meteorológico Nacional</a>. While passing through the <a href="/wiki/Bay_of_Campeche" title="Bay of Campeche">Bay of Campeche</a> and the southern Gulf of Mexico, Diana produced waves up to 12 ft (3.6 m) in height.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> On mainland Mexico, the states of <a href="/wiki/Veracruz" title="Veracruz">Veracruz</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hidalgo_(Mexico)" title="Hidalgo (Mexico)">Hidalgo</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Puebla" title="Puebla">Puebla</a> were hardest hit, with over 75,000 people affected by the hurricane.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mexdis_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-mexdis-14">[14]</a></sup> In <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Poza_Rica,_Veracruz" title="Poza Rica, Veracruz">Poza Rica</a>, high winds toppled trees and electricity poles, which cut off telephone services and electricity supply to the city. Fallen trees and telephone wires also blocked some streets in the area, but the major roads remained open.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OR_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-OR-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>Diana produced torrential rainfall while crossing the country, which peaked at 21.92 in (557 mm) in <a href="/wiki/Aquism%C3%B3n" title="Aquismón">Aquismón</a>, <a href="/wiki/San_Luis_Potos%C3%AD" title="San Luis Potosí">San Luis Potosí</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rain_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-rain-2">[2]</a></sup> Heavy rainfall triggered mudslides and flooding, mostly in the east-central portion of Mexico. The rainfall caused extensive property damage, destroying numerous houses and leaving 3,500 homeless.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> The torrential rain blocked highways and railways across six states. Furthermore, the flooding also destroyed 155 mi² (400 km²) of farmland. In addition, it is estimated that Diana injured 25,000 people.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mexdis_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-mexdis-14">[14]</a></sup> Several miners were reported dead, after their truck plunged into a ravine.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Deseret_News_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-Deseret_News-16">[16]</a></sup> Excluding the 56 people that were missing by the end of 1990, Hurricane Diana caused 139 deaths, and $90.7 million in damage (1990 USD).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mexdis_14-2"><a href="#cite_note-mexdis-14">[14]</a></sup> However, some sources claim that there were as much as 391 fatalities and losses incurred by the storm totaled to $94.5 million (1990 USD).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>Following the storm, the <a href="/wiki/Mexican_Armed_Forces" title="Mexican Armed Forces">Mexican Armed Forces</a> delivered food and basic health care supplies. In addition, there was also a mass vaccination against <a href="/wiki/Typhoid_fever" title="Typhoid fever">typhoid</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Elsewhere">Elsewhere</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hurricane_Diana&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Elsewhere">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>While Diana was crossing the Yucatán Peninsula, it dropped light rainfall in Belize, peaking at 1 in (25 mm) in an unspecified location.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> The remnants of Hurricane Diana eventually moved into the <a href="/wiki/Southwestern_United_States" title="Southwestern United States">Southwestern United States</a>, bringing heavy rainfall to the region.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rain_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-rain-2">[2]</a></sup> In <a href="/wiki/Imperial_County,_California" title="Imperial County, California">Imperial County, California</a>, golf-ball sized hail was reported by the residents, along with rain and lightning, causing briefly power outages throughout the county. A police dispatch of Imperial County also noted local flooding, which resulted in traffic disruptions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> The remnants of Diana also dropped light rainfall in <a href="/wiki/San_Diego_County,_California" title="San Diego County, California">San Diego County, California</a>, with the city of <a href="/wiki/San_Diego" title="San Diego">San Diego</a> receiving only a trace of rain, <a href="/wiki/Vista,_California" title="Vista, California">Vista</a> reporting 0.09 in (2 mm), <a href="/wiki/Del_Mar,_California" title="Del Mar, California">Del Mar</a> had 0.08 in (2 mm), and <a href="/wiki/Oceanside,_California" title="Oceanside, California">Oceanside</a> measuring 0.3 in (8 mm).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p>In Arizona, the remnants of Diana contributed to record cold temperatures for August in <a href="/wiki/Phoenix,_Arizona" title="Phoenix, Arizona">Phoenix</a>. On August 14, the city had a high temperature of 78 °F (26 °C), which was 24 °F (−4 °C) below normal for that date. In <a href="/wiki/Maricopa_County,_Arizona" title="Maricopa County, Arizona">Maricopa County</a>, 3.5 in (89 mm) of rain was observed. Heavy precipitation, with 3.42 in (87 mm) in <a href="/wiki/Desert_Hills,_Arizona" title="Desert Hills, Arizona">Desert Hills</a>, which fell in less than two hours. Several weather-related car accidents occurred, but none were serious. In <a href="/wiki/Apache_Junction,_Arizona" title="Apache Junction, Arizona">Apache Junction</a>, police and city officials rushed to close flooded streets, including portions of state highways and underpasses of <a href="/wiki/Interstate_17" title="Interstate 17">Interstate 17</a>. Crews were pumping 32,000 US gal (120,000 L) of water from the underpasses of Interstate 17, but their efforts were futile. A 19 mi (31 km) portion of <a href="/wiki/Arizona_State_Route_87" title="Arizona State Route 87">State Route 87</a> between <a href="/wiki/Gila_River_Indian_Community" title="Gila River Indian Community">Gila River Indian Community</a> and the intersection with <a href="/wiki/Arizona_State_Route_187" title="Arizona State Route 187">State Route 187</a> was shut down due to flooding. In <a href="/wiki/Sacaton,_Arizona" title="Sacaton, Arizona">Sacaton</a>, about two-thirds of the city was inundated with at least 3 ft (0.91 m) of water.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p>Due to its impact, the name Diana was retired by the <a href="/wiki/World_Meteorological_Organization" title="World Meteorological Organization">World Meteorological Organization</a> in the spring of 1991, and will never be used again for another <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/North_Atlantic_tropical_cyclone" title="North Atlantic tropical cyclone">Atlantic hurricane</a>. It was replaced with <i>Dolly</i> for the <a href="/wiki/1996_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="1996 Atlantic hurricane season">1996 season</a>. This Hurricane Diana is not to be confused with the <a href="/wiki/1955_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="1955 Atlantic hurricane season">1955 season</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Diane" title="Hurricane Diane">Hurricane Diane</a>, which was also retired.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
