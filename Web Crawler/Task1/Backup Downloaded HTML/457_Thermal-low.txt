<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Thermal low</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">
					Jump to:					<a href="#mw-head">navigation</a>, 					<a href="#p-search">search</a>
</div>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:ThermalLow.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="180" data-file-width="359" height="125" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f4/ThermalLow.png/250px-ThermalLow.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/f/f4/ThermalLow.png 1.5x" width="250"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:ThermalLow.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Vertical cross-section of a thermal low</div>
</div>
</div>
<p><b>Thermal lows</b>, or <b>heat lows</b>, are non-<a href="/wiki/Weather_front" title="Weather front">frontal</a> <a href="/wiki/Low-pressure_area" title="Low-pressure area">low-pressure areas</a> that occur over the continents in the <a href="/wiki/Subtropics" title="Subtropics">subtropics</a> during the warm season, as the result of intense heating when compared to their surrounding environments.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-thermal_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-thermal-1">[1]</a></sup> Thermal lows occur near the <a href="/wiki/Sonoran_Desert" title="Sonoran Desert">Sonoran Desert</a>, on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mexican_plateau" title="Mexican plateau">Mexican plateau</a>, in California's <a href="/wiki/Central_Valley_(California)" title="Central Valley (California)">Great Central Valley</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Sahara" title="Sahara">Sahara</a>, over north-west <a href="/wiki/Argentina" title="Argentina">Argentina</a> in <a href="/wiki/South_America" title="South America">South America</a>, over the <a href="/wiki/Kimberley_(Western_Australia)" title="Kimberley (Western Australia)">Kimberley</a> region of north-west <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Iberian_peninsula" title="Iberian peninsula">Iberian peninsula</a>, and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tibetan_plateau" title="Tibetan plateau">Tibetan plateau</a>.</p>
<p>Over land, intense, rapid solar heating of the land surface results in heating of the lowest layers of the atmosphere via reradiated energy in the infrared spectrum. The resulting hotter air is less dense than surrounding cooler air. This, combined with the rising of the hot air, results in the formation of a low pressure area. Elevated areas can enhance the strength of the thermal low as they warm more quickly than the atmosphere which surrounds them at the same altitude. Over the water, instability lows form during the winter when the air overlying the land is colder than the warmer water body. Thermal lows tend to have weak circulations, and can extend to 3,100 metres (10,200 ft) in height. Thermal lows over the western and southern portions of North America, northern Africa, and southeast Asia are strong enough to lead to summer <a href="/wiki/Monsoon" title="Monsoon">monsoon</a> conditions. Development of thermal lows inland of the coastline lead to the development of <a href="/wiki/Sea_breeze" title="Sea breeze">sea breezes</a>. Sea breezes combined with rugged topography near the coast can encourage poor air quality.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Formation">Formation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Thermal_low&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Formation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Rain_in_the_Wah_Wah_Valley.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2736" data-file-width="3648" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1b/Rain_in_the_Wah_Wah_Valley.jpg/220px-Rain_in_the_Wah_Wah_Valley.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1b/Rain_in_the_Wah_Wah_Valley.jpg/330px-Rain_in_the_Wah_Wah_Valley.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1b/Rain_in_the_Wah_Wah_Valley.jpg/440px-Rain_in_the_Wah_Wah_Valley.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Rain_in_the_Wah_Wah_Valley.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
An isolated thunderstorm rolls through <a href="/wiki/Wah_Wah_Valley" title="Wah Wah Valley">Wah Wah Valley</a>, <a href="/wiki/Utah" title="Utah">Utah</a>. This type of monsoonal pattern is very common in the late summer of the southwest US.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>In deserts, lack of ground and plant moisture that would normally provide <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Evaporative_cooling" title="Evaporative cooling">evaporative cooling</a> can lead to intense, rapid solar heating of the lower layers of air. The hot air is less dense than surrounding cooler air. This, combined with the rising of the hot air, results in a low pressure area called a thermal low.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-thermal_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-thermal-1">[1]</a></sup> Over elevated surfaces, heating of the ground exceeds the heating of the surrounding air at the same altitude above <a href="/wiki/Sea_level" title="Sea level">sea level</a>, which creates an associated heat low over the terrain and enhances any thermal lows which would have otherwise existed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> During the cold season, (<a href="/wiki/Winter" title="Winter">winter</a>), warm water bodies such as the <a href="/wiki/Great_Lakes" title="Great Lakes">Great Lakes</a> can induce an instability low.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Thermal lows which develop near sea level can build in height during the warm season, or <a href="/wiki/Summer" title="Summer">summer</a>, to the elevation of the 700 hPa pressure surface,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> which lies near 3,100 metres (10,200 ft) above sea level.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Heat lows normally are stationary and have a weak cyclonic circulation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> As they are strongest at the surface and warm near their center, and weaker aloft where the air is more stable, the thermal low is considered warm core.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> The strongest versions of these features globally are over Arabia, the northern portion of the <a href="/wiki/Indian_subcontinent" title="Indian subcontinent">Indian subcontinent</a>, <a href="/wiki/Arizona" title="Arizona">Arizona</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mexican_plateau" title="Mexican plateau">Mexican plateau</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> northwest <a href="/wiki/Argentina" title="Argentina">Argentina</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> southwestern <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">Spain</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> Australia,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> and northern <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a>. The formation of the heat low over northern Africa leads to a low-level westerly <a href="/wiki/Jet_stream" title="Jet stream">jet stream</a> from June into October.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Role_in_the_monsoon_regime">Role in the monsoon regime</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Thermal_low&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Role in the monsoon regime">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:India_southwest_summer_monsoon_onset_map_en.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1852" data-file-width="1639" height="282" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/59/India_southwest_summer_monsoon_onset_map_en.svg/250px-India_southwest_summer_monsoon_onset_map_en.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/59/India_southwest_summer_monsoon_onset_map_en.svg/375px-India_southwest_summer_monsoon_onset_map_en.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/59/India_southwest_summer_monsoon_onset_map_en.svg/500px-India_southwest_summer_monsoon_onset_map_en.svg.png 2x" width="250"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:India_southwest_summer_monsoon_onset_map_en.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Onset dates and prevailing wind currents of the southwest summer monsoon</div>
</div>
</div>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Monsoon" title="Monsoon">Monsoon</a></div>
<p>Monsoons are caused by the larger amplitude of the seasonal cycle of land temperature compared to that of nearby oceans. This differential warming happens because heat in the ocean is mixed vertically through a "mixed layer" that may be fifty meters deep, through the action of wind and buoyancy-generated <a href="/wiki/Turbulence" title="Turbulence">turbulence</a>, whereas the land surface conducts heat slowly, with the seasonal signal penetrating perhaps a meter or so. Additionally, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Specific_heat_capacity" title="Specific heat capacity">specific heat capacity</a> of liquid water is significantly higher than that of most materials that make up land. Together, these factors mean that the heat capacity of the layer participating in the seasonal cycle is much larger over the oceans than over land, with the consequence that the air over the land warms faster and reaches a higher temperature than the air over the ocean. The hot air over the land tends to rise, creating an area of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Low_pressure" title="Low pressure">low pressure</a>. This creates a steady wind blowing toward the land, bringing the moist near-surface air over the oceans with it.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cause_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-cause-15">[15]</a></sup> Similar <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Precipitation_(meteorology)" title="Precipitation (meteorology)">rainfall</a> is caused by the moist ocean air being lifted upwards by mountains,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MT_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-MT-16">[16]</a></sup> surface heating,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> convergence at the surface,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> divergence aloft, or from storm-produced outflows at the surface.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> However the lifting occurs, the air cools due expansion in lower pressure, which in turn produces <a href="/wiki/Condensation" title="Condensation">condensation</a>.</p>
<p>In winter, the land cools off quickly, but the ocean keeps the heat longer due to its higher specific heat. The hot air over the ocean rises, creating a low pressure area and a breeze from land to ocean while a large area of drying high pressure is formed over the land, increased by wintertime cooling.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cause_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-cause-15">[15]</a></sup> Monsoons are similar to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sea_breezes" title="Sea breezes">sea and land breezes</a>, a term usually referring to the localized, diurnal (daily) cycle of circulation near coastlines everywhere, but they are much larger in scale, stronger and seasonal.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Role_in_sea_breeze_formation">Role in sea breeze formation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Thermal_low&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Role in sea breeze formation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:SeaBreeze.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="264" data-file-width="441" height="150" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/98/SeaBreeze.svg/250px-SeaBreeze.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/98/SeaBreeze.svg/375px-SeaBreeze.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/98/SeaBreeze.svg/500px-SeaBreeze.svg.png 2x" width="250"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:SeaBreeze.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Schematic cross section through a sea breeze front. If the air inland is moist, cumulus often marks the front's location.</div>
</div>
</div>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Sea_breeze" title="Sea breeze">Sea breeze</a></div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Ocean" title="Ocean">sea</a> is warmed by the sun to a greater depth than the land due to its greater <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Specific_heat" title="Specific heat">specific heat</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> The sea therefore has a greater capacity for absorbing heat than the land, so the surface of the sea warms up more slowly than the land's surface. As the temperature of the surface of the <a href="/wiki/Landform" title="Landform">land</a> rises, the land heats the air above it. The warm air is less dense and so it rises. This rising air over the land lowers the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sea_level_pressure" title="Sea level pressure">sea level pressure</a> by about 0.2%. The cooler air above the sea, now with higher sea level pressure, flows towards the land into the lower pressure, creating a cooler breeze near the coast. The strength of the sea breeze is directly proportional to the temperature difference between the land and the sea. If the environmental wind field is greater than 8 knots (15 km/h) and opposing the direction of a possible sea breeze, the sea breeze is not likely to develop.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p>Along the California coast, the cooler water sets up a surface marine layer that is much cooler than inland areas during the summer. At the same time, the intense heating inland creates a pronounced thermal trough aligned with the Great Central Valley and typically linked to the broader thermal low across the North American deserts. As a consequence, a strong pressure gradient is created which draws cool marine air landward. As temperatures plummet, fog and stratus stream in and through the gaps of the Coast Ranges, and especially through the Golden Gate at San Francisco (<i>see</i> <a href="/wiki/San_Francisco_fog" title="San Francisco fog">San Francisco fog</a>). The same thermal trough is sometimes pushed toward the coast, especially in late Fall as higher pressure develops to the east due to cooling further east. This setup often brings the warmest temperatures of the year to the normally cool coastline as the seabreeze stops or is even replaced by a dangerously dry land breeze.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Role_in_air_pollution">Role in air pollution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Thermal_low&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Role in air pollution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In areas where it is hilly or mountainous near the coastline, thermally-forced sea breezes combined with wind circulations up the sides of the mountains can encourage the production of chemicals which can lead to the development of <a href="/wiki/Smog" title="Smog">smog</a>. Pollution has been tracked into the mid-levels of the <a href="/wiki/Troposphere" title="Troposphere">troposphere</a> in the form of <a href="/wiki/Ozone" title="Ozone">ozone</a>, which is concentrated over the circulation of the thermal low as well as adjacent oceanic areas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
