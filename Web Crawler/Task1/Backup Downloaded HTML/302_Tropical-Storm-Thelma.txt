<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Tropical Storm Thelma</h1>

<p><b>Tropical Storm Thelma</b>, known in the Philippines as <b>Tropical Storm Uring</b>, was one of the deadliest <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone" title="Tropical cyclone">tropical cyclones</a> in <a href="/wiki/History_of_the_Philippines" title="History of the Philippines">Philippine history</a>, killing at least 5,081 people. Forming out of a tropical disturbance on November 1, 1991, several hundred kilometers north-northeast of <a href="/wiki/Palau" title="Palau">Palau</a>, the depression that would become Thelma tracked generally westward. After turning southwestward in response to a <a href="/wiki/Cold_front" title="Cold front">cold front</a>, the system intensified into a tropical storm on November 4 as it approached the Philippines. Hours before moving over the <a href="/wiki/Visayas" title="Visayas">Visayas</a>, Thelma attained its peak intensity with estimated ten-minute sustained winds of 75 km/h (45 mph) and a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Barometric_pressure" title="Barometric pressure">barometric pressure</a> of 992 <a href="/wiki/Bar_(unit)" title="Bar (unit)">mbar</a> (<a href="/wiki/Pascal_(unit)" title="Pascal (unit)">hPa</a>; 29.29 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Inches_of_Mercury" title="Inches of Mercury">inHg</a>). Despite moving over land, the system weakened only slightly, emerging over the <a href="/wiki/South_China_Sea" title="South China Sea">South China Sea</a> on November 6 while retaining gale-force winds. Thelma ultimately succumbed to <a href="/wiki/Wind_shear" title="Wind shear">wind shear</a> and degraded to a tropical depression. On November 8, the depression made <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Landfall_(meteorology)" title="Landfall (meteorology)">landfall</a> in Southern <a href="/wiki/Vietnam" title="Vietnam">Vietnam</a> before dissipating hours later.</p>
<p>While passing over the Philippines, Thelma's interaction with the high terrain of some of the islands resulted in torrential rainfall. Through a process known as <a href="/wiki/Orographic_lift" title="Orographic lift">orographic lift</a>, much of the Visayas received 150 mm (6 in) of rain; however, on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Leyte_Island" title="Leyte Island">Leyte Island</a> there was a localized downpour that brought totals to 580.5 mm (22.85 in). With the majority of this falling in a three-hour span, an unprecedented flash flood took place on the island. Much of the land had been <a href="/wiki/Deforestation" title="Deforestation">deforestated</a> or poorly cultivated and was unable to absorb most of the rain, creating a large runoff. This water overwhelmed the Anilao–Malbasag <a href="/wiki/Drainage_basin" title="Drainage basin">watershed</a> and rushed downstream. <a href="/wiki/Ormoc" title="Ormoc">Ormoc City</a>, located past where the Anilao and Malbasag rivers converge, suffered the brunt of the flood. In just three hours, the city was devastated with thousands of homes damaged or destroyed. A total of 4,922 people were killed in the city alone, with 2,300 perishing along the riverbank.</p>
<p>Outside of Ormoc City, 159 people were killed across Leyte and <a href="/wiki/Negros_Occidental" title="Negros Occidental">Negros Occidental</a>. Throughout the country, at least 5,081 people lost their lives while another 1,941–3,084 were missing and presumed dead. This made Thelma the deadliest tropical cyclone in Philippine history, surpassing a storm in 1867 that killed 1,800,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-histdeadly_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-histdeadly-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> until later surpassed by <a href="/wiki/Typhoon_Haiyan" title="Typhoon Haiyan">Typhoon Haiyan (Yolanda)</a> in 2013 which killed at least 6,300 people.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> A total of 4,446 homes were destroyed while another 22,229 were damaged. Total losses amounted to $27.67 million.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[nb 1]</a></sup> Initially, it took over 24 hours for word of the disaster to reach officials due to a crippled communication network around Ormoc City. Within a few days, emergency supply centers were established and aid from various agencies under the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations" title="United Nations">United Nations</a> and several countries flowed into the country. A total of $5.8 million worth of grants and materials was provided collectively in the international relief effort.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Meteorological_history">Meteorological history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tropical_Storm_Thelma&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Meteorological history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Thelma_1991_track.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1236" data-file-width="2000" height="136" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/88/Thelma_1991_track.png/220px-Thelma_1991_track.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/88/Thelma_1991_track.png/330px-Thelma_1991_track.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/88/Thelma_1991_track.png/440px-Thelma_1991_track.png 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Thelma_1991_track.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Map plotting the track and intensity of the storm, according to the <a href="/wiki/Saffir%E2%80%93Simpson_scale" title="Saffir–Simpson scale">Saffir–Simpson scale</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>In late October 1991, a tropical disturbance developed near the <a href="/wiki/Caroline_Islands" title="Caroline Islands">Caroline Islands</a>. Tracking generally west-northwestward, the system gradually became more defined. On October 31, <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_convection" title="Atmospheric convection">convection</a> associated with the system quickly increased, prompting the issuance of a <a href="/wiki/Tropical_Cyclone_Formation_Alert" title="Tropical Cyclone Formation Alert">Tropical Cyclone Formation Alert</a> from the <a href="/wiki/Joint_Typhoon_Warning_Center" title="Joint Typhoon Warning Center">Joint Typhoon Warning Center</a> (JTWC).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ATCR_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-ATCR-5">[4]</a></sup> Early on November 1, the <a href="/wiki/Japan_Meteorological_Agency" title="Japan Meteorological Agency">Japan Meteorological Agency</a> (JMA) began monitoring the system as a tropical depression, at which time the system was situated roughly 415 km (260 mi) north-northeast of <a href="/wiki/Palau" title="Palau">Palau</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IBTrACS_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-IBTrACS-6">[5]</a></sup> Following a satellite-derived surface wind estimate of 45 km/h (30 mph) later that day, the JTWC also began monitoring the low as a tropical depression. Initially, <a href="/wiki/Numerical_weather_prediction" title="Numerical weather prediction">forecast models</a> showed the system continuing on an arcing path out to sea; however, the system turned westward on November 2 and threatened the <a href="/wiki/Philippines" title="Philippines">Philippines</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ATCR_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-ATCR-5">[4]</a></sup> Due to the cyclone's proximity to the country, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Philippine_Atmospheric,_Geophysical_and_Astronomical_Services_Administration" title="Philippine Atmospheric, Geophysical and Astronomical Services Administration">Philippine Atmospheric, Geophysical and Astronomical Services Administration</a> also monitored the storm and <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone_naming" title="Tropical cyclone naming">assigned it with the local name</a> <i>Uring</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NDCC_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-NDCC-7">[6]</a></sup> Late on November 3, the depression turned west-southwestward towards the <a href="/wiki/Visayas" title="Visayas">Visayas</a> in response to an approaching <a href="/wiki/Cold_front" title="Cold front">cold front</a>, an event typical of late-season cyclones in the basin.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OrmocChp2_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-OrmocChp2-8">[7]</a></sup> On November 4, both the JTWC and JMA upgraded the system to a tropical storm, with the latter assigning it the name <i>Thelma</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ATCR_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-ATCR-5">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IBTrACS_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-IBTrACS-6">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>Hours before striking the Philippines on November 4, both agencies reported Thelma to have reached its peak intensity. The JTWC estimated the storm to have attained one-minute sustained winds of 85 km/h (50 mph) while the JMA estimated ten-minute sustained winds at 75 km/h (45 mph). Additionally, its <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Barometric_pressure" title="Barometric pressure">barometric pressure</a> reached 992 <a href="/wiki/Bar_(unit)" title="Bar (unit)">mbar</a> (<a href="/wiki/Pascal_(unit)" title="Pascal (unit)">hPa</a>; 29.29 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Inches_of_Mercury" title="Inches of Mercury">inHg</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ATCR_5-3"><a href="#cite_note-ATCR-5">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IBTrACS_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-IBTrACS-6">[5]</a></sup> Thelma soon made <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Landfall_(meteorology)" title="Landfall (meteorology)">landfall</a> in <a href="/wiki/Samar" title="Samar">Samar</a> before weakening to a minimal tropical storm. Maintaining gale-force winds, the system eventually passed over <a href="/wiki/Palawan_(island)" title="Palawan (island)">Palawan Island</a> on November 6 before moving over the <a href="/wiki/South_China_Sea" title="South China Sea">South China Sea</a>. Despite being back over water, strong <a href="/wiki/Wind_shear" title="Wind shear">wind shear</a> prevented re-intensification and caused Thelma to weaken to a tropical depression by November 7. Now moving westward, the depression eventually made its final landfall over the <a href="/wiki/Mekong_Delta" title="Mekong Delta">Mekong River Delta</a> in Southern <a href="/wiki/Vietnam" title="Vietnam">Vietnam</a> on November 8.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ATCR_5-4"><a href="#cite_note-ATCR-5">[4]</a></sup> Over the next couple of days the system weakened into an area of low pressure as it moved westwards, before it moved into the Andaman Sea during November 10.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1991_ATCR_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-1991_ATCR-9">[8]</a></sup> Once in the Andaman Sea, the systems remnants contributed to the formation of the <a href="/wiki/1991_North_Indian_Ocean_cyclone_season#Cyclonic_Storm_BOB_08" title="1991 North Indian Ocean cyclone season">Karaikal tropical cyclone</a> during the next day.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1991_ATCR_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-1991_ATCR-9">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Impact">Impact</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tropical_Storm_Thelma&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Impact">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:TS_Thelma_05_nov_1991_0459Z.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="700" data-file-width="700" height="220" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/be/TS_Thelma_05_nov_1991_0459Z.jpg/220px-TS_Thelma_05_nov_1991_0459Z.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/be/TS_Thelma_05_nov_1991_0459Z.jpg/330px-TS_Thelma_05_nov_1991_0459Z.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/be/TS_Thelma_05_nov_1991_0459Z.jpg/440px-TS_Thelma_05_nov_1991_0459Z.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:TS_Thelma_05_nov_1991_0459Z.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Tropical Storm Thelma over the Visayas on November 5</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Tropical Storm Thelma struck the Philippines just five months after the <a href="/wiki/Plinian_eruption" title="Plinian eruption">Ultra-Plinian eruption</a> of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/1991_eruption_of_Mount_Pinatubo" title="1991 eruption of Mount Pinatubo">Mount Pinatubo</a>. The eruption resulted in the deaths of roughly 800 people and left nearly 1 million homeless. The country's government was reportedly struggling to deal with the scope of the disaster and the addition of Thelma worsened the situation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>Striking the nation as a weak tropical storm, winds from Thelma gusted up to 95 km/h (60 mph) in <a href="/wiki/Tacloban" title="Tacloban">Tacloban</a>;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[10]</a></sup> these winds caused no known damage. The main destructive force associated with the cyclone was the tremendous rainfall it produced. More than 150 mm (6 in) of rain across much of the Visayas, resulting in widespread flooding.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ATCR_5-5"><a href="#cite_note-ATCR-5">[4]</a></sup> In Tacloban, 140.2 mm (5.52 in) fell over a 24‑hour span. The heaviest rain occurred on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Leyte_Island" title="Leyte Island">Leyte Island</a> due to <a href="/wiki/Orographic_lift" title="Orographic lift">orographic lift</a>, which brought large quantities of moisture into the atmosphere over a relatively small area. Additionally, <a href="/wiki/Monsoon" title="Monsoon">monsoonal</a> winds to the southwest of Thelma converged over the island, further enhancing the precipitation. Near the city of <a href="/wiki/Ormoc" title="Ormoc">Ormoc</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Philippine_National_Oil_Company" title="Philippine National Oil Company">Philippine National Oil Company</a> rain gauge measured 580.5 mm (22.85 in) of precipitation, the highest in relation to the storm. Of this, approximately 500 mm (20 in) fell during a three-hour span around noon local time on November 5.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OrmocChp2_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-OrmocChp2-8">[7]</a></sup> Initially, residents believed that <a href="/wiki/Waterspout" title="Waterspout">waterspouts</a> transported tremendous amounts of water to the island, triggering the floods. This notion was quickly dismissed as improbable, however.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OrmocChp1_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-OrmocChp1-12">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>The hardest hit region was Leyte, where more than 4,000 people lost their lives. A total of 4,446 homes were destroyed while another 22,229 were damaged. The majority of casualties and damage took place in Ormoc when a flash flood devastated the city. At least 81 people were killed outside Ormoc and another 14 went missing; 42 lost their lives in <a href="/wiki/Burauen,_Leyte" title="Burauen, Leyte">Burauen</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OrmocChp1_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-OrmocChp1-12">[11]</a></sup> The entire island of Leyte was left without power and many areas were isolated as roads were washed away.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TPP1_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-TPP1-13">[12]</a></sup> Another 78 people perished and 70 others were left missing in <a href="/wiki/Negros_Occidental" title="Negros Occidental">Negros Occidental</a>. Losses from the storm amounted to $27.67 million; $18.94 million in Leyte and $8.73 million in Negros Occidental. A total of 598,454 people were affected while an estimated 43,000 people were left homeless by the storm across the Philippines.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReliefWeb_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-ReliefWeb-14">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Parker400_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-Parker400-15">[14]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ormoc_City_tragedy">Ormoc City tragedy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tropical_Storm_Thelma&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Ormoc City tragedy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Ph_locator_leyte_ormoc.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="350" data-file-width="300" height="257" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c8/Ph_locator_leyte_ormoc.png/220px-Ph_locator_leyte_ormoc.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/c/c8/Ph_locator_leyte_ormoc.png 1.5x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Ph_locator_leyte_ormoc.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Location of Ormoc on Leyte Island</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The greatest tragedy during Tropical Storm Thelma took place in the city of Ormoc after torrential rains overwhelmed the Anilao–Malbasag <a href="/wiki/Drainage_basin" title="Drainage basin">watershed</a>, sending flood waters rushing down the <a href="/wiki/Deforestation" title="Deforestation">deforested</a> mountainside.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OrmocChp4_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-OrmocChp4-16">[15]</a></sup> This water flowed into the Anilao and Malbasag rivers, located north of Ormoc.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OrmocChp3_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-OrmocChp3-17">[16]</a></sup> The watershed, covering an area of 4,567 <a href="/wiki/Hectare" title="Hectare">hectares</a> (11,285 <a href="/wiki/Acre" title="Acre">acres</a>), is only 3.3 percent forested, with the remainder being used for agricultural and private purposes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OrmocChp4_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-OrmocChp4-16">[15]</a></sup> According to a study in 1990, roughly 90 percent of the watershed had been converted into <a href="/wiki/Coconut" title="Coconut">coconut</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sugarcane" title="Sugarcane">sugarcane</a> plantations. The majority of this land was improperly cultivated since the 1970s, making conditions worse than they normally would have been.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OrmocChp5_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-OrmocChp5-18">[17]</a></sup> The natural structure of the mountains further contributed to the floods, with slopes as steep as 60 percent grade in some areas. In heavy rain events, this feature leaves the upper two-thirds of the mountain range unstable.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OrmocChp3_17-1"><a href="#cite_note-OrmocChp3-17">[16]</a></sup> In the two hours prior to the heaviest rains, the soil in the watershed became saturated, greatly lessening its effectiveness at absorbing further rains. As a result, the tremendous rains that occurred just prior to the flood, during which rainfall rates reached 167 mm (6.6 in) per hour, the land was unable to absorb a majority of the rain. Many landslides ranging from 1 to 3 m (3.3 to 9.8 ft) deep and 50 to 100 m (160 to 330 ft) wide occurred across the region.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OrmocChp4_16-2"><a href="#cite_note-OrmocChp4-16">[15]</a></sup> Altogether, rains were twice as heavy as the land could handle and the many landslips doubled the volume of fluids.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OrmocChp6_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-OrmocChp6-19">[18]</a></sup> At various points along rivers, temporary dams created by debris, namely trees, allowed a build up of water upstream. In some instances, waters reached a depth of 10 m (33 ft) before the dams collapsed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OrmocChp3_17-2"><a href="#cite_note-OrmocChp3-17">[16]</a></sup> Normally, it takes water in the Anilao and Malbasag rivers roughly 3.6 and 5.6 days, respectively, to reach Ormoc City; however, it only took one hour during the flood.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OrmocChp4_16-3"><a href="#cite_note-OrmocChp4-16">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>Ormoc City itself is located in a flood-prone area, with the Anilao and Malbasag rivers converging just north of the city and taking a 90 degree turn towards the bay. In addition to the natural dangers of the river, poorly designed structures on the river made conditions worse.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OrmocChp5_18-1"><a href="#cite_note-OrmocChp5-18">[17]</a></sup> The majority of construction along the river did not take flooding threats into account, and actually increased the threat of these events.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OrmocChp6_19-1"><a href="#cite_note-OrmocChp6-19">[18]</a></sup> Concrete walls and <a href="/wiki/Levee" title="Levee">levees</a> were built into the river rather than on the banks, leading to faster debris damming. Lastly, just after the turn was the Cogon Bridge. This structure constricted the river by as much as 50 percent, enhancing the build up of water. The turn became the final trigger in the disaster as it created an "instantaneous backwater effect," causing massive volumes of water to over-top the riverbank.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OrmocChp5_18-2"><a href="#cite_note-OrmocChp5-18">[17]</a></sup> Around 11:00 a.m. local time on November 5, approximately 22,835 km<sup>3</sup> (5,480 mi<sup>3</sup>) of water inundated 25 km<sup>2</sup> (15.5 mi<sup>2</sup>) of the city.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReliefWeb_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-ReliefWeb-14">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OrmocChp3_17-3"><a href="#cite_note-OrmocChp3-17">[16]</a></sup> In just 15 minutes, the water rose by 2.1 m (7 ft) and further rose to 3.7 m (12 ft) within an hour.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OrmocChp3_17-4"><a href="#cite_note-OrmocChp3-17">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LT1_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-LT1-20">[19]</a></sup> The flooding lasted for roughly three hours, leaving up to 0.6 m (2.0 ft) of sediment behind.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OrmocChp3_17-5"><a href="#cite_note-OrmocChp3-17">[16]</a></sup></p>
<div class="quotebox" style="float: right; clear: right; margin: 0.5em 0 0.8em 1.4em; width:30%; padding: 10px; border: 1px solid #aaa; font-size: 88%; background-color: #F9F9F9;">
<div class="" style="position: relative; text-align: left;">
<div>"The waters kept rising. We had to place our children on top of the refrigerator. Still, the waters kept going up so we all had to climb to the roof. But perhaps we are bless. We all survived."</div>
</div>
<cite style="display: block; text-align: left;">Survivor from Isle Verde<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PolDis_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-PolDis-21">[20]</a></sup></cite></div>
<p>The flood struck the city with little to no warning, catching all those in its path off-guard.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Recon_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-Recon-22">[21]</a></sup> Numerous low-income families lived along the banks of the river, despite being such a high-risk area. Residential and commercial areas were also set up along reclaimed embankments that restricted river flow. Additionally, squatters were allowed to live along the banks of the Anilao river in an area called Isle Verde.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OrmocChp5_18-3"><a href="#cite_note-OrmocChp5-18">[17]</a></sup> Roughly 2,500 people lived on this reclaimed land prior to the flood. The majority of fatalities took place along the banks of the river, with most drowning or being buried in mud or debris. A survivor described the initial event as a gigantic wave crashing over the banks and flooding the city. Isle Verde was virtually wiped out and out of the original 2,500 people that lived there, only 200 survived. It became known as the "Isle of Death" to survivors.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PolDis_21-1"><a href="#cite_note-PolDis-21">[20]</a></sup> Residents reported hundreds of bodies floating down rivers in the area.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LT1_20-1"><a href="#cite_note-LT1-20">[19]</a></sup> The force of the water and mud was enough to crack the walls of city hall.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TPP1_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-TPP1-13">[12]</a></sup> Nearly 3,000 homes were destroyed and more than 11,000 others were damaged.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OrmocChp1_12-2"><a href="#cite_note-OrmocChp1-12">[11]</a></sup> In the city alone, officials confirmed that 4,922 people were killed and another 1,857–3,000 were left missing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Recon_22-1"><a href="#cite_note-Recon-22">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Inquirer1_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-Inquirer1-23">[22]</a></sup> Additionally, 3,020 people were injured.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OrmocChp1_12-3"><a href="#cite_note-OrmocChp1-12">[11]</a></sup> The majority of those missing were likely swept out to sea by the flood and presumed dead.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TPP1_13-2"><a href="#cite_note-TPP1-13">[12]</a></sup> Two days after the storm, several bodies of those swept out to sea washed back ashore.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[23]</a></sup> Officials stated that the death toll could have been in the tens of thousands had the flood occurred at night rather than in the middle of the day.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TPP1_13-3"><a href="#cite_note-TPP1-13">[12]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Aftermath">Aftermath</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tropical_Storm_Thelma&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Aftermath">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="quotebox" style="float: right; clear: right; margin: 0.5em 0 0.8em 1.4em; width:30%; padding: 10px; border: 1px solid #aaa; font-size: 88%; background-color: #F9F9F9;">
<div class="" style="position: relative; text-align: left;">
<div>"It looked like it was a Nazi [death] camp. Children and old people were piled on top of each other."</div>
</div>
<cite style="display: block; text-align: left;">Cebu Provincial Governor Lito Osmena<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TPP1_13-4"><a href="#cite_note-TPP1-13">[12]</a></sup></cite></div>
<p>Initially, it took more than 24 hours for word of the level of devastation to reach officials in <a href="/wiki/Manila" title="Manila">Manila</a> as communications across Leyte were largely destroyed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LT1_20-2"><a href="#cite_note-LT1-20">[19]</a></sup> By November 7, search and rescue operations were underway across Leyte and Negros Occidental. The first shipment of relief supplies, consisting of food rations, rice, sardines, and used clothing, was to be shipped from <a href="/wiki/Cebu" title="Cebu">Cebu</a> later that day.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReliefWeb_14-2"><a href="#cite_note-ReliefWeb-14">[13]</a></sup> On November 8, <a href="/wiki/President_of_the_Philippines" title="President of the Philippines">Philippine President</a> <a href="/wiki/Corazon_Aquino" title="Corazon Aquino">Corazon Aquino</a> declared all of Leyte a <a href="/wiki/Disaster_area" title="Disaster area">disaster area</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TV2_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-TV2-25">[24]</a></sup> A <a href="/wiki/Philippine_Navy" title="Philippine Navy">Philippine Navy</a> vessel set out with heavy earth-moving machinery and the <a href="/wiki/Philippine_Air_Force" title="Philippine Air Force">Philippine Air Force</a> deployed aircraft to assist in rescue efforts. Relief efforts in Ormoc City were hampered by a lack of clear roads and fuel. Amateur radio reports stated that an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/AC-130" title="AC-130">AC-130</a> was able to land at a local airport but materials had to be moved by helicopter from there since roads were blocked.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReliefWeb_14-3"><a href="#cite_note-ReliefWeb-14">[13]</a></sup> Relief efforts were also hampered by continuing rains and the rough terrain of the affected region.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LT1_20-3"><a href="#cite_note-LT1-20">[19]</a></sup> By November 11, approximately 8,300 families had been rescued and another 7,521 were evacuated from affected regions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReliefWeb_14-4"><a href="#cite_note-ReliefWeb-14">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>Supply distribution centers were established in Ormoc, providing residents with food, water, and materials, by November 11.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReliefWeb_14-5"><a href="#cite_note-ReliefWeb-14">[13]</a></sup> People were given a can of sardines and 1 kg (2.2 lb) of rice at these centers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TV2_25-1"><a href="#cite_note-TV2-25">[24]</a></sup> These centers were only able to operate in daylight though due to a lack of fuel and transportation. Water was supplied in limited quantities from Cebu. Medical and sanitation teams were deployed throughout the province, with many coming from surrounding areas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReliefWeb_14-6"><a href="#cite_note-ReliefWeb-14">[13]</a></sup> Residents searched through debris for lumber to construct makeshift coffins while others stacked bodies to be picked up by wheelbarrows or trucks.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LT1_20-4"><a href="#cite_note-LT1-20">[19]</a></sup> Officials had difficulty determining how to best deal with mass casualties as bodies lay across the Ormoc region. Many were found in the coastal <a href="/wiki/Barangay" title="Barangay">barangays</a> of Linao, Camp Downes, and Bantigue as well as the Ormoc pier.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Inquirer1_23-1"><a href="#cite_note-Inquirer1-23">[22]</a></sup> In order to prevent the spread of disease, <a href="/wiki/Mass_grave" title="Mass grave">mass graves</a> were dug, with 700 bodies buried on November 8. Dump trucks were used to transport the dead to these sites as quickly as possible.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TV1_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-TV1-26">[25]</a></sup> As decomposition set in, residents stated that "[the] putrid smell was unbearable." Even months after the storm, bodies were occasionally discovered, some found in drainage systems.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Inquirer1_23-2"><a href="#cite_note-Inquirer1-23">[22]</a></sup> By November 10, four navy vessels were searching debris in the waters near Ormoc for bodies; 16 were recovered that day with more believed to be submerged in the bay.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[26]</a></sup> Roads surrounding the city were finally cleared by November 12; however, electricity remained out. With the deployment of medical teams from <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a>, hospitals in the region returned to full capacity. By November 22, electricity and water had been 70 percent and 60 percent restored, respectively. The emergency phase of assistance ended on November 29 and coordination of disaster relief was returned to the Philippines. By that time, national aid to Ormoc reached $1.1 million, with more than half coming from a presidential grant.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReliefWeb_14-7"><a href="#cite_note-ReliefWeb-14">[13]</a></sup></p>

<p>On November 7, despite no official appeal for international aid, the governments of <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a> and the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> provided $34,783 and $25,000 in funds. The request for assistance came the following day, with the Philippines requesting food, water, medicine, emergency supplies, and heavy machinery. A team from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Undro" title="Undro">United Nations Disaster Relief Organization</a>, specializing with relief coordination and flood management, was also sent. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Red_Cross" title="Red Cross">Red Cross</a> appealed for $418,000 to support 15,000 families for one month. A cash grant of $17,300 from the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a> was received on November 8.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReliefWeb_14-8"><a href="#cite_note-ReliefWeb-14">[13]</a></sup> Two <a href="/wiki/United_States_Air_Force" title="United States Air Force">United States Air Force</a> AC-130s from <a href="/wiki/U.S._Naval_Base_Subic_Bay" title="U.S. Naval Base Subic Bay">Subic bay naval base</a> flew to Cebu carrying ready-to-eat meals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TV1_26-1"><a href="#cite_note-TV1-26">[25]</a></sup> International funding reached $2.5 million on November 12, with grants of $1.05 million, $1 million, $188,000 from the <a href="/wiki/Netherlands" title="Netherlands">Netherlands</a>, Japan, and <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a> respectively. Additionally, the United States provided 55,000 packages of food rations. This total nearly doubled two days later with grants from the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_Development_Programme" title="United Nations Development Programme">United Nations Development Programme</a>, <a href="/wiki/World_Food_Programme" title="World Food Programme">World Food Programme</a>, <a href="/wiki/World_Vision_International" title="World Vision International">World Vision International</a>, <a href="/wiki/M%C3%A9decins_Sans_Fronti%C3%A8res" title="Médecins Sans Frontières">Médecins Sans Frontières</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Caritas_(charity)" title="Caritas (charity)">Caritas</a>, various branches of the Red Cross, and the governments of <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a> and <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand" title="New Zealand">New Zealand</a>. Ultimately, approximately $5.8 million was provided in international assistance from 13 nations, the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations" title="United Nations">United Nations</a>, the Red Cross, and various non-governmental organizations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReliefWeb_14-9"><a href="#cite_note-ReliefWeb-14">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>Isle Verde, where approximately 2,300 people were killed, was declared uninhabitable by officials; however, residents still returned to the area due to a need for land. Eventually, signs that used to warn people not to stay on the islet were eventually taken down and people were no longer warned not to live there. A resettlement community was constructed months later, with plans to house 912 of the 2,668 families that needed to be moved from the area. Those that were not moved were left on Isle Verde despite orders not to stay there.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PolDis_21-2"><a href="#cite_note-PolDis-21">[20]</a></sup> Another resettlement project for 700 families was planned at the cost of $1 million.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReliefWeb_14-10"><a href="#cite_note-ReliefWeb-14">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>The sheer magnitude of the flood event in the Anilao–Malbasag watershed made the region more vulnerable to future flood events. Hillsides became more unstable and the rivers themselves were clogged with debris, raising their water levels and widening their banks. In a post-disaster assessment in October 1992, it was stated that swift cooperation of all agencies from local to governmental was necessary to prevent tragedies of similar caliber in the future.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OrmocChp6_19-2"><a href="#cite_note-OrmocChp6-19">[18]</a></sup> It was urged that residents still living along the river banks be relocated to safer areas; however, by the time of the report, people had already begun repopulating the area. As a way of avoiding similar breaching of the riverbank, it was suggested that the two rivers be dredged and possibly re-channeled. Several points were also brought up about rehabilitating the landscape of the watershed: reforestation, contoured farming, and redesigning of plantations to better retain rainwater.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OrmocChp7_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-OrmocChp7-28">[27]</a></sup> Long-term rehabilitation of the watershed was deemed necessary in addition to repairing infrastructure in Ormoc.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OrmocChp6_19-3"><a href="#cite_note-OrmocChp6-19">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1993, following a request by the Philippine Government, the <a href="/wiki/Japan_International_Cooperation_Agency" title="Japan International Cooperation Agency">Japan International Cooperation Agency</a> conducted as study on flood control for Ormoc and other cities across the country. In 1998, a <a href="/wiki/Philippine_peso" title="Philippine peso">₱</a>800 million ($20.6 million USD) construction project for flood mitigation was approved and later completed in 2001. That year, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tropical_Depression_Auring_(2001)" title="Tropical Depression Auring (2001)">Tropical Depression Auring</a> caused flooding of similar magnitude to Thelma; however, the waters were properly diverted to the sea. A sculpture and monument to the victims, designed by architect Maribeth Ebcas and artist Florence Cinco respectively, called "Gift of Life" was constructed on a 1.3 km<sup>2</sup> (0.8 mi<sup>2</sup>) plot of land. It was designed to also depict a need to respect nature and be a message of hope for residents in Ormoc.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Inquirer1_23-3"><a href="#cite_note-Inquirer1-23">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p>Due to the catastrophic loss of life caused by the storm, the name <i>Thelma</i> was <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone_naming#Retirement" title="Tropical cyclone naming">retired</a> and replaced with <i>Teresa</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TCRR_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-TCRR-29">[28]</a></sup></p>
