<p><b>Hurricane Inez</b> was a <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone_scales" title="Tropical cyclone scales">major hurricane</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[nb 1]</a></sup> that affected the <a href="/wiki/Caribbean" title="Caribbean">Caribbean</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bahamas" title="Bahamas">Bahamas</a>, <a href="/wiki/Florida" title="Florida">Florida</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Mexico" title="Mexico">Mexico</a> in 1966. It was the first storm on record to affect all of those areas. It originated from a <a href="/wiki/Tropical_wave" title="Tropical wave">tropical wave</a> over Africa, and became a tropical depression by September 21. It moved slowly westward, initially failing to intensify much; it was upgraded to Tropical Storm Inez on September 24. The storm strengthened into a hurricane and was quickly intensifying when it struck the French <a href="/wiki/Overseas_region" title="Overseas region">overseas region</a> of <a href="/wiki/Guadeloupe" title="Guadeloupe">Guadeloupe</a> on September 27. Inez severely damaged the island's banana and sugar crops, and thousands of homes were damaged, leaving 10,000 people homeless. Damage on Guadeloupe was estimated at $50 million,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[nb 2]</a></sup> and there were 40 deaths. After entering the Caribbean, Inez briefly weakened before restrengthening, attaining peak <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Maximum_sustained_winds" title="Maximum sustained winds">sustained winds</a> of 150 mph (240 km/h) on September 28. That day a <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_hunters" title="Hurricane hunters">hurricane hunters</a> flight reported a gust of 197 mph (317 km/h), which was the highest recorded at the time.</p>
<p>Continuing westward, Inez made <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Landfall_(meteorology)" title="Landfall (meteorology)">landfall</a> as a small hurricane on the <a href="/wiki/Barahona_Province" title="Barahona Province">Barahona Peninsula</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Dominican_Republic" title="Dominican Republic">Dominican Republic</a>. There, the storm flooded many rivers and destroyed over 800 houses. Nationwide, there were about 100 deaths and $12 million in damage. After affecting the country, Inez struck southwestern Haiti, where it was considered the worst hurricane since the 1920s. As many as 1,000 people were killed, and 60,000 people were left homeless. Damage totaled $20.35 million in Haiti, prompting the United States government to send aid for the first time in nearly ten years due to previous mishandling of funds. Inez weakened quickly over Hispaniola, although it reintensified into a major hurricane before striking southeastern Cuba on September 30. In the country, 125,000 people were forced to evacuate, and there were three deaths and $20 million in damage.</p>
<p>The hurricane moved slowly over Cuba for two days before emerging into the Atlantic Ocean near the Bahamas. It was expected to continue out to sea, although it stalled and resumed its previous westward path. In the Bahamas, heavy rainfall and high tides caused flooding, which killed five people and left $15.5 million in damage. Hurricane-force winds occurred throughout the Florida Keys, where 160 homes and 190 trailers were damaged. <a href="/wiki/Sea_spray" title="Sea spray">Salt spray</a> damaged crops in the region, and there was $5 million in damage and four deaths. In the <a href="/wiki/Straits_of_Florida" title="Straits of Florida">Straits of Florida</a>, Inez capsized a boat of <a href="/wiki/Cuban_exile" title="Cuban exile">Cuban refugees</a>, killing 45 people. In the northern Gulf of Mexico, a helicopter crashed after carrying evacuees from an oil rig, killing 11 people. The hurricane threatened the northern <a href="/wiki/Yucat%C3%A1n_Peninsula" title="Yucatán Peninsula">Yucatán Peninsula</a>, and although it remained offshore, Inez produced flooding and caused some power outages. At its final landfall, Inez flooded portions of <a href="/wiki/Tamaulipas" title="Tamaulipas">Tamaulipas</a> and cut off roads to <a href="/wiki/Tampico" title="Tampico">Tampico</a>. About 84,000 people were left homeless, and the hurricane destroyed at least 2,500 houses. Damage was estimated at $104 million, and there were 74 deaths in Mexico.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Meteorological_history">Meteorological history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hurricane_Inez&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Meteorological history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Inez_1966_track.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="633" data-file-width="1024" height="136" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e0/Inez_1966_track.png/220px-Inez_1966_track.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e0/Inez_1966_track.png/330px-Inez_1966_track.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e0/Inez_1966_track.png/440px-Inez_1966_track.png 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Inez_1966_track.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Map plotting the track and intensity of the storm, according to the <a href="/wiki/Saffir%E2%80%93Simpson_scale" title="Saffir–Simpson scale">Saffir–Simpson scale</a></div>

<p>The origins of Inez were from a <a href="/wiki/Tropical_wave" title="Tropical wave">tropical wave</a> that formed over the <a href="/wiki/Sahara" title="Sahara">Sahara</a> in central Africa on September 15 from the <a href="/wiki/Monsoon" title="Monsoon">monsoon</a>. It moved westward in the <a href="/wiki/Prevailing_winds" title="Prevailing winds">prevailing winds</a>, exiting the west coast of Africa on September 18. The precursor to Inez was a disturbance along the northern portion of the wave,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nasa_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-nasa-4">[2]</a></sup> which was considered a tropical depression by September 18 in the annual tropical cyclone summary. Tracked by satellites, the system moved to the west-southwest,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-5">[3]</a></sup> and was officially classified a tropical depression on September 21 about halfway between the Lesser Antilles and Africa.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HURDAT_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-HURDAT-6">[4]</a></sup> The system remained weak as it turned toward the west-northwest. A <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hurricane_Hunters" title="Hurricane Hunters">Hurricane Hunters</a> flight indicated some intensification in the system, prompting the <a href="/wiki/National_Hurricane_Center" title="National Hurricane Center">National Hurricane Center</a> (NHC) to initiate advisories on Tropical Storm Inez, located about 800 mi (1,300 km) east of <a href="/wiki/Martinique" title="Martinique">Martinique</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-5">[3]</a></sup> With warm <a href="/wiki/Sea_surface_temperature" title="Sea surface temperature">water temperatures</a>, the storm developed a circular area of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Convection_(meteorology)" title="Convection (meteorology)">convection</a> over its center.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nasa_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-nasa-4">[2]</a></sup> Inez slowed while curving westward around a <a href="/wiki/Subtropical_ridge" title="Subtropical ridge">ridge</a> to its north and quickly intensified,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-5">[3]</a></sup> reaching hurricane status on September 26.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HURDAT_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-HURDAT-6">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>On September 27, Inez strengthened to reach winds of 120 mph (195 km/h), making it a Category 3 hurricane on the <a href="/wiki/Saffir%E2%80%93Simpson_scale" title="Saffir–Simpson scale">Saffir–Simpson scale</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HURDAT_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-HURDAT-6">[4]</a></sup> Later that day, it moved directly over the island of <a href="/wiki/Guadeloupe" title="Guadeloupe">Guadeloupe</a>. At the time, Inez was a small cyclone, with hurricane-force winds extending 50 mi (80 km) from the center;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_5-3"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-5">[3]</a></sup> meteorologists labeled it as a "micro-hurricane... because of its characteristic small, tight cloud structure and strong maximum surface wind."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nasa_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-nasa-4">[2]</a></sup> Its structure became disrupted by the island, and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Barometric_pressure" title="Barometric pressure">central pressure</a> rose from 961 mbar (28.4 inHg) to 970 mbar (29 inHg), indicative of weakening. However, Inez re-intensified after entering the eastern Caribbean Sea, reaching a minimum pressure of 929 mbar (27.4 inHg) south of <a href="/wiki/Puerto_Rico" title="Puerto Rico">Puerto Rico</a> on September 28. The Hurricane Hunters reported <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Maximum_sustained_winds" title="Maximum sustained winds">sustained winds</a> of 197 mph (317 km/h) at a height of 8,000 ft (2,400 m), the strongest winds recorded for the storm. The same flight estimated surface winds between 150 to 175 mph (241 to 282 km/h) near the center.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_5-4"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-5">[3]</a></sup> The peak intensity listed in the <a href="/wiki/HURDAT" title="HURDAT">Atlantic hurricane database</a> is 150 mph (240 km/h), making Inez a strong <a href="/wiki/List_of_Category_4_Atlantic_hurricanes" title="List of Category 4 Atlantic hurricanes">Category 4</a> hurricane.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HURDAT_6-3"><a href="#cite_note-HURDAT-6">[4]</a></sup></p>

<p>While near peak intensity, Inez was located about 160 mi (260 km) southwest of <a href="/wiki/San_Juan,_Puerto_Rico" title="San Juan, Puerto Rico">San Juan, Puerto Rico</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Eye_(cyclone)" title="Eye (cyclone)">eye</a> was tracked by radar from the island. Its increasing <a href="/wiki/Outflow_(meteorology)" title="Outflow (meteorology)">outflow</a> caused <a href="/wiki/1966_Atlantic_hurricane_season#Tropical_Storm_Judith" title="1966 Atlantic hurricane season">Tropical Storm Judith</a> to the east over the central Atlantic to dissipate. On September 29, the small but powerful hurricane made <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Landfall_(meteorology)" title="Landfall (meteorology)">landfall</a> on the <a href="/wiki/Barahona_Province" title="Barahona Province">Barahona Peninsula</a>, and quickly weakened over land. It briefly moved offshore and struck the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tiburon_Peninsula,_Haiti" title="Tiburon Peninsula, Haiti">southern peninsula</a> of <a href="/wiki/Haiti" title="Haiti">Haiti</a> near <a href="/wiki/Jacmel" title="Jacmel">Jacmel</a>. The eye emerged into the <a href="/wiki/Windward_Passage" title="Windward Passage">Windward Passage</a> near <a href="/wiki/L%C3%A9og%C3%A2ne" title="Léogâne">Léogâne</a>, although severely weakened. The pressure rose to 987 mbar (29.1 inHg),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_5-5"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-5">[3]</a></sup> and the winds decreased to 90 mph (150 km/h) over land. However, Inez quickly re-intensified over water, reaching winds of 115 mph (185 km/h) on September 30 before striking southeastern Cuba near <a href="/wiki/Guant%C3%A1namo_Bay" title="Guantánamo Bay">Guantánamo Bay</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HURDAT_6-4"><a href="#cite_note-HURDAT-6">[4]</a></sup> When Inez was over Cuba, forecasters had difficulty in predicting the storm's future; it was expected that the hurricane would move to the north off the east coast of the United States due to a weakness in the ridge to the north. The hurricane drifted west-northwestward within light steering currents, weakening over the high terrain but later moving offshore the southern coast of Cuba. While over water, the eye reorganized, and the cyclone maintained hurricane intensity. Inez eventually turned to a northward drift over central Cuba and emerged into the Atlantic Ocean on October 2.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_5-6"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-5">[3]</a></sup></p>

<p>Shortly after exiting Cuba into the Atlantic, Inez weakened into a tropical storm, and was moving northeastward through the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bahamas" title="Bahamas">Bahamas</a> about 24 hours later than forecast.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_5-7"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-5">[3]</a></sup> At the time, the storm was expected to continue to the northeast around the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bermuda_high" title="Bermuda high">Bermuda high</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nasa_4-3"><a href="#cite_note-nasa-4">[2]</a></sup> With little outflow and a large, diffuse circulation, Inez did not restrengthen much despite otherwise favorable conditions,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_5-8"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-5">[3]</a></sup> potentially due to a nearby <a href="/wiki/Trough_(meteorology)" title="Trough (meteorology)">trough</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nasa_4-4"><a href="#cite_note-nasa-4">[2]</a></sup> The movement slowed, and the 1966 hurricane summary remarked that "Inez probably became nearly stationary closer to the United States mainland than any other storm." On October 3, Inez turned to the west-southwest as another ridge strengthened over the western <a href="/wiki/Gulf_of_Mexico" title="Gulf of Mexico">Gulf of Mexico</a>. Over the subsequent few days, the eye moved directly over the <a href="/wiki/Florida_Keys" title="Florida Keys">Florida Keys</a> from <a href="/wiki/Key_Largo,_Florida" title="Key Largo, Florida">Key Largo</a> to <a href="/wiki/Key_West,_Florida" title="Key West, Florida">Key West</a> and continued through the Gulf of Mexico while slowly intensifying. On October 7, Inez brushed the northern coast of the <a href="/wiki/Yucat%C3%A1n_Peninsula" title="Yucatán Peninsula">Yucatán Peninsula</a> with hurricane-force winds. By then, the ridge had begun to weaken, allowing the hurricane to turn more west-northwestward.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_5-9"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-5">[3]</a></sup> On October 8, Inez attained a secondary peak intensity of 140 mph (220 km/h),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HURDAT_6-5"><a href="#cite_note-HURDAT-6">[4]</a></sup> with a pressure of 948 mbar (28.0 inHg). Briefly threatening the coastline of Texas, Inez turned to the southwest after the ridge again strengthened. On October 10, the hurricane made its final landfall near <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tampico,_Tamaulipas" title="Tampico, Tamaulipas">Tampico, Tamaulipas</a>, in northeastern Mexico. It weakened rapidly over the region's high terrain, dissipating on October 11.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_5-10"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-5">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>The unusual track of Inez marked the first time on record that a tropical cyclone affected the <a href="/wiki/Lesser_Antilles" title="Lesser Antilles">Lesser</a> and <a href="/wiki/Greater_Antilles" title="Greater Antilles">Greater Antilles</a>, Bahamas, Florida, and Mexico.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_5-11"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-5">[3]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Associated_Press" title="Associated Press">Associated Press</a> described Inez as the "first to strike the Yucatán Peninsula in many years."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lewis_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-lewis-7">[5]</a></sup> It was also the first to move across the Gulf of Mexico without turning to the north so late in the season, and the 65 advisories issued by the NHC was the highest number at the time. The path taken by Inez was compared to <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Betsy" title="Hurricane Betsy">Hurricane Betsy</a> in 1965, which also slowed and moved erratically due to a strong ridge.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_5-12"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-5">[3]</a></sup> Forecasters had difficulty with the hurricane because of its unusual path, as well as the inability to fly the Hurricane Hunters into Inez while it was over Cuba.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Preparations">Preparations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hurricane_Inez&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Preparations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In Guadeloupe, which was the first area the hurricane affected, residents evacuated to schools set up as shelters. <a href="/wiki/Pan_American_World_Airways" title="Pan American World Airways">Pan American World Airways</a> canceled flights to the region.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-daytona_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-daytona-9">[7]</a></sup> When Inez was near peak intensity, the NHC warned residents in southern Haiti and the Dominican Republic of the danger posed by the hurricane. The agency emphasized that the eye was visible on radar to calm tension of an unexpected change in track. In general, <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone_warnings_and_watches" title="Tropical cyclone warnings and watches">hurricane warnings</a> were issued 24 hours in advance, with the exception of southeastern Florida due to the hurricane's slowing track.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_5-13"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-5">[3]</a></sup> Overall, 250,000 people evacuated along the path of Inez.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[8]</a></sup> At Guantánamo Bay, officials ordered residents at the <a href="/wiki/Guantanamo_Bay_Naval_Base" title="Guantanamo Bay Naval Base">naval base</a> to evacuate to storm shelters. Large ships at the port there were moved to Jamaica before the storm struck.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gitmo_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-gitmo-11">[9]</a></sup> Throughout eastern Cuba, Premier <a href="/wiki/Fidel_Castro" title="Fidel Castro">Fidel Castro</a> ordered about 125,000 people to evacuate in low-lying areas, mostly in <a href="/wiki/Oriente_Province" title="Oriente Province">Oriente Province</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-spok_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-spok-12">[10]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/United_States_Air_Force" title="United States Air Force">United States Air Force</a> tracking stations in the Bahamas were secured or moved to safer locations. Residents in the Florida Keys boarded up windows and store fronts,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lodi_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-lodi-13">[11]</a></sup> and hundreds of people evacuated to a shelter in Key West. In southeastern Florida, government offices and schools closed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tol_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-tol-14">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>Along the Yucatán peninsula, about 15,000 people were evacuated from the coastal city of <a href="/wiki/Progreso,_Yucat%C3%A1n" title="Progreso, Yucatán">Progreso</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lewis_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-lewis-7">[5]</a></sup> in a region that seldom experiences hurricanes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-spt107_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-spt107-15">[13]</a></sup> In the northern Gulf of Mexico, a helicopter crashed after evacuating workers from an <a href="/wiki/Oil_platform" title="Oil platform">oil platform</a> about 65 mi (105 km) south-southwest of <a href="/wiki/Morgan_City,_Louisiana" title="Morgan City, Louisiana">Morgan City, Louisiana</a>, in otherwise calm conditions; 11 people died, including the pilot.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[14]</a></sup> When Inez was approaching its final landfall, the <a href="/wiki/National_Weather_Service" title="National Weather Service">United States Weather Bureau</a> issued hurricane warnings from <a href="/wiki/Brownsville,_Texas" title="Brownsville, Texas">Brownsville</a> to <a href="/wiki/Port_Isabel,_Texas" title="Port Isabel, Texas">Port Isabel, Texas</a>, with gale warnings northward to <a href="/wiki/Corpus_Christi,_Texas" title="Corpus Christi, Texas">Corpus Christi</a>. At least 2,000 people evacuated from Port Isabel alone,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[15]</a></sup> and many shrimp boats were secured.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[16]</a></sup> In the Tampico area where Inez made its final landfall, over 31,000 people evacuated to higher grounds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-upi1013_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-upi1013-19">[17]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Impact">Impact</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hurricane_Inez&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Impact">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>Overall, Hurricane Inez killed about 1,000 people, mostly on Hispaniola, and caused over $200 million in damage. Crop damage was less than other storms of similar magnitude, due to Inez's small size and its occurrence late in the season.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_5-14"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-5">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Caribbean_and_Bahamas">Caribbean and Bahamas</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hurricane_Inez&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Caribbean and Bahamas">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>When Inez struck Guadeloupe, a station on the island reported sustained winds of 80 mph (130 km/h), with gusts to 94 mph (151 km/h), before communications failed. Rain on the island reached 6.5 in (170 mm).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_5-15"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-5">[3]</a></sup> The high winds destroyed the roofs of thousands of houses, leaving 10,000 people homeless. There was heavy damage to both the banana and sugar crops, described as "flattened" by the <a href="/wiki/Tampa_Bay_Times" title="Tampa Bay Times">St. Petersburg Times</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-spt930_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-spt930-20">[18]</a></sup> The storm flooded a power plant in <a href="/wiki/Pointe-%C3%A0-Pitre" title="Pointe-à-Pitre">Pointe-à-Pitre</a> and downed many power lines, causing power outages.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pitts_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-pitts-21">[19]</a></sup> The Associated Press described the hurricane as having "virtually leveled a whole district of Pointe-à-Pitre".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lewiston_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-lewiston-22">[20]</a></sup> Hundreds of people were injured, although because of damaged hospitals and the power outage, most only received makeshift treatment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-victoria_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-victoria-23">[21]</a></sup> Inez left 40 people dead and $50 million in damage on the island.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_5-16"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-5">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lodi_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-lodi-13">[11]</a></sup> Strong winds also affected nearby <a href="/wiki/Antigua" title="Antigua">Antigua</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-daytona_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-daytona-9">[7]</a></sup> and damage was reported from that island to <a href="/wiki/Dominica" title="Dominica">Dominica</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jay_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-jay-24">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Virgin_Islands" title="United States Virgin Islands">United States Virgin Islands</a>, a station on <a href="/wiki/Saint_Croix,_U.S._Virgin_Islands" title="Saint Croix, U.S. Virgin Islands">St. Croix</a> reported 45 mph (72 km/h) gusts. When Inez passed south of Puerto Rico, its rainbands produced gusty winds along the island's southern coast, reaching 50 mph (80 km/h) in <a href="/wiki/Pe%C3%B1uelas,_Puerto_Rico" title="Peñuelas, Puerto Rico">Peñuelas</a>. On <a href="/wiki/Isla_de_Mona" title="Isla de Mona">Isla de Mona</a>, wind gusts were estimated at 80 mph (130 km/h).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_5-17"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-5">[3]</a></sup> Flooding occurred in southern Puerto Rico, and high winds damaged 20 houses.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lewiston_22-1"><a href="#cite_note-lewiston-22">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>Hurricane Inez struck the Dominican Republic near peak intensity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_5-18"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-5">[3]</a></sup> Along the Barahona Peninsula, more than 800 homes were destroyed, and nationwide about 5,000 people were left homeless. There was heavy damage to the country's cotton industry,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-victoria_23-1"><a href="#cite_note-victoria-23">[21]</a></sup> as well as the coffee, sugar cane, and cocoa crops along the Barahona Peninsula. In the capital city of Santo Domingo, heavy rains flooded hundreds of homes along the <a href="/wiki/Ozama_River" title="Ozama River">Ozama River</a>, forcing thousands to evacuate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-spt930_20-1"><a href="#cite_note-spt930-20">[18]</a></sup> High seas there damaged a portion of a seawall. Many rivers along the Barahona Peninsula were also flooded.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pitts_21-1"><a href="#cite_note-pitts-21">[19]</a></sup> Throughout the country, Inez killed about 100 people,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_5-19"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-5">[3]</a></sup> including 55 in <a href="/wiki/Juancho" title="Juancho">Juancho</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-victoria_23-2"><a href="#cite_note-victoria-23">[21]</a></sup> and left $12 million in damage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_5-20"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-5">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>In neighboring Haiti, high winds downed power lines and radio towers, which cut outside communication. High rainfall led to flash flooding between mountain ranges,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-victoria_23-3"><a href="#cite_note-victoria-23">[21]</a></sup> in a location nicknamed the "valley of death"; there, the Weather Bureau remarked that "local winds... may well have exceeded 160 mph (260 km/h)".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jay_24-1"><a href="#cite_note-jay-24">[22]</a></sup> Thousands of homes were either destroyed or lost their roof.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pitts_21-2"><a href="#cite_note-pitts-21">[19]</a></sup> The Haitian government reported over 1,000 deaths in the weeks after the storm. The United States government reported that the hurricane "killed no more than 50" in Haiti, and "nor was there a real emergency," due to president <a href="/wiki/Fran%C3%A7ois_Duvalier" title="François Duvalier">François Duvalier</a> misusing previous aid.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-novak_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-novak-25">[23]</a></sup> The U.S. assessment was incorrect, as the 1966 annual tropical cyclone summary in the <a href="/wiki/Monthly_Weather_Review" title="Monthly Weather Review">Monthly Weather Review</a> indicated 750 deaths in Haiti,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_5-21"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-5">[3]</a></sup> mostly in Jacmel. President Duvalier considered the hurricane the "worst storm... since the 1920s."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lodi_13-2"><a href="#cite_note-lodi-13">[11]</a></sup> In the country, 1,000 people were injured and 60,000 were left homeless, and damage was estimated at $20.35 million.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-re121_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-re121-26">[24]</a></sup> Throughout Hispaniola, about 1,500 people were seriously injured, and many people were still recovering from <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Flora" title="Hurricane Flora">Hurricane Flora</a> in 1963.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jay_24-2"><a href="#cite_note-jay-24">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p>A station at Guantánamo Bay reported winds of 138 mph (222 km/h) when Inez moved ashore in Cuba, and rainfall reached 12 in (300 mm).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_5-22"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-5">[3]</a></sup> Tides were 10 ft (3.0 m) above normal there.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jay_24-3"><a href="#cite_note-jay-24">[22]</a></sup> The hurricane knocked down trees and power lines, and many roofs were damaged on the naval base.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gitmo_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-gitmo-11">[9]</a></sup> In the city of <a href="/wiki/Guant%C3%A1namo" title="Guantánamo">Guantánamo</a>, the hurricane destroyed 500 houses.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-spt107_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-spt107-15">[13]</a></sup> High winds heavily damaged the country's sugar and coffee crops.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-spok_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-spok-12">[10]</a></sup> When Inez re-entered the Gulf of Mexico, it threatened northwestern Cuba and produced 12 in (300 mm) of rainfall. High waves and strong winds affected the coastline.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jay_24-4"><a href="#cite_note-jay-24">[22]</a></sup> In <a href="/wiki/La_Habana_Province" title="La Habana Province">La Habana Province</a>, flooding forced 21,000 people to evacuate,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-spt107_15-2"><a href="#cite_note-spt107-15">[13]</a></sup> mostly in low-lying areas. One building was destroyed in Havana.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ap107_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-ap107-27">[25]</a></sup> Nationwide, Inez injured at least 38 people,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lodi_13-3"><a href="#cite_note-lodi-13">[11]</a></sup> and caused five deaths,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_5-23"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-5">[3]</a></sup> three of which occurred after Inez moved southwestward from the Florida Keys.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-spt107_15-3"><a href="#cite_note-spt107-15">[13]</a></sup> Damage totaled $20 million.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_5-24"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-5">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the Bahamas, the hurricane dropped heavy rainfall over three days, reaching 14.31 in (363 mm) in <a href="/wiki/Nassau,_Bahamas" title="Nassau, Bahamas">Nassau</a>. There, Inez spawned a small tornado that produced winds of over 100 mph (160 km/h),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_5-25"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-5">[3]</a></sup> killing one person and injuring three. High tides flooded portions of Nassau.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jay_24-5"><a href="#cite_note-jay-24">[22]</a></sup> Two waterspouts were reported in <a href="/wiki/Freeport,_Bahamas" title="Freeport, Bahamas">Freeport</a>. Wind gusts in the archipelago reached 90 mph (140 km/h) at <a href="/wiki/West_End,_Grand_Bahama" title="West End, Grand Bahama">West End, Grand Bahama</a>, and sustained winds reached 64 mph (103 km/h) in Freeport. Five people died throughout the country, and damage was estimated at $15.5 million.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_5-26"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-5">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="United_States">United States</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hurricane_Inez&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: United States">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

</div>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/Straits_of_Florida" title="Straits of Florida">Straits of Florida</a>, high waves from Inez overturned a 24 ft (7.3 m) <a href="/wiki/Blockade_runner" title="Blockade runner">blockade runner</a> carrying <a href="/wiki/Cuban_exile" title="Cuban exile">Cuban exiles</a> to Miami. Ten of the passengers escaped on a damaged raft, but the only survivor was the captain;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-up1006_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-up1006-28">[26]</a></sup> 45 people died due to the boat capsizing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_5-27"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-5">[3]</a></sup> High waves also disabled three ships and beached a Coast Guard boat near Miami;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tol_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-tol-14">[12]</a></sup> a lifeguard and people on the beach assisted in rescuing the crew of nine Coast Guardsmen amid rough seas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jay_24-6"><a href="#cite_note-jay-24">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p>When the hurricane moved across the Florida Keys, it dropped light rainfall, reaching 4.80 in (122 mm) near <a href="/wiki/Kendall,_Florida" title="Kendall, Florida">Kendall</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[27]</a></sup> Inez was considered a "dry hurricane", and about 1 in (25 mm) of the precipitation that fell was largely in the form of sea water spray.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-usda_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-usda-30">[28]</a></sup> Light rainfall occurred in Miami, where strong winds damaged crops due to <a href="/wiki/Sea_spray" title="Sea spray">salt spray</a> onto local vegetation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[29]</a></sup> During its passage, Inez produced above normal tides, reaching 5 ft (1.5 m) above normal on Big Pine Key. Inez produced strong winds while it was intensifying,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_5-28"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-5">[3]</a></sup> and the strongest winds occurred after the passage of the eye.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jay_24-7"><a href="#cite_note-jay-24">[22]</a></sup> The highest sustained winds were 98 mph (158 km/h) on <a href="/wiki/Plantation_Key" title="Plantation Key">Plantation Key</a>, and peak gusts were 120 mph (190 km/h) in the <a href="/wiki/Dry_Tortugas" title="Dry Tortugas">Dry Tortugas</a>. On <a href="/wiki/Big_Pine_Key,_Florida" title="Big Pine Key, Florida">Big Pine Key</a>, sustained winds were estimated at 150 mph (240 km/h), with gusts to 165 mph (266 km/h). On the Florida mainland, the highest gusts were 92 mph (148 km/h) at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Flamingo,_Florida" title="Flamingo, Florida">Flamingo</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_5-29"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-5">[3]</a></sup> The winds damaged smaller tree limbs and uprooted some trees. About 20% of the local avocado crop was blown off trees, although most was salvageable. Winds and salt spray severely damaged vegetables in southern <a href="/wiki/Miami-Dade_County,_Florida" title="Miami-Dade County, Florida">Dade County</a>, including up to 80% losses of tomatoes in some farms.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-usda_30-1"><a href="#cite_note-usda-30">[28]</a></sup> Power outages were reported in Key West.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tol_14-2"><a href="#cite_note-tol-14">[12]</a></sup> In the Florida Keys, the hurricane damaged 160 homes and 190 trailers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ap107_27-1"><a href="#cite_note-ap107-27">[25]</a></sup> Statewide, damage totaled $5 million,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_5-30"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-5">[3]</a></sup> and there were four deaths; three were elderly people who died of heart attacks while installing shutters, and the fourth was a teenager who was swept by large surf.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tol_14-3"><a href="#cite_note-tol-14">[12]</a></sup> Inez was the last hurricane to threaten the <a href="/wiki/Miami_metropolitan_area" title="Miami metropolitan area">Miami metropolitan area</a> until <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_David" title="Hurricane David">Hurricane David</a> in 1979,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[30]</a></sup> and the last hurricane to strike the Florida Keys until <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Kate_(1985)" title="Hurricane Kate (1985)">Hurricane Kate</a> in 1985.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[31]</a></sup></p>
<p>When Inez was making its final landfall in Mexico, its outer circulation reached southern Texas, producing winds of 22 mph (35 km/h) and trace rainfall. Tides reached 3.1 ft (0.94 m) above normal in Corpus Christi,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_5-31"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-5">[3]</a></sup> and 10 ft (3.0 m) waves caused $5,000 in damage to piers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[32]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mexico">Mexico</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hurricane_Inez&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Mexico">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>While the hurricane was off the north coast of the Yucatán peninsula, Inez produced sustained winds of 69 mph (111 km/h), with gusts to 81 mph (130 km/h), in <a href="/wiki/M%C3%A9rida,_Yucat%C3%A1n" title="Mérida, Yucatán">Mérida</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_5-32"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-5">[3]</a></sup> The winds cut power and knocked down trees in Progreso, as well as nearby cities. High waves destroyed a dock in <a href="/wiki/Telchac_Puerto" title="Telchac Puerto">Telchac Puerto</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lewis_7-2"><a href="#cite_note-lewis-7">[5]</a></sup> Heavy rainfall flooded streets in Mérida, although damage was not severe.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[33]</a></sup></p>
<p>At the hurricane's final landfall, a station in Tampico reported sustained winds of 115 mph (185 km/h), with gusts to 127 mph (204 km/h) before communications were cut. Inez dropped heavy rainfall upon moving ashore, causing flooding. In <a href="/wiki/Soto_la_Marina,_Tamaulipas" title="Soto la Marina, Tamaulipas">Soto la Marina, Tamaulipas</a>, rainfall totaled 10.12 in (257 mm).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_5-33"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-5">[3]</a></sup> In northeastern Mexico, more than 3,000 people were stranded by flooding after dozens of rivers and streams rose above their banks, forcing some families to hold onto trees.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-upi1013_19-1"><a href="#cite_note-upi1013-19">[17]</a></sup> The floods washed out roads around Tampico and cut communications and the water supply.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[34]</a></sup> In Tamaulipas, 15 people died while trying to deliver supplies to residents along the Tambesi River.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[35]</a></sup> Throughout the country, the hurricane left about 84,000 people homeless,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ap1015_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-ap1015-38">[36]</a></sup> and destroyed at least 2,500 houses.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[37]</a></sup> Agricultural damage totaled over $80 million, and property damage was estimated at $24 million in Tampico and <a href="/wiki/Ciudad_Madero" title="Ciudad Madero">Ciudad Madero</a> alone.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-upi1013_19-2"><a href="#cite_note-upi1013-19">[17]</a></sup> Overall, Inez killed 74 people in Mexico.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ap1015_38-1"><a href="#cite_note-ap1015-38">[36]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Aftermath">Aftermath</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hurricane_Inez&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Aftermath">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/List_of_retired_Atlantic_hurricane_names" title="List of retired Atlantic hurricane names">List of retired Atlantic hurricane names</a></div>
<p>In the days after the storm struck Guadeloupe, there was a food shortage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lewiston_22-2"><a href="#cite_note-lewiston-22">[20]</a></sup> Officials quickly cleared roads and worked to restore the damaged banana crop. <a href="/wiki/Charles_de_Gaulle" title="Charles de Gaulle">Charles de Gaulle</a> visited the island, and the French population unified to provide assistance to the island.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fr_40-0"><a href="#cite_note-fr-40">[38]</a></sup> In late October 1966, the French government set up a relief fund for Guadeloupe totaling $34 million (₣170 million <a href="/wiki/French_franc" title="French franc">francs</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[39]</a></sup> The government initiated plans to rebuild the island to withstand the winds of strong hurricanes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fr_40-1"><a href="#cite_note-fr-40">[38]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/United_States_Agency_for_International_Development" title="United States Agency for International Development">United States Agency for International Development</a> flew 20,000 pounds of blankets and clothing to the island.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-spt930_20-2"><a href="#cite_note-spt930-20">[18]</a></sup> Due to the heavy damage in Hispaniola, <a href="/wiki/VMM-365" title="VMM-365">VMM-365</a>, a <a href="/wiki/United_States_Marine_Corps" title="United States Marine Corps">United States Marine Corps</a> helicopter division, spent 454 hours and flew 740 <a href="/wiki/Sortie" title="Sortie">sorties</a> to provide 186,676 tons of relief supplies to Haiti and the Dominican Republic. The division also helped evacuate 142 hospital patients.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[40]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Dominican_Air_Force" title="Dominican Air Force">Dominican Air Force</a> also assisted in transporting injured people from outlying areas to <a href="/wiki/Santo_Domingo" title="Santo Domingo">Santo Domingo</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-victoria_23-4"><a href="#cite_note-victoria-23">[21]</a></sup> The country deployed 5,000 troops to send food, medicine, and clothing to the storm-damaged areas in the interior,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-spt930_20-3"><a href="#cite_note-spt930-20">[18]</a></sup> as well as Barahona Peninsula.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pitts_21-3"><a href="#cite_note-pitts-21">[19]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Holy_See" title="Holy See">Holy See</a> sent $2,000 to assist the residents affected by the storm in Haiti.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-re121_26-1"><a href="#cite_note-re121-26">[24]</a></sup> After a nearly ten year boycott due to the actions of Haitian president François Duvalier, the United States provided a $1.3 million loan to the country to help rebuild infrastructure and provide education. Industrialist <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Clint_Murchison,_Jr." title="Clint Murchison, Jr.">Clint Murchison</a> gave $50,000 in flour and food from a mill he owned in the country.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[41]</a></sup> The United States also sent vaccines and 4,360 pounds of food.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-vidn_44-0"><a href="#cite_note-vidn-44">[42]</a></sup></p>
<p>In Cuba, sailors on the <a href="/wiki/USS_America_(CV-66)" title="USS America (CV-66)">USS America (CV-66)</a> spent about 1,700 hours to assist Guantánamo Bay in recovery.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[43]</a></sup> The sole survivor of the sinking boat carrying Cuban exiles was tried in a military tribunal. The man was held responsible for the 44 boat deaths and was quickly executed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-r121_46-0"><a href="#cite_note-r121-46">[44]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/United_States_Coast_Guard" title="United States Coast Guard">United States Coast Guard</a> deployed boats, helicopters, and airplanes to find victims of the boat capsized in the Straits of Florida.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-up1006_28-1"><a href="#cite_note-up1006-28">[26]</a></sup> In the United States, the price for lettuce and tomatoes rose due to Inez's crop damage in Florida and reduced crop output in California.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[45]</a></sup> The country's military sent helicopters and planes to join the <a href="/wiki/Mexican_Air_Force" title="Mexican Air Force">Mexican Air Force</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ap1015_38-2"><a href="#cite_note-ap1015-38">[36]</a></sup> as well as trucks by land,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-upi1013_19-3"><a href="#cite_note-upi1013-19">[17]</a></sup> to deliver tons of supplies, including food, clothing, and medicine to flood-affected areas in Tamaulipas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[46]</a></sup> In the weeks after the storm, many residents had minimal food access, and some areas remained flooded. Conditions in Tampico, located near where the storm moved ashore, gradually returned to normal.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ap1015_38-3"><a href="#cite_note-ap1015-38">[36]</a></sup> Heavy rainfall in Mexico restored reservoirs, which assisted future crop growing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[47]</a></sup></p>
<p>The name Inez was retired and will never again be used for an Atlantic hurricane name.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[48]</a></sup></p>
