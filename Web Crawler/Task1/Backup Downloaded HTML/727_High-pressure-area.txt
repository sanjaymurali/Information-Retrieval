<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">High-pressure area</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"><span class="mw-redirectedfrom">  (Redirected from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/w/index.php?title=High_pressure_area&amp;redirect=no" title="High pressure area">High pressure area</a>)</span></div>

<p>A <b>high-pressure area</b>, <b>high</b> or <b>anticyclone</b> is a region where the <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_pressure" title="Atmospheric pressure">atmospheric pressure</a> at the surface of the planet is greater than its surrounding environment.</p>
<p>Winds within high-pressure areas flow outward from the higher pressure areas near their centers towards the lower pressure areas further from their centers. Gravity adds to the forces causing this general movement, because the higher pressure compresses the column of air near the center of the area into greater density – and so greater weight compared to lower pressure, lower density, and lower weight of the air outside the center.</p>
<p>However, because the planet is rotating underneath the atmosphere, and frictional forces arise as the planetary surface drags some atmosphere with it, the air flow from center to periphery is not direct, but is twisted due to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coriolis_effect" title="Coriolis effect">Coriolis effect</a>, or the merely apparent <a href="/wiki/Force" title="Force">force</a> that arise when the observer is in a rotating frame of reference. Viewed from above this twist in wind direction is in the same direction as the rotation of the planet.</p>
<p>The strongest high-pressure areas are associated with cold air masses which push away out of polar regions during the winter when there is less sun to warm neighboring regions. These Highs change character and weaken once they move further over relatively warmer water bodies.</p>
<p>Somewhat weaker but more common are high-pressure areas caused by atmospheric subsidence, that is, areas where large masses of cooler drier air descend from an elevation of 8 to 15 km after the lower temperatures have precipitated out the water vapor.</p>
<p>Many of the features of Highs may be understood in context of middle- or <a href="/wiki/Mesoscale_meteorology" title="Mesoscale meteorology">meso-scale</a> and relatively enduring dynamics of a planet's <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_circulation" title="Atmospheric circulation">atmospheric circulation</a>. For example, massive atmospheric subsidences occur as part of the descending branches of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ferrel_cell" title="Ferrel cell">Ferrel cells</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hadley_cell" title="Hadley cell">Hadley cells</a>. Hadley cells help form the <a href="/wiki/Subtropical_ridge" title="Subtropical ridge">subtropical ridge</a>, steer <a href="/wiki/Tropical_wave" title="Tropical wave">tropical waves</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone" title="Tropical cyclone">tropical cyclones</a> across the ocean and is strongest during the summer. The subtropical ridge also helps form most of the world's <a href="/wiki/Desert" title="Desert">deserts</a>.</p>
<p>On <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">English-language</a> <a href="/wiki/Weather_map" title="Weather map">weather maps</a>, high-pressure centers are identified by the letter H. Weather maps in other languages may use different letters or symbols.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Wind_circulation_in_the_northern_and_southern_hemispheres">Wind circulation in the northern and southern hemispheres</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=High-pressure_area&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Wind circulation in the northern and southern hemispheres">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The direction of wind flow around an atmospheric high-pressure area and a <a href="/wiki/Low-pressure_area" title="Low-pressure area">low-pressure area</a>, as seen from above, depends on the hemisphere. High-pressure systems rotate clockwise in the northern Hemisphere; low-pressure systems rotate clockwise in the southern hemisphere.</p>
<p>The scientific terms in English used to describe the weather systems generated by highs and lows were introduced in the mid-1800s, mostly by the British. The scientific theories which explain the general phenomena originated about two centuries earlier.</p>
<p>The term <a href="/wiki/Cyclone" title="Cyclone">Cyclone</a> was coined by <a href="/wiki/Henry_Piddington" title="Henry Piddington">Henry Piddington</a> of the British East India Company to describe the devastating storm of December 1789 in Coringa, India.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> A cyclone forms around a low-pressure area. <a href="/wiki/Anticyclone" title="Anticyclone">Anticyclone</a>, the term for the kind of weather around a high-pressure area, was coined in 1877 by <a href="/wiki/Francis_Galton" title="Francis Galton">Francis Galton</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> to indicate an area whose winds revolved in the opposite direction of a <a href="/wiki/Cyclone" title="Cyclone">cyclone</a>. In British English, the opposite direction of clockwise is referred to as anticlockwise, making the label <i>anticyclones</i> a logical extension.</p>
<p>A simple rule of thumb is that for high-pressure areas, where generally air flows from the center outward, the <a href="/wiki/Coriolis_force" title="Coriolis force">coriolis force</a> given by the earth's rotation to the air circulation is in the opposite direction of earth's apparent rotation if viewed from above the hemisphere's pole. So, both the earth and winds around a low-pressure area rotate counter-clockwise in the northern hemisphere, and clockwise in the southern. The opposite to these two cases occurs in the case of a high. These results derive from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coriolis_effect" title="Coriolis effect">Coriolis effect</a>; that article explains in detail the physics, and provides an animation of a model to aid understanding.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Formation">Formation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=High-pressure_area&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Formation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>High-pressure systems form due to downward motion through the <a href="/wiki/Troposphere" title="Troposphere">troposphere</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere" title="Atmosphere">atmospheric</a> layer where <a href="/wiki/Weather" title="Weather">weather</a> occurs. Preferred areas within a <a href="/wiki/Synoptic_scale_meteorology" title="Synoptic scale meteorology">synoptic</a> flow pattern in higher levels of the troposphere are beneath the western side of troughs.</p>
<p>On weather maps, these areas show converging winds (<a href="/wiki/Contour_line#Wind" title="Contour line">isotachs</a>), also known as <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Convergence_(meteorology)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Convergence (meteorology) (page does not exist)">convergence</a>, near or above the level of non-divergence, which is near the 500 <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/hectopascal" title="wikt:hectopascal">hPa</a> pressure surface about midway up through the troposphere, and about half the atmospheric pressure at the surface.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>High-pressure systems are alternatively referred to as anticyclones. On English-language weather maps, high-pressure centers are identified by the letter H in English,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> within the <a href="/wiki/Contour_line#Barometric_pressure" title="Contour line">isobar</a> with the highest pressure value. On constant pressure upper level charts, it is located within the highest height line contour.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AMS_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-AMS-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Typical_conditions">Typical conditions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=High-pressure_area&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Typical conditions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Subtropicalridge2000091412.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The subtropical ridge shows up as a large area of black (dryness) on this water vapor satellite image from September 2000</div>

<p>Highs are frequently associated with light winds at the surface and <a href="/wiki/Subsidence_(atmosphere)" title="Subsidence (atmosphere)">subsidence</a> through the lower portion of the <a href="/wiki/Troposphere" title="Troposphere">troposphere</a>. In general, subsidence will dry out an air mass by <a href="/wiki/Adiabatic_process" title="Adiabatic process">adiabatic</a>, or compressional, heating.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Appendix_G:_Glossary_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Appendix_G:_Glossary-8">[8]</a></sup> Thus, high pressure typically brings clear skies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-usatoday.com_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-usatoday.com-9">[9]</a></sup> During the day, since no clouds are present to reflect sunlight, there is more incoming shortwave <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solar_radiation" title="Solar radiation">solar radiation</a> and temperatures rise. At night, the absence of clouds means that <a href="/wiki/Outgoing_longwave_radiation" title="Outgoing longwave radiation">outgoing longwave radiation</a> (i.e. heat energy from the surface) is not absorbed, giving cooler <a href="/wiki/Diurnal_temperature_variation" title="Diurnal temperature variation">diurnal</a> low temperatures in all seasons. When surface winds become light, the subsidence produced directly under a high-pressure system can lead to a buildup of particulates in urban areas under the <a href="/wiki/Ridge_(meteorology)" title="Ridge (meteorology)">ridge</a>, leading to widespread <a href="/wiki/Haze" title="Haze">haze</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Haze_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Haze-10">[10]</a></sup> If the low level <a href="/wiki/Relative_humidity" title="Relative humidity">relative humidity</a> rises towards 100 percent overnight, <a href="/wiki/Fog" title="Fog">fog</a> can form.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fog_characteristics_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Fog_characteristics-11">[11]</a></sup></p>

<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:High_pressure_symbol.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The letter <a href="/wiki/H" title="H">H</a> is used to represent a high-pressure area.</div>

<p>Strong, vertically shallow high-pressure systems moving from higher latitudes to lower latitudes in the northern hemisphere are associated with continental arctic air masses.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cbc.ca_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-cbc.ca-12">[12]</a></sup> Once arctic air moves over an unfrozen ocean, the air mass modifies greatly over the warmer water and takes on the character of a maritime air mass, which reduces the strength of the high-pressure system.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-faa.gov_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-faa.gov-13">[13]</a></sup> When extremely cold air moves over relatively warm oceans, <a href="/wiki/Polar_low" title="Polar low">polar lows</a> can develop.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rasmussen.2C_E.A_2003_pp_612_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-Rasmussen.2C_E.A_2003_pp_612-14">[14]</a></sup> However, warm and moist (or maritime tropical) air masses that move poleward from tropical sources are slower to modify than arctic air masses.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-userpages.umbc.edu_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-userpages.umbc.edu-15">[15]</a></sup></p>

</div>

<p>In terms of <a href="/wiki/Climatology" title="Climatology">climatology</a>, high pressure forms at the <a href="/wiki/Horse_latitudes" title="Horse latitudes">horse latitudes</a>, or torrid zone,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> between the latitudes of 20 and 40 degrees from the <a href="/wiki/Equator" title="Equator">equator</a>, as a result of air that has been uplifted at the <a href="/wiki/Equator" title="Equator">equator</a>. As the hot air rises it cools, losing moisture; it is then transported poleward where it descends, creating the high-pressure area.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> This is part of the Hadley cell circulation and is known as the subtropical ridge or subtropical high, and is strongest in the summer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> The subtropical ridge is a warm core high-pressure system, meaning it strengthens with height.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NH_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-NH-19">[19]</a></sup> Many of the world's deserts are caused by these climatological high-pressure systems.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>Some climatological high-pressure areas acquire regionally based names. The land-based <a href="/wiki/Siberian_High" title="Siberian High">Siberian High</a> often remains quasi-stationary for more than a month during the most frigid time of the year, making it unique in that regard. It is also a bit larger and more persistent than its counterpart in <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North America</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> Surface winds accelerating down valleys down the western <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pacific_ocean" title="Pacific ocean">Pacific ocean</a> coastline, causing the winter monsoon.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> Arctic high-pressure systems such as the Siberian High are cold core, meaning that they weaken with height.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NH_19-1"><a href="#cite_note-NH-19">[19]</a></sup> The influence of the <a href="/wiki/Azores_High" title="Azores High">Azores High</a>, also known as the Bermuda High, brings fair weather over much of the North <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Ocean" title="Atlantic Ocean">Atlantic Ocean</a> and mid to late summer <a href="/wiki/Heat_wave" title="Heat wave">heat waves</a> in western <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> Along its southerly periphery, the clockwise circulation often impels <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Easterly_wave" title="Easterly wave">easterly waves</a>, and tropical cyclones that develop from them, across the ocean towards landmasses in the western portion of ocean basins during the <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="Atlantic hurricane season">hurricane season</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AOML_FAQ_G5_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-AOML_FAQ_G5-24">[24]</a></sup> The highest barometric pressure ever recorded on Earth was 1,085.7 hectopascals (32.06 inHg) measured in <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Tonsontsengel,_Mongolia&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Tonsontsengel, Mongolia (page does not exist)">Tonsontsengel, Mongolia</a> on 19 December 2001.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Connection_to_wind">Connection to wind</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=High-pressure_area&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Connection to wind">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Geostrophic_wind" title="Geostrophic wind">Geostrophic wind</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Wind" title="Wind">Wind</a> flows from areas of high pressure to areas of <a href="/wiki/Low-pressure_area" title="Low-pressure area">low pressure</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> This is due to <a href="/wiki/Density" title="Density">density</a> differences between the two <a href="/wiki/Air_mass" title="Air mass">air masses</a>. Since stronger high-pressure systems contain cooler or drier air, the air mass is more dense and flows towards areas that are warm or moist, which are in the vicinity of low pressure areas in advance of their associated <a href="/wiki/Cold_front" title="Cold front">cold fronts</a>. The stronger the pressure difference, or pressure gradient, between a high-pressure system and a low-pressure system, the stronger the wind. The <a href="/wiki/Coriolis_force" title="Coriolis force">coriolis force</a> caused by the <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a>'s rotation is what gives winds within high-pressure systems their clockwise circulation in the northern hemisphere (as the wind moves outward and is deflected right from the center of high pressure) and counterclockwise circulation in the southern hemisphere (as the wind moves outward and is deflected left from the center of high pressure). Friction with land slows down the wind flowing out of high-pressure systems and causes wind to flow more outward than would be the case in the absence of friction. This is known as a <a href="/wiki/Geostrophic_wind" title="Geostrophic wind">geostrophic wind</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
