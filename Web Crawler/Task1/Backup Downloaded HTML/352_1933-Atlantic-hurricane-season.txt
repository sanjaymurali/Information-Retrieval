<p>The <b>1933 Atlantic hurricane season</b> was the second most active <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="Atlantic hurricane season">Atlantic hurricane season</a> on record, with 20 storms forming in the northwest <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Ocean" title="Atlantic Ocean">Atlantic Ocean</a>. The season ran through the summer and the first half of fall in 1933, with activity as early as May and as late as November. A tropical cyclone was active for all but 13 days from June 28 to October 7. The year was surpassed in total number of <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone" title="Tropical cyclone">tropical cyclones</a> by the <a href="/wiki/2005_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="2005 Atlantic hurricane season">2005 season</a>, which broke the record with 28 storms. Tropical cyclones that did not approach populated areas or shipping lanes, especially if they were relatively weak and of short duration, may have remained undetected. Because technologies such as <a href="/wiki/Satellite_imagery" title="Satellite imagery">satellite monitoring</a> were not available until the 1960s, historical data on tropical cyclones from this period are often not reliable. Compensating for the lack of comprehensive observation, one hurricane researcher estimates the season could have produced 24 tropical cyclones.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Of the 20 storms during the season, 11 attained hurricane status. Six of those were major hurricanes, with sustained winds of over 111 mph (179 km/h). Two of the hurricanes reached winds of 160 mph (260 km/h), which is a <a href="/wiki/List_of_Category_5_Atlantic_hurricanes" title="List of Category 5 Atlantic hurricanes">Category 5</a>–the highest of 5– on the modern <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saffir-Simpson_Hurricane_Scale" title="Saffir-Simpson Hurricane Scale">Saffir-Simpson Hurricane Scale</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[nb 1]</a></sup> The season produced several deadly storms, with eight storms killing more than 20 people. All but 3 of the 20 known storms affected land at some point during their durations.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Season_summary">Season summary</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=1933_Atlantic_hurricane_season&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Season summary">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The 1933 season was the most active of its time, surpassing the previous record-holder of 19 storms in <a href="/wiki/1887_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="1887 Atlantic hurricane season">1887</a>. Fifteen of the season's storms made <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Landfall_(meteorology)" title="Landfall (meteorology)">landfall</a> as tropical cyclones, and another struck land as an extratropical storm. Eight tropical storms, including six hurricanes, hit the United States during the season, including the <a href="/wiki/1933_Chesapeake%E2%80%93Potomac_hurricane" title="1933 Chesapeake–Potomac hurricane">Chesapeake–Potomac hurricane</a>, which the <a href="/wiki/National_Oceanic_and_Atmospheric_Administration" title="National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration">U.S. Weather Bureau</a> described as one of the most severe in history along the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mid-Atlantic_States" title="Mid-Atlantic States">Mid-Atlantic States</a>. Seven tropical storms, including four hurricanes, hit <a href="/wiki/Mexico" title="Mexico">Mexico</a>, two of which caused severe damage in the <a href="/wiki/Tampico" title="Tampico">Tampico</a> area.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-5">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>The season was continuously active, with six storms forming during the month of August alone.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-meta_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-meta-6">[5]</a></sup> At the time, many storms received the distinction of being the earliest <i>n</i>th storm to form, such as the earliest fifth tropical storm to form in a season. Most of the records were broken in later years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-andyhatzos_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-andyhatzos-7">[6]</a></sup> During the season, the U.S. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Weather_Bureau" title="Weather Bureau">Weather Bureau</a> issued <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone_warnings_and_watches#Tropical_Storm_Warning" title="Tropical cyclone warnings and watches">storm</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone_warnings_and_watches#Hurricane_Warning" title="Tropical cyclone warnings and watches">hurricane warnings</a> for eight storms, including coastal portions of Texas, as well as from Florida to <a href="/wiki/Massachusetts" title="Massachusetts">Massachusetts</a>, forcing the evacuations of thousands of people. The deadliest storm of the season was <a href="/wiki/1933_Tampico_hurricane" title="1933 Tampico hurricane">a hurricane</a> that struck <a href="/wiki/Tampico" title="Tampico">Tampico</a>, Mexico, killing over 184 residents. The costliest hurricane was the Chesapeake–Potomac hurricane, which caused $27 million in damage from <a href="/wiki/North_Carolina" title="North Carolina">North Carolina</a> to <a href="/wiki/New_Jersey" title="New Jersey">New Jersey</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[nb 2]</a></sup> The hurricane produced rainfall that resulted in severe crop damage in <a href="/wiki/Maryland" title="Maryland">Maryland</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-5">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>In addition to the 20 tropical storms, there were several tropical depressions of lesser intensity. The first developed on June 1 in the northwest Caribbean and dissipated a few days later. Another depression developed on July 11 over Panama and also quickly dissipated. A tropical depression developed on July 17 in the northeastern Atlantic west of the Azores, and one ship reported hurricane-force winds; however, there was little evidence that the tropical system was organized, so it was not classified as a tropical storm. Originally, there was a tropical storm in the database in the Caribbean in the middle of August, but it was downgraded to a tropical depression due to lack of any reports of gale-force winds. Similarly, there was a tropical storm in the database in late September, but it was also downgraded to a tropical depression due to lack of gale-force winds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-meta_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-meta-6">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>The season produced the highest <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Accumulated_Cyclone_Energy" title="Accumulated Cyclone Energy">Accumulated Cyclone Energy</a> (ACE) on record with a total of 259. The measurement is a method to compare tropical cyclone activity between seasons. Originally, 1933 had an ACE of 213,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ACE_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-ACE-9">[7]</a></sup> which was surpassed by <a href="/wiki/1950_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="1950 Atlantic hurricane season">1950</a> and <a href="/wiki/2005_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="2005 Atlantic hurricane season">2005</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[8]</a></sup> However, the <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_hurricane_reanalysis_project" title="Atlantic hurricane reanalysis project">Atlantic hurricane reanalysis project</a> found that the storms in 1933 were stronger than initially reported.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ACE_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-ACE-9">[7]</a></sup></p>

<p>The first storm of the season formed on May 14 in the southwestern <a href="/wiki/Caribbean_Sea" title="Caribbean Sea">Caribbean Sea</a> off the east coast of Nicaragua. It moved quickly to the north-northwest, passing just northeast of the eastern tip of Honduras. On May 15, the storm turned to the west and entered the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_of_Mexico" title="Gulf of Mexico">Gulf of Mexico</a> while moving around the <a href="/wiki/Yucat%C3%A1n_Peninsula" title="Yucatán Peninsula">Yucatán Peninsula</a>. The next day, it attained peak winds of 50 mph (85 km/h), based on a ship report. Turning southward, the storm made landfall on <a href="/wiki/Ciudad_del_Carmen" title="Ciudad del Carmen">Ciudad del Carmen</a> in Mexico early on May 19 with winds of 40 mph (64 km/h). It dissipated over land later that day.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-meta_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-meta-6">[5]</a></sup></p>

<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/1933_Trinidad_hurricane" title="1933 Trinidad hurricane">1933 Trinidad hurricane</a></div>
<p>A westward-moving <a href="/wiki/Tropical_wave" title="Tropical wave">tropical wave</a> spawned a tropical depression on June 24 about halfway between the southern <a href="/wiki/Lesser_Antilles" title="Lesser Antilles">Lesser Antilles</a> and the coast of Africa. The depression quickly intensified into a tropical storm, and by June 27 it became a hurricane off the northern coast of <a href="/wiki/Guyana" title="Guyana">Guyana</a>. At 2100 UTC that day, the hurricane struck extreme southern <a href="/wiki/Trinidad" title="Trinidad">Trinidad</a> with winds of 85 mph (137 km/h). After crossing the island, the hurricane moved across the <a href="/wiki/Paria_Peninsula" title="Paria Peninsula">Paria Peninsula</a> in northeastern Venezuela with the same intensity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-meta_6-3"><a href="#cite_note-meta-6">[5]</a></sup> The hurricane was earliest to affect the region on record, and was the first storm in over 50 years to affected Trinidad and Venezuela. In Trinidad, 13 people were killed, about 1,000 people were left homeless, and damage was estimated at $3 million. In Venezuela, the hurricane caused power outages, destroyed several houses, and killed several people.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-5">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>After crossing Venezuela into the eastern Caribbean, the hurricane moved northwestward and gradually intensified. On July 3 it struck western Cuba with winds of 100 mph (160 km/h).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-meta_6-4"><a href="#cite_note-meta-6">[5]</a></sup> In the country, the storm killed 22 people, while damage amounted to $4 million.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cuba_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Cuba-11">[9]</a></sup> A building <a href="/wiki/Ridge_(meteorology)" title="Ridge (meteorology)">high pressure area</a> turned the hurricane westward in the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_of_Mexico" title="Gulf of Mexico">Gulf of Mexico</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_5-3"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-5">[4]</a></sup> and after further strengthening the storm attained peak winds of 110 mph (175 km/h) on July 5. It turned to the west-southwest and made landfall in northeastern Mexico on July 7 with winds of 100 mph (160 km/h), about halfway between <a href="/wiki/Tampico" title="Tampico">Tampico</a> and the Mexico/United States border. It dissipated about 11 hours later over Mexico.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-meta_6-5"><a href="#cite_note-meta-6">[5]</a></sup> There were several deaths and heavy damage in the area where the storm moved ashore.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_5-4"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-5">[4]</a></sup> Across its path, the hurricane killed 35 people.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-deadliest_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-deadliest-12">[10]</a></sup></p>

<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/1933_Texas_tropical_storm" title="1933 Texas tropical storm">1933 Texas tropical storm</a></div>
<p>The third tropical storm of the season (initially classified as two separate storms but later identified as a single track) was first observed on July 14 near <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/St._Kitts" title="St. Kitts">St. Kitts</a>. It moved quickly westward and passed just south of <a href="/wiki/Puerto_Rico" title="Puerto Rico">Puerto Rico</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hispaniola" title="Hispaniola">Hispaniola</a> as a weak storm. The system curved slightly to the west-northwest and brushed the northern coast of <a href="/wiki/Jamaica" title="Jamaica">Jamaica</a> before turning slightly westward and hitting the <a href="/wiki/States_of_Mexico" title="States of Mexico">Mexican state</a> of <a href="/wiki/Quintana_Roo" title="Quintana Roo">Quintana Roo</a>. As it crossed the <a href="/wiki/Yucat%C3%A1n_Peninsula" title="Yucatán Peninsula">Yucatán Peninsula</a> the cyclone weakened and eventually crossed into the <a href="/wiki/Bay_of_Campeche" title="Bay of Campeche">Bay of Campeche</a>. It moved quickly to the northwest, and made landfall near <a href="/wiki/Matagorda_Bay" title="Matagorda Bay">Matagorda Bay</a>, in Texas on July 23 as a 45 mph (70 km/h) tropical storm. The system turned to the northeast, and became extratropical to the east of <a href="/wiki/Dallas" title="Dallas">Dallas</a>, Texas. The extratropical system moved slowly through northern <a href="/wiki/Louisiana" title="Louisiana">Louisiana</a>, turned to the northeast, and dissipated over northeastern <a href="/wiki/Arkansas" title="Arkansas">Arkansas</a> near <a href="/wiki/Memphis,_Tennessee" title="Memphis, Tennessee">Memphis, Tennessee</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_5-5"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-5">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-unisys_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-unisys-13">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>While passing near Jamaica, the storm dropped heavy rainfall, including 9 inches (230 mm) in <a href="/wiki/Kingston,_Jamaica" title="Kingston, Jamaica">Kingston</a> which led to flooding and washouts. The rainfall also damaged several bridges and roads and resulting in delays in train schedules.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-july181933_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-july181933-14">[12]</a></sup> Mudslides and overflowing rivers flooded several towns with knee-deep waters. Moderate winds downed several banana trees across the island.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-july201933_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-july201933-15">[13]</a></sup> Prior to the arrival of the storm in Texas, numerous coastal residents boarded up their houses and businesses and voluntarily evacuated further inland. Upon making landfall, the storm produced high tides.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-july221933_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-july221933-16">[14]</a></sup> In eastern Texas and western Louisiana, the system dropped very heavy <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Precipitation_(meteorology)" title="Precipitation (meteorology)">precipitation</a>, which in places reached accumulations exceeding 20 inches (500 mm). The highest storm total occurred in <a href="/wiki/Logansport,_Louisiana" title="Logansport, Louisiana">Logansport, Louisiana</a>, which reported 21.30 inches (541 mm) in a 4-day period.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lahist_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-lahist-17">[15]</a></sup> In Louisiana, the flooding severely damaged crops and forced about 250 families near <a href="/wiki/Shreveport,_Louisiana" title="Shreveport, Louisiana">Shreveport</a> to evacuate their flooded homes. The torrential rainfall also resulted in overflowing rivers; numerous highways, roads, and railroads were either impassable or closed, with some locations experiencing water depths of up to 20 feet (6.1 m).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-july271933_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-july271933-18">[16]</a></sup> Total damage reached nearly $2 million.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1933ussum_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-1933ussum-19">[17]</a></sup></p>

<p>A dissipating <a href="/wiki/Cold_front" title="Cold front">cold front</a> spawned a tropical depression east of Bermuda on July 24. It moved east-northeastward initially, and intensified into a tropical storm early on July 25. Based on ship observations, it is estimated the storm attained peak winds of 60 mph (95 km/h). The storm turned to the northeast and was absorbed by a cold front southeast of Newfoundland early on July 27.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-meta_6-6"><a href="#cite_note-meta-6">[5]</a></sup></p>

<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/1933_Florida%E2%80%93Mexico_hurricane" title="1933 Florida–Mexico hurricane">1933 Florida–Mexico hurricane</a></div>
<p>On July 24, a tropical storm was detected. Located to the southeast of <a href="/wiki/Antigua" title="Antigua">Antigua</a>, it tracked west-northwestward, passing near <a href="/wiki/Saint_Thomas,_U.S._Virgin_Islands" title="Saint Thomas, U.S. Virgin Islands">St. Thomas</a> with winds of up to 60 mph (95 km/h). The storm strengthened and attained hurricane status the next day north of <a href="/wiki/Puerto_Rico" title="Puerto Rico">Puerto Rico</a>, and it continued its west-northwest movement. After moving through the northern <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bahamas" title="Bahamas">Bahamas</a>, the hurricane struck near <a href="/wiki/Fort_Pierce,_Florida" title="Fort Pierce, Florida">Fort Pierce, Florida</a>, with winds of 85 mph (135 km/h). The hurricane crossed the state and weakened to minimal tropical storm intensity. It turned to the west-southwest and re-strengthened to a hurricane on August 4 off the coast of Texas. It weakened again to tropical storm status and made its final landfall near <a href="/wiki/Brownsville,_Texas" title="Brownsville, Texas">Brownsville, Texas</a>, on August 5 as a strong tropical storm. The system rapidly dissipated over northern <a href="/wiki/Mexico" title="Mexico">Mexico</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_5-6"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-5">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>While moving over <a href="/wiki/Saint_Christopher" title="Saint Christopher">Saint Christopher</a>, the storm killed six people. Heavy rain was reported throughout the <a href="/wiki/Virgin_Islands" title="Virgin Islands">Virgin Islands</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-july261933_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-july261933-20">[18]</a></sup> The hurricane caused the drowning of one person in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bahamas" title="Bahamas">Bahamas</a>, and moderate winds produced severe structural damage to the buildings in the <a href="/wiki/Archipelago" title="Archipelago">archipelago</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-july281933_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-july281933-21">[19]</a></sup> In Florida, the National Weather Bureau issued storm warnings between Miami to <a href="/wiki/Titusville,_Florida" title="Titusville, Florida">Titusville</a>, while <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_governors_of_Florida" title="List of governors of Florida">Governor</a> <a href="/wiki/David_Sholtz" title="David Sholtz">David Sholtz</a> issued a mandatory evacuation for 4,200 residents in vulnerable areas around <a href="/wiki/Lake_Okeechobee" title="Lake Okeechobee">Lake Okeechobee</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-july301933_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-july301933-22">[20]</a></sup> Damage in Florida was minimal, limited to minor crops, roofs, and signs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-july311933_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-july311933-23">[21]</a></sup> In southern Texas, the hurricane produced moderate damage of $500,000, including disrupted telephone and telegraph lines.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-aug51933_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-aug51933-24">[22]</a></sup> The hurricane produced high tides along the coast of Texas, covering parts of <a href="/wiki/South_Padre_Island" title="South Padre Island">South Padre Island</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-texhis_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-texhis-25">[23]</a></sup> and heavy rains in northern Mexico caused heavy damage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_5-7"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-5">[4]</a></sup></p>

<p><b>The Chesapeake Bay-Potomac Hurricane of 1933</b><br/></p>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/1933_Chesapeake%E2%80%93Potomac_hurricane" title="1933 Chesapeake–Potomac hurricane">1933 Chesapeake–Potomac hurricane</a></div>
<p>A ship first reported a tropical depression near the Cape Verde islands on August 13. This system would become one of the most destructive hurricanes of the season. The storm moved towards the northwest and attained hurricane status on August 16. The hurricane continued to strengthen, and on August 21, it passed about 150 miles (240 km) southwest of <a href="/wiki/Bermuda" title="Bermuda">Bermuda</a> as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saffir-Simpson_Hurricane_Scale" title="Saffir-Simpson Hurricane Scale">Category 3 hurricane</a>. <a href="/wiki/St._George%27s,_Bermuda" title="St. George's, Bermuda">St. George's</a> avoided a direct hit but reported wind speeds of up to 64 mph (103 km/h). On August 22, the hurricane turned west-northwest and reached its peak intensity, with maximum sustained winds of 140 mph (220 km/h), equivalent to a Category 4 hurricane in the modern-day <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saffir-Simpson_Hurricane_Scale" title="Saffir-Simpson Hurricane Scale">Saffir-Simpson Hurricane Scale</a>. However, it weakened quickly afterwards. On August 23, the storm made landfall on the <a href="/wiki/Outer_Banks" title="Outer Banks">Outer Banks</a> of <a href="/wiki/North_Carolina" title="North Carolina">North Carolina</a> as a Category 1 hurricane and continued to quickly weaken as it moved inland, away from its energy source.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NOAA1_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-NOAA1-26">[24]</a></sup> The storm turned to the north, then to the northeast, passing through <a href="/wiki/Virginia" title="Virginia">Virginia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Maryland" title="Maryland">Maryland</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Pennsylvania" title="Pennsylvania">Pennsylvania</a> before weakening to a tropical depression over New York.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_5-8"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-5">[4]</a></sup> The storm became extratropical on August 25 and turned to the east, moving through Atlantic Canada and dissipating on August 28.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-meta_6-7"><a href="#cite_note-meta-6">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>The hurricane caused damage ranging from moderate to severe in the corridor between North Carolina and New Jersey, due to high <a href="/wiki/Tide" title="Tide">tides</a> and strong winds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_5-9"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-5">[4]</a></sup> In the state of Maryland, the storm's effects resulted in severe crop damage, and many boats and piers were damaged or destroyed due to high tides and <a href="/wiki/Storm_surge" title="Storm surge">storm surge</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_5-10"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-5">[4]</a></sup> The hurricane produced heavy rainfall along its path, with a peak of 13.28 inches (337.3 mm) in <a href="/wiki/York,_Pennsylvania" title="York, Pennsylvania">York, Pennsylvania</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WPCMAX_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-WPCMAX-27">[25]</a></sup> Overall, the hurricane caused $27 million in damage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_5-11"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-5">[4]</a></sup> and 31 deaths.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-noaa_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-noaa-28">[26]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hc_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-hc-29">[27]</a></sup></p>

<p>The seventh tropical storm of the season was first observed in the eastern Caribbean on August 14. It strengthened to reach winds of 45 mph (72 km/h). After passing just south of <a href="/wiki/Jamaica" title="Jamaica">Jamaica</a>, the storm turned to the northwest and crossed over both the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Isle_of_Youth" title="Isle of Youth">Isle of Youth</a> and western <a href="/wiki/Cuba" title="Cuba">Cuba</a> on August 18. It curved northward and dissipated west of Florida on August 21.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-meta_6-8"><a href="#cite_note-meta-6">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>On <a href="/wiki/Trinidad" title="Trinidad">Trinidad</a>, rainfall from this storm and a subsequent tropical depression were the heaviest in nine years, which caused rivers to overflow and flooded parts of the island. Several boats were damaged or driven ashore from rough seas. The two storms caused damage to fields, highways, and houses, and caused the loss of crops such as <a href="/wiki/Cocoa_bean" title="Cocoa bean">cocoa</a> and bananas. In all, damage totaled $3 million and there were 13 deaths on Trinidad.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1933aug19_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-1933aug19-30">[28]</a></sup> The storm produced heavy rainfall in eastern Jamaica, including a record 24-hour total of 12.2 inches (310 mm) in the Corporate Area,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-aug171933_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-aug171933-31">[29]</a></sup> and Dallas Castle received 20.2 inches (510 mm) in 24 hours.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-may291982_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-may291982-32">[30]</a></sup> This flooded and damaged properties and water systems in <a href="/wiki/Kingston,_Jamaica" title="Kingston, Jamaica">Kingston</a> and <a href="/wiki/Saint_Andrew_Parish,_Jamaica" title="Saint Andrew Parish, Jamaica">Saint Andrew</a>, leading to a water famine until the water mains were fixed. Damage totaled to over $2.5 million,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1933aug19_30-1"><a href="#cite_note-1933aug19-30">[28]</a></sup> and 70 people were reported killed due to the flooding.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-deadliest_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-deadliest-12">[10]</a></sup> Damage was minimal in both Cuba and Florida.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_5-12"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-5">[4]</a></sup></p>

<p><b>The Great Cuba-Brownsville Hurricane of 1933</b><br/></p>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/1933_Cuba%E2%80%93Brownsville_hurricane" title="1933 Cuba–Brownsville hurricane">1933 Cuba–Brownsville hurricane</a></div>
<p>The eighth storm of the season was one of two storms in the 1933 Atlantic hurricane season to reach the intensity of a <a href="/wiki/List_of_Category_5_Atlantic_hurricanes" title="List of Category 5 Atlantic hurricanes">Category 5</a> strength on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saffir-Simpson_Hurricane_Scale" title="Saffir-Simpson Hurricane Scale">Saffir-Simpson Hurricane Scale</a>. It formed on August 22 off the west coast of Africa, and for much of its duration it maintained a west-northwest track. The system intensified into a tropical storm on August 26 and into a hurricane on August 28. Passing north of the <a href="/wiki/Lesser_Antilles" title="Lesser Antilles">Lesser Antilles</a>, the hurricane rapidly intensified as it approached the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Turks_and_Caicos" title="Turks and Caicos">Turks and Caicos</a> islands. It reached Category 5 status and its peak winds of 160 mph (260 km/h) on August 31. Subsequently, it weakened before striking northern Cuba on September 1 with winds of 120 mph (190 km/h).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-meta_6-9"><a href="#cite_note-meta-6">[5]</a></sup> In the country, the hurricane left about 100,000 people homeless and killed over 70 people. Damage was heaviest near the storm's path, and the strong winds destroyed houses and left areas without power. Damage was estimated at $11 million.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[31]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pb_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-pb-34">[32]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[33]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[nb 3]</a></sup></p>
<p>After exiting from Cuba, the hurricane entered the Gulf of Mexico and restrengthened. On September 2, it re-attained winds of 140 mph (230 km/h).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-meta_6-10"><a href="#cite_note-meta-6">[5]</a></sup> Initially the hurricane posed a threat to the area around <a href="/wiki/Corpus_Christi,_Texas" title="Corpus Christi, Texas">Corpus Christi, Texas</a>, and the local <a href="/wiki/National_Oceanic_and_Atmospheric_Administration" title="National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration">United States Weather Bureau</a> forecaster advised people to stay away from the Texas coastline during the busy <a href="/wiki/Labor_Day" title="Labor Day">Labor Day Weekend</a>. Officials declared martial law in the city and mandated evacuations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-corp_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-corp-37">[34]</a></sup> However, the hurricane turned more to the west and struck near <a href="/wiki/Brownsville,_Texas" title="Brownsville, Texas">Brownsville</a> early on September 5 with winds estimated at 125 mph (205 km/h).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-meta_6-11"><a href="#cite_note-meta-6">[5]</a></sup> It quickly dissipated after causing heavy damage in the <a href="/wiki/Rio_Grande_Valley" title="Rio Grande Valley">Rio Grande Valley</a>. High winds caused heavy damage to the citrus crop. The hurricane left $16.9 million in damage and 40 deaths in southern Texas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-loc_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-loc-38">[35]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hpc_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-hpc-39">[36]</a></sup></p>

<p>On August 23, another tropical storm was observed north of Puerto Rico. It moved northwestward for three days, slowly strengthening as it moved over the open ocean. <p><b>The Treasure Coast Hurricane of 1933</b><br/></p>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/1933_Treasure_Coast_hurricane" title="1933 Treasure Coast hurricane">1933 Treasure Coast hurricane</a></div>
<p>A tropical storm was first observed on August 31, 225 miles (360 km) north-northeast of the island of <a href="/wiki/Antigua" title="Antigua">Antigua</a>. The storm rapidly intensified as it moved quickly to the west-northwest, attaining hurricane status later that day, and major hurricane strength on September 1, while located to the north of <a href="/wiki/Puerto_Rico" title="Puerto Rico">Puerto Rico</a>. It continued west-northwestward and attained its peak intensity, with maximum sustained winds of 140 mph (225 km/h), on September 2. The hurricane, then at Category 4 on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saffir-Simpson_Hurricane_Scale" title="Saffir-Simpson Hurricane Scale">Saffir-Simpson Hurricane Scale</a>, moved through the northern Bahamas at peak intensity and weakened slightly before making landfall on <a href="/wiki/Jupiter,_Florida" title="Jupiter, Florida">Jupiter, Florida</a>, with winds of 125 mph (200 km/h) on September 4. The system weakened rapidly over Florida to tropical storm status, and after turning to the north, decelerated. The weakening storm slowly moved through <a href="/wiki/Georgia_(U.S._state)" title="Georgia (U.S. state)">Georgia</a> before dissipating near the Georgia/<a href="/wiki/South_Carolina" title="South Carolina">South Carolina</a> border on September 7.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_5-15"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-5">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>On <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eleuthera_Island" title="Eleuthera Island">Eleuthera Island</a>, the Category 4 hurricane blew away the roofs of buildings, wrecked wharves, and lost boats. Hurricane warnings were issued for the eastern Florida coastline, and 3,000 people were evacuated around <a href="/wiki/Lake_Okeechobee" title="Lake Okeechobee">Lake Okeechobee</a> to safer areas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sept41933_40-0"><a href="#cite_note-sept41933-40">[37]</a></sup> In southeastern Florida, the strong winds broke many glass windows and downed trees and power lines; severe house damage was reported near the landfall location. The hurricane's powerful winds also severely damaged crops, including 4 million boxes of <a href="/wiki/Citrus" title="Citrus">citrus</a> fruit across the state.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_5-16"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-5">[4]</a></sup> In total, Florida suffered $2 million in damage and two deaths.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Florida_41-0"><a href="#cite_note-Florida-41">[38]</a></sup></p>

<p><b>The Outer Banks Hurricane of 1933</b><br/></p>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/1933_Outer_Banks_hurricane" title="1933 Outer Banks hurricane">1933 Outer Banks hurricane</a></div>
<p>On September 8, an area of disturbed weather to the east of the <a href="/wiki/Lesser_Antilles" title="Lesser Antilles">Lesser Antilles</a> organized into a tropical storm. It moved north-northeastward, and after a turn to the northwest, the system intensified to hurricane strength on September 10. It steadily intensified and reached a peak strength of 140 mph (225 km/h) on September 15. It slowed as it turned to the north, striking southeastern <a href="/wiki/North_Carolina" title="North Carolina">North Carolina</a> just west of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cape_Hatteras,_North_Carolina" title="Cape Hatteras, North Carolina">Cape Hatteras</a> as a Category 2 hurricane.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NOAA1_26-1"><a href="#cite_note-NOAA1-26">[24]</a></sup> After moving through the <a href="/wiki/Outer_Banks" title="Outer Banks">Outer Banks</a>, the system accelerated to the northeast and became extratropical on September 18 about halfway between <a href="/wiki/Cape_Cod" title="Cape Cod">Cape Cod</a> and the southern tip of <a href="/wiki/Nova_Scotia" title="Nova Scotia">Nova Scotia</a>. The extratropical storm passed over Nova Scotia, <a href="/wiki/Newfoundland_(island)" title="Newfoundland (island)">Newfoundland</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Labrador" title="Labrador">Labrador</a> before dissipating east of <a href="/wiki/Greenland" title="Greenland">Greenland</a> on September 22.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_5-17"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-5">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>Strong winds from the hurricane downed trees and power lines in southeastern North Carolina, causing damage to many houses. The hurricane produced a storm surge that flooded coastal streets with 3 to 4 feet (0.9–1.2 m) of water. In all, the hurricane caused at least 21 deaths, primarily due to drowning in high waters.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_5-18"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-5">[4]</a></sup> Damage totaled around $4.5 million.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[39]</a></sup></p>

<p>On September 10, as Hurricane Twelve was intensifying over the waters of the Atlantic Ocean, another area of disturbed weather developed into a tropical storm over the western <a href="/wiki/Caribbean_Sea" title="Caribbean Sea">Caribbean Sea</a> off the coast of <a href="/wiki/Guatemala" title="Guatemala">Guatemala</a>. It moved slowly northward and strengthened, becoming a hurricane on September 12 just east of <a href="/wiki/Belize" title="Belize">Belize</a>. On the next day, the hurricane made landfall on the <a href="/wiki/Mexico" title="Mexico">Mexican</a> state of <a href="/wiki/Quintana_Roo" title="Quintana Roo">Quintana Roo</a>, and the system weakened to a tropical storm as it moved northwestward across the <a href="/wiki/Yucat%C3%A1n_Peninsula" title="Yucatán Peninsula">Yucatán Peninsula</a>. On September 14 it again regained hurricane status over the <a href="/wiki/Bay_of_Campeche" title="Bay of Campeche">Bay of Campeche</a>. The hurricane struck <a href="/wiki/Tampico" title="Tampico">Tampico</a> on September 15 and then dissipated.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_5-19"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-5">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>The storm caused severe damage in Tampico and further inland, leaving several thousand homeless. According to <i>The New York Times</i>, at least 67 people were killed. <a href="/wiki/Tuxpan" title="Tuxpan">Tuxpan</a>, south of Tampico, also suffered heavy damage with many houses and office buildings destroyed. Total property losses were estimated at several million dollars.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[40]</a></sup></p>

<p><b>The Great Tampico Hurricane of 1933</b><br/></p>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/1933_Tampico_hurricane" title="1933 Tampico hurricane">1933 Tampico hurricane</a></div>
<p>On the other side of the <a href="/wiki/Caribbean_Sea" title="Caribbean Sea">Caribbean Sea</a>, what would become the second Category 5 hurricane of the season, and the 14th tropical storm of the year, was first observed on September 16 to the east of the southern <a href="/wiki/Leeward_Islands" title="Leeward Islands">Leeward Islands</a>. The cyclone, then a tropical depression, tracked to the west-northwest through the islands, slowly strengthening into a tropical storm on September 18. It attained hurricane strength on September 19 near <a href="/wiki/Jamaica" title="Jamaica">Jamaica</a>, then began a period of rapid intensification south of that island. On September 21, a ship measured a central pressure of 929 millibars (27 inHg), indicating that the hurricane had intensified to a peak of 160 mph (260 km/h).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-meta_6-12"><a href="#cite_note-meta-6">[5]</a></sup> Continuing west-northwestward, the hurricane weakened somewhat and made landfall 40 miles (65 km) south of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cozumel_Island" title="Cozumel Island">Cozumel Island</a> on September 22. Winds at landfall were estimated to have been 140 mph (230 km/h), equivalent to a Category 4 hurricane by modern classification. The hurricane weakened slightly over the <a href="/wiki/Yucat%C3%A1n_Peninsula" title="Yucatán Peninsula">Yucatán Peninsula</a>, but then re-strengthened over the Gulf of Mexico to reach a second peak intensity of 110 mph (175 km/h) on September 24.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-meta_6-13"><a href="#cite_note-meta-6">[5]</a></sup> Shortly thereafter, the storm made landfall near <a href="/wiki/Tampico" title="Tampico">Tampico</a>. It dissipated on September 25 over Mexico.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_5-20"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-5">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>Near Jamaica, the hurricane caused rough seas, although damage, if any, is unknown.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_5-21"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-5">[4]</a></sup> While moving across the Yucatán Peninsula, the storm produced heavy rainfall and strong winds. In <a href="/wiki/Cozumel" title="Cozumel">Cozumel</a>, the winds destroyed a 300-foot (91 m) pier and several buildings. Rough seas sunk several ships, and one person drowned. The rainfall caused several rivers to overflow, causing flooding and damage to roads and railroads in the state of <a href="/wiki/Veracruz" title="Veracruz">Veracruz</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sept221933_44-0"><a href="#cite_note-sept221933-44">[41]</a></sup> Many people in low-lying areas around Tampico evacuated for the storm.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sept231933_45-0"><a href="#cite_note-sept231933-45">[42]</a></sup></p>
<p>Reports indicate much of the city of Tampico was destroyed, and the total number of deaths and injuries amounted to over 5,000. Most of the deaths occurred from flood waters, which were 10 to 15 feet (3.0–4.6 m) deep and covered the entire city;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sept261933_46-0"><a href="#cite_note-sept261933-46">[43]</a></sup> many bodies were washed out to sea, and were never recovered. The flooding washed out roads and railroads, delaying relief efforts into the devastated area. Water and food supplies in and around Tampico were damaged or contaminated, resulting in a threat of famine or disease that further aggravated the situation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gdsept281933_47-0"><a href="#cite_note-gdsept281933-47">[44]</a></sup> Torrential rains caused more flooding, and the powerful winds damaged or destroyed nearly every building in the city and left many homeless.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sept28ap_48-0"><a href="#cite_note-sept28ap-48">[45]</a></sup> The strong winds downed numerous power lines, leaving the entire city in blackout, and destroyed two large water towers. There were at least 10 cases of looting; all of the perpetrators were executed. Damage in and around Tampico totaled over $10 million,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sept291933_49-0"><a href="#cite_note-sept291933-49">[46]</a></sup> and the storm killed over 184 people.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-deadliest_12-2"><a href="#cite_note-deadliest-12">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>The thousands of victims took refuge in churches, theatres, and public buildings. Immediately after the storm, the Mexican military placed the city under <a href="/wiki/Martial_law" title="Martial law">martial law</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sept291933_49-1"><a href="#cite_note-sept291933-49">[46]</a></sup> Military and federal authorities dispatched trains with food, water, and medicine,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sept28ap_48-1"><a href="#cite_note-sept28ap-48">[45]</a></sup> and planes bearing engineers and doctors. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_Presidents_of_Mexico" title="List of Presidents of Mexico">Mexican president</a> <a href="/wiki/Abelardo_L._Rodr%C3%ADguez" title="Abelardo L. Rodríguez">Abelardo L. Rodríguez</a> rallied citizens to aid the affected people in the storm area. The local chamber of deputies allowed $140,000 in funds for the storm victims.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gdsept281933_47-1"><a href="#cite_note-gdsept281933-47">[44]</a></sup></p>

<p>A tropical disturbance moved through the Lesser Antilles in late September. After crossing Hispaniola, a tropical depression developed on October 1 north of the Dominican Republic. <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/1933_Cuba%E2%80%93Bahamas_hurricane" title="1933 Cuba–Bahamas hurricane">1933 Cuba–Bahamas hurricane</a></div>
<p>On October 1, a tropical storm developed off the northeast coast of Nicaragua. It moved slowly northward and steadily intensified, becoming a hurricane on October 3 just west of <a href="/wiki/Jamaica" title="Jamaica">Jamaica</a>. The hurricane turned to the north-northwest and hit the <a href="/wiki/Cuba" title="Cuba">Cuban</a> province of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Havana_Province" title="Havana Province">La Habana</a> with winds of 110 mph (175 km/h) on October 4. The hurricane passed over <a href="/wiki/Havana" title="Havana">Havana</a> and turned to the northeast and strengthened, becoming a major hurricane as it moved south of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Miami,_Florida" title="Miami, Florida">Miami, Florida</a>. The hurricane reached a peak intensity of 125 mph (205 km/h) while passing through the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bahamas" title="Bahamas">Bahamas</a> on October 6. The hurricane weakened as it accelerated to the northeast, and it became extratropical on October 8 to the south of <a href="/wiki/Nova_Scotia" title="Nova Scotia">Nova Scotia</a>. It paralleled the Nova Scotia coast, turned to the east-southeast, and lost its tropical characteristics on October 9 over the open north Atlantic Ocean.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-unisys_13-2"><a href="#cite_note-unisys-13">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>One person was killed in Jamaica due to flooding.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jam1_50-0"><a href="#cite_note-jam1-50">[47]</a></sup> In Cuba, people boarded up numerous buildings, and emergency workers assisted authorities in spreading the word about the impending storm; residents in vulnerable areas evacuated to shelters on higher ground. The hurricane's powerful winds destroyed several houses in <a href="/wiki/Camag%C3%BCey" title="Camagüey">Camagüey</a>, and heavy rainfall overflowed numerous rivers in low-lying districts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-oct41933_51-0"><a href="#cite_note-oct41933-51">[48]</a></sup> The winds damaged and disrupted telephone and telegraph lines and injured a few people in <a href="/wiki/Havana" title="Havana">Havana</a>. Despite government orders for police to kill any looters, large-scale looting occurred in Havana after the storm. Two looters were shot to death, and a third was injured. Two civilians were also wounded by snipers who fired to disperse thieves.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-oct51933_52-0"><a href="#cite_note-oct51933-52">[49]</a></sup> Residents in southeast Florida boarded up for the storm while the National Weather Bureau issued storm warnings for portions of the coastline.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-oct41933_51-1"><a href="#cite_note-oct41933-51">[48]</a></sup> The hurricane produced strong winds and rain in the <a href="/wiki/Florida_Keys" title="Florida Keys">Florida Keys</a> and extreme southern Florida, but damage was minimal.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-oct51933_52-1"><a href="#cite_note-oct51933-52">[49]</a></sup> In northwest Miami, the hurricane spawned a tornado that damaged three houses and injured two.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ncdc_53-0"><a href="#cite_note-ncdc-53">[50]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ap105_54-0"><a href="#cite_note-ap105-54">[51]</a></sup> In Nova Scotia, the former hurricane left heavy damage to crops due to strong winds and heavy rain. Sunken boats killed nine people, and damage was estimated at around $1 million (1933 <a href="/wiki/Canadian_dollar" title="Canadian dollar">CAD</a>), including $250,000 in lost apple crop.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ec_55-0"><a href="#cite_note-ec-55">[52]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59">[nb 4]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Hurricane_Eighteen">Hurricane Eighteen</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=1933_Atlantic_hurricane_season&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Hurricane Eighteen">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>After a two-week period of inactivity, a tropical depression was detected in the western <a href="/wiki/Caribbean_Sea" title="Caribbean Sea">Caribbean Sea</a> on October 25. It moved to the east-northeast then curved to the northwest while slowly intensifying. On October 29, it strengthened into a hurricane near Jamaica and reached peak winds of 90 mph (150 km/h) before striking the western portion of the island. The hurricane turned to the northeast and weakened. It made landfall on southeastern <a href="/wiki/Cuba" title="Cuba">Cuba</a> as a strong tropical storm on October 31. The weakening storm changed its course to the north-northwest, as it drifted through Cuba and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bahamas" title="Bahamas">Bahamas</a>. On November 4, the storm turned once more to the northeast, accelerated, and was absorbed by an approaching cold front on November 7 near <a href="/wiki/Bermuda" title="Bermuda">Bermuda</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-meta_6-16"><a href="#cite_note-meta-6">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>While moving near western Jamaica, the hurricane produced strong winds of about 80 mph (130 km/h),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-meta_6-17"><a href="#cite_note-meta-6">[5]</a></sup> which wrecked about 90% of the banana crop.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ap112_60-0"><a href="#cite_note-ap112-60">[56]</a></sup> One plantation lost 500 banana trees, and there was also damage to corn, coffee, and yams.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dg112_61-0"><a href="#cite_note-dg112-61">[57]</a></sup> Many houses were wrecked or washed away,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ap112_60-1"><a href="#cite_note-ap112-60">[56]</a></sup> leaving hundreds homeless.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dg112_61-1"><a href="#cite_note-dg112-61">[57]</a></sup> The storm cut power and telegraph lines and blocked roads, limiting communication.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ap112_60-2"><a href="#cite_note-ap112-60">[56]</a></sup> Rail lines were cut, and small bridges were washed away.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lp_62-0"><a href="#cite_note-lp-62">[58]</a></sup> Damage was estimated at $3 million,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ap112_60-3"><a href="#cite_note-ap112-60">[56]</a></sup> and there were 23 deaths.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dg112_61-2"><a href="#cite_note-dg112-61">[57]</a></sup> The storm also produced strong winds and rainfall in Cuba.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lp_62-1"><a href="#cite_note-lp-62">[58]</a></sup></p>

<p>Almost simultaneous to Hurricane 19, a tropical storm developed a short distance east of the central <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bahamas" title="Bahamas">Bahamas</a> on October 26. It moved north-northeastward, then northeastward, steadily strengthening along its path. On October 27, a <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_pressure" title="Atmospheric pressure">barometric pressure</a> of 993 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mbar" title="Mbar">mbar</a> (29.32 inHg) was recorded within the storm, and on October 28 the storm reached a peak intensity of 70 mph (110 km/h). On October 29, the storm became <a href="/wiki/Extratropical_cyclone" title="Extratropical cyclone">extratropical</a> and turned north to hit <a href="/wiki/Nova_Scotia" title="Nova Scotia">Nova Scotia</a>. Wedged between two <a href="/wiki/Ridge_(meteorology)" title="Ridge (meteorology)">high pressure systems</a>, it continued northward until dissipating over extreme eastern portions of <a href="/wiki/Quebec" title="Quebec">Quebec</a> on October 30.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-octmwr_63-0"><a href="#cite_note-octmwr-63">[59]</a></sup></p>
<p>In Atlantic Canada, the former tropical storm produced winds of 52 mph (83 km/h) in Nova Scotia, along with heavy rainfall of 2.3 in (58 mm) in <a href="/wiki/Amherst,_Nova_Scotia" title="Amherst, Nova Scotia">Amherst</a>. The storm sank one boat and washed another ashore. Winds were strong enough to knock down telephone and telegraph lines, mainly due to fallen trees which also covered roads and damaged houses. Flooding washed out several bridges and roads, covering one highway with 6 ft (1.8 m) of water, and entered the basements of houses. In New Brunswick, the storm damaged or destroyed 72 bridges. One man was struck by a car due to poor visibility from the storm.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-64"><a href="#cite_note-64">[60]</a></sup></p>

<p>After another calm period, the final tropical storm of the season was first observed on November 15 in the southwestern <a href="/wiki/Caribbean_Sea" title="Caribbean Sea">Caribbean Sea</a>. It moved slowly westward, never strengthening beyond a minimal tropical storm in its short lifetime. On November 16, it struck the southeastern coast of <a href="/wiki/Nicaragua" title="Nicaragua">Nicaragua</a>, and it dissipated soon after on November 17.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-unisys_13-3"><a href="#cite_note-unisys-13">[11]</a></sup></p>
