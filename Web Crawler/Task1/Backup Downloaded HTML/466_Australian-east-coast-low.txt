<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Australian east coast low</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>

<p><b>Australian east coast lows</b> (known locally as <b>east coast lows</b> and sometimes as east coast cyclones<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Leslie_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Leslie-1">[1]</a></sup>) are <a href="/wiki/Extratropical_cyclone" title="Extratropical cyclone">extratropical cyclones</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> The most intense of these systems have many of the characteristics of <a href="/wiki/Subtropical_cyclone" title="Subtropical cyclone">subtropical cyclones</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dowdy11_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Dowdy11-3">[3]</a></sup> They develop between 25˚ south and 40˚ south and within 5˚ of the Australian coastline,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Leslie_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Leslie-1">[1]</a></sup> typically during the winter months.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Holland_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Holland-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lim_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Lim-5">[5]</a></sup> Each year there are about ten "significant impact" maritime lows.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BOMECL_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-BOMECL-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>Prior to the introduction of satellite imagery in the early 1960s, many east coast lows were classified as tropical cyclones. These storms which mostly affect the south east coast should not be confused with <a href="/wiki/Australian_region_tropical_cyclone" title="Australian region tropical cyclone">Australian region tropical cyclones</a> which typically affect the northern half of the continent.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Harper_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Harper-7">[7]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Characterisation">Characterisation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Australian_east_coast_low&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Characterisation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Explosive_cyclogenesis" title="Explosive cyclogenesis">Explosive cyclogenesis</a> is seen on average just once per year, but these storms cause significant wind and flood damage when they occur.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Holland_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-Holland-4">[4]</a></sup> Australian east coast cyclones vary in size from <a href="/wiki/Mesoscale_meteorology" title="Mesoscale meteorology">mesoscale</a> (approximately 10–100 km (6–62 mi)) to <a href="/wiki/Synoptic_scale_meteorology" title="Synoptic scale meteorology">synoptic scale</a> (approximately 100–1,000 km (62–621 mi)).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GuyCarp_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-GuyCarp-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hopkins_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hopkins-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>Australian east coast cyclones, although variable in size and intensity, are typically characterised by widespread heavy rainfall.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hopkins_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-Hopkins-9">[9]</a></sup> These storms usually occur along the subtropical east coast of Australia from <a href="/wiki/Gladstone,_Queensland" title="Gladstone, Queensland">Gladstone</a> in Queensland to the Victoria/New South Wales border, and often affect large populated cities such as <a href="/wiki/Sydney" title="Sydney">Sydney</a> and <a href="/wiki/Brisbane" title="Brisbane">Brisbane</a>, between which over one-third of the Australian population resides.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hopkins_9-2"><a href="#cite_note-Hopkins-9">[9]</a></sup> Rain associated damages attributed to east coast cyclones are estimated in millions to tens of million dollars annually and are a major contributor to the total weather-associated insurance losses for all of Australia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hopkins_9-3"><a href="#cite_note-Hopkins-9">[9]</a></sup> Seven per cent of all major Australian disasters since 1967 can be directly attributed to east coast cyclones.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hopkins_9-4"><a href="#cite_note-Hopkins-9">[9]</a></sup> Australian east coast lows often intensify rapidly overnight making them one of the more dangerous weather systems to affect the New South Wales coast.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BOMECL_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-BOMECL-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Pattern">Pattern</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Australian_east_coast_low&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Pattern">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The incidence of these types of storms can be seen to fluctuate quite widely from one year to the next, with none in some years and the highest incidence being twelve in 1978/79.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Harper_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-Harper-7">[7]</a></sup> Another feature of east coast low development is the tendency for clustering of events when conditions remain favourable. For example, near Brisbane, almost one third of events occur within 20 days of a preceding event.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Harper_7-2"><a href="#cite_note-Harper-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>Correlations of east coast cyclones with the interannual differences of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/El_Ni%C3%B1o_Southern_Oscillation" title="El Niño Southern Oscillation">Southern Oscillation Index</a> (SOI) indicate a strong preference for these storms to form just after a large swing from negative to positive Southern Oscillation index values and especially between swings from negative SOI the year before and positive SOI the year after. This suggests a preference for formation of east coast cyclones between extreme events of the Southern Oscillation Index.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hopkins_9-5"><a href="#cite_note-Hopkins-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Examples">Examples</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Australian_east_coast_low&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Examples">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul>
<li>5–6 June 2016<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> An east coast low combined with a <a href="/wiki/King_tide" title="King tide">King tide</a> brought severe coastal damage, notably to <a href="/wiki/Collaroy,_New_South_Wales" title="Collaroy, New South Wales">Collaroy</a> and <a href="/wiki/Coogee,_New_South_Wales" title="Coogee, New South Wales">Coogee, New South Wales</a> along with flooding along the east coast and later severe flooding to <a href="/wiki/Tasmania" title="Tasmania">Tasmania</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></li>
</ul>

Heavy rain and strong to gale force winds with extreme gusts of 64 kn (119 km/h; 74 mph) at <a href="/wiki/Wollongong" title="Wollongong">Wollongong</a> and 53 kn (98 km/h; 61 mph) near Sydney Airport.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Leslie_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-Leslie-1">[1]</a></sup></li>
<li>September 1995, A$8 million damage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Leslie_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-Leslie-1">[1]</a></sup></li>
<li>August 1990, two lows in early August cause A$12 million damage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Leslie_1-4"><a href="#cite_note-Leslie-1">[1]</a></sup></li>
<li>5 August 1986, 24-hour rainfall totals: over 300mm in the Sydney area.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BOMhist_29-3"><a href="#cite_note-BOMhist-29">[29]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Sydney_Observatory" title="Sydney Observatory">Sydney's Observatory</a> Hill recorded 327.91 mm (12.91 in) of rain, an all-time daily record for the location.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Erdman_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-Erdman-30">[30]</a></sup></li>
<li>17–23 July 1984, A$53m insurance costs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GuyCarp_8-2"><a href="#cite_note-GuyCarp-8">[8]</a></sup></li>
<li>26 May 1974, storm saw the shipwreck of the <a href="/wiki/MV_Sygna" title="MV Sygna">MV <i>Sygna</i></a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Holland_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-Holland-4">[4]</a></sup></li>
<li>6 July 1973, the cargo ship <i><a href="/wiki/Cherry_Venture" title="Cherry Venture">Cherry Venture</a></i> ran aground on <a href="/wiki/Teewah_Beach" title="Teewah Beach">Teewah Beach</a> in <a href="/wiki/South_East_Queensland" title="South East Queensland">South East Queensland</a> during the storm.</li>
<li>June 1967, a series of east coast cyclones had a major impact on the northern New South Wales and southern Queensland coasts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GuyCarp_8-3"><a href="#cite_note-GuyCarp-8">[8]</a></sup></li>
<li>June 1950, a series of east coast cyclones develop off the New South Wales coast during which Sydney registered its highest monthly rainfall on record, 642.7 mm (25.3 in).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GuyCarp_8-4"><a href="#cite_note-GuyCarp-8">[8]</a></sup></li>
<li>20 August 1857, The <i><a href="/wiki/Dunbar_(ship)" title="Dunbar (ship)">Dunbar</a></i>, a sailing ship carrying 122 people from England, was wrecked off <a href="/wiki/Sydney_Heads" title="Sydney Heads">South Head</a> while trying to seek shelter in <a href="/wiki/Port_Jackson" title="Port Jackson">Sydney Harbour</a>, leaving only one survivor.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BOMhist_29-4"><a href="#cite_note-BOMhist-29">[29]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
