<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Condensation</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

<p><b>Condensation</b> is the change of the physical <a href="/wiki/State_of_matter" title="State of matter">state of matter</a> from <a href="/wiki/Gas" title="Gas">gas phase</a> into <a href="/wiki/Liquid" title="Liquid">liquid phase</a>, and is the reverse of <a href="/wiki/Evaporation" title="Evaporation">evaporation</a>. The word most often refers to the <a href="/wiki/Water_cycle" title="Water cycle">water cycle</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gold_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Gold-1">[1]</a></sup> It can also be defined as the change in the state of water vapour to liquid water when in contact with a liquid or solid surface or <a href="/wiki/Cloud_condensation_nuclei" title="Cloud condensation nuclei">cloud condensation nuclei</a> within the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Earth%27s_atmosphere" title="Earth's atmosphere">atmosphere</a>. When the transition happens from the gaseous phase into the solid phase directly, the change is called <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Deposition_(physics)" title="Deposition (physics)">deposition</a> (or desublimation, see <a href="/wiki/Sublimation_(phase_transition)" title="Sublimation (phase transition)">Sublimation (phase transition)</a>.</p>
<p></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Initiation">Initiation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Condensation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Initiation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Condensation is initiated by the formation of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atomic_clusters" title="Atomic clusters">atomic/molecular clusters</a> of that species within its gaseous volume—like <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rain_drop" title="Rain drop">rain drop</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Snow_flake" title="Snow flake">snow flake</a> formation within <a href="/wiki/Cloud" title="Cloud">clouds</a>—or at the contact between such gaseous phase and a liquid or solid surface.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reversibility_scenarios">Reversibility scenarios</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Condensation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Reversibility scenarios">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A few distinct reversibility scenarios emerge here with respect to the nature of the surface.</p>
<ul>
<li>absorption into the surface of a liquid (either of the same substance or one of its solvents)—is reversible as <a href="/wiki/Evaporation" title="Evaporation">evaporation</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gold_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Gold-1">[1]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Adsorption" title="Adsorption">adsorption</a> (as dew droplets) onto solid surface at pressures and temperatures higher than the species' <a href="/wiki/Triple_point" title="Triple point">triple point</a>—also reversible as evaporation.</li>
<li>adsorption onto solid surface (as supplemental layers of solid) at pressures and temperatures lower than the species' <a href="/wiki/Triple_point" title="Triple point">triple point</a>—is reversible as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sublimation_(Physics)" title="Sublimation (Physics)">sublimation</a>.</li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Most_common_scenarios">Most common scenarios</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Condensation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Most common scenarios">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Condensation commonly occurs when a <a href="/wiki/Vapor" title="Vapor">vapor</a> is cooled and/or compressed to its <a href="/wiki/Dew_point" title="Dew point">saturation limit</a> when the molecular density in the gas phase reaches its maximal threshold. Vapor cooling and compressing equipment that collects condensed liquids is called a <a href="/wiki/Condenser_(heat_transfer)" title="Condenser (heat transfer)">"condenser"</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="How_condensation_is_measured">How condensation is measured</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Condensation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: How condensation is measured">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Psychrometry" title="Psychrometry">Psychrometry</a> measures the rates of condensation through evaporation into the air moisture at various atmospheric pressures and temperatures. Water is the product of its vapor condensation—condensation is the process of such phase conversion.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Applications_of_condensation">Applications of condensation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Condensation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Applications of condensation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Condensation is a crucial component of <a href="/wiki/Distillation" title="Distillation">distillation</a>, an important laboratory and industrial chemistry application.</p>
<p>Because condensation is a naturally occurring phenomenon, it can often be used to generate water in large quantities for human use. Many structures are made solely for the purpose of collecting water from condensation, such as <a href="/wiki/Air_well_(condenser)" title="Air well (condenser)">air wells</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fog_fence" title="Fog fence">fog fences</a>. Such systems can often be used to retain soil moisture in areas where active <a href="/wiki/Desertification" title="Desertification">desertification</a> is occurring—so much so that some organizations educate people living in affected areas about water condensers to help them deal effectively with the situation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>It is also a crucial process in forming particle tracks in a <a href="/wiki/Cloud_chamber" title="Cloud chamber">cloud chamber</a>. In this case, ions produced by an incident particle act as nucleation centers for the condensation of the vapor producing the visible "cloud" trails.</p>
<p>Furthermore, condensation is a critical step in many industrial processes, such as power generation, water desalination, thermal management, refrigeration, and air conditioning.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Enright_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Enright-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Biological_adaptation">Biological adaptation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Condensation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Biological adaptation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Numerous living beings use water made accessible by condensation. A few examples of these are the <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australian</a> <a href="/wiki/Thorny_devil" title="Thorny devil">thorny devil</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Darkling_beetle" title="Darkling beetle">darkling beetles</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Namibia" title="Namibia">Namibian</a> coast, and the <a href="/wiki/Sequoia_sempervirens" title="Sequoia sempervirens">coast redwoods</a> of the <a href="/wiki/West_Coast_of_the_United_States" title="West Coast of the United States">West Coast of the United States</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Condensation_in_building_construction">Condensation in building construction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Condensation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Condensation in building construction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Window_in_Ireland.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1728" data-file-width="2288" height="166" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8c/Window_in_Ireland.jpg/220px-Window_in_Ireland.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8c/Window_in_Ireland.jpg/330px-Window_in_Ireland.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8c/Window_in_Ireland.jpg/440px-Window_in_Ireland.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Window_in_Ireland.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Condensation on a window during a rain shower.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Condensation in building <a href="/wiki/Construction" title="Construction">construction</a> is an unwanted <a href="/wiki/Phenomenon" title="Phenomenon">phenomenon</a> as it may cause <a href="/wiki/Damp_(structural)" title="Damp (structural)">dampness</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mold_health_issues" title="Mold health issues">mold health issues</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wood" title="Wood">wood</a> <a href="/wiki/Dry_rot" title="Dry rot">rot</a>, <a href="/wiki/Corrosion" title="Corrosion">corrosion</a>, weakening of mortar and masonry walls, and energy penalties due to increased <a href="/wiki/Heat_transfer" title="Heat transfer">heat transfer</a>. To alleviate these issues, the indoor air humidity needs to be lowered, or air ventilation in the building needs to be improved. This can be done in a number of ways, for example opening windows, turning on extractor fans, using dehumidifiers, drying clothes outside and covering pots and pans whilst cooking. Air conditioning or ventilation systems can be installed that help remove moisture from the air, and move air throughout a building.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PropertyHive_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-PropertyHive-4">[4]</a></sup> The amount of water vapour that can be stored in the air can be increased simply by increasing the temperature.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PropertyHive_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-PropertyHive-4">[4]</a></sup> However, this can be a double edged sword as most condensation in the home occurs when warm, moisture heavy air comes into contact with a cool surface. As the air is cooled, it can no longer hold as much water vapour. This leads to deposition of water on the cool surface. This is very apparent when central heating is used in combination with single glazed windows in winter.</p>
<p>Interstructure condensation may be caused by <a href="/wiki/Thermal_bridge" title="Thermal bridge">thermal bridges</a>, insufficient or lacking insulation, <a href="/wiki/Damp_proofing" title="Damp proofing">damp proofing</a> or <a href="/wiki/Insulated_glazing" title="Insulated glazing">insulated glazing</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
