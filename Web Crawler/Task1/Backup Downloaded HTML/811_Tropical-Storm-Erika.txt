<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Tropical Storm Erika</h1>

<p><b>Tropical Storm Erika</b> of August 2015 was the deadliest <a href="/wiki/Natural_disaster" title="Natural disaster">natural disaster</a> in <a href="/wiki/Dominica" title="Dominica">Dominica</a> since <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_David" title="Hurricane David">Hurricane David</a> in <a href="/wiki/1979_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="1979 Atlantic hurricane season">1979</a>. The fifth <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone_naming" title="Tropical cyclone naming">named storm</a> of the <a href="/wiki/2015_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="2015 Atlantic hurricane season">season</a>, Erika developed from a westward-moving <a href="/wiki/Tropical_wave" title="Tropical wave">tropical wave</a> while well east of the <a href="/wiki/Lesser_Antilles" title="Lesser Antilles">Lesser Antilles</a>. Hostile conditions, including dry air and <a href="/wiki/Wind_shear" title="Wind shear">wind shear</a>, inhibited significant development, and Erika failed to acquire <a href="/wiki/Maximum_sustained_wind" title="Maximum sustained wind">sustained winds</a> beyond 50 mph (85 km/h). Contrary to predictions of a northwesterly recurvature, the cyclone persisted on a westerly course and passed through the <a href="/wiki/Leeward_Islands" title="Leeward Islands">Leeward Islands</a> and emerged over the <a href="/wiki/Caribbean_Sea" title="Caribbean Sea">Caribbean Sea</a> on August 27. Erika succumbed to adverse conditions the following day, dissipating as a tropical cyclone near the <a href="/wiki/Dominican_Republic" title="Dominican Republic">Dominican Republic</a>. The remnant system persisted for several more days, ultimately moving across the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_of_Mexico" title="Gulf of Mexico">Gulf of Mexico</a> before dissipating over <a href="/wiki/Georgia_(U.S._state)" title="Georgia (U.S. state)">Georgia</a> on September 2.</p>
<p>Several islands experienced heavy rainfall during the passage of Erika, especially <a href="/wiki/Dominica" title="Dominica">Dominica</a>. The storm's asymmetric structure, coupled with the mountainous terrain of the island and ample <a href="/wiki/Precipitable_water" title="Precipitable water">moisture aloft</a>, led to rainfall accumulations up to 33 in (850 mm). With grounds already saturated from antecedent rains, tremendous <a href="/wiki/Surface_runoff" title="Surface runoff">runoff</a> quickly overwhelmed river basins and triggered catastrophic floods. Accompanying mudslides worsened the situation, temporarily damming the rivers before collapsing. Hundreds of homes were left uninhabitable and thousands of people were displaced; the entire town of <a href="/wiki/Petite_Savanne" title="Petite Savanne">Petite Savanne</a> was evacuated and subsequently abandoned as a result of the storm. All told, 30 people died across the island in the nation's worst disaster since Hurricane David. The storm's devastating effects in Dominica prompted an influx of international assistance. Aid from multiple nations and intergovernmental organizations poured in to assist victims of the storm. Thousands of homes needed to be built or repaired, including 500–1,000 for the relocation of all of Petite Savanne's residents.</p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Guadeloupe" title="Guadeloupe">Guadeloupe</a>, heavy rainfall in the vicinity of <a href="/wiki/Basse-Terre" title="Basse-Terre">Basse-Terre</a> caused flooding and mudslides, forcing roads to temporarily close. Approximately 250,000 people in <a href="/wiki/Puerto_Rico" title="Puerto Rico">Puerto Rico</a> were left without electricity. The island experienced US$17.37 million<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[nb 1]</a></sup> in agricultural damage. In the <a href="/wiki/Dominican_Republic" title="Dominican Republic">Dominican Republic</a>, a weather station in Barahona measured 24.26 in (616 mm) of rain, including 8.8 in (220 mm) in a single hour. About 823 homes suffered damage and 7,345 people were displaced. Five people died in <a href="/wiki/Haiti" title="Haiti">Haiti</a>, four from a weather-related traffic accident and one from a landslide. Total damage from Erika is estimated to be over US$500 million, with US$482.8 million in Dominica alone. Owing to the disastrous effects on Dominica, the name <i>Erika</i> was <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone_naming#Retirement" title="Tropical cyclone naming">retired</a> in the spring of 2016, and will never again be used for an Atlantic tropical cyclone. It will be replaced with <i>Elsa</i> for the 2021 Atlantic hurricane season.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[1]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Meteorological_history">Meteorological history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tropical_Storm_Erika&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Meteorological history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Erika_2015_track.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1854" data-file-width="3000" height="136" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2b/Erika_2015_track.png/220px-Erika_2015_track.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2b/Erika_2015_track.png/330px-Erika_2015_track.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2b/Erika_2015_track.png/440px-Erika_2015_track.png 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Erika_2015_track.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Map plotting the track and intensity of the storm, according to the <a href="/wiki/Saffir%E2%80%93Simpson_scale" title="Saffir–Simpson scale">Saffir–Simpson scale</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>On August 20, 2015, the <a href="/wiki/National_Hurricane_Center" title="National Hurricane Center">National Hurricane Center</a> (NHC) began monitoring a <a href="/wiki/Tropical_wave" title="Tropical wave">tropical wave</a> over western Africa, near the Atlantic shore.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[2]</a></sup> The disorganized system emerged over the Atlantic Ocean several hundred miles southeast of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cape_Verde_Islands" title="Cape Verde Islands">Cape Verde Islands</a> the following day.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TCR_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-TCR-4">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[4]</a></sup> Moving quickly west, the system bypassed the Cape Verde Islands to the south on August 22,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[5]</a></sup> but remained largely disorganized. A sharp <a href="/wiki/Trough_(meteorology)" title="Trough (meteorology)">trough</a> developed within expanding deep <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_convection" title="Atmospheric convection">convection</a> on August 23, though a well-defined circulation did not consolidate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TCR_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-TCR-4">[3]</a></sup> Organization of the low improved throughout the day as environmental conditions favored <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclogenesis" title="Tropical cyclogenesis">tropical cyclogenesis</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[6]</a></sup> The trough acquired <a href="/wiki/Gale" title="Gale">gale</a>-force winds the following day and finally developed a closed circulation by 18:00 <a href="/wiki/Coordinated_Universal_Time" title="Coordinated Universal Time">UTC</a>. The formation of a closed low marked the transition into a <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone" title="Tropical cyclone">tropical cyclone</a>; accordingly, the system was classified as Tropical Storm Erika, the fifth <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone_naming" title="Tropical cyclone naming">named storm</a> of the <a href="/wiki/2015_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="2015 Atlantic hurricane season">annual hurricane season</a>. Upon its designation, Erika was situated roughly 1,035 mi (1,665 km) east of the <a href="/wiki/Lesser_Antilles" title="Lesser Antilles">Lesser Antilles</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TCR_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-TCR-4">[3]</a></sup> The storm maintained a brisk westward trajectory, steered by a <a href="/wiki/Subtropical_ridge" title="Subtropical ridge">subtropical ridge</a> to the north.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TCR_4-3"><a href="#cite_note-TCR-4">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-D1_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-D1-8">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>At the time of Erika's classification, <a href="/wiki/Numerical_weather_prediction" title="Numerical weather prediction">forecast models</a> diverged significantly on the potential future of Erika. Statistical guidance and the <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Weather_Research_and_Forecasting_model" title="Hurricane Weather Research and Forecasting model">HWRF</a> depicted a <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone_scales#Atlantic_and_East_Pacific" title="Tropical cyclone scales">hurricane</a> while the <a href="/wiki/European_Centre_for_Medium-Range_Weather_Forecasts" title="European Centre for Medium-Range Weather Forecasts">ECMWF</a> and <a href="/wiki/Global_Forecast_System" title="Global Forecast System">GFS</a> showed a weaker system due to increasing <a href="/wiki/Wind_shear" title="Wind shear">wind shear</a>. The models that intensified the storm indicated a more northerly track while those that kept it weaker had the system continuing along a westward course. Accordingly, the NHC noted low confidence in their five-day forecast for Erika.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-D1_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-D1-8">[7]</a></sup> This uncertainty ultimately reflected in above-average forecast errors when compared to all tropical cyclones, but roughly average for weak, poorly organized systems.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TrackReview_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-TrackReview-9">[8]</a></sup> Throughout August 25 and into early August 26, the <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_convection" title="Atmospheric convection">convective</a> organization of Erika fluctuated due to <a href="/wiki/Wind_shear" title="Wind shear">wind shear</a> and entrainment of dry air, periodically leaving the center of circulation devoid of convection.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[9]</a></sup> Early on August 27, Erika managed to intensify slightly, attaining its peak intensity with <a href="/wiki/Maximum_sustained_wind" title="Maximum sustained wind">maximum sustained winds</a> of 50 mph (85 km/h) and a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Barometric_pressure" title="Barometric pressure">barometric pressure</a> of 1001 <a href="/wiki/Bar_(unit)" title="Bar (unit)">mbar</a> (<a href="/wiki/Pascal_(unit)" title="Pascal (unit)">hPa</a>; 29.56 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Inches_of_Mercury" title="Inches of Mercury">inHg</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TCR_4-4"><a href="#cite_note-TCR-4">[3]</a></sup> Around 09:00 UTC, the disorganized center of Erika passed over the northern tip of <a href="/wiki/Guadeloupe" title="Guadeloupe">Guadeloupe</a>, before emerging over the eastern <a href="/wiki/Caribbean_Sea" title="Caribbean Sea">Caribbean Sea</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TCR_4-5"><a href="#cite_note-TCR-4">[3]</a></sup></p>

<p>Convection remained poorly organized and mostly confined to eastern portions of the cyclone as it traversed the eastern Caribbean Sea.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-D10_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-D10-11">[10]</a></sup> Multiple circulation centers were noted during the overnight of August 27–28, all rotating around a broad general center. One such center moved over <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/St._Croix" title="St. Croix">St. Croix</a> and produced gale-force gusts. Environmental conditions ahead of the storm became increasingly hostile, and forecasters at the NHC continued to note unusually high uncertainty in their forecasts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[11]</a></sup> Further structural degradation ensued on August 28 as Erika approached the <a href="/wiki/Dominican_Republic" title="Dominican Republic">Dominican Republic</a>. Based on data from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hurricane_Hunters" title="Hurricane Hunters">Hurricane Hunters</a>, Erika degenerated into a broad area of low pressure—no longer meeting the definition of a tropical cyclone—shortly after 12:00 UTC near the southeastern coast of the Dominican Republic.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TCR_4-6"><a href="#cite_note-TCR-4">[3]</a></sup> Operationally, the NHC maintained advisories on Erika until 13:30 UTC on August 29.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[12]</a></sup> It was also noted during those advisories that the center was completely devoid of convection, and whatever convection remained was reduced to the eastern half of the system.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-D18_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-D18-14">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>Around 21:00 UTC on August 28, the remnants of Erika made <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Landfall_(meteorology)" title="Landfall (meteorology)">landfall</a> along the southeastern coast of the Dominican Republic.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[14]</a></sup> The disheveled system emerged over the <a href="/wiki/Windward_Passage" title="Windward Passage">Windward Passage</a> early on August 29.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-D18_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-D18-14">[13]</a></sup> Thereafter, the system skirted the northern coast of <a href="/wiki/Cuba" title="Cuba">Cuba</a> and entered the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_of_Mexico" title="Gulf of Mexico">Gulf of Mexico</a> on August 31. Turning northward, the disturbance eventually crossed northern <a href="/wiki/Florida" title="Florida">Florida</a> on September 2 before dissipating over <a href="/wiki/Georgia_(U.S._state)" title="Georgia (U.S. state)">Georgia</a> the following day.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TCR_4-7"><a href="#cite_note-TCR-4">[3]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Caribbean">Caribbean</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tropical_Storm_Erika&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Caribbean">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Various local governments issued <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone_warnings_and_watches" title="Tropical cyclone warnings and watches">tropical storm watches and warnings</a> for their respective nations throughout the duration of Erika. Watches and warnings covered the Lesser Antilles from <a href="/wiki/Guadeloupe" title="Guadeloupe">Guadeloupe</a> northward, almost the entirety of the <a href="/wiki/Greater_Antilles" title="Greater Antilles">Greater Antilles</a>, and <a href="/wiki/The_Bahamas" title="The Bahamas">the Bahamas</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TCR_4-8"><a href="#cite_note-TCR-4">[3]</a></sup> The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Red_Cross" title="Red Cross">Red Cross</a> opened four shelters to the public across the United States Virgin Islands.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[15]</a></sup> The local Health Department also opened special needs shelters on St. Croix and St. Thomas. All non-essential government personnel were given administrative leave on August 27. Owing to rough seas, local ports temporarily closed. Although airports remained open, several airlines cancelled flights.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[16]</a></sup> A territory-wide curfew was imposed until the afternoon of August 28.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Newsday1_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-Newsday1-18">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>Schools, ports, and beaches were closed across the Dominican Republic.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AFP830_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-AFP830-19">[18]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Dominican_Army" title="Dominican Army">Army of the Dominican Republic</a> deployed 275 soldiers and alongside the nation's <a href="/wiki/Dominican_Air_Force" title="Dominican Air Force">Air Force</a>, assisted with the evacuation of residents. The <a href="/wiki/Dominican_Navy" title="Dominican Navy">Navy</a> was placed on standby for potential search and rescue operations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[19]</a></sup> Officials in Haiti suspended air service, banned highway travel between departments, and ordered small watercraft to remain at port.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RW_1_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-RW_1-21">[20]</a></sup> Evacuation shelters were opened across all departments and 254 prisoners were relocated from <a href="/wiki/Gona%C3%AFves" title="Gonaïves">Gonaïves</a> to three other facilities.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[21]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Florida">Florida</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tropical_Storm_Erika&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Florida">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On August 28, <a href="/wiki/List_of_Governors_of_Florida" title="List of Governors of Florida">Florida Governor</a> <a href="/wiki/Rick_Scott" title="Rick Scott">Rick Scott</a> declared a <a href="/wiki/State_of_emergency" title="State of emergency">state of emergency</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[22]</a></sup> Despite the storm dissipating shortly thereafter, Scott kept the emergency declaration in place and stated, "This storm has been completely unpredictable the whole time...we will still get a lot of rain and a lot of flooding."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TWCemergency_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-TWCemergency-24">[23]</a></sup> Thirty members of the <a href="/wiki/Florida_National_Guard" title="Florida National Guard">Florida National Guard</a> were positioned while another 8,000 were placed on standby.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TWCemergency_24-1"><a href="#cite_note-TWCemergency-24">[23]</a></sup> A collective 17 flights were cancelled at <a href="/wiki/Miami_International_Airport" title="Miami International Airport">Miami International Airport</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fort_Lauderdale%E2%80%93Hollywood_International_Airport" title="Fort Lauderdale–Hollywood International Airport">Fort Lauderdale–Hollywood International Airport</a> on August 28.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[24]</a></sup> Schools in <a href="/wiki/Hendry_County,_Florida" title="Hendry County, Florida">Hendry County</a> preemptively closed for August 31,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TWCemergency_24-2"><a href="#cite_note-TWCemergency-24">[23]</a></sup> with officials citing flooding concerns.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[25]</a></sup> Sandbags were distributed to residents in <a href="/wiki/Doral,_Florida" title="Doral, Florida">Doral</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hallandale_Beach,_Florida" title="Hallandale Beach, Florida">Hallandale Beach</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hollywood,_Florida" title="Hollywood, Florida">Hollywood</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Sweetwater,_Miami-Dade_County,_Florida" title="Sweetwater, Miami-Dade County, Florida">Sweetwater</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[26]</a></sup> The last hurricane to impact the state was <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Wilma" title="Hurricane Wilma">Hurricane Wilma</a> in 2005. Since that time, the state's population grew by 2 million with many presumed to have never experienced a hurricane before.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TWCemergency_24-3"><a href="#cite_note-TWCemergency-24">[23]</a></sup> Some newer residents remained apathetic over the storm, dismissing it as "another excuse to skip work or school or to party."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-USAT828_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-USAT828-28">[27]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Caribbean_2">Caribbean</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tropical_Storm_Erika&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Caribbean">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Guadeloupe" title="Guadeloupe">Guadeloupe</a> where Erika made landfall, rains reached 100 mm (3.9 in) in <a href="/wiki/Basse-Terre" title="Basse-Terre">Basse-Terre</a> while gusts peaked at 100 km/h (62 mph) on <a href="/wiki/La_D%C3%A9sirade" title="La Désirade">La Désirade</a>. Some landslides and flooding were reported, with roads in Basse-Terre temporarily closed. <a href="/wiki/%C3%89lectricit%C3%A9_de_France" title="Électricité de France">Électricité de France</a> reported that 1,600 customers lost power during the storm.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[34]</a></sup> Rough seas in <a href="/wiki/Martinique" title="Martinique">Martinique</a> grounded boats.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[35]</a></sup> Landslides and felled trees blocked several roadways across the island.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[36]</a></sup> Elsewhere in the Lesser Antilles, effects from Erika were negligible.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CDEMA_3_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-CDEMA_3-38">[37]</a></sup></p>
<p>The outer bands of Erika brought much needed rain <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/2015_Puerto_Rican_drought" title="2015 Puerto Rican drought">drought-stricken Puerto Rico</a>;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Newsday1_18-1"><a href="#cite_note-Newsday1-18">[17]</a></sup> a station in <a href="/wiki/Adjuntas,_Puerto_Rico" title="Adjuntas, Puerto Rico">Adjuntas</a> recorded 4.45 in (113 mm) of rain.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TCR_4-9"><a href="#cite_note-TCR-4">[3]</a></sup> Tropical storm-force wind gusts, peaking at 59 mph (95 km/h) in <a href="/wiki/Maricao,_Puerto_Rico" title="Maricao, Puerto Rico">Maricao</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TCR_4-10"><a href="#cite_note-TCR-4">[3]</a></sup> caused substantial disruption to the power grid, leaving approximately 250,000 people without electricity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[38]</a></sup> Thirty-six homes sustained roof damage across interior locations of the territory and agriculture sustained US$17.37 million in damage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PRicoNCDC_33-1"><a href="#cite_note-PRicoNCDC-33">[32]</a></sup></p>

<p>A weather station in <a href="/wiki/Barahona,_Dominican_Republic" title="Barahona, Dominican Republic">Barahona, Dominican Republic</a>, measured 24.26 in (616 mm) of rain during Erika's passage, including 8.8 in (220 mm) in a single hour. Surrounding areas reported significantly less rain, however.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[39]</a></sup> Throughout the country, 823 homes suffered damage and 7,345 people were displaced. Erika blocked over 400 roads and left many power outages.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[40]</a></sup> Winds gusts of 50 mph (85 km/h) in <a href="/wiki/Azua_Province" title="Azua Province">Azua</a> caused at least 400 million <a href="/wiki/Dominican_peso" title="Dominican peso">DOP</a> (US$8.88 million) in damage to the banana crop.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DR_1_31-1"><a href="#cite_note-DR_1-31">[30]</a></sup> Heavy rains also impacted <a href="/wiki/Haiti" title="Haiti">Haiti</a>, which was still recovering from a <a href="/wiki/2010_Haiti_earthquake" title="2010 Haiti earthquake">catastrophic earthquake in 2010</a>; an estimated 60,000 people remained in emergency housing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AFP830_19-2"><a href="#cite_note-AFP830-19">[18]</a></sup> Four persons died and eleven others were hospitalized in <a href="/wiki/L%C3%A9og%C3%A2ne" title="Léogâne">Léogâne</a>, when a truck crashed into a bus on rain-slicked roads and exploded.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HA_1_32-1"><a href="#cite_note-HA_1-32">[31]</a></sup> A fifth death occurred during a landslide in <a href="/wiki/Port-au-Prince" title="Port-au-Prince">Port-au-Prince</a>. Two people in the area were injured after a house collapsed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AFP830_19-3"><a href="#cite_note-AFP830-19">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>The remnants of Erika later brought much needed rains to <a href="/wiki/Cuba" title="Cuba">Cuba</a>, which was suffering from its worst drought since 1901.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AFP830_19-4"><a href="#cite_note-AFP830-19">[18]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="United_States">United States</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tropical_Storm_Erika&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: United States">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Later, Erika's remnants dropped locally heavy rainfall across Florida, particularly in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Miami_Metropolitan_Area" title="Miami Metropolitan Area">Miami Metropolitan Area</a>. A station northwest of <a href="/wiki/Sweetwater,_Miami-Dade_County,_Florida" title="Sweetwater, Miami-Dade County, Florida">Sweetwater</a> observed 9 in (230 mm) of rain.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WPC_42-0"><a href="#cite_note-WPC-42">[41]</a></sup> Minor flash flooding left streets in <a href="/wiki/Wynwood" title="Wynwood">Wynwood</a> impassable.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[42]</a></sup> Farther north in <a href="/wiki/Flagler_County,_Florida" title="Flagler County, Florida">Flagler County</a>, scattered thunderstorms produced isolated instances of heavy rain; 2.2 in (56 mm) fell within one hour in <a href="/wiki/Palm_Coast,_Florida" title="Palm Coast, Florida">Palm Coast</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[43]</a></sup> In the greater <a href="/wiki/Jacksonville,_Florida" title="Jacksonville, Florida">Jacksonville</a> area, lightning struck the Sally B. Mathis Elementary School, resulting in damage to the air conditioning unit and evacuation of students.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[44]</a></sup> In neighboring <a href="/wiki/Georgia_(U.S._state)" title="Georgia (U.S. state)">Georgia</a>, moisture from Erika's remnants fueled scattered severe thunderstorms. Large hail caused US$2.3 million in damage near <a href="/wiki/Woodstock,_Georgia" title="Woodstock, Georgia">Woodstock</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GeorgiaHail_34-1"><a href="#cite_note-GeorgiaHail-34">[33]</a></sup> Strong winds gusting up to 60 mph (95 km/h) accompanied these storms, downing trees and power lines in <a href="/wiki/Cherokee_County,_Georgia" title="Cherokee County, Georgia">Cherokee</a>, <a href="/wiki/Polk_County,_Georgia" title="Polk County, Georgia">Polk</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Troup_County,_Georgia" title="Troup County, Georgia">Troup</a> counties.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[45]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[46]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[47]</a></sup> Additional rainfall extended north through <a href="/wiki/The_Carolinas" title="The Carolinas">the Carolinas</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WPC_42-1"><a href="#cite_note-WPC-42">[41]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Effects_in_Dominica">Effects in Dominica</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tropical_Storm_Erika&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Effects in Dominica">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:282px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Petite_Savanne,_Dominica,_after_Tropical_Storm_Erika.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="300" data-file-width="449" height="187" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/5/5f/Petite_Savanne%2C_Dominica%2C_after_Tropical_Storm_Erika.jpg/280px-Petite_Savanne%2C_Dominica%2C_after_Tropical_Storm_Erika.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/5/5f/Petite_Savanne%2C_Dominica%2C_after_Tropical_Storm_Erika.jpg/420px-Petite_Savanne%2C_Dominica%2C_after_Tropical_Storm_Erika.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/5/5f/Petite_Savanne%2C_Dominica%2C_after_Tropical_Storm_Erika.jpg 2x" width="280"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Petite_Savanne,_Dominica,_after_Tropical_Storm_Erika.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The rugged terrain of Dominica makes it prone to landslides in heavy rain events. The town of Petite Savanne <i>(pictured)</i>, situated on steep cliffs, was rendered uninhabitable after numerous landslides devastated the area.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Although Erika passed to the north of Dominica as a weak tropical storm, torrential rains associated with the storm impacted the country.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TCR_4-11"><a href="#cite_note-TCR-4">[3]</a></sup> Unusually high <a href="/wiki/Precipitable_water" title="Precipitable water">precipitable water</a> values of 2.6 in (67 mm) were present over the island as Erika moved through. Orographic influence exacerbated rainfall rates, and this was further compounded with the formation of a temporary <a href="/wiki/Mesoscale_meteorology" title="Mesoscale meteorology">mesoscale low</a> over the island as the primary circulation of Erika continued west. More than 8 in (200 mm) of rain fell across the entire island in less than 48 hours, with an island-wide average of 24 in (610 mm).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[nb 2]</a></sup> During the period of heaviest rain, average rainfall across the entire island exceeded 3.5 in (90 mm) per hour. Rates along the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Macoucherie_River&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Macoucherie River (page does not exist)">Macoucherie River</a> reached 11 in (270 mm) per hour. The heaviest rain was concentrated around the nation's tallest mountain, <a href="/wiki/Morne_Diablotins" title="Morne Diablotins">Morne Diablotins</a>, where an estimated 31 to 33 in (800 to 850 mm) fell. Rainfall across <a href="/wiki/Morne_Diablotin_National_Park" title="Morne Diablotin National Park">Morne Diablotin National Park</a> generally exceeded 24 in (600 mm). A secondary peak accumulation of 26 to 28 in (650 to 700 mm) occurred in the southeastern part of the island around <a href="/wiki/Morne_Trois_Pitons_National_Park" title="Morne Trois Pitons National Park">Morne Trois Pitons</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DischargeReport_49-1"><a href="#cite_note-DischargeReport-49">[48]</a></sup> Observed accumulations include 17 in (430 mm) at <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Gleau_Gommier&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Gleau Gommier (page does not exist)">Gleau Gommier</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WaDip_1_51-0"><a href="#cite_note-WaDip_1-51">[49]</a></sup> 12.62 in (321 mm) at <a href="/wiki/Canefield_Airport" title="Canefield Airport">Canefield Airport</a>, and 8.74 in (222 mm) at <a href="/wiki/Douglas%E2%80%93Charles_Airport" title="Douglas–Charles Airport">Douglas–Charles Airport</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TCR_4-12"><a href="#cite_note-TCR-4">[3]</a></sup> With grounds already saturated from nearly two weeks of rain prior to Erika, much of the precipitation did not penetrate the surface; in multiple watersheds, <a href="/wiki/Surface_runoff" title="Surface runoff">runoff</a> exceeded 60 percent of accumulations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DischargeReport_49-2"><a href="#cite_note-DischargeReport-49">[48]</a></sup></p>
<p>Catastrophic <a href="/wiki/Flash_flood" title="Flash flood">flash flooding</a> and mudslides ensued across the island,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TCR_4-13"><a href="#cite_note-TCR-4">[3]</a></sup> resulting in Dominica's worst natural disaster since <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_David" title="Hurricane David">Hurricane David</a> in 1979.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DavidMWR_52-0"><a href="#cite_note-DavidMWR-52">[50]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[51]</a></sup> Across the island's rugged terrain, the mudslides temporarily dammed overflowing rivers creating a buildup of water. These earthen dams subsequently collapsed, leading to amplified flooding downstream. Flow rates in these localized events vastly exceeded values expected by <a href="/wiki/International_Commission_on_Large_Dams" title="International Commission on Large Dams">International Commission on Large Dams</a> modeling. A post-storm study of 16 rivers on the island revealed peak <a href="/wiki/Discharge_(hydrology)" title="Discharge (hydrology)">discharge</a> values in excess of 1,000 m<sup>3</sup>/s along 5 rivers, with a maximum of 2,876 m<sup>3</sup>/s along the <a href="/wiki/Malabuka_River" title="Malabuka River">Malabuka River</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DischargeReport_49-3"><a href="#cite_note-DischargeReport-49">[48]</a></sup></p>
<p>Nearly 17,000 of the nation's 71,293 people were severely impacted by the storm.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-UNICEF_1_54-0"><a href="#cite_note-UNICEF_1-54">[52]</a></sup> A total of 30 people were killed,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TCR_4-14"><a href="#cite_note-TCR-4">[3]</a></sup> 20 others were injured, and 574 people were left homeless.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IFRC_Oct2015_29-1"><a href="#cite_note-IFRC_Oct2015-29">[28]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IFRC_Jan2016_30-1"><a href="#cite_note-IFRC_Jan2016-30">[29]</a></sup> More than 890 homes were destroyed or rendered uninhabitable.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[53]</a></sup> Infrastructure suffered tremendous damage: 6 percent of bridges and 17 percent of roads were wiped out. Furthermore, half of the nation's bridges and a quarter of its roads were damaged.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[54]</a></sup> Total losses amounted to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/East_Caribbean_dollar" title="East Caribbean dollar">EC$</a>1.3 billion (US$482.8 million), roughly 90 percent of Dominica's <a href="/wiki/Gross_domestic_product" title="Gross domestic product">gross domestic product</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IFRC_Jan2016_30-2"><a href="#cite_note-IFRC_Jan2016-30">[29]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Prime_Minister_of_Dominica" title="Prime Minister of Dominica">Dominica Prime Minister</a> <a href="/wiki/Roosevelt_Skerrit" title="Roosevelt Skerrit">Roosevelt Skerrit</a> stated in a national announcement on August 28 that, "The visual damage I saw today, I fear, may have set our development process back by 20 years".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RW_1_21-1"><a href="#cite_note-RW_1-21">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>Flooding crippled transportation and communication, with eight bridges severely damaged or destroyed, leaving many areas of the island isolated.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IFRC_Jan2016_30-3"><a href="#cite_note-IFRC_Jan2016-30">[29]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CDEMA_5_57-0"><a href="#cite_note-CDEMA_5-57">[55]</a></sup> <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Boetica&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Boetica (page does not exist)">Boetica</a> and <a href="/wiki/Delices" title="Delices">Delices</a> remained cut-off by ground for more than three weeks.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[56]</a></sup> More than 12 major rivers topped their banks.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RW_2_59-0"><a href="#cite_note-RW_2-59">[57]</a></sup> Approximately 45 percent of residents lost electricity,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CDEMA_5_57-1"><a href="#cite_note-CDEMA_5-57">[55]</a></sup> and almost the entirety of the water supply network was damaged.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IFRC_Jan2016_30-4"><a href="#cite_note-IFRC_Jan2016-30">[29]</a></sup> Additionally, 50 percent of telephone service was lost.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CDEMA_5_57-2"><a href="#cite_note-CDEMA_5-57">[55]</a></sup> Both Canefield and Douglas–Charles airports were flooded, with water rising above a small airplane at the latter.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-twc_60-0"><a href="#cite_note-twc-60">[58]</a></sup> Douglas–Charles Airport sustained more than EC$39.5 million (US$14.6 million) in damage and was closed until September 18.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61"><a href="#cite_note-61">[59]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62"><a href="#cite_note-62">[60]</a></sup> The main river running through <a href="/wiki/Roseau" title="Roseau">Roseau</a>, the nation's capital, burst its banks during the overnight of August 26–27, flooding surrounding areas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ErikaAP_63-0"><a href="#cite_note-ErikaAP-63">[61]</a></sup> One person was killed during a mudslide and one building collapsed in the city.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-twc_60-1"><a href="#cite_note-twc-60">[58]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-slno_64-0"><a href="#cite_note-slno-64">[62]</a></sup></p>
<p>The small community of <a href="/wiki/Petite_Savanne" title="Petite Savanne">Petite Savanne</a> was virtually destroyed, with 217 homes leveled and all residents forced to evacuate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CDEMA_8_65-0"><a href="#cite_note-CDEMA_8-65">[63]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Carib360_1_66-0"><a href="#cite_note-Carib360_1-66">[64]</a></sup> Twenty people died or were presumed dead in the village, accounting for two-thirds of the deaths attributed to Erika in Dominica.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TCR_4-15"><a href="#cite_note-TCR-4">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-67"><a href="#cite_note-67">[65]</a></sup> Residents dug through mud and debris, often with their bare hands, to rescue those trapped and in many cases did so with nearby homes on the verge of collapse.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RW_2_59-1"><a href="#cite_note-RW_2-59">[57]</a></sup> The village was isolated for several days and residents buried victims before help arrived.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-68"><a href="#cite_note-68">[66]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Local_response">Local response</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tropical_Storm_Erika&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Local response">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Immediately following Erika's devastating impact in Dominica on August 27, the <a href="/wiki/Organisation_of_Eastern_Caribbean_States" title="Organisation of Eastern Caribbean States">Organisation of Eastern Caribbean States</a> convened to determine an assessment plan.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-69"><a href="#cite_note-69">[67]</a></sup> The Government of Dominica declared nine areas special disaster areas: Petite Savanne, Pichelin, Good Hope, Bath Estate, Dubique, Campbell, Coulibistrie, San Sauveur, and Petite Soufriere.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CDEMA_4_70-0"><a href="#cite_note-CDEMA_4-70">[68]</a></sup> The National Emergency Operations Centre was activated and worked in conjunction with various local agencies to conduct relief missions. Approximately 100 personnel were deployed for various relief measures, including search and rescue.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CDEMA_7_71-0"><a href="#cite_note-CDEMA_7-71">[69]</a></sup> The Dominica Spa Health and Wellness Association worked in conjunction with the Trinidad-based Centre for Human Development to provide psychological support to victims;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-72"><a href="#cite_note-72">[70]</a></sup> eight trauma specialists were sent to Dominica.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-73"><a href="#cite_note-73">[71]</a></sup></p>
<p>The psychiatric unit of Princess Margaret Hospital conducted field assessments and provided emotional support to residents. In a report on September 18, they noted that residents in <a href="/wiki/Coulibistrie" title="Coulibistrie">Coulibistrie</a> remained in a state of shock for weeks after Erika, with many struggling to begin the recovery process.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-74"><a href="#cite_note-74">[72]</a></sup> The Ministry of Health highlighted concerns over post-storm diseases, particularly from mosquitoes, due to a major increase in the usage of water storage containers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-75"><a href="#cite_note-75">[73]</a></sup> An outbreak of <a href="/wiki/Gastroenteritis" title="Gastroenteritis">Gastroenteritis</a> ensued in the weeks following Erika, with 153 cases confirmed by September 22. Cases were primarily in <a href="/wiki/Portsmouth,_Dominica" title="Portsmouth, Dominica">Portsmouth</a>, <a href="/wiki/Saint_Joseph,_Dominica" title="Saint Joseph, Dominica">Saint Joseph</a>, and Roseau.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-76"><a href="#cite_note-76">[74]</a></sup></p>
<p>A unique means of supplying food and water the communities of Boetica and Delices, only accessible by air due to a gorge 260 ft (80 m) wide and 200 ft (61 m) deep, was established in the form of a <a href="/wiki/Zip-line" title="Zip-line">zip-line</a> in late-September. This served as the primary means of aid until a 98 ft (30 m) <a href="/wiki/Bailey_bridge" title="Bailey bridge">Bailey bridge</a> was constructed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-77"><a href="#cite_note-77">[75]</a></sup> By April 2016, the cost of infrastructure rehabilitation projects reached EC$7 million (US$3 million).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-78"><a href="#cite_note-78">[76]</a></sup> Supplementing reconstruction efforts by China and the United Kingdom, the nation's government allocated a further EC$30 million (US$11 million) to enhancing road safety.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-79"><a href="#cite_note-79">[77]</a></sup> The Food and Agriculture Organization allocated EC$300,000 (US$110,000) for farmers in October.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-80"><a href="#cite_note-80">[78]</a></sup> Through June 2016, the government provided victims with EC$5–6 million (US$1.8–2.2 million), primarily to cover shelters and food.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-81"><a href="#cite_note-81">[79]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="International_assistance">International assistance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tropical_Storm_Erika&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: International assistance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>Within 48 hours of the storm, Governments of Canada, France, the United Kingdom, the United States, the European Union, and various intergovernmental organizations under the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations" title="United Nations">United Nations</a>—such as the <a href="/wiki/Pan_American_Health_Organization" title="Pan American Health Organization">Pan American Health Organization</a> (PAHO), <a href="/wiki/UNICEF" title="UNICEF">UNICEF</a>, and <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_Development_Programme" title="United Nations Development Programme">United Nations Development Programme</a>—pledged aid; Antigua and Barbuda, Barbados, Montserrat, Saint Lucia, and Trinidad and Tobago provided support teams—firefighters, police, search and rescue, and military personnel—and established supply routes with Dominica.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CDEMA_3_38-1"><a href="#cite_note-CDEMA_3-38">[37]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Caribbean_Disaster_Emergency_Management_Agency" title="Caribbean Disaster Emergency Management Agency">Caribbean Disaster Emergency Management Agency</a> (CDEMA) pledged assistance to Dominica, offering two helicopters with supplies and medics from <a href="/wiki/Trinidad" title="Trinidad">Trinidad</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Newsday1_18-2"><a href="#cite_note-Newsday1-18">[17]</a></sup> Support teams from the CDEMA arrived in Dominica on August 28.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CDEMA_3_38-2"><a href="#cite_note-CDEMA_3-38">[37]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Regional_Security_System" title="Regional Security System">Regional Security System</a> deployed 83 personnel whom conducted search and rescue operations and assisted in the evacuation of hundreds of residents.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-82"><a href="#cite_note-82">[80]</a></sup> China and the <a href="/wiki/Caribbean_Development_Bank" title="Caribbean Development Bank">Caribbean Development Bank</a> offered US$300,000 and US$200,000 in aid, respectively, to the nation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ET829_83-0"><a href="#cite_note-ET829-83">[81]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-84"><a href="#cite_note-84">[82]</a></sup> China later provided an additional US$200,000 in October.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-85"><a href="#cite_note-85">[83]</a></sup> The Government of Venezuela airlifted 2,000 tons of food and supplies to Dominica and pledged to provide a helicopter carrier ship on August 29.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-86"><a href="#cite_note-86">[84]</a></sup> Supplies from Venezuela were bolstered to 10 tons by September 1, and the ship PDV <i>Marina</i> was deployed that day with an additional 13 tons worth fuel, machinery, and other supplies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-87"><a href="#cite_note-87">[85]</a></sup></p>
<p>Further assistance was provided by the Governments of Cuba, Grenada, and St. Kitts and Nevis by August 31,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CDEMA_6_88-0"><a href="#cite_note-CDEMA_6-88">[86]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-89"><a href="#cite_note-89">[87]</a></sup> the latter of which donated EC$1 million (US$368,000).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-90"><a href="#cite_note-90">[88]</a></sup> The British-flagged <a href="/wiki/RFA_Lyme_Bay_(L3007)" title="RFA Lyme Bay (L3007)">RFA <i>Lyme Bay</i></a> arrived in Dominica on September 2, carrying medical supplies, marines,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CDEMA_8_65-1"><a href="#cite_note-CDEMA_8-65">[63]</a></sup> and a <a href="/wiki/Westland_Lynx" title="Westland Lynx">Lynx Mk.8 helicopter</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-91"><a href="#cite_note-91">[89]</a></sup> The helicopter flew 25 flights over a period of five days to isolated communities, providing them with 2 tonnes of supplies daily. More than 20 tonnes of water and 5.3 tonnes of food were provided by RFA <i>Lyme Bay</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-92"><a href="#cite_note-92">[90]</a></sup> The Governments of the British Virgin Islands and Taiwan each pledged US$100,000 in funds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-93"><a href="#cite_note-93">[91]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-94"><a href="#cite_note-94">[92]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Digicel" title="Digicel">Digicel</a> and <a href="/wiki/LIME_(Cable_%26_Wireless)" title="LIME (Cable &amp; Wireless)">LIME</a> coordinated restoration efforts to the nation's <a href="/wiki/Fiber-optic_communication" title="Fiber-optic communication">fiber-optic</a> networks.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CDEMA_10_95-0"><a href="#cite_note-CDEMA_10-95">[93]</a></sup> The former requested donations from 32 countries while the latter assisted in establishing supply routes to isolated communities.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-96"><a href="#cite_note-96">[94]</a></sup> Digical also partnered with the Dominican Red Cross, paying for transportation so relief teams could access isolated towns.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-97"><a href="#cite_note-97">[95]</a></sup></p>

<p>The Government of the <a href="/wiki/Cayman_Islands" title="Cayman Islands">Cayman Islands</a> donated US$500,000 and requested residents of the territory to donate supplies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-98"><a href="#cite_note-98">[96]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a>'s government provided US$100,000-worth of generators, water purifiers, and water tanks through the Japan International Cooperation Agency.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-99"><a href="#cite_note-99">[97]</a></sup> A further <a href="/wiki/Trinidad_and_Tobago_dollar" title="Trinidad and Tobago dollar">TT$</a>5 million (US$784,000) was provided by Trinidad and Tobago.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-100"><a href="#cite_note-100">[98]</a></sup> The Government of Guyana provided a shipment of 68 tonnes of rice collected through public and private donations, three times more than targeted.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-101"><a href="#cite_note-101">[99]</a></sup> Through its Excess Rainfall Programme, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Caribbean_Catastrophe_Risk_Insurance_Facility" title="Caribbean Catastrophe Risk Insurance Facility">Caribbean Catastrophe Risk Insurance Facility</a> provided €6.5 million (US$2.4 million) to the Government of Dominica—the maximum payout allowable.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-102"><a href="#cite_note-102">[100]</a></sup> Thousands of school supplies, including books and writing utensils, were collected by the <a href="/wiki/Caribbean_Community" title="Caribbean Community">Caribbean Community</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-103"><a href="#cite_note-103">[101]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Adventist_Development_and_Relief_Agency" title="Adventist Development and Relief Agency">Adventist Development and Relief Agency</a> (ADRA), <a href="/wiki/All_Hands_Volunteers" title="All Hands Volunteers">All Hands Volunteers</a>, <a href="/wiki/Convoy_of_Hope" title="Convoy of Hope">Convoy of Hope</a>, <a href="/wiki/ChildFund" title="ChildFund">ChildFund</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Organization_of_American_States" title="Organization of American States">Organization of American States</a> also provided assistance.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-104"><a href="#cite_note-104">[102]</a></sup></p>
<p>The initial response from UNICEF included the distribution of over 600 hygiene kits and 4,000 water purification tablets.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-UNICEF_1_54-1"><a href="#cite_note-UNICEF_1-54">[52]</a></sup> Logistical support, namely satellite phones, solar chargers, and laptops, were provided by the <a href="/wiki/International_Telecommunication_Union" title="International Telecommunication Union">International Telecommunication Union</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-105"><a href="#cite_note-105">[103]</a></sup> Four tonnes of medical supplies provided by the PAHO arrived on September 9,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-106"><a href="#cite_note-106">[104]</a></sup> and the Government of Brazil supplied additional medical items in October.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-107"><a href="#cite_note-107">[105]</a></sup> After an initial release of funds on September 3, the <a href="/wiki/International_Federation_of_Red_Cross_and_Red_Crescent_Societies" title="International Federation of Red Cross and Red Crescent Societies">International Federation of Red Cross and Red Crescent Societies</a> (IFRC) launched an emergency appeal on September 10 for approximately US$1 million to assist 12,000 people.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-108"><a href="#cite_note-108">[106]</a></sup> The society's relief operation lasted until April 10, 2016, and provided 12,382 people with life-saving assistance. The IFRC's primary goal was to restore access to clean water; more than half a million litres of water was provided during the seven-month operation. In regards to health, psychosocial support was made available to 873 people, roughly half of the original goal. Hygiene awareness and distribution of kits proved successful, with all 1,000 kits supplied to those in need. The Dominican Red Cross mass-promoted the Red Cross First Aid mobile app to 8,000 phones, though only 399 people downloaded it. Cash grants of US$334—by way of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/VISA" title="VISA">VISA</a> debit cards—were provided to 279 families to cover lost property. A total of 1,000 mattresses were provided to survivors and a request for additional beds was made; however, limited funds prevented fulfillment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-109"><a href="#cite_note-109">[107]</a></sup></p>
<p>In March 2017, the CDEMA approved a €9.6 million (US$10.5 million) plan to address gaps in Dominica's disaster response legislation uncovered during the response to Erika.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-110"><a href="#cite_note-110">[108]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Reconstruction">Reconstruction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tropical_Storm_Erika&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Reconstruction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The destruction of Petite Savanne forced the evacuation of 823 people; the village was later deemed uninhabitable and a new town needed to be built elsewhere.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CNN1_111-0"><a href="#cite_note-CNN1-111">[109]</a></sup> Many were temporarily relocated to the Dominica Grammar School in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Roseau,_Dominica" title="Roseau, Dominica">Roseau</a>; however, members of the public looked upon the evacuees with scorn and insulted them for disrupting their daily lives. Parliamentary Representative Kenneth Darroux pleaded with residents to be understanding and patient with those whom were displaced.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-112"><a href="#cite_note-112">[110]</a></sup> Plans for a new settlement were established in February 2016, with plans to build 500–1,000 homes. Similarly, all residents of Dubique were forced to evacuate; they were relocated to temporary homes in Grand Bay provided by Venezuela.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CNN1_111-1"><a href="#cite_note-CNN1-111">[109]</a></sup> Ten two- and three-bedroom homes were provided by the ADRA. Costs for resettlement and reconstruction exceeded EC$91 million (US$14.3 million).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-113"><a href="#cite_note-113">[111]</a></sup> Power was restored to all areas of the nation, except for Petite Savanne and Dubique, by September 28.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CDEMA_12_114-0"><a href="#cite_note-CDEMA_12-114">[112]</a></sup> Restoration to the nation's water system was completed in January 2016; however, permanent systems were still to be constructed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-115"><a href="#cite_note-115">[113]</a></sup></p>
<p>French organizations provided €70,000 (US$77,000) for the reconstruction of two schools.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-116"><a href="#cite_note-116">[114]</a></sup> Repair of Douglas–Charles airport was deemed a priority, though the first phase of rehabilitation for runways and <a href="/wiki/Airport_apron" title="Airport apron">aprons</a> took more than a year to complete. In addition to repairs, greater flood defense systems were implemented, including dredging, flood walls, and river training.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-117"><a href="#cite_note-117">[115]</a></sup> Construction of 25 planned homes by the ADRA began in February 2016, with projected completion in 2017; 50 volunteers were involved in the project.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-118"><a href="#cite_note-118">[116]</a></sup> In November 2016, Dominica signed an agreement with the European Union for an €8.9 million (US$9.6 million) rehabilitation project.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-119"><a href="#cite_note-119">[117]</a></sup> In a join Dominica–United Kingdom project, a new road from Loubiere to Bagatelle in southeastern Dominica was announced in March 2017. The EC$100 million (US$37 million) plan would ensure the road could handle heavy rain events, with 12–14 culverts and 3 bridges. Furthermore, road edge failure issues were to be addressed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-120"><a href="#cite_note-120">[118]</a></sup></p>
