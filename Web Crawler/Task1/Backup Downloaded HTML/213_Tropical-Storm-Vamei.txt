<p><b>Tropical Storm Vamei</b> was a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pacific_typhoon" title="Pacific typhoon">Pacific</a> <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone" title="Tropical cyclone">tropical cyclone</a> that formed closer to the <a href="/wiki/Equator" title="Equator">equator</a> than any other tropical cyclone in the Pacific Ocean. The last storm of the <a href="/wiki/2001_Pacific_typhoon_season" title="2001 Pacific typhoon season">2001 Pacific typhoon season</a>, Vamei developed on December 26 at <a href="/wiki/1st_parallel_north" title="1st parallel north">1.4° N</a> in the <a href="/wiki/South_China_Sea" title="South China Sea">South China Sea</a>. It strengthened quickly and made <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Landfall_(meteorology)" title="Landfall (meteorology)">landfall</a> along extreme southeastern <a href="/wiki/Peninsular_Malaysia" title="Peninsular Malaysia">Peninsular Malaysia</a>. Vamei rapidly dissipated over <a href="/wiki/Sumatra" title="Sumatra">Sumatra</a> on December 28, and the remnants eventually re-organized in the North <a href="/wiki/Indian_Ocean" title="Indian Ocean">Indian Ocean</a>.</p>
<p>Though Vamei was officially designated as a <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone#Intensity_classifications" title="Tropical cyclone">tropical storm</a>, its intensity is disputed; some agencies classify it as a <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone#Intensity_classifications" title="Tropical cyclone">typhoon</a>, based on sustained winds of 140 km/h (85 mph) and the appearance of an <a href="/wiki/Eye_(cyclone)" title="Eye (cyclone)">eye</a>. The storm brought flooding and landslides to eastern Peninsular Malaysia, causing $3.6 million in damage (2001 <a href="/wiki/United_States_dollar" title="United States dollar">USD</a>, $4.87 million 2017 USD) and five deaths.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Meteorological_history">Meteorological history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tropical_Storm_Vamei&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Meteorological history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Vamei_2001_track.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="633" data-file-width="1024" height="136" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4b/Vamei_2001_track.png/220px-Vamei_2001_track.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4b/Vamei_2001_track.png/330px-Vamei_2001_track.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4b/Vamei_2001_track.png/440px-Vamei_2001_track.png 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Vamei_2001_track.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Map plotting the track and intensity of the storm, according to the <a href="/wiki/Saffir%E2%80%93Simpson_scale" title="Saffir–Simpson scale">Saffir–Simpson scale</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>On December 19, a small low-level circulation was located along the northwest coastline of <a href="/wiki/Borneo" title="Borneo">Borneo</a>; at the same time a plume of cold air progressed southward through the <a href="/wiki/South_China_Sea" title="South China Sea">South China Sea</a> on the southeastern periphery of a <a href="/wiki/Ridge_(meteorology)" title="Ridge (meteorology)">ridge</a> over the <a href="/wiki/Far_East" title="Far East">Far East</a>. The vortex drifted southwestward, reaching open water by December 21. The northerly air surge was deflected after interacting with the circulation, and at the same time a portion of the air surge crossed the <a href="/wiki/Equator" title="Equator">equator</a>. The southerly flow turned eastward, then northward, and in combination with the northerly flow it wrapped into the vortex, resulting in rapid <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclogenesis" title="Tropical cyclogenesis">development</a> of the low-level circulation, just a short distance north of the equator.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nps_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-nps-1">[1]</a></sup> By December 25, an area of scattered <a href="/wiki/Convection" title="Convection">convection</a> persisted about 370 km (230 mi) east of <a href="/wiki/Singapore" title="Singapore">Singapore</a> within an area of low <a href="/wiki/Wind_shear" title="Wind shear">wind shear</a>, in association with the low-level circulation. Continuing slowly westward, the convection deepened and organized further,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gp_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-gp-2">[2]</a></sup> and at 1200 <a href="/wiki/Coordinated_Universal_Time" title="Coordinated Universal Time">UTC</a> on December 26 the disturbance developed into a tropical depression about 230 km (145 mi) east of Singapore, or 156 km (97 mi) north of the equator.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jma_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-jma-3">[3]</a></sup> This was the first recorded occurrence of a tropical cyclone near the equator.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nps_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-nps-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>The depression strengthened further and officially attained tropical storm status at 0000 UTC on December 27, based on the analysis by the <a href="/wiki/Japan_Meteorological_Agency" title="Japan Meteorological Agency">Japan Meteorological Agency</a> (JMA),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jma_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-jma-3">[3]</a></sup> though the <a href="/wiki/Joint_Typhoon_Warning_Center" title="Joint Typhoon Warning Center">Joint Typhoon Warning Center</a> (JTWC) unofficially classified it as a tropical storm six hours prior. Shortly thereafter, an <a href="/wiki/Eye_(cyclone)" title="Eye (cyclone)">eye</a> with a 39 km (24 mi) diameter became apparent on satellite imagery, along with <a href="/wiki/Squall#Tropical_cyclones" title="Squall">rainbands</a> extending southward to the opposite side of the equator. At 0600 UTC, the JMA first classified the system as Tropical Storm Vamei, about 65 km (40 mi) northeast of Singapore,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gp_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-gp-2">[2]</a></sup> and the agency estimated the storm attained peak winds of 85 km/h (50 mph) at the same time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jma_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-jma-3">[3]</a></sup> However, the JTWC upgraded Vamei to typhoon status with peak winds of 140 km/h (85 mph), based on a <a href="/wiki/United_States_Navy" title="United States Navy">United States Navy</a> ship report from within the eye; a second ship reported wind gusts of 195 km/h (120 mph) in the southern portion of the eyewall. The storm was small and compact, with gales extending about 45 km (30 mi) from its center. At about 0830 UTC on December 27, Vamei made <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Landfall_(meteorology)" title="Landfall (meteorology)">landfall</a> approximately 60 km (35 mi) northeast of Singapore,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gp_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-gp-2">[2]</a></sup> in the southeastern portion of the <a href="/wiki/Malaysia" title="Malaysia">Malaysian</a> state of <a href="/wiki/Johor" title="Johor">Johor</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kjc_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-kjc-4">[4]</a></sup> Initially, the <a href="/wiki/Ministry_of_Science,_Technology_and_Innovation_(Malaysia)" title="Ministry of Science, Technology and Innovation (Malaysia)">Malaysian Meteorological Department</a> (MetMalaysia) classified the cyclone as a tropical storm,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nst1220_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-nst1220-5">[5]</a></sup> though it was later re-assessed as a typhoon at landfall.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nst1114_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-nst1114-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>Tropical Storm Vamei weakened quickly as it crossed the extreme southern portion of the Malay Peninsula, and late on December 27 the JMA downgraded it to tropical depression status before the cyclone emerged into the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Straits_of_Malacca" title="Straits of Malacca">Straits of Malacca</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jma_3-3"><a href="#cite_note-jma-3">[3]</a></sup> The JTWC initially maintained it as a minimal tropical storm, though the agency downgraded the storm to depression status as the center again approached land.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gp_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-gp-2">[2]</a></sup> Early on December 28, Vamei moved ashore along northeastern <a href="/wiki/Sumatra" title="Sumatra">Sumatra</a>, and at 0600 UTC the JMA classified the storm as dissipated.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jma_3-4"><a href="#cite_note-jma-3">[3]</a></sup> However, convection persisted near the circulation over land, believed to have been caused by the process known as upper-level <a href="/wiki/Deformation_(meteorology)" title="Deformation (meteorology)">diffluence</a>. On December 29, what was originally believed to be a separate system reached the southeastern <a href="/wiki/Bay_of_Bengal" title="Bay of Bengal">Bay of Bengal</a>. In a post-season re-evaluation, the JTWC classified the system as a continuation of Vamei, based on analysis of satellite imagery that indicated the circulation of Vamei crossed Sumatra without dissipating. Convection re-developed, and late on December 30 the JTWC classified the cyclone as a tropical storm about 390 km (245 mi) west-southwest of the northwestern tip of Sumatra; initially, due to being treated as a separate system, it was classified as Tropical Cyclone 05B. Vamei quickly developed good <a href="/wiki/Outflow_(meteorology)" title="Outflow (meteorology)">outflow</a> and organization, though increased wind shear on December 31 rapidly weakened the storm; by late that day, the center was exposed from the deep convection, and Vamei quickly dissipated.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gp_2-4"><a href="#cite_note-gp-2">[2]</a></sup></p>

<p>Vamei formed and reached tropical storm strength at <a href="/wiki/1st_parallel_north" title="1st parallel north">1.5º N</a>, only 156 km (97 mi) from the equator.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jma_3-5"><a href="#cite_note-jma-3">[3]</a></sup> This broke the previous record of Typhoon Sarah in the <a href="/wiki/1956_Pacific_typhoon_season" title="1956 Pacific typhoon season">1956 Pacific typhoon season</a>, which reached tropical storm strength at <a href="/wiki/2nd_parallel_north" title="2nd parallel north">2.2º N</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gp_2-5"><a href="#cite_note-gp-2">[2]</a></sup> Due to a lack of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coriolis_effect" title="Coriolis effect">Coriolis effect</a> near the equator, the formation of Vamei was previously considered impossible.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gp_2-6"><a href="#cite_note-gp-2">[2]</a></sup> However, a study by the <a href="/wiki/Naval_Postgraduate_School" title="Naval Postgraduate School">Naval Postgraduate School</a> indicated that the probability for a similar equatorial development was at least once every four centuries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nps_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-nps-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Vamei developed in a vortex that appears every winter along the northwest coast of <a href="/wiki/Borneo" title="Borneo">Borneo</a> and is maintained by the interaction between <a href="/wiki/Monsoon" title="Monsoon">monsoonal winds</a> and the local topography. Often, the vortex remains near the coastline, and in an analysis of 51 winters, only six reported the vortex as being over the equatorial waters for four days or more. As the area in the South China Sea between Borneo and Singapore is only 665 km (415 mi) wide, a vortex needs to move slowly to develop. A persistent northerly wind surge for more than five days, which is needed to enhance the vortex, is present, on average, nine days each winter. The probability for a pre-existing tropical disturbance to develop into a tropical cyclone is between 10–30 percent. Thus, the conditions which resulted in the formation of Vamei are believed to occur once every 100–400 years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nps_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-nps-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Preparations_and_impact">Preparations and impact</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tropical_Storm_Vamei&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Preparations and impact">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>Four days prior to Vamei moving ashore, the Malaysian Meteorological Department (MetMalaysia) issued storm advisories for potentially affected areas. Subsequently, the agency issued warnings for heavy rainfall, high winds, and rough seas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kjc_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-kjc-4">[4]</a></sup> However, few citizens knew of the passage of the rare storm.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>Offshore of Malaysia, two <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._Navy" title="U.S. Navy">U.S. Navy</a> ships in Vamei's eyewall were damaged by strong winds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nps_1-4"><a href="#cite_note-nps-1">[1]</a></sup> Upon moving ashore, the storm brought <a href="/wiki/Storm_surge" title="Storm surge">storm surge</a> damage to portions of southeastern <a href="/wiki/Peninsular_Malaysia" title="Peninsular Malaysia">Peninsular Malaysia</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NSF_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-NSF-8">[8]</a></sup> Vamei brought strong winds and heavy rainfall to portions of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Melaka" title="Melaka">Melaka</a>, <a href="/wiki/Negeri_Sembilan" title="Negeri Sembilan">Negeri Sembilan</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Selangor" title="Selangor">Selangor</a> as well as to <a href="/wiki/Johor" title="Johor">Johor</a>, where rainfall reached over 200 mm (8 in) in <a href="/wiki/Senai" title="Senai">Senai</a>. Additionally, monsoonal moisture, influenced by the storm, produced moderate to heavy precipitation across various regions of peninsular Malaysia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kjc_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-kjc-4">[4]</a></sup> The passage of the cyclone resulted in flooding and mudslides, which forced the evacuation of more than 13,195 people in Johor and <a href="/wiki/Pahang" title="Pahang">Pahang</a> states into 69 shelters.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-vameidam_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-vameidam-10">[10]</a></sup> Along <a href="/wiki/Mount_Pulai" title="Mount Pulai">Gunung Pulai</a>, the rainfall caused a landslide which destroyed four houses and killed five people. River flooding was also reported, as a result of the precipitation from Vamei as well as previous rainfall. Damage from the flooding was estimated at <a href="/wiki/Malaysian_ringgit" title="Malaysian ringgit">RM</a>13.7 million (2001 <a href="/wiki/Malaysian_ringgit" title="Malaysian ringgit">MYR</a>, $3.6 million 2001 USD). About 40 percent of the damage occurred to crops at a farm in <a href="/wiki/Kota_Tinggi" title="Kota Tinggi">Kota Tinggi</a>. Moderate damage to transportation, education, and health-care facilities was also reported. The Malaysian government provided affected families up to RM5,000 (2001 MYR, $1,300 2001 USD) in assistance for food, clothing, and repairs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-vameidam_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-vameidam-10">[10]</a></sup> Vamei also brought heavy rainfall to <a href="/wiki/Singapore" title="Singapore">Singapore</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> which caused air traffic disruptions at the <a href="/wiki/Singapore_Changi_Airport" title="Singapore Changi Airport">Singapore Changi Airport</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> The passage of the cyclone resulted in many downed trees.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2004, the name "Vamei" was retired and replaced with "Peipah", becoming the first retired name since the <a href="/wiki/Japan_Meteorological_Agency" title="Japan Meteorological Agency">Japan Meteorological Agency</a> began naming Pacific typhoons in 2000. Vamei was one of four tropical cyclones in the current naming list that was retired without attaining typhoon status; the others were <a href="/wiki/Tropical_Storm_Bilis" title="Tropical Storm Bilis">Tropical Storm Bilis</a> in 2006, <a href="/wiki/Tropical_Storm_Washi" title="Tropical Storm Washi">Tropical Storm Washi</a> in 2011 and Sonamu in 2013.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2008wmo_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-2008wmo-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
