<p>A <b>launch pad</b> is an above-ground platform from which a <a href="/wiki/Rocket" title="Rocket">rocket</a>-powered <a href="/wiki/Missile" title="Missile">missile</a> or <a href="/wiki/Space_vehicle" title="Space vehicle">space vehicle</a> is vertically launched. A <a href="/wiki/Spaceport" title="Spaceport">spaceport</a> (or <b>launch complex</b>) is a facility which includes, and provides required support for, one or more launch pads. A launch pad generally contains a fixed or mobile <a href="/wiki/Service_structure" title="Service structure">service structure</a>, which provides one or more access platforms to inspect and maintain the vehicle, and an <i>umbilical structure</i> which provides the vehicle with <a href="/wiki/Liquid-propellant_rocket" title="Liquid-propellant rocket">propellants</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cryogenic" title="Cryogenic">cryogenic</a> fluids, electrical power, communications, and <a href="/wiki/Telemetry" title="Telemetry">telemetry</a> prior to launch. The service structure also provides access to the crew cabin for vehicles carrying humans. The pad may contain a flame deflection structure to prevent the intense heat of the rocket exhaust from damaging the vehicle or pad structures, and a sound suppression system spraying large quantities of water may be employed. The pad may also be protected by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lightning_arrestor" title="Lightning arrestor">lightning arrestors</a>.</p>
<p>A launch pad is distinct from a <a href="/wiki/Missile_launch_facility" title="Missile launch facility">missile launch facility</a> (or <b>missile silo</b>), which also launches a missile vertically but is located underground in order to help harden it against enemy attack, or conceal it from surveillance.</p>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cryogenic_propellant" title="Cryogenic propellant">Cryogenic propellants</a> (<a href="/wiki/Liquid_oxygen" title="Liquid oxygen">liquid oxygen</a> oxidizer, and <a href="/wiki/Liquid_hydrogen" title="Liquid hydrogen">liquid hydrogen</a> fuel) need to be continuously topped off (i.e., boil-off replaced) during the launch sequence (<a href="/wiki/Countdown" title="Countdown">countdown</a>), as the vehicle awaits liftoff. This becomes particularly important as complex sequences may be interrupted by planned or unplanned holds to fix problems.</p>
<p>Most rockets need stable support for a few seconds after ignition while the engines build up to stable, full <a href="/wiki/Thrust" title="Thrust">thrust</a>. Therefore, the vehicle is commonly held on the pad by hold-down arms or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Explosive_bolt" title="Explosive bolt">explosive bolts</a>, which are triggered when the vehicle is stable and ready to fly, at which point all umbilical connections with the pad are released.</p>

<p>There are several different types of launch site, determined by the means by which the <a href="/wiki/Space_vehicle" title="Space vehicle">space vehicle</a> gets to the pad.</p>
<ul>
<li>The first large rocket, the <a href="/wiki/V-2_rocket" title="V-2 rocket">V-2</a>, travelled horizontally with its tail forward to the launch site at <a href="/wiki/Peenem%C3%BCnde" title="Peenemünde">Peenemünde</a>. This method was used for all large Soviet rockets, including the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/N1_rocket" title="N1 rocket">N1</a> and <a href="/wiki/Energia" title="Energia">Energia</a>.</li>
<li>In a similar manner, at the Soviet launch site near <a href="/wiki/Volgograd" title="Volgograd">Volgograd</a>, a silo used to launch test rockets would have its top opened and a second stage and payload would be driven in horizontally and tilted on top of a first stage already in the silo, the nose cone and some of the second stage remaining visible above ground. Hence no surface pad is used; Russian silos are reusable. This method was only used for the Cosmos series of small satellite launching vehicles.</li>
<li>A method of assembling a large space vehicle vertically in a <a href="/wiki/Vehicle_Assembly_Building" title="Vehicle Assembly Building">Vehicle Assembly Building</a> on a <a href="/wiki/Mobile_Launcher_Platform" title="Mobile Launcher Platform">Mobile Launcher Platform</a> (MLP), which contained the umbilical structure, was invented for the <a href="/wiki/Apollo_(spacecraft)" title="Apollo (spacecraft)">Apollo</a>/<a href="/wiki/Saturn_V" title="Saturn V">Saturn V</a> manned Moon landing vehicle. The MLP is then carried by a <a href="/wiki/Crawler-transporter" title="Crawler-transporter">Crawler-transporter</a>, which slowly drives to one of two pads at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Launch_Complex_39" title="Launch Complex 39">Launch Complex 39</a> at the <a href="/wiki/Kennedy_Space_Center" title="Kennedy Space Center">Kennedy Space Center</a>. This facility was later used for the smaller Apollo/<a href="/wiki/Saturn_IB" title="Saturn IB">Saturn IB</a>, and then the <a href="/wiki/Space_Shuttle" title="Space Shuttle">Space Shuttle</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> A similar system is used to launch <a href="/wiki/Ariane_5" title="Ariane 5">Ariane 5</a> rockets at <a href="/wiki/ELA-3" title="ELA-3">ELA-3</a> at <a href="/wiki/Guiana_Space_Centre" title="Guiana Space Centre">Guiana Space Centre</a>, a French spaceport near <a href="/wiki/Kourou" title="Kourou">Kourou</a> in <a href="/wiki/French_Guiana" title="French Guiana">French Guiana</a>.</li>
<li>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Titan_III" title="Titan III">Titan III</a> and <a href="/wiki/Titan_IV" title="Titan IV">Titan IV</a> launch vehicles were transported with a mobile launcher platform on two parallel standard gauge railroad tracks from the integration building to launch areas at <a href="/wiki/Cape_Canaveral_Air_Force_Station_Space_Launch_Complex_40" title="Cape Canaveral Air Force Station Space Launch Complex 40">Cape Canaveral Air Force Station Space Launch Complex 40</a> and 41. This system is still in use for the <a href="/wiki/Atlas_V" title="Atlas V">Atlas V</a>.</li>
<li>At <a href="/wiki/Vandenberg_Air_Force_Base" title="Vandenberg Air Force Base">Vandenberg Air Force Base</a>, in California, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Titan_(rocket)" title="Titan (rocket)">Titan</a> series of rockets were set up vertically in a gantry in a windowless building at <a href="/wiki/Vandenberg_AFB_Space_Launch_Complex_4" title="Vandenberg AFB Space Launch Complex 4">SLC-4</a>, the outside walls of which were rolled away just at launch. This was done for purposes of military secrecy. Similar systems are used at <a href="/wiki/Vandenberg_AFB_Space_Launch_Complex_6" title="Vandenberg AFB Space Launch Complex 6">SLC-6</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cape_Canaveral_Air_Force_Station_Launch_Complex_37" title="Cape Canaveral Air Force Station Launch Complex 37">LC37</a> at Cape Canaveral Air Force Station for the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Delta_IV_rocket" title="Delta IV rocket">Delta IV rocket</a>, <a href="/wiki/ELA-1" title="ELA-1">ELA-1</a> &amp; <a href="/wiki/ELA-2" title="ELA-2">2</a> at CSG for the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ariane_rocket" title="Ariane rocket">Ariane 1-4</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kagoshima_Space_Center" title="Kagoshima Space Center">Kagoshima</a> for the <a href="/wiki/M-V" title="M-V">M-V</a>.</li>
<li>In the 1920s, <a href="/wiki/Hermann_Oberth" title="Hermann Oberth">Hermann Oberth</a> described a method in which the vehicle is assembled vertically on a floating barge, which he used in the movie <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Frau_im_Mond" title="Frau im Mond">Frau im Mond</a></i>. This was seriously considered for use at Kennedy Space Center Launch Complex 39 for the Saturn V,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> but was rejected due to the instability of the top-heavy unfuelled rocket and gantry. However, the <a href="/wiki/Sea_Launch" title="Sea Launch">Sea Launch</a> service uses the converted self-propelled oil drilling platform <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ocean_Odyssey" title="Ocean Odyssey">Ocean Odyssey</a></i> to transport <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Zenit_3SL" title="Zenit 3SL">Zenit 3SL</a> rockets horizontally to the <a href="/wiki/Equator" title="Equator">Equator</a>, and then to erect and launch them into <a href="/wiki/Geostationary_transfer_orbit" title="Geostationary transfer orbit">geostationary transfer orbits</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Dnepr_(rocket)" title="Dnepr (rocket)">Dnepr</a> rockets are transported vertically and then inserted into a silo, as they are converted cold-launch <a href="/wiki/R-36_(missile)" title="R-36 (missile)">R-36 Voevoda</a> ICBMs.</li>
</ul>
