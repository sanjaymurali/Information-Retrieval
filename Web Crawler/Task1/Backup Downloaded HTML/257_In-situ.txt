<p><i><b>In situ</b></i> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ɪ/: 'i' in 'kit'">ɪ</span><span title="'n' in 'nigh'">n</span></span> <span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'s' in 'sigh'">s</span><span title="/ɪ/: 'i' in 'kit'">ɪ</span><span title="/tj/: 't' in 'tune'">tj</span><span title="/uː/: 'oo' in 'goose'">uː</span></span>/</a></span></span> or <span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ɪ/: 'i' in 'kit'">ɪ</span><span title="'n' in 'nigh'">n</span></span> <span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'s' in 'sigh'">s</span><span title="/aɪ/: 'i' in 'tide'">aɪ</span><span title="/tʃ/: 'ch' in 'China'">tʃ</span><span title="/uː/: 'oo' in 'goose'">uː</span></span>/</a></span></span>; often not italicized in English<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MWCD_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-MWCD-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AMA10section12.1.1_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-AMA10section12.1.1-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>) is a <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> phrase that translates literally to "on site"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> or "in position".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> It means "locally", "on site", "on the premises" or "in place" to describe an event where it takes place, and is used in many different contexts. For example, in fields such as <a href="/wiki/Physics" title="Physics">physics</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chemistry" title="Chemistry">chemistry</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Biology" title="Biology">biology</a>, <i>in situ</i> may describe the way a measurement is taken, that is, in the same place the phenomenon is occurring without isolating it from other systems or altering the original conditions of the test.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Aerospace">Aerospace</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=In_situ&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Aerospace">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aerospace_industry" title="Aerospace industry">aerospace industry</a>, equipment on-board aircraft must be tested <i>in situ</i>, or in place, to confirm everything functions properly as a system. Individually, each piece may work but interference from nearby equipment may create unanticipated problems. Special test equipment is available for this <i>in situ</i> testing.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Archaeology">Archaeology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=In_situ&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Archaeology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Hohokam_Arrowhead_Sahuarita_Arizona_2014.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="968" data-file-width="1295" height="164" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/49/Hohokam_Arrowhead_Sahuarita_Arizona_2014.jpg/220px-Hohokam_Arrowhead_Sahuarita_Arizona_2014.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/49/Hohokam_Arrowhead_Sahuarita_Arizona_2014.jpg/330px-Hohokam_Arrowhead_Sahuarita_Arizona_2014.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/49/Hohokam_Arrowhead_Sahuarita_Arizona_2014.jpg/440px-Hohokam_Arrowhead_Sahuarita_Arizona_2014.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>

<p>In <a href="/wiki/Archaeology" title="Archaeology">archaeology</a>, <i>in situ</i> refers to an artifact that has not been moved from its original place of deposition. In other words, it is stationary, meaning "still." An artifact being <i>in situ</i> is critical to the interpretation of that artifact and, consequently, of the culture which formed it. Once an artifact's 'find-site' has been recorded, the artifact can then be moved for conservation, further interpretation and display. An artifact that is not discovered <i>in situ</i> is considered out of context and as not providing an accurate picture of the associated culture. However, the out of context artifact can provide scientists with an example of types and locations of <i>in situ</i> artifacts yet to be discovered. When excavating a burial site or surface deposit "in situ" refers to cataloging, recording, mapping, photographing human remains in the position they are discovered.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>The label <i>in situ</i> indicates only that the object has not been "newly" moved. Thus, an archaeological <i>in situ</i> find may be an object that was historically looted from another place, an item of "booty" of a past war, a traded item, or otherwise of foreign origin. Consequently, the <i>in situ</i> find site may still not reveal its <a href="/wiki/Provenance" title="Provenance">provenance</a>, but with further detective work may help uncover links that otherwise would remain unknown. It is also possible for archaeological layers to be reworked on purpose or by accident (by humans, natural forces or animals). For example, in a <a href="/wiki/Tell_(archaeology)" title="Tell (archaeology)">Tell</a> mound, where layers are not typically uniform or horizontal, or in land cleared or tilled for farming.</p>
<p>The term <i>in situ</i> is often used to describe ancient sculpture that was carved in place such as the <a href="/wiki/Great_Sphinx_of_Giza" title="Great Sphinx of Giza">Sphinx</a> or <a href="/wiki/Petra" title="Petra">Petra</a>. This distinguishes it from statues that were carved and moved like the <a href="/wiki/Colossi_of_Memnon" title="Colossi of Memnon">Colossi of Memnon</a>, which was moved in ancient times.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Art">Art</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=In_situ&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Art">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In art, <i>in situ</i> refers to a work of art made specifically for a host site, or that a work of art takes into account the site in which it is installed or exhibited. For a more detailed account see: <a href="/wiki/Site-specific_art" title="Site-specific art">Site-specific art</a>. The term can also refer to a work of art created at the site where it is to be displayed, rather than one created in the artist's studio and then installed elsewhere (<i>e.g.,</i> a sculpture carved <i>in situ</i>). In <a href="/wiki/Architectural_sculpture" title="Architectural sculpture">architectural sculpture</a> the term is frequently employed to describe sculpture that is carved on a building, frequently from scaffolds, after the building has been erected.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FriedlandSobocinski2015_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-FriedlandSobocinski2015-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Webb1996_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Webb1996-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Astronomy">Astronomy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=In_situ&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Astronomy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A fraction of the <a href="/wiki/Globular_cluster" title="Globular cluster">globular star clusters</a> in <a href="/wiki/Milky_Way" title="Milky Way">our galaxy</a>, as well as those in other massive galaxies, might have formed <i>in situ</i>. The rest might have been accreted from now defunct dwarf galaxies.</p>
<p>In astronomy, <i>in situ</i> also refers to <i>in situ</i> <a href="/wiki/Nebular_hypothesis" title="Nebular hypothesis">planet formation</a>, in which planets are hypothesized to have been formed in the orbit that they are currently observed to be in rather than migrating from a different orbit.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Biology_and_biomedical_engineering">Biology and biomedical engineering</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=In_situ&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Biology and biomedical engineering">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>In <a href="/wiki/Biology" title="Biology">biology</a> and <a href="/wiki/Biomedical_engineering" title="Biomedical engineering">biomedical engineering</a>, <i>in situ</i> means to examine the phenomenon exactly in place where it occurs (i.e., without moving it to some special medium).</p>
<p>In the case of observations or photographs of living animals, it means that the organism was observed (and photographed) in the wild, exactly as it was found and exactly where it was found. This means it was not taken out of the area. The organism had not been moved to another (perhaps more convenient) location such as an aquarium.</p>
<p>This phrase <i>in situ</i> when used in laboratory science such as cell science can mean something intermediate between <i><a href="/wiki/In_vivo" title="In vivo">in vivo</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/In_vitro" title="In vitro">in vitro</a></i>. For example, examining a <a href="/wiki/Cell_(biology)" title="Cell (biology)">cell</a> within a whole <a href="/wiki/Organ_(anatomy)" title="Organ (anatomy)">organ</a> intact and under <a href="/wiki/Perfusion" title="Perfusion">perfusion</a> may be <i>in situ</i> investigation. This would not be <i>in vivo</i> as the donor is sacrificed by experimentation, but it would not be the same as working with the cell alone (a common scenario for <i>in vitro</i> experiments).</p>
<p><i>In vitro</i> was among the first attempts to qualitatively and quantitatively analyze natural occurrences in the lab. Eventually, the limitation of <i>in vitro</i> experimentation was that they were not conducted in natural environments. To compensate for this problem, <i>in vivo</i> experimentation allowed testing to occur in the original organism or environment. To bridge the dichotomy of benefits associated with both methodologies, <i>in situ</i> experimentation allowed the controlled aspects of <i>in vitro</i> to become coalesced with the natural environmental compositions of <i>in vivo</i> experimentation.</p>
<p>In conservation of <a href="/wiki/Germplasm" title="Germplasm">genetic resources</a>, "<a href="/wiki/In_situ_conservation" title="In situ conservation"><i>in situ</i> conservation</a>" (also "on-site <a href="/wiki/Conservation_movement" title="Conservation movement">conservation</a>") is the process of protecting an <a href="/wiki/Endangered_species" title="Endangered species">endangered</a> <a href="/wiki/Plant" title="Plant">plant</a> or <a href="/wiki/Animal" title="Animal">animal</a> <a href="/wiki/Species" title="Species">species</a> in its natural <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Habitat_(ecology)" title="Habitat (ecology)">habitat</a>, as opposed to <a href="/wiki/Ex_situ_conservation" title="Ex situ conservation"><i>ex situ</i> conservation</a> (also "off-site conservation").</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Chemistry_and_chemical_engineering">Chemistry and chemical engineering</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=In_situ&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Chemistry and chemical engineering">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In chemistry, <i>in situ</i> typically means "in the reaction mixture."</p>
<p>There are numerous situations in which chemical intermediates are synthesized <i>in situ</i> in various processes. This may be done because the species is unstable, and cannot be isolated, or simply out of convenience. Examples of the former include the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Corey-Chaykovsky_reagent" title="Corey-Chaykovsky reagent">Corey-Chaykovsky reagent</a> and <a href="/wiki/Adrenochrome" title="Adrenochrome">adrenochrome</a>.</p>
<p>In biomedical engineering, protein nanogels made by the in situ polymerization method provide a versatile platform for storage and release of therapeutic proteins. It has tremendous applications for cancer treatment, vaccination, diagnosis, regenerative medicine, and therapies for loss-of-function genetic diseases.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>In chemical engineering, <i>in situ</i> often refers to industrial plant "operations or procedures that are performed in place". For example, aged catalysts in industrial reactors may be regenerated in place (<i>in situ</i>) without being removed from the reactors.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Civil_engineering">Civil engineering</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=In_situ&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Civil engineering">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Architecture" title="Architecture">architecture</a> and <a href="/wiki/Building" title="Building">building</a>, <i>in situ</i> refers to construction which is carried out at the building site using raw materials. Compare that with <i>prefabricated</i> construction, in which building components are made in a factory and then transported to the building site for assembly. For example, <a href="/wiki/Concrete_slab" title="Concrete slab">concrete slabs</a> may be <i>in situ</i> (also "cast-in-place") or <i>prefabricated</i>.</p>
<p><i><b>In situ</b></i> techniques are often more labour-intensive, and take longer, but the materials are cheaper, and the work is versatile and adaptable. <i>Prefabricated</i> techniques are usually much quicker, therefore saving money on labour costs, but factory-made parts can be expensive. They are also inflexible, and must often be designed on a grid, with all details fully calculated in advance. Finished units may require special handling due to excessive dimensions.</p>
<p>The phrase may also refer to those assets which are present at or near a project site. In this case, it is used to designate the state of an unmodified sample taken from a given stockpile.</p>
<p>Site construction usually involves grading the existing soil surface so that material is "cut" out of one area and "filled" in another area creating a flat pad on an existing slope. The term "in situ" distinguishes soil still in its existing condition from soil modified (filled) during construction. The differences in the soil properties for supporting foundations, accepting underground utilities, and shedding storm water persist indefinitely.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Computer_science">Computer science</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=In_situ&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Computer science">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><span class="citation-needed-content" style="padding-left:0.1em; padding-right:0.1em; color:#595959; border:1px solid #DDD;">In <a href="/wiki/Computer_science" title="Computer science">computer science</a> an <i>in situ</i> operation is one that occurs without interrupting the normal state of a system</span><sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>. For example, a file backup may be restored over a running system, without needing to take the system down to perform the restore. In the context of a database, a restore would allow the database system to continue to be available to users while a restore happened. An <i>in situ</i> upgrade would allow an <a href="/wiki/Operating_system" title="Operating system">operating system</a>, <a href="/wiki/Firmware" title="Firmware">firmware</a> or <a href="/wiki/Computer_program" title="Computer program">application</a> to be upgraded while the system was still running, perhaps without the need to reboot it, depending on the sophistication of the system.</p>
<p><span class="citation-needed-content" style="padding-left:0.1em; padding-right:0.1em; color:#595959; border:1px solid #DDD;">An <a href="/wiki/Algorithm" title="Algorithm">algorithm</a> is said to be an <a href="/wiki/In-place_algorithm" title="In-place algorithm"><i>in situ</i> algorithm, or in-place algorithm</a>, if the extra amount of memory required to execute the algorithm is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/O(1)" title="O(1)">O(1)</a></span><sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>, that is, does not exceed a constant no matter how large the input. For example, <a href="/wiki/Heapsort" title="Heapsort">heapsort</a> is an <i>in situ</i> sorting algorithm.</p>
<p>In designing <a href="/wiki/User_interface" title="User interface">user interfaces</a>, <span class="citation-needed-content" style="padding-left:0.1em; padding-right:0.1em; color:#595959; border:1px solid #DDD;">the term <i>in situ</i> means that a particular user action can be performed without going to another window</span><sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>, for example, if a word processor displays an image and allows you to edit the image without launching a separate image editor, this is called <i>in situ editing.</i></p>
<p><span class="citation-needed-content" style="padding-left:0.1em; padding-right:0.1em; color:#595959; border:1px solid #DDD;">In Big Data space, in situ data would mean bringing the computation to where data is located, rather than the other way.</span><sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> like in traditional RDBMS systems where data is moved to computational space.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Design_and_advertising">Design and advertising</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=In_situ&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Design and advertising">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In design and advertising the term typically means the superimposing of theoretical design elements onto photographs of real world locations. This is a pre-visualization tool to aid in illustrating a proof of concept.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Earth_and_atmospheric_sciences">Earth and atmospheric sciences</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=In_situ&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Earth and atmospheric sciences">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Physical_geography" title="Physical geography">physical geography</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Earth_science" title="Earth science">Earth sciences</a>, <i>in situ</i> typically describes natural material or processes prior to transport. For example, <i>in situ</i> is used in relation to the distinction between <a href="/wiki/Weathering" title="Weathering">weathering</a> and <a href="/wiki/Erosion" title="Erosion">erosion</a>, the difference being that erosion requires a transport medium (such as <a href="/wiki/Aeolian_processes" title="Aeolian processes">wind</a>, <a href="/wiki/Glacier" title="Glacier">ice</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Water" title="Water">water</a>), whereas weathering occurs <i>in situ</i>. <a href="/wiki/Geochemistry" title="Geochemistry">Geochemical</a> processes are also often described as occurring to material <i>in situ</i>.</p>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_sciences" title="Atmospheric sciences">atmospheric sciences</a>, <i>in situ</i> refers to obtained through direct contact with the respective subject, such as a <a href="/wiki/Radiosonde" title="Radiosonde">radiosonde</a> measuring a parcel of air or an <a href="/wiki/Anemometer" title="Anemometer">anemometer</a> measuring wind, as opposed to <a href="/wiki/Remote_sensing" title="Remote sensing">remote sensing</a> such as weather <a href="/wiki/Weather_radar" title="Weather radar">radar</a> or <a href="/wiki/Weather_satellite" title="Weather satellite">satellites</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Economics">Economics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=In_situ&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Economics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In economics, <i>in situ</i> is used when referring to the <i>in place</i> storage of a product, usually a natural resource. More generally, it refers to any situation where there is no out-of-pocket cost to store the product so that the only storage cost is the opportunity cost of waiting longer to get your money when the product is eventually sold. Examples of <i>in situ</i> storage would be oil and gas wells, all types of mineral and gem mines, stone quarries, timber that has reached an age where it could be harvested, and agricultural products that do not need a physical storage facility such as hay.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Electrochemistry">Electrochemistry</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=In_situ&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Electrochemistry">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Electrochemistry" title="Electrochemistry">electrochemistry</a>, the phrase in situ refers to performing electrochemical experiments under operating conditions of the electrochemical cell, i.e., under potential control. This is opposed to doing ex situ experiments that are performed under the absence of potential control. Potential control preserves the electrochemical environment essential to maintain the double layer structure intact and the electron transfer reactions occurring at that particular potential in the electrode/electrolyte interphasial region.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Environmental_remediation">Environmental remediation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=In_situ&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Environmental remediation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><i>In situ</i> can refer to where a clean up or <a href="/wiki/Environmental_remediation" title="Environmental remediation">remediation</a> of a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Polluted" title="Polluted">polluted</a> site is performed using and simulating the natural processes in the <a href="/wiki/Soil" title="Soil">soil</a>, contrary to <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ex_situ" title="Ex situ">ex situ</a></i> where contaminated soil is excavated and cleaned elsewhere, off site.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Experimental_physics">Experimental physics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=In_situ&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Experimental physics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Experimental_physics" title="Experimental physics">experimental physics</a> <i>in situ</i> typically refers to a method of data collection or manipulation of a sample without exposure to an external environment. For example, the Si(111) 7x7 surface reconstruction is visible using a <a href="/wiki/Scanning_tunneling_microscope" title="Scanning tunneling microscope">scanning tunneling microscope</a> when it is prepared and analyzed <i>in situ</i>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Experimental_psychology">Experimental psychology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=In_situ&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Experimental psychology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In psychology <a href="/wiki/Experiment" title="Experiment">experiments</a>, <i>in situ</i> typically refers to those experiments done in a field setting as opposed to a laboratory setting.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Gastronomy">Gastronomy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=In_situ&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Gastronomy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Gastronomy" title="Gastronomy">gastronomy</a>, "in situ" refers to the art of cooking with the different resources that are available on the site of the event. Here you are not going to the restaurant, but the restaurant comes to your home.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Law">Law</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=In_situ&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Law">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Legal" title="Legal">legal</a> contexts, <i>in situ</i> is often used for its literal meaning. For example, in <a href="/wiki/Hong_Kong" title="Hong Kong">Hong Kong</a>, "<i>in situ</i> land exchange" involves the government exchanging the original or expired <a href="/wiki/Leasehold_estate" title="Leasehold estate">lease</a> of a piece of land with a new <a href="/wiki/Land_grant" title="Land grant">grant</a> or re-grant with the same piece of land or a portion of that.</p>
<p>In the field of recognition of governments under public international law the term <i>in situ</i> is used to distinguish between an <a href="/wiki/Government_in_exile" title="Government in exile">exiled government</a> and a government with effective control over the territory, i.e. the government <i>in situ</i>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Linguistics">Linguistics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=In_situ&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Linguistics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Linguistics" title="Linguistics">linguistics</a>, specifically <a href="/wiki/Syntax" title="Syntax">syntax</a>, an element may be said to be <i>in situ</i> if it is pronounced in the position where it is interpreted. For example, <a href="/wiki/Question" title="Question">questions</a> in languages such as <a href="/wiki/Chinese_language" title="Chinese language">Chinese</a> have <i>in situ</i> wh-elements, with structures comparable to "John bought what?" with <i>what</i> in the same position in the sentence as the grammatical object would be in its affirmative counterpart (for example, "John bought bread"). An example of an <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">English</a> wh-element that is not <i>in situ</i> (see <a href="/wiki/Wh-movement" title="Wh-movement">wh-movement</a>): "What did John buy?"</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Literature">Literature</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=In_situ&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Literature">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Literature" title="Literature">literature</a> <i>in situ</i> is used to describe a condition. The <a href="/wiki/Rosetta_Stone" title="Rosetta Stone">Rosetta Stone</a>, for example, was originally erected in a courtyard, for public viewing. Most pictures of the famous stone are not <i>in situ</i> pictures of it erected, as it would have been originally. The stone was uncovered as part of building material, within a wall. Its <b>in situ</b> condition today is that it is erected, vertically, on public display at the <a href="/wiki/British_Museum" title="British Museum">British Museum</a> in London, England.</p>

<p>In <a href="/wiki/Oncology" title="Oncology">oncology</a>: for a <a href="/wiki/Carcinoma" title="Carcinoma">carcinoma</a>, <i><a href="/wiki/Carcinoma_in_situ" title="Carcinoma in situ">in situ</a></i> means that <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Malignant" title="Malignant">malignant</a> cells are present as a tumor but have not metastasized, or invaded, beyond the basement membrane of where the tumor was discovered. This can happen anywhere in the body, such as the skin, breast tissue, or lung. This type of tumor can often, depending on where it is located, be removed by <a href="/wiki/Surgery" title="Surgery">surgery</a>.</p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Anatomy" title="Anatomy">anatomy</a>: <i>in situ</i> refers to viewing structures as they appear in normal healthy bodies. For example, one can open up a <a href="/wiki/Cadaver" title="Cadaver">cadaver</a>'s abdominal cavity and view the liver <i>in situ</i> or one can look at an isolated liver that has been removed from the cadaver's body.</p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Nursing" title="Nursing">nursing</a>, "in situ" describes any devices or appliances on the patient's body that remain in their desired and optimal position.</p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Medical_simulation" title="Medical simulation">medical simulation</a>, "in situ" refers to the practice of clinical professionals using high fidelity patient simulators to train for clinical practice in patient care environments, such as wards, operating rooms, and other settings, rather than in dedicated simulation training facilities.</p>
<p>In biomedical, protein nanogels made by the in situ polymerization method provide a versatile platform for storage and release of therapeutic proteins. It has tremendous applications for cancer treatment, vaccination, diagnosis, regenerative medicine, and therapies for loss-of-function genetic diseases.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Mining">Mining</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=In_situ&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Mining">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><i>In situ leaching</i> or <i>in situ recovery</i> refers to the mining technique of injecting water underground to dissolve ore and bringing the uranium-impregnated water to the surface for extraction.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Petroleum_production">Petroleum production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=In_situ&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Petroleum production">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><i>In situ</i> refers to recovery techniques which apply heat or solvents to <a href="/wiki/Heavy_crude_oil" title="Heavy crude oil">heavy crude oil</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bitumen" title="Bitumen">bitumen</a> reservoirs beneath the earth's crust. There are several varieties of <i>in situ</i> techniques, but the ones which work best in the <a href="/wiki/Oil_sands" title="Oil sands">oil sands</a> use heat (steam).</p>
<p>The most common type of <i>in situ</i> petroleum production is referred to as SAGD (<a href="/wiki/Steam-assisted_gravity_drainage" title="Steam-assisted gravity drainage">steam-assisted gravity drainage</a>) this is becoming very popular in the Alberta Oil Sands.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="RF_transmission">RF transmission</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=In_situ&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: RF transmission">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Radio_frequency" title="Radio frequency">radio frequency</a> (RF) transmission systems, <i>in situ</i> is often used to describe the location of various components while the system is in its standard transmission mode, rather than operation in a test mode. For example, if an <i>in situ</i> <a href="/wiki/Wattmeter" title="Wattmeter">wattmeter</a> is used in a commercial broadcast transmission system, the <a href="/wiki/Wattmeter" title="Wattmeter">wattmeter</a> can accurately measure power while the station is "on air".</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Space-related">Space-related</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=In_situ&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Space-related">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Future space exploration or <a href="/wiki/Terraforming" title="Terraforming">terraforming</a> may rely on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/In-situ_resource_utilization" title="In-situ resource utilization">obtaining supplies <i>in situ</i></a>, such as previous plans to power the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Orion_(Constellation_program)" title="Orion (Constellation program)">Orion space vehicle</a> with fuel minable on the moon. The <a href="/wiki/Mars_Direct" title="Mars Direct">Mars Direct</a> mission concept is based primarily on the <i>in situ</i> fuel production using <a href="/wiki/Sabatier_reaction" title="Sabatier reaction">Sabatier reaction</a>.</p>
<p>In the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Space_sciences" title="Space sciences">space sciences</a>, <i>in situ</i> refers to measurements of the particle and field environment that the satellite is embedded in, such as the detection of energetic particles in the <a href="/wiki/Solar_wind" title="Solar wind">solar wind</a>, or magnetic field measurements from a <a href="/wiki/Magnetometer" title="Magnetometer">magnetometer</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Urban_planning">Urban planning</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=In_situ&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: Urban planning">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In urban planning, in-situ upgrading is an approach to and method of upgrading <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Informal_settlement" title="Informal settlement">informal settlements</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Vacuum_technology">Vacuum technology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=In_situ&amp;action=edit&amp;section=26" title="Edit section: Vacuum technology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vacuum_technology" title="Vacuum technology">vacuum technology</a>, <i>in situ</i> baking refers to heating parts of the vacuum system while they are under vacuum in order to drive off volatile substances that may be <a href="/wiki/Absorption_(chemistry)" title="Absorption (chemistry)">absorbed</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Adsorbed" title="Adsorbed">adsorbed</a> on the walls so they cannot cause <a href="/wiki/Outgassing" title="Outgassing">outgassing</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Road_assistance">Road assistance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=In_situ&amp;action=edit&amp;section=27" title="Edit section: Road assistance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The term <i>in situ</i>, used as "repair in situ", means to repair a vehicle at the place where it has a breakdown.</p>
