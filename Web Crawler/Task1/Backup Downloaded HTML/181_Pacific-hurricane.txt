<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Pacific hurricane</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">This article is about tropical cyclones that form in the northern hemisphere east of the <a href="/wiki/International_Date_Line" title="International Date Line">International Date Line</a>. For storms that form in the southern hemisphere, also occasionally known as hurricanes, see <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/South_Pacific_tropical_cyclone_season" title="South Pacific tropical cyclone season">South Pacific tropical cyclone season</a>.</div>

<p>A <b>Pacific hurricane</b> is a mature <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone" title="Tropical cyclone">tropical cyclone</a> that develops within the eastern and central Pacific Ocean to the east of 180°W, north of the equator. For tropical cyclone warning purposes, the northern Pacific is divided into three regions: the eastern (North America to 140°W), central (140°W to 180°), and western (180° to 100°E), while the southern Pacific is divided into 2 sections, the Australian region (90E to 160°E), the southern Pacific basin between (160°E to 120°W).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> Identical phenomena in the western north Pacific are called <a href="/wiki/Typhoon" title="Typhoon">typhoons</a>. This separation between the two basins has a practical convenience, however, as tropical cyclones rarely form in the central north Pacific and few cross the dateline.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pacific_hurricane&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Pacific_hurricane_tracks_1980-2005.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3200" data-file-width="5200" height="135" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2c/Pacific_hurricane_tracks_1980-2005.jpg/220px-Pacific_hurricane_tracks_1980-2005.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2c/Pacific_hurricane_tracks_1980-2005.jpg/330px-Pacific_hurricane_tracks_1980-2005.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2c/Pacific_hurricane_tracks_1980-2005.jpg/440px-Pacific_hurricane_tracks_1980-2005.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Pacific_hurricane_tracks_1980-2005.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Tracks of East Pacific tropical cyclones <span class="nowrap">(1980–2005)</span></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Documentation of Pacific hurricanes dates to the <a href="/wiki/New_Spain" title="New Spain">Spanish colonization of Mexico</a>, when the military and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mission_(Christianity)" title="Mission (Christianity)">missions</a> wrote about "tempestades". In 1730, such accounts indicated an understanding of the storms. After observing the rotating nature of tropical cyclones, meteorologist <a href="/wiki/William_Charles_Redfield" title="William Charles Redfield">William Charles Redfield</a> expanded his study to include storms in the eastern North Pacific Ocean in the middle of the 19th century. Between June and October 1850, Redfield observed five tropical cyclones along "the southwestern coast of North America", along with one in each of the three subsequent years. In 1895, <a href="/wiki/Cleveland_Abbe" title="Cleveland Abbe">Cleveland Abbe</a> reported the presence of many storms between 5° to 15°–<a href="/wiki/Latitude" title="Latitude">N</a> in the eastern Pacific, although many such storms dissipated before affecting the Mexican coast. Two years later, the German Hydrography Office <i>Deutsche Seewarte</i> documented 45 storms from 1832 to 1892 off the west coast of Mexico.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-calif_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-calif-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Despite the documentation of storms in the region, the official position of the <a href="/wiki/National_Weather_Service" title="National Weather Service">United States Weather Bureau</a> denied the existence of such storms. In 1910, the agency reported on global tropical cyclones, noting that "the occurrence of tropical storms is confined to the summer and autumn months of the respective hemispheres and to the western parts of the several oceans." In 1913, the Weather Bureau reinforced their position by excluding Pacific storms among five <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone_basins" title="Tropical cyclone basins">tropical cyclone basins</a>; however, the agency acknowledged the existence of "certain cyclones that have been traced for a relatively short distance along a northwest course... west of Central America."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-calif_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-calif-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>After <a href="/wiki/California" title="California">California</a> became a state and the discovery of gold there in 1848, shipping traffic began increasing steadily in the eastern Pacific. Such activity increased further after the <a href="/wiki/Panama_Canal" title="Panama Canal">Panama Canal</a> opened in 1914, and the shipping lanes moved closer to the coast. By around 1920, Pacific hurricanes were officially recognized due to widespread ship observations, radio service, and a newly created weather network in western Mexico. Within 60 years, further studies of the region's tropical activity indicated that the eastern Pacific was the second most active basin in the world.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-calif_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-calif-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>During the 1920s, a few documents in the <a href="/wiki/Monthly_Weather_Review" title="Monthly Weather Review">Monthly Weather Review</a> reported additional storms within 2,000 mi (3,200 km) off the Mexican coastline.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tingley_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Tingley-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ENPTC_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-ENPTC-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Eastern_and_Central_Pacific_hurricane_database">The Eastern and Central Pacific hurricane database</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pacific_hurricane&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: The Eastern and Central Pacific hurricane database">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Eastern Pacific hurricane best track database was initially compiled on <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_tape" title="Magnetic tape">magnetic tape</a> in 1976 for the seasons between 1949 and 1975, at the NHC to help with the development of two <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone_forecast_model" title="Tropical cyclone forecast model">tropical cyclone forecast models</a>, which required tracks of past cyclones as a base for its predictions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Stat_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Stat-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Comp_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Comp-6">[6]</a></sup> The database was based on records held by the United States Navy and were interpolated from 12 hourly intervals to 6 hourly intervals based on a scheme devised by Hiroshi Akima in 1970.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EPAC_Book_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-EPAC_Book-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Intra_Seasonal_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Intra_Seasonal-8">[8]</a></sup> Initially tracks for the Central Pacific region and tracks for tropical depressions that did not develop into tropical storms or hurricanes were not included within the database.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Intra_Seasonal_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-Intra_Seasonal-8">[8]</a></sup> After the database had been created Arthur Pike of the NHC made some internal adjustments, while in 1980 a review was made by Arnold Court under contract from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_States_National_Weather_Service" title="United States National Weather Service">United States National Weather Service</a> and resulted in additions and/or modifications to 81 tracks in the database.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EPAC_Book_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-EPAC_Book-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1949-1983_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-1949-1983-9">[9]</a></sup> Between 1976–1987, the NHC archived best track data from the <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Pacific_Hurricane_Center" title="Eastern Pacific Hurricane Center">Eastern Pacific Hurricane Center</a> <small>(EPHC)</small>, and in 1982 started including information on Central Pacific tropical storms and hurricanes started to be included in the database based on data from the <a href="/wiki/Joint_Typhoon_Warning_Center" title="Joint Typhoon Warning Center">Joint Typhoon Warning Center</a> and research done by Samuel Shaw of the <a href="/wiki/Central_Pacific_Hurricane_Center" title="Central Pacific Hurricane Center">Central Pacific Hurricane Center</a> <small>(CPHC)</small> in 1981.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Stat_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-Stat-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EPAC_Book_7-2"><a href="#cite_note-EPAC_Book-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>The format of the database was completely revised by the NHC during 1984, so that the format could resemble the Atlantic database before they took over the warning responsibility from the EPHC for the Eastern Pacific during 1988.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EPAC_Book_7-3"><a href="#cite_note-EPAC_Book-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1949-1983_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-1949-1983-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> During 2008 and 2013 several revisions were made to the database to extend tracks in land, based on reports in the <a href="/wiki/Mariners_Weather_Log" title="Mariners Weather Log">Mariners Weather Log</a> and extrapolation of the tracks since the EPHC stopped issuing advisories on systems before they made landfall.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EPAC_Metadata_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-EPAC_Metadata-11">[11]</a></sup> The archives format was significantly changed during 2013 to include non-synoptic best track times, non-developing tropical depressions and wind radii.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HURDAT2_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-HURDAT2-12">[12]</a></sup> During February 2016, the NHC released the <a href="/wiki/1959_Mexico_hurricane" title="1959 Mexico hurricane">1959 Mexico hurricane</a>'s reanalysis, which was the first system to be reassessed, using methods developed for the Atlantic reanalysis process.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Climatology">Climatology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pacific_hurricane&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Climatology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The presence of a semi-permanent <a href="/wiki/High-pressure_area" title="High-pressure area">high-pressure area</a> known as the <a href="/wiki/North_Pacific_High" title="North Pacific High">North Pacific High</a> in the eastern Pacific is a dominant factor against formation of tropical cyclones in the winter, as the Pacific High results in <a href="/wiki/Wind_shear" title="Wind shear">wind shear</a> that causes environmental conditions for tropical cyclone formation to be not conducive. Its effects in the central Pacific basin are usually related to keeping cyclones away from the Hawaiian Islands. Due to westward <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Trade_wind" title="Trade wind">trade winds</a>, hurricanes in the Pacific rarely head eastward, unless recurved by a trough. A second factor preventing tropical cyclones from forming during the winter is the occupation of a semi-permanent <a href="/wiki/Low-pressure_area" title="Low-pressure area">low-pressure area</a> designated the <a href="/wiki/Aleutian_Low" title="Aleutian Low">Aleutian Low</a> between January and April. Its presence over western Canada and the northwestern United States contributes to the area's occurrences of precipitation in that duration. In addition, its effects in the central Pacific near 160° W causes tropical waves that form in the area to drift northward into the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_of_Alaska" title="Gulf of Alaska">Gulf of Alaska</a> and dissipate. The retreat of this low allows the Pacific High to also retreat into the central Pacific, leaving a warm and moist environment in its wake. The <a href="/wiki/Intertropical_Convergence_Zone" title="Intertropical Convergence Zone">Intertropical Convergence Zone</a> comes northward into the East Pacific in mid-May permitting the formation of the earliest <a href="/wiki/Tropical_wave" title="Tropical wave">tropical waves</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Longshore2009_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-Longshore2009-14">[14]</a></sup> coinciding with the start of the eastern Pacific hurricane season on May 15.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-season_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-season-15">[15]</a></sup></p>

</div>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_Pacific_hurricane_seasons" title="List of Pacific hurricane seasons">Hurricane season</a> runs between May 15 and November 30 each year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CPHC_climatology_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-CPHC_climatology-16">[16]</a></sup> These dates encompass the vast majority of tropical cyclone activity in this region.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Regional_Specialized_Meteorological_Center" title="Regional Specialized Meteorological Center">Regional Specialized Meteorological Center</a> for this basin is the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>' <a href="/wiki/National_Hurricane_Center" title="National Hurricane Center">National Hurricane Center</a>. Previous forecasters are the <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Pacific_Hurricane_Center" title="Eastern Pacific Hurricane Center">Eastern Pacific Hurricane Center</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Joint_Hurricane_Warning_Center" title="Joint Hurricane Warning Center">Joint Hurricane Warning Center</a>. The RSMC monitors the eastern Pacific and issues reports, watches and warnings about tropical weather systems and cyclones as defined by the <a href="/wiki/World_Meteorological_Organization" title="World Meteorological Organization">World Meteorological Organization</a>.</p>
<p>This area is, on average, the second-most active basin in the world. There are an average of 16 tropical storms annually, with 9 becoming hurricanes, and 4 becoming major hurricanes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> Tropical cyclones in this region frequently affect mainland <a href="/wiki/Mexico" title="Mexico">Mexico</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Revillagigedo_Islands" title="Revillagigedo Islands">Revillagigedo Islands</a>. Less often, a system will affect the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Continental_United_States" title="Continental United States">Continental United States</a> or <a href="/wiki/Central_America" title="Central America">Central America</a>. Northbound hurricanes typically reduce to tropical storms or dissipate before reaching the United States: there is only one recorded case of a Pacific system reaching <a href="/wiki/California" title="California">California</a> as a hurricane in almost 200 years of observations—the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/1858_San_Diego_Hurricane" title="1858 San Diego Hurricane">1858 San Diego Hurricane</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-aoml_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-aoml-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>Most east Pacific hurricanes originate from a tropical wave that drifts westward across the intertropical convergence zone, and across northern parts of South America. Once it reaches the Pacific, a surface low begins to develop, however, with only little or no convection. After reaching the Pacific, it starts to move north-westward and eventually west. By that time, it develops convection and thunderstorm activity from the warm ocean temperatures but remains disorganized. Once the tropical wave becomes organized, it becomes a tropical depression. Formation usually occurs from south of the Gulf of Tehuantepec to south of Baja California with a more westerly location earlier in the season. In the eastern Pacific, development is more centered than anywhere else. If wind shear is low, a tropical cyclone can undergo <a href="/wiki/Rapid_intensification" title="Rapid intensification">rapid intensification</a> as a result of very warm oceans, becoming a major hurricane. Tropical cyclones weaken once they reach unfavorable areas for a tropical cyclone formation. Their remnants sometimes reach Hawaii and cause showers there.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>There are a few types of Pacific hurricane tracks: one is a westerly track, another moves north-westward along Baja California and another moves north. Sometimes storms can move north-east either across Central America or mainland Mexico and possibly enter the Caribbean Sea becoming a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/North_Atlantic_basin_tropical_cyclone" title="North Atlantic basin tropical cyclone">North Atlantic basin tropical cyclone</a>, but these are rare.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Central_Pacific">Central Pacific</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pacific_hurricane&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Central Pacific">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Hurricane season runs from June 1 to November 30, with a strong peak in August and September. However, tropical cyclones have formed outside those dates.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CPHC_climatology_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-CPHC_climatology-16">[16]</a></sup> Should a tropical cyclone enter the central north Pacific from the western north Pacific, where they occur year-round, or from the eastern north Pacific, where the season starts in May, it is not known if such a system will be considered out of season or not.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Central_Pacific_Hurricane_Center" title="Central Pacific Hurricane Center">Central Pacific Hurricane Center</a> is the RSMC for this basin and monitors the storms that develop or move into the defined area of responsibility. A previous forecaster was the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Joint_Hurricane_Warning_Center" title="Joint Hurricane Warning Center">Joint Hurricane Warning Center</a>.</p>
<p>Central Pacific hurricanes are rare and on average 3 or 4 storms form or move in this area per year. Most often, storms that occur in the area are weak and often decline in strength upon entry. The only land masses impacted by tropical cyclones in this region are <a href="/wiki/Hawaii" title="Hawaii">Hawaii</a> and <a href="/wiki/Johnston_Atoll" title="Johnston Atoll">Johnston Atoll</a>. Due to the small size of the islands in relation to the Pacific Ocean, direct hits and landfalls are rare.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Steering_factors">Steering factors</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pacific_hurricane&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Steering factors">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone_track_forecasting" title="Tropical cyclone track forecasting">Tropical cyclone track forecasting</a> and <a href="/wiki/Subtropical_ridge" title="Subtropical ridge">Subtropical ridge</a></div>
<p>Hurricanes in the Eastern Pacific tend to move westward out to sea, harming no land. However, hurricanes can recurve to the north or northeast, hitting Central America or Mexico early and late in the hurricane season.</p>
