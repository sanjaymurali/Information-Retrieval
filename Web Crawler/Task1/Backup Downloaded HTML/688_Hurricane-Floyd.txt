<p><b>Hurricane Floyd</b> was a very powerful <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cape_Verde-type_hurricane" title="Cape Verde-type hurricane">Cape Verde-type hurricane</a> which struck the east coast of the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>. It was the sixth <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_named_tropical_cyclones" title="List of named tropical cyclones">named storm</a>, fourth <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone" title="Tropical cyclone">hurricane</a>, and third major hurricane in the <a href="/wiki/1999_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="1999 Atlantic hurricane season">1999 Atlantic hurricane season</a>. Floyd triggered the fourth largest evacuation in <a href="/wiki/History_of_the_United_States" title="History of the United States">US history</a> (behind <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Irma" title="Hurricane Irma">Hurricane Irma</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Gustav" title="Hurricane Gustav">Hurricane Gustav</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Rita" title="Hurricane Rita">Hurricane Rita</a>) when 2.6 million coastal residents of five states were ordered from their homes as it approached. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cape_Verde-type_hurricane" title="Cape Verde-type hurricane">Cape Verde-type hurricane</a> formed off the coast of <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a> and lasted from September 7 to September 19, peaking in strength as a very strong Category 4 hurricane—just 2 mph short of the highest possible rating on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saffir%E2%80%93Simpson_Hurricane_Scale" title="Saffir–Simpson Hurricane Scale">Saffir–Simpson Hurricane Scale</a>. It was among the largest <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/North_Atlantic_tropical_cyclone" title="North Atlantic tropical cyclone">Atlantic hurricanes</a> of its strength ever recorded.</p>
<p>Floyd was once forecast to strike <a href="/wiki/Florida" title="Florida">Florida</a>, but turned away. Instead, Floyd struck <a href="/wiki/The_Bahamas" title="The Bahamas">The Bahamas</a> at peak strength, causing heavy damage. It then paralleled the <a href="/wiki/East_Coast_of_the_United_States" title="East Coast of the United States">East Coast of the United States</a>, causing massive evacuations and costly preparations from Florida through the Mid-Atlantic states. The storm weakened significantly, however, before striking the <a href="/wiki/Cape_Fear_(region)" title="Cape Fear (region)">Cape Fear</a> region, <a href="/wiki/North_Carolina" title="North Carolina">North Carolina</a> as a very strong Category 2 hurricane, and caused further damage as it traveled up the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mid-Atlantic_States" title="Mid-Atlantic States">Mid-Atlantic</a> region and into <a href="/wiki/New_England" title="New England">New England</a>.</p>
<p>The hurricane produced torrential rainfall in <a href="/wiki/Eastern_North_Carolina" title="Eastern North Carolina">Eastern North Carolina</a>, adding more rain to an area hit by <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Dennis_(1999)" title="Hurricane Dennis (1999)">Hurricane Dennis</a> just weeks earlier. The rains caused widespread flooding over a period of several weeks; nearly every river basin in the eastern part of the state exceeded 500-year flood levels. In total, Floyd was responsible for 57 fatalities and $6.9 billion (1999 <a href="/wiki/United_States_dollar" title="United States dollar">USD</a>; $9.92 billion 2017 USD) in damage. Due to the destruction, the <a href="/wiki/World_Meteorological_Organization" title="World Meteorological Organization">World Meteorological Organization</a> <a href="/wiki/List_of_retired_Atlantic_hurricane_names" title="List of retired Atlantic hurricane names">retired</a> the name <i>Floyd</i>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Meteorological_history">Meteorological history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hurricane_Floyd&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Meteorological history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Floyd_1999_track.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1022" data-file-width="1024" height="220" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5e/Floyd_1999_track.png/220px-Floyd_1999_track.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5e/Floyd_1999_track.png/330px-Floyd_1999_track.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5e/Floyd_1999_track.png/440px-Floyd_1999_track.png 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Floyd_1999_track.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Map plotting the track and intensity of the storm, according to the <a href="/wiki/Saffir%E2%80%93Simpson_scale" title="Saffir–Simpson scale">Saffir–Simpson scale</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Floyd originated as a <a href="/wiki/Tropical_wave" title="Tropical wave">tropical wave</a> that exited the coast of <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a> on September 2. It moved steadily westward and remained disorganized and devoid of deep convection until September 7, when a curved band of deep convection <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclogenesis" title="Tropical cyclogenesis">developed</a> over the center in response to a developing anticyclone. At this point, the <a href="/wiki/National_Hurricane_Center" title="National Hurricane Center">National Hurricane Center</a> designated it as Tropical Depression Eight, while it was approximately 1,000 mi (1,600 km) east of the <a href="/wiki/Lesser_Antilles" title="Lesser Antilles">Lesser Antilles</a>. A strong <a href="/wiki/Ridge_(meteorology)" title="Ridge (meteorology)">ridge</a> of high pressure to its north forced the developing tropical cyclone westward over warmer waters, allowing it to strengthen to Tropical Storm Floyd on September 8.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tcr_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-tcr-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Although a large storm, Floyd initially lacked a well-defined inner core, resulting in slow strengthening and preventing rapid intensification. On September 10 it organized enough to reach hurricane status, and Floyd approached major hurricane strength on September 11 while north of the <a href="/wiki/Leeward_Islands" title="Leeward Islands">Leeward Islands</a>. The central Atlantic <a href="/wiki/Tropical_upper_tropospheric_trough" title="Tropical upper tropospheric trough">upper tropospheric trough</a>, along with an <a href="/wiki/Cold-core_low" title="Cold-core low">upper-level low</a> in the eastern <a href="/wiki/Caribbean_Sea" title="Caribbean Sea">Caribbean Sea</a>, produced vertical <a href="/wiki/Wind_shear" title="Wind shear">wind shear</a> over the hurricane and caused its winds to weaken to 85 mph (137 km/h). A turn to the west, caused by building of high pressures, was followed by a period of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rapid_deepening" title="Rapid deepening">rapid intensification</a>: in 24 hours <a href="/wiki/Maximum_sustained_wind" title="Maximum sustained wind">maximum sustained winds</a> increased from 110 to 155 mph (177 to 249 km/h),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[nb 1]</a></sup> while the <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_pressure" title="Atmospheric pressure">pressure</a> dropped to 921 <a href="/wiki/Bar_(unit)" title="Bar (unit)">mb</a> (921 <a href="/wiki/Pascal_(unit)" title="Pascal (unit)">hPa</a>; 27.2 <a href="/wiki/Inch_of_mercury" title="Inch of mercury">inHg</a>) by morning on September 13, the third lowest <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_pressure" title="Atmospheric pressure">pressure</a> for a hurricane <i>not</i> to reach Category 5 intensity in the <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Ocean" title="Atlantic Ocean">Atlantic Ocean</a>—only <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Opal" title="Hurricane Opal">Hurricane Opal</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Gloria" title="Hurricane Gloria">Hurricane Gloria</a> had lower pressures than Floyd.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HURDAT_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-HURDAT-3">[2]</a></sup> One contributor to the intensification was the high oceanic heat content along the storm's path.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tcr_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-tcr-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Hurricane Floyd remained just below Category 5 status on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saffir%E2%80%93Simpson_Hurricane_Scale" title="Saffir–Simpson Hurricane Scale">Saffir–Simpson Hurricane Scale</a> for 12 hours while crossing <a href="/wiki/The_Bahamas" title="The Bahamas">The Bahamas</a>, making <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Landfall_(meteorology)" title="Landfall (meteorology)">landfalls</a> on <a href="/wiki/Eleuthera" title="Eleuthera">Eleuthera</a> and <a href="/wiki/Abaco_Islands" title="Abaco Islands">Abaco</a> islands,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tcr_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-tcr-1">[1]</a></sup> before an <a href="/wiki/Eyewall_replacement_cycle" title="Eyewall replacement cycle">eyewall replacement cycle</a> weakened it to a Category 3 hurricane with winds of 120 mph (190 km/h). The new, larger eyewall contracted slightly, and the hurricane briefly re-intensified to Category 4 status. A strong mid- to upper-level trough eroded the western portion of the high-pressure ridge, steering Floyd to the northwest. It paralleled the eastern <a href="/wiki/Florida" title="Florida">Florida</a> coast 110 miles (180 km) off shore, and steadily weakened because of entrainment of dry air and upper-level shear.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tcr_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-tcr-1">[1]</a></sup> The storm remained extremely large, however; at its peak, tropical storm-force winds spanned a diameter of 580 mi (930 km), making Floyd one of the largest Atlantic hurricanes of its intensity ever recorded.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Legacy_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Legacy-4">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Floyd accelerated to the north and northeast, and weakened greatly to a Category 2 hurricane. It made <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Landfall_(meteorology)" title="Landfall (meteorology)">landfall</a> in <a href="/wiki/Cape_Fear_(region)" title="Cape Fear (region)">Cape Fear</a>, <a href="/wiki/North_Carolina" title="North Carolina">North Carolina</a> with winds of 105 mph (169 km/h) on September 16. After crossing over North Carolina and southeastern <a href="/wiki/Virginia" title="Virginia">Virginia</a>, it briefly re-entered the western <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Ocean" title="Atlantic Ocean">Atlantic Ocean</a> before making a second landfall on <a href="/wiki/Jones_Beach_Island" title="Jones Beach Island">Jones Beach</a>, <a href="/wiki/Long_Island" title="Long Island">Long Island</a> on September 17. The storm gradually lost its tropical characteristics due to an approaching frontal zone and became <a href="/wiki/Extratropical_cyclone" title="Extratropical cyclone">extratropical</a> over southern <a href="/wiki/Maine" title="Maine">Maine</a> late on September 17. The extratropical storm continued to the northeast, and after passing over the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Canadian_Maritimes" title="Canadian Maritimes">Canadian Maritimes</a>, it was absorbed by a cold front to the east of <a href="/wiki/Newfoundland_and_Labrador" title="Newfoundland and Labrador">Newfoundland</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tcr_1-4"><a href="#cite_note-tcr-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<div style="clear:both;"></div>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Preparations">Preparations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hurricane_Floyd&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Preparations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Although Floyd's track prediction was above average while out at sea, the forecasts as it approached the coastline were merely average compared to forecasts from the previous ten years. The official forecasts did not predict Floyd's northward track nor its significant weakening before landfall.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[4]</a></sup> Nearly all of the <a href="/wiki/East_Coast_of_the_United_States" title="East Coast of the United States">East Coast</a>, from <a href="/wiki/Florida_City,_Florida" title="Florida City, Florida">Florida City, Florida</a>, to <a href="/wiki/Plymouth,_Massachusetts" title="Plymouth, Massachusetts">Plymouth, Massachusetts</a>, was under a Hurricane Warning at some point; however, only a fraction of this area actually received hurricane-force winds. The last time such widespread hurricane warnings occurred was during <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Donna" title="Hurricane Donna">Hurricane Donna</a> in 1960, although the warnings for that storm were accurate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tcr_1-5"><a href="#cite_note-tcr-1">[1]</a></sup></p>

<p>Initial fears were of a direct hit as a large Category 4 hurricane in Florida, potentially costlier and deadlier than <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Andrew" title="Hurricane Andrew">Hurricane Andrew</a> had been in 1992. In preparation for a potentially catastrophic landfall, more than one million Florida residents were told to evacuate, of which 272,000 were in <a href="/wiki/Miami-Dade_County,_Florida" title="Miami-Dade County, Florida">Miami-Dade County</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[5]</a></sup> U.S. President <a href="/wiki/Bill_Clinton" title="Bill Clinton">Bill Clinton</a> declared a federal <a href="/wiki/State_of_emergency" title="State of emergency">state of emergency</a> in both <a href="/wiki/Florida" title="Florida">Florida</a> and <a href="/wiki/Georgia_(U.S._state)" title="Georgia (U.S. state)">Georgia</a> in anticipation of the storm's approach.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[6]</a></sup> As the storm turned to the north, more people were evacuated as a progressively larger area was threatened. The massive storm caused what was at the time the largest peacetime <a href="/wiki/Emergency_evacuation" title="Emergency evacuation">evacuation</a> ever in the U.S., with around 2.6 million evacuating coastal areas in Florida, <a href="/wiki/Georgia_(U.S._state)" title="Georgia (U.S. state)">Georgia</a>, and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carolinas" title="Carolinas">Carolinas</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-extremes0999_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-extremes0999-8">[7]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Floyd-_Traffic.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="205" data-file-width="250" height="180" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/87/Floyd-_Traffic.jpg/220px-Floyd-_Traffic.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/8/87/Floyd-_Traffic.jpg 1.5x" width="220"/></a>

Evacuation on <a href="/wiki/Interstate_26" title="Interstate 26">Interstate 26</a></div>

</div>
<p>With the storm predicted to hit near <a href="/wiki/Cape_Canaveral" title="Cape Canaveral">Cape Canaveral</a> with winds of over 140 mph (230 km/h), all but 80 of <a href="/wiki/Kennedy_Space_Center" title="Kennedy Space Center">Kennedy Space Center</a>'s 12,500-person workforce were evacuated. The <a href="/wiki/Hangar" title="Hangar">hangars</a> that house three space shuttles can withstand winds of only 105 mph (169 km/h), and a direct hit could have resulted in potentially billions of dollars in damage of space equipment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[8]</a></sup> In the theoretical scenario, the damage would be caused by water, always a potential problem in an area only nine feet above sea level. If water entered the facility, it would damage the electronics as well as requiring a complete inspection of all hardware.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[9]</a></sup> When Floyd actually passed by the area, <a href="/wiki/Kennedy_Space_Center" title="Kennedy Space Center">Kennedy Space Center</a> only reported light winds with minor water intrusion. Damage was minor overall, and was repaired easily.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>A <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone_warnings_and_watches" title="Tropical cyclone warnings and watches">hurricane warning</a> was issued for the North Carolina coastline 27 hours prior to landfall. However, due to the size of the storm, initial forecasts predicted nearly all of the state would be affected in one form or another. School systems and businesses as far west as <a href="/wiki/Asheville,_North_Carolina" title="Asheville, North Carolina">Asheville</a> shut down for the day landfall was predicted, but, as it turned out, only the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atlantic_Coastal_Plain" title="Atlantic Coastal Plain">Coastal Plain</a> sustained significant damage; much of the state west of <a href="/wiki/Raleigh,_North_Carolina" title="Raleigh, North Carolina">Raleigh</a> escaped unscathed. In <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York City</a>, public schools were closed on September 16, 1999, the day Floyd hit the area. This was a rare decision by the city, as <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York City</a> public schools close on average once every few years. Before Floyd, the last time New York City closed its schools was for the <a href="/wiki/North_American_blizzard_of_1996" title="North American blizzard of 1996">Blizzard of 1996</a>. After Floyd, the next time its public schools would close was due to a blizzard on March 5, 2001.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[11]</a></sup> Walt Disney World also closed for the first time in its history due to the storm.</p>

<p>With a death toll of 65, Hurricane Floyd was the deadliest <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> hurricane since <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Agnes" title="Hurricane Agnes">Hurricane Agnes</a> in 1972. The storm also was one of the costliest in the nation's history, amounting to $6.9 billion (1999 USD). Most of the deaths and damage were from inland, freshwater flooding in eastern North Carolina.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Bahamas">Bahamas</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hurricane_Floyd&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Bahamas">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Hurricane Floyd lashed the Bahamas with winds of 155 mph (249 km/h) and waves up to 50 ft (15 m) in height.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-extremes0999_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-extremes0999-8">[7]</a></sup> A 20 ft (6.10 m) storm surge inundated many islands with over five ft (1.5 m) of water throughout.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-baha_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-baha-13">[12]</a></sup> The wind and waves toppled power and communication lines, severely disrupting electricity and telephone services for days. Damage was greatest at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Abaco_Island" title="Abaco Island">Abaco Island</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cat_Island_(Bahamas)" title="Cat Island (Bahamas)">Cat Island</a>, <a href="/wiki/San_Salvador_Island" title="San Salvador Island">San Salvador Island</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eleuthera_Island" title="Eleuthera Island">Eleuthera Island</a>, where Floyd uprooted trees and destroyed a significant number of houses.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[13]</a></sup> Numerous restaurants, hotels, shops, and homes were devastated, severely limiting in the recovery period tourism on which many rely for economic well-being.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[14]</a></sup> Damaged water systems left tens of thousands across the archipelago without water, electricity, or food. Despite the damage, however, few deaths were reported, as only one person drowned in <a href="/wiki/Freeport,_Bahamas" title="Freeport, Bahamas">Freeport</a>, and there were few injuries reported.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-baha_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-baha-13">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>To help the affected citizens, the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Bahamas_Red_Cross_Society&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Bahamas Red Cross Society (page does not exist)">Bahamas Red Cross Society</a> opened 41 shelters, though within one week many returned home.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[15]</a></sup> The Bahamas required $435,000 (1999 USD; $625 thousand 2017 USD) in aid following the storm, much of it in food parcels.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-baha_13-2"><a href="#cite_note-baha-13">[12]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Inter-American_Development_Bank" title="Inter-American Development Bank">Inter-American Development Bank</a> loaned $21 million (1999 USD; $30.2 million 2017 USD) to the archipelago to restore bridges, roads, seawalls, docks, and other building projects in the aftermath of the hurricane.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[16]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Southeastern_United_States">Southeastern United States</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hurricane_Floyd&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Southeastern United States">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Although over a million <a href="/wiki/Florida" title="Florida">Florida</a> residents were evacuated, the state was only lightly affected by Hurricane Floyd. Strong waves and tropical storm-force winds resulted in significant beach erosion on the east coast of Florida, as well as the loss of some boat piers in <a href="/wiki/Brevard_County,_Florida" title="Brevard County, Florida">Brevard</a> and <a href="/wiki/Volusia_County,_Florida" title="Volusia County, Florida">Volusia Counties</a>. Moderate rainfall occurred along the coastline, amounting to a maximum of 3.2 in (81 mm) in <a href="/wiki/Sanford,_Florida" title="Sanford, Florida">Sanford</a>. Floyd's winds downed hundreds of trees, damaging 357 houses. Damage in northeast Florida amounted to $46.5 million (1999 USD; $66.9 million 2017 USD), a fraction of what was originally expected.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tcr_1-6"><a href="#cite_note-tcr-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>The states of <a href="/wiki/Georgia_(U.S._state)" title="Georgia (U.S. state)">Georgia</a> and <a href="/wiki/South_Carolina" title="South Carolina">South Carolina</a>, although threatened by the storm, were largely spared when it turned northward. Some areas of eastern South Carolina reported up to 16 in (410 mm) of rain.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tcr_1-7"><a href="#cite_note-tcr-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="North_Carolina">North Carolina</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hurricane_Floyd&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: North Carolina">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>North Carolina received the brunt of the storm's destruction. In all, Hurricane Floyd caused 51 fatalities in North Carolina, much of them from freshwater flooding, as well as billions in damage.</p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Floyd1999NCLandfall.gif"><img alt="An animated map showing rain moving over North Carolina" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="455" data-file-width="773" height="129" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/24/Floyd1999NCLandfall.gif/220px-Floyd1999NCLandfall.gif" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/24/Floyd1999NCLandfall.gif/330px-Floyd1999NCLandfall.gif 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/24/Floyd1999NCLandfall.gif/440px-Floyd1999NCLandfall.gif 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Floyd1999NCLandfall.gif" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Radar imagery of Hurricane Floyd making landfall in North Carolina.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The storm surge from the large hurricane amounted to 9–10 ft (2.7–3.0 m) along the southeastern portion of the state. The hurricane also spawned numerous tornadoes, most of which caused only minor damage. Damage to power lines left over 500,000 customers without electricity at some point during the storm's passage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tcr_1-8"><a href="#cite_note-tcr-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Just weeks prior to Floyd hitting, <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Dennis_(1999)" title="Hurricane Dennis (1999)">Hurricane Dennis</a> brought up to 15 in (380 mm) of rain to southeastern North Carolina. When Hurricane Floyd moved across the state in early September, it produced torrential rainfall, amounting to a maximum of 19.06 in (484 mm) in <a href="/wiki/Wilmington,_North_Carolina" title="Wilmington, North Carolina">Wilmington</a>. Though it moved quickly, the extreme rainfall was due to Floyd's interaction with an approaching cold front across the area.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tcr_1-9"><a href="#cite_note-tcr-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Extensive flooding, especially along NC Hwy 91 and the White Oak Loop neighborhood, led to overflowing rivers; nearly every river basin in eastern North Carolina reached 500 year or greater flood levels.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-usgs-flooding_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-usgs-flooding-18">[17]</a></sup> Most localized flooding happened overnight; Floyd dropped nearly 17 in (430 mm) of rain during the hours of its passage and many residents were not aware of the flooding until the water came into their homes. The National Guard and the Coast Guard performed nearly 1700 fresh water rescues of people trapped on the roofs of their homes due to the rapid rise of the water. By contrast, many of the worst affected areas did not reach peak flood levels for several weeks after the storm, as the water accumulated in rivers and moved downstream (see flood graphic at right).</p>
<p>The passage of <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Irene_(1999)" title="Hurricane Irene (1999)">Hurricane Irene</a> four weeks later contributed an additional six in (150 mm) of rain over the still-saturated area, causing further flooding.</p>

<div class="legend" style="-webkit-column-break-inside: avoid;page-break-inside: avoid;break-inside: avoid-column"><span class="legend-color" style="display:inline-block; width:1.5em; height:1.5em; margin:1px 0; border:1px solid black; background-color: #ff0630; color:black; font-size:100%; text-align:center;"> </span> as of September 23</div>
<div class="legend" style="-webkit-column-break-inside: avoid;page-break-inside: avoid;break-inside: avoid-column"><span class="legend-color" style="display:inline-block; width:1.5em; height:1.5em; margin:1px 0; border:1px solid black; background-color: #67d364; color:black; font-size:100%; text-align:center;"> </span> as of September 30</div>
</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Tar_River" title="Tar River">Tar River</a> suffered the worst flooding, exceeding 500-year flood levels along its lower stretches; it crested 24 ft (7.3 m) above flood stage. Flooding began in <a href="/wiki/Rocky_Mount,_North_Carolina" title="Rocky Mount, North Carolina">Rocky Mount</a>, as much as 30% of which was underwater for several days. In <a href="/wiki/Tarboro,_North_Carolina" title="Tarboro, North Carolina">Tarboro</a>, much of the downtown was under several feet of water.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[18]</a></sup> Nearby, the town of <a href="/wiki/Princeville,_North_Carolina" title="Princeville, North Carolina">Princeville</a> was largely destroyed when the waters of the Tar poured over the town's levee, covering the town with over 20 ft (6.1 m) of floodwater for ten days.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[19]</a></sup> Further downstream, <a href="/wiki/Greenville,_North_Carolina" title="Greenville, North Carolina">Greenville</a> suffered very heavy flooding; damages in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pitt_County" title="Pitt County">Pitt County</a> alone were estimated at $1.6 billion (1999 USD, $1.87 billion 2006 USD).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-extremes0999_8-2"><a href="#cite_note-extremes0999-8">[7]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Washington,_North_Carolina" title="Washington, North Carolina">Washington</a>, where the peak flood level was observed, was likewise devastated. Some residents in Greenville had to swim six feet underwater to reach the front doors of their homes and apartments.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[20]</a></sup> Due to the heavy flooding in downtown Greenville, the <a href="/wiki/East_Carolina_Pirates" title="East Carolina Pirates">East Carolina Pirates</a> were forced to relocate their football game against #9 <a href="/wiki/University_of_Miami" title="University of Miami">Miami</a> to <a href="/wiki/NC_State_Wolfpack" title="NC State Wolfpack">N.C. State</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Carter%E2%80%93Finley_Stadium" title="Carter–Finley Stadium">Carter–Finley Stadium</a> in Raleigh, where they beat the Hurricanes 27-23.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Neuse_River" title="Neuse River">Neuse River</a>, <a href="/wiki/Roanoke_River" title="Roanoke River">Roanoke River</a>, <a href="/wiki/Waccamaw_River" title="Waccamaw River">Waccamaw River</a>, and <a href="/wiki/New_River_(North_Carolina)" title="New River (North Carolina)">New River</a> exceeded 500-year flood levels, although damage was lower in these areas (compared to the Tar River) because of lower population densities. Because most of the <a href="/wiki/Cape_Fear_River" title="Cape Fear River">Cape Fear River</a> basin was west of the peak rainfall areas, the city of <a href="/wiki/Wilmington,_North_Carolina" title="Wilmington, North Carolina">Wilmington</a> was spared the worst flooding despite having the highest localized rainfall; however, the Northeast Cape Fear River (a tributary) did exceed 500-year flood levels. Of the state's eastern rivers, only the <a href="/wiki/Lumber_River" title="Lumber River">Lumber River</a> escaped catastrophic flooding.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-usgs-flooding_18-1"><a href="#cite_note-usgs-flooding-18">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>Rainfall and strong winds affected many homes across the state, destroying 7,000, leaving 17,000 uninhabitable, and damaging 56,000. Ten thousand people resided in temporary shelters following the storm. The extensive flooding resulted in significant crop damage. As quoted by North Carolina Secretary of Health and Human Services H. David Bruton, "Nothing since the Civil War has been as destructive to families here. The recovery process will be much longer than the water-going-down process."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-extremes0999_8-3"><a href="#cite_note-extremes0999-8">[7]</a></sup> Around 31,000 jobs were lost from over 60,000 businesses through the storm, causing nearly $4 billion (1999 USD, $4.7 billion 2006 USD) in lost business revenue.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ncjobs_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-ncjobs-23">[22]</a></sup> In much of the affected area, officials urged people to either boil water or buy bottled water during Floyd's aftermath.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cnnflooding_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-cnnflooding-24">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p>In contrast to the problems eastern North Carolina experienced, much of the western portion of the state remained under a severe drought.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-extremes0999_8-4"><a href="#cite_note-extremes0999-8">[7]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Virginia">Virginia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hurricane_Floyd&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Virginia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div style="clear:right;"></div>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Floydfranklin.jpg"><img alt="A flooded area, with houses in the background" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="150" data-file-width="200" height="150" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/4/4b/Floydfranklin.jpg" width="200"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Floydfranklin.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>

<p>As in North Carolina, Floyd produced torrential rainfall in the most eastern portions of <a href="/wiki/Virginia" title="Virginia">Virginia</a>, amounting to a peak of 16.57 in (421 mm) in <a href="/wiki/Newport_News,_Virginia" title="Newport News, Virginia">Newport News</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tcr_1-10"><a href="#cite_note-tcr-1">[1]</a></sup> The rainfall led to overflowing rivers in the <a href="/wiki/Chowan_River" title="Chowan River">Chowan River</a> Basin, some of which exceeded 500-year flood levels.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-usgs-flooding_18-2"><a href="#cite_note-usgs-flooding-18">[17]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Blackwater_River_(Virginia)" title="Blackwater River (Virginia)">Blackwater River</a> reached 100-year flood levels and flooded <a href="/wiki/Franklin,_Virginia" title="Franklin, Virginia">Franklin</a> with 12 ft (3.7 m) of water. Extensive road damage occurred there, isolating the area from the rest of the state. Some 182 businesses and 150 houses were underwater in Franklin from the worst flooding in 60 years. In addition, two dams along the <a href="/wiki/Rappahannock_River" title="Rappahannock River">Rappahannock River</a> burst from the extreme flooding. Throughout all of Virginia, Floyd damaged 9,250 houses, killed three people, and caused $101 million in damage (1999 USD; $145 million 2017 USD).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[24]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mid-Atlantic">Mid-Atlantic</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hurricane_Floyd&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Mid-Atlantic">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Effects_of_Hurricane_Floyd_in_New_York" title="Effects of Hurricane Floyd in New York">Effects of Hurricane Floyd in New York</a> and <a href="/wiki/Effects_of_Hurricane_Floyd_in_Pennsylvania" title="Effects of Hurricane Floyd in Pennsylvania">Effects of Hurricane Floyd in Pennsylvania</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:177px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Floyd_1999_rainfall.gif"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="907" data-file-width="635" height="250" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3b/Floyd_1999_rainfall.gif/175px-Floyd_1999_rainfall.gif" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3b/Floyd_1999_rainfall.gif/263px-Floyd_1999_rainfall.gif 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3b/Floyd_1999_rainfall.gif/350px-Floyd_1999_rainfall.gif 2x" width="175"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Floyd_1999_rainfall.gif" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Rainfall from Floyd in the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>By the time Floyd hit the shore, it was significantly weaker than it was at sea, due to the collapsing of its concentric eyes. This had little effect on the inland damage caused by the storm, however, and Floyd produced torrential rains and high winds throughout the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mid-Atlantic_States" title="Mid-Atlantic States">Mid-Atlantic</a> as far north as <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York City</a> and <a href="/wiki/Long_Island" title="Long Island">Long Island</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tcr_1-11"><a href="#cite_note-tcr-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Chestertown,_Maryland" title="Chestertown, Maryland">Chestertown, Maryland</a>, reported a maximum rainfall total of 14 in (360 mm), with other locales reporting similar values.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tcr_1-12"><a href="#cite_note-tcr-1">[1]</a></sup> Extreme river flooding caused moderate damage to bridges and roads, resulting in a damage toll of $7.9 million (1999 USD; $11.4 million 2017 USD) throughout the state.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[25]</a></sup> In addition, over 250,000 residents were without electricity because of high winds blowing down power lines.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-extremes0999_8-5"><a href="#cite_note-extremes0999-8">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>Rainfall amounts peaked at 13.34 in (339 mm) in <a href="/wiki/Somerville,_New_Jersey" title="Somerville, New Jersey">Somerville, New Jersey</a>, and 12.36 in (314 mm) in <a href="/wiki/Kent_County,_Delaware" title="Kent County, Delaware">Vernon, Delaware</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Raritan_River" title="Raritan River">Raritan River</a> basin experienced record flooding as a result of Floyd's heavy rains, 4.5 ft (1.4 m) higher than the previous record flood crest.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-boundbrook_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-boundbrook-27">[26]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Bound_Brook,_New_Jersey" title="Bound Brook, New Jersey">Bound Brook, New Jersey</a>, and neighboring South Bound Brook, were especially hard hit by a record flooding event: a 42-foot (12.8 m) flood crest,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[27]</a></sup> 14 ft (4.3 m) above flood stage, sent 12 ft (3.7 m) of water on Main Street and drowned three people.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-boundbrook_27-1"><a href="#cite_note-boundbrook-27">[26]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-raritan_river_crests_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-raritan_river_crests-29">[28]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Manville,_New_Jersey" title="Manville, New Jersey">Manville, New Jersey</a> was hit nearly as hard, with record-breaking floods coming from the Raritan River and the nearby <a href="/wiki/Millstone_River" title="Millstone River">Millstone River</a>, which join in Manville. Manville's flooding was also exacerbated by the Delaware-Raritan Canal, a section of which runs parallel to the Millstone River and, for a stretch, are only separated by several feet (a runner's path). Princeton University in Princeton, NJ, for several days declared municipal tap water unsafe to drink, advised students in dorms not to shower, and provided bottled drinking water.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Rochelle_Park,_New_Jersey" title="Rochelle Park, New Jersey">Rochelle Park, New Jersey</a> hub of <a href="/wiki/Electronic_Data_Systems" title="Electronic Data Systems">Electronic Data Systems</a> was inundated by the nearby <a href="/wiki/Saddle_River_(Passaic_River)" title="Saddle River (Passaic River)">Saddle River</a>, disrupting service to as many as 8,000 ATMs across the United States.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rochelle_park_eds_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-rochelle_park_eds-30">[29]</a></sup> Flooding in an adjoining <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bell_Atlantic" title="Bell Atlantic">Bell Atlantic</a> switching facility cut off phone service to one million customers in the area.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rochelle_park_bell_atlantic_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-rochelle_park_bell_atlantic-31">[30]</a></sup></p>

<p>Still a large tropical cyclone when it hit the Mid Atlantic states, Floyd produced a strong storm surge, peaking at 2.8 ft (0.85 m) with a storm tide of 9.34 ft (2.85 m) in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Philadelphia,_Pennsylvania" title="Philadelphia, Pennsylvania">Philadelphia</a> with effects felt in parts of northern <a href="/wiki/Pennsylvania" title="Pennsylvania">Pennsylvania</a> including the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wilkes-Barre/Scranton_metro_area" title="Wilkes-Barre/Scranton metro area">Wilkes-Barre/Scranton metro area</a>. Some 1,260,000 citizens across the three states lost power because of the storm, and flooding left many in Pennsylvania homeless. In <a href="/wiki/New_York_(state)" title="New York (state)">New York</a>, over 10 inches of rain triggered <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mudslide" title="Mudslide">mudslides</a> on the bluffs overlooking the <a href="/wiki/Hudson_River" title="Hudson River">Hudson River</a> near the <a href="/wiki/Tappan_Zee_Bridge" title="Tappan Zee Bridge">Tappan Zee Bridge</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-extremes0999_8-6"><a href="#cite_note-extremes0999-8">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="New_England">New England</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hurricane_Floyd&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: New England">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Effects_of_Hurricane_Floyd_in_New_England" title="Effects of Hurricane Floyd in New England">Effects of Hurricane Floyd in New England</a></div>
<p>Floyd caused large power outages and flood damage across New England, with over five inches (130 mm) of rain being dropped over most of the area. <a href="/wiki/Danbury,_Connecticut" title="Danbury, Connecticut">Danbury, Connecticut</a> received up to 15 inches (380 mm) of rain from the storm, resulting in extensive flooding in the city and surrounding areas. Mudslides were reported in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Berkshire_Mountains" title="Berkshire Mountains">Berkshire Mountains</a> of western <a href="/wiki/Massachusetts" title="Massachusetts">Massachusetts</a>. Several major highways and a countless number of local roads in Connecticut and Massachusetts were closed for several days due to flooding, mudslides, and downed trees and power lines. Because New England had been in a severe <a href="/wiki/Drought" title="Drought">drought</a> leading up to Floyd, the storm's floodwaters quickly receded.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-extremes0999_8-7"><a href="#cite_note-extremes0999-8">[7]</a></sup> East of Floyd's center, large trees were uprooted by the storm's 70 mph winds, causing widespread power outages and minor structural damage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NCDC2_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-NCDC2-32">[31]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Canada">Canada</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hurricane_Floyd&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Canada">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The remnants of Floyd continued into Canada entering the <a href="/wiki/Canadian_Hurricane_Centre" title="Canadian Hurricane Centre">Canadian Hurricane Centre</a>'s area of responsibility on the evening and regional offices in <a href="/wiki/Quebec" title="Quebec">Quebec</a> and <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Canada" title="Atlantic Canada">Atlantic Canada</a> on September 16. Floyd became an extratropical low pressure system gradually losing strength and accelerating in the upper flow. In general, winds were below 90 kilometres per hour (56 mph) over Canada. However, near <a href="/wiki/Quebec_City" title="Quebec City">Quebec City</a> (Quebec province), the weather station on <a href="/wiki/%C3%8Ele_d%27Orl%C3%A9ans" title="Île d'Orléans">île d'Orléans</a> recorded gusts up to 119 kilometres per hour (74 mph),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EC3_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-EC3-33">[32]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EC4_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-EC4-34">[33]</a></sup> and 83 km/h (52 mph) in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Maritimes" title="Maritimes">Maritimes</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bowyer_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bowyer-35">[34]</a></sup> Damages to corn and other crops were recorded on the South shore of the <a href="/wiki/Saint_Lawrence_River" title="Saint Lawrence River">Saint Lawrence River</a> from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Les_Appalaches_Regional_County_Municipality,_Quebec" title="Les Appalaches Regional County Municipality, Quebec">l'Amiante</a> to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bellechasse_Regional_County_Municipality,_Quebec" title="Bellechasse Regional County Municipality, Quebec">Bellechasse</a> regions, according to the <i>Régie des assurances agricoles du Québec</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-QC_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-QC-36">[35]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the Maritime provinces, waves reached 9 metres (30 ft), equivalent of those caused by a winter storm.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bowyer_35-1"><a href="#cite_note-Bowyer-35">[34]</a></sup> Rain fall was still substantial with up to 120 millimetres (4.7 in) over <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Townships" title="Eastern Townships">Eastern Townships</a> and <a href="/wiki/Beauce,_Quebec" title="Beauce, Quebec">Beauce</a> regions of Quebec, and the central part of <a href="/wiki/New_Brunswick" title="New Brunswick">New Brunswick</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EC1_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-EC1-37">[36]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EC2_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-EC2-38">[37]</a></sup> Region along the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/St_Lawrence_River" title="St Lawrence River">St Lawrence River</a>, between <a href="/wiki/Montreal" title="Montreal">Montreal</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gasp%C3%A9_Peninsula" title="Gaspé Peninsula">Gaspé Peninsula</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Newfoundland_(island)" title="Newfoundland (island)">Newfoundland</a> received from 50 to 80 millimetres (3.1 in)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EC_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-EC-39">[38]</a></sup> Rain caused some damages to crops.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-QC_36-1"><a href="#cite_note-QC-36">[35]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Aftermath">Aftermath</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hurricane_Floyd&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Aftermath">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>The Hurricane Floyd disaster was followed by what many judged to be a very slow federal response. Fully three weeks after the storm hit, <a href="/wiki/Jesse_Jackson" title="Jesse Jackson">Jesse Jackson</a> complained to <a href="/wiki/Federal_Emergency_Management_Agency" title="Federal Emergency Management Agency">FEMA</a> Director <a href="/wiki/James_Lee_Witt" title="James Lee Witt">James Lee Witt</a> on his <a href="/wiki/CNN" title="CNN">CNN</a> program <i>Both Sides Now</i>, "It seemed there was preparation for Hurricane Floyd, but then came Flood Floyd. Bridges are overwhelmed, levees are overwhelmed, whole towns under water ... [it's] an awesome scene of tragedy. So there's a great misery index in North Carolina." Witt responded, "We're starting to move the camper trailers in. It's been so wet it's been difficult to get things in there, but now it's going to be moving very quickly. And I think you're going to see a—I think the people there will see a big difference [within] this next weekend!"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[39]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ecological_effects">Ecological effects</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hurricane_Floyd&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Ecological effects">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Floyd_runoff.png"><img alt="A satellite image depicting run-off offshore of North Carolina" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="500" data-file-width="500" height="220" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c4/Floyd_runoff.png/220px-Floyd_runoff.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c4/Floyd_runoff.png/330px-Floyd_runoff.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c4/Floyd_runoff.png/440px-Floyd_runoff.png 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Floyd_runoff.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Runoff in the aftermath of the hurricane</div>
</div>

<p>Runoff from the hurricane created significant problems for the ecology of North Carolina's rivers and sounds.</p>
<p>In the immediate aftermath of the storm, freshwater runoff, sediment, and decomposing organic matter caused salinity and oxygen levels in <a href="/wiki/Pamlico_Sound" title="Pamlico Sound">Pamlico Sound</a> and its tributary rivers to drop to nearly zero. This raised fears of massive fish and shrimp kills, as had happened after <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Fran" title="Hurricane Fran">Hurricane Fran</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Bonnie_(1998)" title="Hurricane Bonnie (1998)">Hurricane Bonnie</a>, and the state government responded quickly to provide financial aid to fishing and shrimping industries. Strangely, however, the year's shrimp and crab harvests were extremely prosperous; one possible explanation is that runoff from <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Dennis_(1999)" title="Hurricane Dennis (1999)">Hurricane Dennis</a> caused marine animals to begin migrating to saltier waters, so they were less vulnerable to Floyd's ill effects.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Legacy_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-Legacy-4">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Pollution from runoff was also a significant fear. Numerous pesticides were found in low but measurable quantities in the river waters, particularly in the Neuse River. Overall, however, the concentration of contaminants was slightly lower than had been measured in Hurricane Fran, likely because Floyd simply dropped more water to dilute them.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-usgs-flooding_18-3"><a href="#cite_note-usgs-flooding-18">[17]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Hog_Waste_Water_Contamination">Hog Waste Water Contamination</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hurricane_Floyd&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Hog Waste Water Contamination">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>When the hurricane hit North Carolina, it flooded hog waste lagoons and released 25 million gallons of manure into the rivers, which contaminated the water supply and reduced water quality.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[40]</a></sup> <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Ronnie_Kennedy&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Ronnie Kennedy (page does not exist)">Ronnie Kennedy</a>, Duplin County director for environmental health, said that of 310 private wells he had tested for contamination since the storm, 9 percent, or three times the average across eastern North Carolina, had faecal coliform bacteria. Normally, tests showing any hint of faeces in drinking water, an indication that it can be carrying disease-causing pathogens, are cause for immediate action.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[41]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Retirement">Retirement</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hurricane_Floyd&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Retirement">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/List_of_retired_Atlantic_hurricane_names" title="List of retired Atlantic hurricane names">List of retired Atlantic hurricane names</a></div>
<p>Because of the hurricane's destruction in <a href="/wiki/North_Carolina" title="North Carolina">North Carolina</a> and elsewhere in the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone_naming" title="Tropical cyclone naming">name</a> <i>Floyd</i> was <a href="/wiki/List_of_retired_Atlantic_hurricane_names" title="List of retired Atlantic hurricane names">retired</a> by the <a href="/wiki/World_Meteorological_Organization" title="World Meteorological Organization">World Meteorological Organization</a> in the spring of 2000, and it will never again be used for another future <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/North_Atlantic_tropical_cyclone" title="North Atlantic tropical cyclone">Atlantic hurricane</a>. The name was replaced with <i><a href="/wiki/Tropical_Storm_Franklin_(2005)" title="Tropical Storm Franklin (2005)">Franklin</a></i> in the <a href="/wiki/2005_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="2005 Atlantic hurricane season">2005 season</a>.</p>
