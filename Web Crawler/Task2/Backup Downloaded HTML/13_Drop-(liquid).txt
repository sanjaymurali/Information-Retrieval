<p>A <b>drop</b> or <b>droplet</b> is a small column of <a href="/wiki/Liquid" title="Liquid">liquid</a>, bounded completely or almost completely by <a href="/wiki/Free_surface" title="Free surface">free surfaces</a>. A drop may form when liquid accumulates at the lower end of a tube or other surface boundary, producing a hanging drop called a <a href="/wiki/Pendant" title="Pendant">pendant</a> drop. Drops may also be formed by the <a href="/wiki/Condensation" title="Condensation">condensation</a> of a <a href="/wiki/Vapor" title="Vapor">vapor</a> or by <a href="/wiki/Atomizer_nozzle" title="Atomizer nozzle">atomization</a> of a larger mass of liquid.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Surface_tension">Surface tension</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Drop_(liquid)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Surface tension">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>Liquid forms drops because the liquid exhibits <a href="/wiki/Surface_tension" title="Surface tension">surface tension</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>A simple way to form a drop is to allow liquid to flow slowly from the lower end of a vertical tube of small diameter. The surface tension of the liquid causes the liquid to hang from the tube, forming a pendant. When the drop exceeds a certain size it is no longer stable and detaches itself. The falling liquid is also a drop held together by surface tension.</p>

<p>This relationship is the basis of a convenient method of measuring surface tension, commonly used in the petroleum industry. More sophisticated methods are available to take account of the developing shape of the pendant as the drop grows. These methods are used if the surface tension is unknown.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Medicine" title="Medicine">medicine</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dropper" title="Dropper">droppers</a> and IV infusion sets have a <a href="/wiki/Technical_standard" title="Technical standard">standardized</a> <a href="/wiki/Diameter" title="Diameter">diameter</a>, in such a way that 1 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Millilitre" title="Millilitre">millilitre</a> is equivalent to 20 <a href="/wiki/Drop_(unit)" title="Drop (unit)">drops</a>. And, for the cases when smaller amounts are necessary (such as paediatrics), microdroppers or paediatric infusion sets are used, in which 1 millilitre = 60 microdrops.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Drop_adhesion_to_a_solid">Drop adhesion to a solid</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Drop_(liquid)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Drop adhesion to a solid">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The drop <a href="/wiki/Adhesion" title="Adhesion">adhesion</a> to a solid can be divided to two categories: lateral adhesion and normal adhesion. Lateral adhesion resembles friction (though <a href="/wiki/Tribology" title="Tribology">tribologically</a> lateral adhesion is a more accurate term) and refers to the force required to slide a drop on the surface, namely the force to detach the drop from its position on the surface only to translate it to another position on the surface. Normal adhesion is the adhesion required to detach a drop from the surface in the normal direction, namely the force to cause the drop to fly off from the surface. The measurement of both adhesion forms can be done with the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Centrifugal_Adhesion_Balance&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Centrifugal Adhesion Balance (page does not exist)">Centrifugal Adhesion Balance</a> (CAB). The CAB uses a combination of centrifugal and gravitational forces to obtain any ratio of lateral and normal forces. For example, it can apply a normal force at zero lateral force for the drop to fly off away from the surface in the normal direction or it can induce a lateral force at zero normal force (simulating zero <a href="/wiki/Gravity" title="Gravity">gravity</a>).</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Droplet">Droplet</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Drop_(liquid)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Droplet">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The term <b>droplet</b> is a diminutive form of 'drop' - and as a guide is typically used for liquid particles of less than 500 µm diameter. In <a href="/wiki/Pesticide_application" title="Pesticide application">spray application</a>, droplets are usually described by their perceived size (i.e., diameter) whereas the dose (or number of infective particles in the case of <a href="/wiki/Biopesticide" title="Biopesticide">biopesticides</a>) is a function of their volume. This increases by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Volume_of_a_sphere" title="Volume of a sphere">a cubic function</a> relative to diameter; thus a 50 µm droplet represents a dose in 65 pl and a 500 µm drop represents a dose in 65 nanolitres.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Speed">Speed</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Drop_(liquid)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Speed">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A droplet with a diameter of 3 mm has a terminal velocity of approximately 8 m/s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-uvasim_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-uvasim-5">[5]</a></sup> Drops smaller than <span class="nowrap">1 mm</span> in diameter will attain 95% of their terminal velocity within <span class="nowrap">2 m</span>. But above this size the distance to get to terminal velocity increases sharply. An example is a drop with a diameter of <span class="nowrap">2 mm</span> that may achieve this at <span class="nowrap">5.6 m</span>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-uvasim_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-uvasim-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Optics">Optics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Drop_(liquid)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Optics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Due to the different <a href="/wiki/Refractive_index" title="Refractive index">refractive index</a> of <a href="/wiki/Water" title="Water">water</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Earth%27s_atmosphere" title="Earth's atmosphere">air</a>, <a href="/wiki/Refraction" title="Refraction">refraction</a> and <a href="/wiki/Reflection_(physics)" title="Reflection (physics)">reflection</a> occur on the surfaces of <a href="/wiki/Rain" title="Rain">raindrops</a>, leading to <a href="/wiki/Rainbow" title="Rainbow">rainbow</a> formation.</p>

<p>The major source of sound when a droplet hits a liquid surface is the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Liquid_bubble#Pulsation" title="Liquid bubble">resonance of excited bubbles</a> trapped underwater. These oscillating bubbles are responsible for most liquid sounds, such as running water or splashes, as they actually consist of many drop-liquid collisions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Shape">Shape</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Drop_(liquid)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Shape">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Raindrops_sizes.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="923" data-file-width="745" height="273" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/57/Raindrops_sizes.svg/220px-Raindrops_sizes.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/57/Raindrops_sizes.svg/330px-Raindrops_sizes.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/57/Raindrops_sizes.svg/440px-Raindrops_sizes.svg.png 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Raindrops_sizes.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The shapes of raindrops, depending on their sizes.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The classic shape associated with a drop (with a pointy end in its upper side) comes from the observation of a droplet clinging to a surface. The shape of a drop falling through a gas is actually more or less spherical for drops less than 2mm in diameter.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pruppacher_1971_86.E2.80.9394_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Pruppacher_1971_86.E2.80.9394-8">[8]</a></sup> Larger drops tend to be flatter on the bottom part due to the pressure of the gas they move through.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> As a result, as drops get larger, a concave depression forms which leads to the eventual breakup of the drop.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Size">Size</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Drop_(liquid)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Size">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Raindrop sizes typically range from 0.5 mm to 4 mm, with size distributions quickly decreasing past diameters larger than 2-2.5 mm.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>Scientists traditionally thought that the variation in the size of raindrops was due to collisions on the way down to the ground. In 2009 French researchers succeeded in showing that the distribution of sizes is due to the drops' interaction with air, which deforms larger drops and causes them to fragment into smaller drops, effectively limiting the largest raindrops to about 6 mm diameter.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> However, drops up to 10 mm (equivalent in volume to a sphere of radius 4.5 mm) are theoretically stable and could be levitated in a wind tunnel.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pruppacher_1971_86.E2.80.9394_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-Pruppacher_1971_86.E2.80.9394-8">[8]</a></sup> The largest recorded raindrop was 8.8 mm in diameter, located at the base of a <a href="/wiki/Cumulus_congestus_cloud" title="Cumulus congestus cloud">cumulus congestus cloud</a> in the vicinity of <a href="/wiki/Kwajalein_Atoll" title="Kwajalein Atoll">Kwajalein Atoll</a> in July 1999. A raindrop of identical size was detected over northern Brazil in September 1995.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
