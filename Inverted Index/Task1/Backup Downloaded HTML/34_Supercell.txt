<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">This article is about the weather phenomenon. For other uses, see <a class="mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Supercell_(disambiguation)" title="Supercell (disambiguation)">Supercell (disambiguation)</a>.</div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Supercell02.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1128" data-file-width="1821" height="155" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2f/Supercell02.svg/250px-Supercell02.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2f/Supercell02.svg/375px-Supercell02.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2f/Supercell02.svg/500px-Supercell02.svg.png 2x" width="250"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Supercell02.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Moisture streams in from the side of the precipitation free base and merges into a line of warm uplift region where the tower of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thundercloud" title="Thundercloud">thundercloud</a> is tipped by high altitude shear winds. The high shear causes horizontal <a href="/wiki/Vorticity" title="Vorticity">vorticity</a> which is tilted within the updraft to become vertical vorticity, and the mass of clouds spins as it gains altitude up to the cap, which can be up to the 55,000 feet (17,000 m)–70,000 feet (21,000 m) above ground for the largest storms, and trailing anvil. The capped, moisture laden air is cooled enough to precipitate as it is rotated toward the cooler region, represented by the turbulent air of the <a href="/wiki/Mammatus_cloud" title="Mammatus cloud">mammatus clouds</a> where the warm air is spilling over top of the cooler, invading air. The cap is formed where shear winds block further uplift for a time, until a relative weakness allows a breakthrough of the cap (an <a href="/wiki/Overshooting_top" title="Overshooting top">overshooting top</a>); Cooler air to the right in the image may or may not form a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shelf_cloud" title="Shelf cloud">shelf cloud</a>, but the precipitation zone will occur where the <a href="/wiki/Heat_engine" title="Heat engine">heat engine</a> of the uplift intermingles with the invading, colder air. As the cooler but drier air circulates into the warm, moisture laden inflow, the <a href="/wiki/Cloud_base" title="Cloud base">cloud base</a> will frequently form a wall, and the cloud base often experiences a lowering, which, in extreme cases, are where <a href="/wiki/Tornado" title="Tornado">tornadoes</a> are formed.</div>

<p>A <b>supercell</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Thunderstorm" title="Thunderstorm">thunderstorm</a> characterized by the presence of a <a href="/wiki/Mesocyclone" title="Mesocyclone">mesocyclone</a>: a deep, persistently rotating <a href="/wiki/Vertical_draft" title="Vertical draft">updraft</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> For this reason, these storms are sometimes referred to as <b>rotating thunderstorms</b>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> Of the four classifications of thunderstorms (supercell, <a href="/wiki/Squall_line" title="Squall line">squall line</a>, <a href="/wiki/Multicellular_thunderstorm" title="Multicellular thunderstorm">multi-cell</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Pulse_storm" title="Pulse storm">single-cell</a>), supercells are the overall least common and have the potential to be the most severe. Supercells are often isolated from other thunderstorms, and can dominate the local weather up to 32 kilometres (20 mi) away.</p>
<p>Supercells are often put into three classification types: Classic, Low-precipitation (LP), and High-precipitation (HP). LP supercells are usually found in climates that are more arid, such as the high plains of the United States, and HP supercells are most often found in moist climates. Supercells can occur anywhere in the world under the right pre-existing weather conditions, but they are most common in the <a href="/wiki/Great_Plains" title="Great Plains">Great Plains</a> of the United States in an area known as <a href="/wiki/Tornado_Alley" title="Tornado Alley">Tornado Alley</a> and in the <a class="extiw" href="https://es.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pasillo_de_los_Tornados" title="es:Pasillo de los Tornados">Tornado Corridor</a> of <a href="/wiki/Argentina" title="Argentina">Argentina</a>, <a href="/wiki/Uruguay" title="Uruguay">Uruguay</a> and southern <a href="/wiki/Brazil" title="Brazil">Brazil</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Characteristics">Characteristics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Supercell&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Characteristics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Supercells are usually found isolated from other thunderstorms, although they can sometimes be embedded in a <a href="/wiki/Squall_line" title="Squall line">squall line</a>. Typically, supercells are found in the warm sector of a low pressure system propagating generally in a north easterly direction in line with the cold front of the low pressure system. Because they can last for hours, they are known as quasi-steady-state storms. Supercells have the capability to deviate from the mean wind. If they track to the right or left of the mean wind (relative to the vertical <a href="/wiki/Wind_shear" title="Wind shear">wind shear</a>), they are said to be "right-movers" or "left-movers," respectively. Supercells can sometimes develop two separate updrafts with opposing rotations, which splits the storm into two supercells: one left-mover and one right-mover.</p>
<p>Supercells can be any size – large or small, low or high topped. They usually produce copious amounts of <a href="/wiki/Hail" title="Hail">hail</a>, torrential <a href="/wiki/Rain" title="Rain">rainfall</a>, strong <a href="/wiki/Wind" title="Wind">winds</a>, and substantial <a href="/wiki/Downburst" title="Downburst">downbursts</a>. Supercells are one of the few types of clouds that typically spawn <a href="/wiki/Tornado" title="Tornado">tornadoes</a> within the <a href="/wiki/Mesocyclone" title="Mesocyclone">mesocyclone</a>, although only 30% or fewer do so.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geography">Geography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Supercell&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Geography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Supercells can occur anywhere in the world under the right weather conditions. The first storm to be identified as the supercell type was the <a href="/wiki/Wokingham" title="Wokingham">Wokingham</a> storm over <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a>, which was studied by <a href="/wiki/Keith_Browning" title="Keith Browning">Keith Browning</a> and Frank Ludlam in 1962.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Browning_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Browning-4">[4]</a></sup> Browning did the initial work that was followed up by <a href="/wiki/Leslie_R._Lemon" title="Leslie R. Lemon">Lemon</a> and <a href="/wiki/Charles_A._Doswell_III" title="Charles A. Doswell III">Doswell</a> to develop the modern conceptual model of the supercell.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lemon.2FDoswell_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Lemon.2FDoswell-5">[5]</a></sup> To the extent that records are available, supercells are most frequent in the <a href="/wiki/Great_Plains" title="Great Plains">Great Plains</a> of the central United States and southern Canada extending into the southeastern U.S. and northern <a href="/wiki/Mexico" title="Mexico">Mexico</a>; east-central Argentina and adjacent regions of Uruguay; Bangladesh and parts of eastern India; South Africa; and eastern Australia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Supercells occur occasionally in many other <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mid-latitude" title="Mid-latitude">mid-latitude</a> regions, including Eastern China and throughout Europe. The areas with highest frequencies of supercells are similar to those with the most occurrences of tornadoes; see <a href="/wiki/Tornado_climatology" title="Tornado climatology">tornado climatology</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tornado_Alley" title="Tornado Alley">Tornado Alley</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Anatomy_of_a_supercell">Anatomy of a supercell</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Supercell&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Anatomy of a supercell">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The current conceptual model of a supercell was described in <i>Severe Thunderstorm Evolution and Mesocyclone Structure as Related to Tornadogenesis</i> by Leslie R. Lemon and Charles A. Doswell III. (See <a href="/wiki/Lemon_technique" title="Lemon technique">Lemon technique</a>).</p>
<p>Supercells derive their rotation through tilting of horizontal <a href="/wiki/Vorticity" title="Vorticity">vorticity</a> (an invisible horizontal <a href="/wiki/Vortex" title="Vortex">vortex</a>) caused by <a href="/wiki/Wind_shear" title="Wind shear">wind shear</a>. Strong updrafts lift the air turning about a horizontal axis and cause this air to turn about a vertical axis. This forms the <i>deep rotating updraft</i>, the <a href="/wiki/Mesocyclone" title="Mesocyclone">mesocyclone</a>.</p>
<p>A <i>cap</i> or <a href="/wiki/Capping_inversion" title="Capping inversion">capping inversion</a> is usually required to form an updraft of sufficient strength. The cap puts an inverted (warm-above-cold) layer above a normal (cold-above-warm) <a href="/wiki/Boundary_layer" title="Boundary layer">boundary layer</a>, and by preventing warm surface air from rising, allows one or both of the following:</p>
<ul>
<li>Air below the cap warms and/or becomes more moist</li>
<li>Air above the cap cools</li>
</ul>
<p>This creates a warmer, moister layer below a cooler layer, which is increasingly unstable (because warm air is less dense and tends to rise). When the cap weakens or moves, explosive development follows.</p>
<p>In North America, supercells usually show up on Doppler radar as starting at a point or hook shape on the southwestern side, fanning out to the northeast. The heaviest precipitation is usually on the southwest side, ending abruptly short of the <i>rain-free updraft base</i> or <i>main updraft</i> (not visible to radar). The <i><a href="/wiki/Rear_flank_downdraft" title="Rear flank downdraft">rear flank downdraft</a></i>, or RFD, carries precipitation counterclockwise around the north and northwest side of the updraft base, producing a "<a href="/wiki/Hook_echo" title="Hook echo">hook echo</a>" that indicates the presence of a mesocyclone.</p>

<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Overshooting_top"><a href="/wiki/Overshooting_top" title="Overshooting top">Overshooting top</a></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Supercell&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Overshooting top">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>This "dome" feature appears above the strongest updraft location on the anvil of the storm. It is a result of a very powerful updraft; enough to break through the upper levels of the troposphere. An observer who is at ground level too close to the storm is unable to see the overshooting top due to the fact that the anvil blocks the sight of this feature. The overshooting is visible from satellite images as a "bubbling" amidst the otherwise smooth upper surface of the anvil cloud.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Anvil">Anvil</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Supercell&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Anvil">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>An anvil forms when the storm's updraft collides with the upper levels of the lowest layer of the atmosphere, or the troposphere, and has nowhere else to go due to the laws of fluid dynamics- specifically pressure, humidity, and density. The anvil is very cold and virtually precipitation free even though virga can be seen falling from the forward sheared anvil. Since there is so little moisture in the anvil, winds can move freely. The clouds take on their anvil shape when the rising air reaches 15,200–21,300 metres (50,000–70,000 ft) or more. The anvil's distinguishing feature is that it juts out in front of the storm like a shelf. In some cases, it can even shear backwards, called a backsheared anvil, another sign of a very strong updraft.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Precipitation-free_base">Precipitation-free base</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Supercell&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Precipitation-free base">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>This area, typically on the southern side of the storm in North America, is relatively precipitation free. This is located beneath the main updraft, and is the main area of inflow. While no precipitation may be visible to an observer, large hail may be falling from this area. A region of this area is called the Vault. It is more accurately called the main updraft area.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Wall_cloud">Wall cloud</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Supercell&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Wall cloud">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Wall_cloud" title="Wall cloud">wall cloud</a> forms near the downdraft/updraft interface. This "interface" is the area between the <i>precipitation area</i> and the <i>precipitation-free base</i>. Wall clouds form when rain-cooled air from the downdraft is pulled into the updraft. This wet, cold air quickly saturates as it is lifted by the updraft, forming a cloud that seems to "descend" from the precipitation-free base. Wall clouds are common and are not exclusive to supercells; only a small percentage actually produce a tornado, but if a storm does produce a tornado it usually exhibits wall clouds that persist for more than ten minutes. Wall clouds that seem to move violently up or down, and violent movements of cloud fragments (scud or fractus) near the wall cloud are indications that a tornado could form.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Mammatus_clouds">Mammatus clouds</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Supercell&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Mammatus clouds">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p><a href="/wiki/Mammatus_cloud" title="Mammatus cloud">Mammatus</a> (Mamma, Mammatocumulus) are bulbous or pillow-like cloud formations extending from beneath the anvil of a thunderstorm. These clouds form as cold air in the anvil region of a storm sinks into warmer air beneath it. Mammatus are most apparent when they are lit from one side or below and are therefore at their most impressive near sunset or shortly after sunrise when the sun is low in the sky. Mammatus are not exclusive to supercells and can be associated with developed thunderstorms and cumulonimbus.</p>
<h4><span id="Forward_Flank_Downdraft_(FFD)"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Forward_Flank_Downdraft_.28FFD.29">Forward Flank Downdraft (FFD)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Supercell&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Forward Flank Downdraft (FFD)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>This is generally the area of heaviest and most widespread precipitation. For most supercells, the precipitation core is bounded on its leading edge by a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shelf_cloud" title="Shelf cloud">shelf cloud</a> that results from rain-cooled air within the precipitation core spreading outward and interacting with warmer, moist air from outside of the cell. Between the precipitation-free base and the FFD, a "vaulted" or "cathedral" feature can be observed. In <i>high precipitation supercells</i> an area of heavy precipitation may occur beneath the main updraft area where the vault would alternately be observed with classic supercells.</p>
<h3><span id="Rear_Flank_Downdraft_(RFD)"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Rear_Flank_Downdraft_.28RFD.29">Rear Flank Downdraft (RFD)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Supercell&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Rear Flank Downdraft (RFD)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Rear_flank_downdraft" title="Rear flank downdraft">Rear flank downdraft</a></div>
<p>The RFD of a supercell is a very complex and not yet fully understood feature. RFD mainly occur within classic and HP supercells although RFDs have been observed within LP supercells. The RFD of a supercell is believed to play a large part in tornadogenesis by further tightening rotation within the surface mesocyclone. RFDs are caused by mid level steering winds of a supercell colliding with the updraft tower and moving around it in all directions; specifically the flow that is redirected downward is referred to as the RFD. This downward surge of relatively cool mid level air, due to interactions between dew points, humidity, and condensation of the converging of air masses, can reach very high speeds and is known to cause widespread wind damage. The radar signature of an RFD is a hook like structure where sinking air has brought with it precipitation.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Vault">Vault</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Supercell&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Vault">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>A vault is not observed with all supercells. The vault can only be identified visibly due to it visibly appearing to be free of precipitation but usually containing large hail. On Doppler radar, the region of very high precipitation echos with a very sharp gradient perpendicular to the RFD.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Flanking_line">Flanking line</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Supercell&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Flanking line">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>A <a href="/wiki/Flanking_line_(meteorology)" title="Flanking line (meteorology)">flanking line</a> is a line of smaller <a href="/wiki/Cumulonimbus_cloud" title="Cumulonimbus cloud">cumulonimbi</a> or cumulus that form in the warm rising air pulled in by the main updraft. Due to convergence and lifting along this line, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Landspouts" title="Landspouts">landspouts</a> sometimes occur on the outflow boundary of this region.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Radar_features_of_a_supercell">Radar features of a supercell</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Supercell&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Radar features of a supercell">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Supercell_in_Wichita_Falls.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="659" data-file-width="713" height="277" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/94/Supercell_in_Wichita_Falls.svg/300px-Supercell_in_Wichita_Falls.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/94/Supercell_in_Wichita_Falls.svg/450px-Supercell_in_Wichita_Falls.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/94/Supercell_in_Wichita_Falls.svg/600px-Supercell_in_Wichita_Falls.svg.png 2x" width="300"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Supercell_in_Wichita_Falls.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Radar reflectivity map</div>
</div>
</div>
<ul>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Hook_echo" title="Hook echo">Hook echo</a></b> or <b>Pendant</b></li>
</ul>
<p>The "hook echo" is the area of confluence between the main updraft and the rear flank downdraft (RFD). This indicates the position of the mesocyclone, and probably a tornado.</p>
<ul>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Bounded_weak_echo_region" title="Bounded weak echo region">Bounded weak echo region</a></b> (or BWER)</li>
</ul>
<p>This is a region of low radar reflectivity bounded above by an area of higher radar reflectivity with an <a href="/wiki/Tilted_updraft" title="Tilted updraft">untilted updraft</a>. This is evidence of a strong updraft, and oftentimes the presence of a <a href="/wiki/Tornado" title="Tornado">tornado</a>.</p>
<ul>
<li><b>Inflow notch</b></li>
</ul>
<p>A "notch" of weak reflectivity on the inflow side of the cell. This is not a <i>V-Notch</i>.</p>
<ul>
<li><b>V Notch</b></li>
</ul>
<p>A "V" shaped notch on the leading edge of the cell, opening away from the main downdraft. This is an indication of divergent flow around a powerful updraft.</p>
<ul>
<li><b>Hail spike</b></li>
</ul>
<p>This <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Three_body_scatter_spike" title="Three body scatter spike">three body scatter spike</a> is a region of weak echoes found radially behind the main reflectivity core at higher elevations when large hail is present.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TBSS_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-TBSS-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Supercell_variations">Supercell variations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Supercell&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Supercell variations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Supercell thunderstorms are sometimes classified by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Meteorologist" title="Meteorologist">meteorologists</a> and <a href="/wiki/Skywarn" title="Skywarn">storm spotters</a> into three categories. However, not all supercells fit neatly into any one category, being hybrid storms, and many supercells may fall into different categories during different periods of their lifetimes. The standard definition given above is referred to as the <b>Classic</b> supercell. All types of supercells typically produce severe weather.</p>
<h3><span id="Low_Precipitation_(LP)"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Low_Precipitation_.28LP.29">Low Precipitation (LP)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Supercell&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Low Precipitation (LP)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>LP supercells contain a small and relatively light precipitation (rain/hail) core that is well separated from the updraft. The updraft is intense and LPs are inflow dominant storms. The updraft tower is typically more strongly tilted and the deviant rightward motion lesser than for other supercell types. The forward flank downdraft (FFD) is noticeably weaker than for other supercell types and the rear-flank downdraft (RFD) is much weaker—even visually absent in many cases. Like classic supercells, LP supercells tend to form within stronger mid-to-upper level storm-relative wind shear,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-variations_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-variations-8">[8]</a></sup> however, the atmospheric environment leading to their formation is not well understood. The moisture profile of the atmosphere, particularly the depth of the elevated dry layer, also appears to be important<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Grant_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Grant-9">[9]</a></sup> and the low-to-mid level shear may also be important.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-midtropo_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-midtropo-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>This type of supercell may be easily identifiable with "sculpted" cloud striations in the updraft base or even a "corkscrewed" or "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Barber_pole" title="Barber pole">barber pole</a>" appearance on the updraft, and sometimes an almost "anorexic" look compared to classic supercells. This is because they often form within drier moisture profiles (often initiated by <a href="/wiki/Dry_line" title="Dry line">dry lines</a>) leaving LPs with little available moisture despite high mid-to-upper level environmental winds. They most often dissipate rather than turning into classic or HP supercells, although it is still not unusual for LPs to do the latter, especially when moving into a much moister air mass. LPs were first formally described by <a href="/wiki/Howard_Bluestein" title="Howard Bluestein">Howard Bluestein</a> in the early 1980s<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bluestein_LP_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bluestein_LP-11">[11]</a></sup> although storm chasing scientists noticed them throughout the 1970s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-_12-0"><a href="#cite_note--12">[12]</a></sup> Classic supercells may wither yet maintain updraft rotation as they decay, becoming more like the LP type in a process known as "downscale transition" that also applies to LP storms and this process is thought to be how many LPs dissipate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>LP supercells rarely spawn tornadoes and those that form tend to be weak, small, and high based tornadoes but strong tornadoes have been observed. These storms although generating lesser precipitation amounts and producing smaller precipitation cores can generate huge hail. LPs may produce hail larger than <a href="/wiki/Baseball_(ball)" title="Baseball (ball)">baseballs</a> in clear air where no rainfall is visible.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> LPs are thus hazardous to people and animals caught outside as well as to storm chasers and spotters. Due to the lack of a heavy precipitation core, LP supercells often exhibit relatively weak radar reflectivity without clear evidence of a <a href="/wiki/Hook_echo" title="Hook echo">hook echo</a>, when in fact they are producing a tornado at the time. LP supercells may not even be recognized as supercells in reflectivity data unless one is trained or experienced on their radar characteristics.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-recognition_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-recognition-15">[15]</a></sup> This is where observations by <a href="/wiki/Storm_spotting" title="Storm spotting">storm spotter</a> and <a href="/wiki/Storm_chasing" title="Storm chasing">storm chasers</a> may be of vital importance in addition to <a href="/wiki/Pulse-Doppler_radar" title="Pulse-Doppler radar">Doppler</a> velocity (and polarimetric) radar data. High-based shear <a href="/wiki/Funnel_cloud" title="Funnel cloud">funnel clouds</a> sometimes form midway between the base and the top of the storm, descending from the main Cb (<a href="/wiki/Cumulonimbus_cloud" title="Cumulonimbus cloud">cumulonimbus</a>) cloud.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Lightning discharges may be less frequent compared to other supercell types, but on occasion LPs are prolific sparkers, and the discharges are more likely to occur as intracloud lightning rather than cloud-to-ground lightning.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North America</a>, these storms most prominently form in the semi-arid <a href="/wiki/Great_Plains" title="Great Plains">Great Plains</a> during the spring and summer months. Moving east and southeast, they often collide with moist air masses from the Gulf of Mexico, leading to the formation of HP supercells in areas just to the west of <a href="/wiki/Interstate_35" title="Interstate 35">Interstate 35</a> before dissipating (or coalescing into <a href="/wiki/Squall_line" title="Squall line">squall lines</a>) at variable distances farther east. LP supercells have been observed as far east as <a href="/wiki/Illinois" title="Illinois">Illinois</a> and <a href="/wiki/Indiana" title="Indiana">Indiana</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> however. LP supercells can occur as far north as <a href="/wiki/Montana" title="Montana">Montana</a>, <a href="/wiki/North_Dakota" title="North Dakota">North Dakota</a>, and even in the Prairie Provinces of <a href="/wiki/Alberta" title="Alberta">Alberta</a>, <a href="/wiki/Saskatchewan" title="Saskatchewan">Saskatchewan</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Manitoba" title="Manitoba">Manitoba</a> in <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a>. They have also been observed by storm chasers in <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Argentina" title="Argentina">Argentina</a> (the <a href="/wiki/Pampas" title="Pampas">Pampas</a>).<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>LP supercells are quite sought after by storm chasers, because the limited amount of precipitation makes sighting tornadoes at a safe distance much less difficult than with a classic or HP supercell and more so because of the unobscured storm structure unveiled. During spring and early summer, areas in which LP supercells are readily spotted include southwestern <a href="/wiki/Oklahoma" title="Oklahoma">Oklahoma</a> and northwestern <a href="/wiki/Texas" title="Texas">Texas</a>, among other parts of the western <a href="/wiki/Great_Plains" title="Great Plains">Great Plains</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h3><span id="High_Precipitation_(HP)"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="High_Precipitation_.28HP.29">High Precipitation (HP)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Supercell&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: High Precipitation (HP)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft">

<p>The <b>HP supercell</b> has a much heavier precipitation core that can wrap all the way around the mesocyclone. These are especially dangerous storms, since the mesocyclone is wrapped with rain and can hide a tornado (if present) from view. These storms also cause flooding due to heavy rain, damaging <a href="/wiki/Downburst" title="Downburst">downbursts</a> and weak tornadoes, although they are also known to produce strong to violent tornadoes. They have a lower potential for damaging hail than Classic and LP supercells, although damaging hail is possible. It has been observed by some spotters that they tend to produce more cloud-to-ground and intracloud lightning than the other types. Also, unlike the LP and Classic types, severe events usually occur at the front (southeast) of the storm. The HP supercell is the most common type of supercell in the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> east of <a href="/wiki/Interstate_35" title="Interstate 35">Interstate 35</a>, in the southern parts of the provinces of <a href="/wiki/Ontario" title="Ontario">Ontario</a> and <a href="/wiki/Quebec" title="Quebec">Quebec</a> in <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a>, and in the central portions of Argentina and <a href="/wiki/Uruguay" title="Uruguay">Uruguay</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mini-supercell_or_low-topped_supercell">Mini-supercell or low-topped supercell</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Supercell&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Mini-supercell or low-topped supercell">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Whereas <a class="external text" href="http://www.spc.noaa.gov/faq/tornado/scelllbl.html" rel="nofollow">classic</a>, HP, and LP refer to different precipitation regimes and mesoscale frontal structures, another variation was identified in the early 1990s by Jon Davies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> These smaller storms were initially called mini-supercells<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> but are now commonly referred to as low-topped supercells. These are also subdivided into Classic, HP and LP types.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Effects">Effects</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Supercell&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Effects">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Supercell04.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="720" data-file-width="1152" height="188" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/66/Supercell04.jpg/300px-Supercell04.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/66/Supercell04.jpg/450px-Supercell04.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/66/Supercell04.jpg/600px-Supercell04.jpg 2x" width="300"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Supercell04.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Satellite view of a supercell</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Supercells can produce large hail, damaging winds, deadly <a href="/wiki/Tornado" title="Tornado">tornadoes</a>, flooding, dangerous cloud-to-ground <a href="/wiki/Lightning" title="Lightning">lightning</a>, and heavy rain.</p>
<p>Severe events associated with a supercell almost always occur in the area of the updraft/downdraft interface. In the <a href="/wiki/Northern_Hemisphere" title="Northern Hemisphere">Northern Hemisphere</a>, this is most often the rear flank (southwest side) of the precipitation area in <b>LP</b> and <b>classic</b> supercells, but sometimes the leading edge (southeast side) of <b>HP</b> supercells.</p>
<p>While tornadoes are perhaps the most dramatic of these severe events, all are dangerous. High winds caused by powerful outflow can reach over 148 km/h (92 mph)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Downburst" title="Downburst">downbursts</a> can cause tornado-like damage. Flooding is the leading cause of death associated with severe weather.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p>Note that none of these severe events are exclusive to supercells, although these events are highly predictable once a supercell has formed.</p>

<p>The supercell is a global phenomenon, appearing in places all over the world in slightly varying forms.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Asia">Asia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Supercell&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Asia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Some reports suggest that the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Maharashtra_floods_of_2005" title="The Maharashtra floods of 2005">deluge</a> on 26 July 2005 in <a href="/wiki/Mumbai" title="Mumbai">Mumbai</a>, <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a> was caused by a supercell when there was a cloud formation 15 kilometres (9.3 mi) high over the city. On this day 944 mm (37.2 in) of rain fell over the city, of which 700 mm (28 in) fell in just four hours. The rainfall coincided with a high tide, which exacerbated conditions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p>Supercells occur commonly from March–May in Bangladesh, West Bengal and the bordering north-eastern Indian states including Tripura. Supercells that produce very high winds with hail and occasional tornadoes are observed in these regions, with them also occurring along the Northern Plains of India and Pakistan. On March 23, 2013, a massive tornado ripped through Brahmanbaria district in Bangladesh, killing 20 and injuring 200.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Australia">Australia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Supercell&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Australia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On April 14, 1999, <a href="/wiki/1999_Sydney_hailstorm" title="1999 Sydney hailstorm">a severe storm</a> later classified as a supercell hit the east coast of New South Wales. It is estimated that the storm dropped 500,000 tonnes (490,000 long tons; 550,000 short tons) worth of hailstones during its course. At the time it was the most costly disaster in Australia's insurance history, causing an approximated A$2.3 billion worth of damage, of which A$1.7 billion was covered by insurance.</p>
<p>On February 27, 2007, a supercell hit <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Canberra,_Australia" title="Canberra, Australia">Canberra</a>, dumping nearly thirty-nine centimetres (15 inches) of ice in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/City,_Australian_Capital_Territory" title="City, Australian Capital Territory">Civic</a>. The ice was so heavy that a newly built shopping center's roof collapsed, birds were killed in the hail produced from the supercell, and people were stranded. The following day many homes in Canberra were subjected to flash flooding, caused either by storm water infrastructure's inability to cope or through mud slides from cleared land.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<p>On 6 March 2010, <a href="/wiki/2010_Victorian_storms" title="2010 Victorian storms">supercell storms</a> hit <a href="/wiki/Melbourne" title="Melbourne">Melbourne</a>. The storms caused flash flooding in the center of the city and tennis ball-sized (10 cm or 4 in) hailstones hit cars and buildings, causing more than $220 million worth of damage, and sparking 40,000-plus insurance claims. In just 18 minutes, 19 cm (7.5 in) of rain fell, causing havoc as streets were flooded and trains, planes and cars were brought to a standstill.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sevwx_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-sevwx-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<p>That same month, on <a href="/wiki/2010_Western_Australian_storms" title="2010 Western Australian storms">March 22, 2010</a> a supercell hit <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Perth,_Western_Australia" title="Perth, Western Australia">Perth</a>. This storm was one of the worst in the city's history, causing hail stones of 6 centimetres (2.4 in) in size and torrential rain. The city had its average March rainfall in just seven minutes during the storm. Hail stones caused severe property damage, from dented cars to smashed windows.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-abc_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-abc-26">[26]</a></sup> The storm itself caused more than 100 million dollars in damage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-businessweek_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-businessweek-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="South_America">South America</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Supercell&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: South America">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>An area in South America known as the <a class="extiw" href="https://es.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pasillo_de_los_Tornados" title="es:Pasillo de los Tornados">Tornado Corridor</a> is considered to be the second most frequent location for severe weather, after Tornado Alley in the United States.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> The region, which covers portions of <a href="/wiki/Argentina" title="Argentina">Argentina</a>, <a href="/wiki/Uruguay" title="Uruguay">Uruguay</a>, <a href="/wiki/Paraguay" title="Paraguay">Paraguay</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Brazil" title="Brazil">Brazil</a> during the spring and summer, often experiences strong thunderstorms, which may include tornadoes. One of the first known South American supercell thunderstorms to include tornadoes occurred on September 16, 1816 and destroyed the town of Rojas (240 kilometres (150 mi) west of the city of Buenos Aires).<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>On September 20, 1926, an EF4 tornado struck the city of Encarnación (Paraguay), killing over 300 people and making it the second deadliest tornado in South America. On 21 April 1970, the town of Fray Marcos in the Department of Florida, Uruguay experienced an F4 tornado that killed 11, the strongest in the history of the nation. January 10, 1973 saw the most severe tornado in the history of South America: The <a href="/wiki/San_Justo_tornado" title="San Justo tornado">San Justo tornado</a>, 105 km north of the city of <a class="extiw" href="https://es.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ciudad_de_Santa_Fe_(Argentina)" title="es:Ciudad de Santa Fe (Argentina)">Santa Fe</a> (Argentina), was rated EF5, making it the strongest tornado ever recorded in the southern hemisphere, with winds exceeding 400 km/h. On April 13, 1993, in less than 24 hours in the province of <a href="/wiki/Buenos_Aires" title="Buenos Aires">Buenos Aires</a> was given the largest tornado outbreak in the history of South America. There were more than 300 tornadoes recorded, with intensities between F1 and F3. The most affected towns were Henderson (EF3), Urdampilleta (EF3) and Mar del Plata (EF2). In December 2000, a series of twelve tornadoes (only registered) affected the Greater Buenos Aires and the province of Buenos Aires, causing serious damage. One of them struck the town of Guernica, and, just two weeks later, in January 2001, an EF3 again devastated Guernica, killing 2 people.</p>
<p>The December 26, 2003 Tornado F3 happened in <a href="/wiki/C%C3%B3rdoba,_Argentina" title="Córdoba, Argentina">Cordoba</a>, with winds exceeding 300 km/h, which hit Córdoba Capital, hit just 6 km from the city center, in the area known as CPC Route 20, especially neighborhoods of San Roque and Villa Fabric, killing 5 people and injuring hundreds. The tornado that hit the State of <a href="/wiki/S%C3%A3o_Paulo" title="São Paulo">São Paulo</a> in 2004 was one of the most destructive in the state. Destroyed several industrial buildings, with 400 houses, left 1 dead and 11 wounded. The tornado was rated EF3, but many claim it was a tornado EF4. In November 2009, four tornadoes category F1 and F2 reached the town of Posadas (capital of the province of <a href="/wiki/Misiones_Province" title="Misiones Province">Misiones</a>, Argentina), generating serious damage in the city. Three of the tornado affected area of the airport, causing damage in Barrio Belén. On April 4, 2012, the Gran Buenos Aires was hit by the storm Buenos Aires, with intensities F1 and F2, which left nearly 30 dead in various locations.</p>
<p>On February 21, 2014, in Berazategui (province of Buenos Aires), a tornado of intensity F1 caused material damage including a car was, with two occupants inside, which was elevated a few feet off the ground and flipped over asphalt, both the driver and his passenger were slightly injured. The tornado caused no fatalities. The severe weather that occurred on Tuesday 8/11 had features rarely seen in such magnitude in Argentina. In many towns of <a href="/wiki/La_Pampa_Province" title="La Pampa Province">La Pampa</a>, <a href="/wiki/San_Luis,_Argentina" title="San Luis, Argentina">San Luis</a>, Buenos Aires and Cordoba, intense hail stones fell up to 6 cm in diameter. On Sunday December 8, 2013, severe storms took place in the center and the coast. The most affected province was Córdoba, storms and supercells type "bow echos" also developed in Santa Fe and San Luis.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Europe">Europe</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Supercell&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Europe">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Spanish_plume" title="Spanish plume">Spanish plume</a></div>
<p>In 2009, on the night of Monday May 25, a supercell formed over <a href="/wiki/Belgium" title="Belgium">Belgium</a>. It was described by Belgian meteorologist Frank Deboosere as "one of the worst storms in recent years" and caused much damage in Belgium - mainly in the provinces of East Flanders (around Ghent), Flemish Brabant (around Brussels) and Antwerp. The storm occurred between about 1:00am and 4:00am local time. An incredible 30,000 lightning flashes were recorded in 2 hours - including 10,000 cloud-to-ground strikes. Hailstones up to 6 centimetres (2.4 in) across were observed in some places and wind gusts over 90 km/h (56 mph); in Melle near Ghent a gust of 101 km/h (63 mph) was reported. Trees were uprooted and blown onto several motorways. In Lillo (east of Antwerp) a loaded goods train was blown from the rail tracks.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<p>On August 18, 2011, the rock festival <a href="/wiki/Pukkelpop" title="Pukkelpop">Pukkelpop</a> in Kiewit, Hasselt (Belgium) may have been seized by a supercell with <a href="/wiki/Mesocyclone" title="Mesocyclone">mesocyclone</a> around 18:15. Tornado-like winds were reported, trees of over 30 centimetres (12 in) diameter were felled and tents came down. Severe hail scourged the campus. Five people reportedly died and over 140 people were injured. One more died a week later. The event was suspended. Buses and trains were mobilised to bring people home.</p>
<p>On June 28, 2012, three supercells affected England. Two of them formed over the Midlands, producing hailstones reported to be larger than golfballs, with conglomerate stones up to 10 cm across. Burbage in Leicestershire saw some of the most severe hail. Another supercell produced a tornado near Sleaford, in Lincolnshire.</p>
<p>A third supercell affected the <a href="/wiki/North_East_England" title="North East England">North East</a> region of England. The storm struck the <a href="/wiki/Tyneside" title="Tyneside">Tyneside</a> area directly and without warning during evening rush hour causing widespread damage and travel chaos, with people abandoning cars and being trapped due to lack of public transport. Flooded shopping malls were evacuated, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Newcastle_Central_station" title="Newcastle Central station">Newcastle Central station</a> was shut, as was the <a href="/wiki/Tyne_and_Wear_Metro" title="Tyne and Wear Metro">Tyne and Wear Metro</a>, and main road routes were flooded leading to massive tailbacks. 999 land line services were knocked out in some areas and the damage ran to huge amounts only visible the next day after water cleared. Many parts of <a href="/wiki/County_Durham" title="County Durham">County Durham</a> and <a href="/wiki/Northumberland" title="Northumberland">Northumberland</a> were also affected, with thousands of homes across the North East left without power due to lightning strikes. Lightning was seen to hit the Tyne Bridge (Newcastle).</p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a>, the mini-supercell, or low-topped supercell, is very common, especially when showers and thunderstorms develop in cooler polar air masses with a strong jet stream above, especially in the left exit-region of a <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Jetstreak&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Jetstreak (page does not exist)">jetstreak</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="North_America">North America</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Supercell&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: North America">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Tornado_Alley" title="Tornado Alley">Tornado Alley</a> is a region of the central United States where severe weather is common, particularly tornadoes. Supercell thunderstorms can affect this region at any time of the year, but they are most common in the spring. <a href="/wiki/Tornado_watch" title="Tornado watch">Tornado watches</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tornado_warning" title="Tornado warning">warnings</a> are frequently necessary in the spring and summer. Most places from the <a href="/wiki/Great_Plains" title="Great Plains">Great Plains</a> to the <a href="/wiki/East_Coast_of_the_United_States" title="East Coast of the United States">East Coast of the United States</a> and north as far as the <a href="/wiki/Canadian_Prairies" title="Canadian Prairies">Canadian Prairies</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Great_Lakes_region" title="Great Lakes region">Great Lakes region</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Saint_Lawrence_River" title="Saint Lawrence River">St. Lawrence River</a> will experience one or more supercells each year.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Gainesville,_Georgia" title="Gainesville, Georgia">Gainesville, Georgia</a> was the site of the <a href="/wiki/1936_Tupelo%E2%80%93Gainesville_tornado_outbreak" title="1936 Tupelo–Gainesville tornado outbreak">fifth deadliest tornado in U.S. history</a> in 1936, where Gainesville was devastated and 203 people were killed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/1980_Grand_Island_tornado_outbreak" title="1980 Grand Island tornado outbreak">1980 Grand Island tornado outbreak</a> affected the city of <a href="/wiki/Grand_Island,_Nebraska" title="Grand Island, Nebraska">Grand Island, Nebraska</a> on June 3, 1980. Seven tornadoes touched down in or near the city that night, killing 5 and injuring 200.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/2007_Elie,_Manitoba_tornado" title="2007 Elie, Manitoba tornado">Elie, Manitoba tornado</a> was an <a href="/wiki/Fujita_scale" title="Fujita scale">F5</a> that struck the town of <a href="/wiki/Elie,_Manitoba" title="Elie, Manitoba">Elie, Manitoba</a> on June 22, 2007. While several houses were leveled, no one was injured or killed by the tornado.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tornado_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-tornado-33">[33]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup></p>
<p>A massive <a href="/wiki/1999_Oklahoma_tornado_outbreak" title="1999 Oklahoma tornado outbreak">tornado outbreak</a> on May 3, 1999 spawned an F5 tornado in the area of <a href="/wiki/Oklahoma_City" title="Oklahoma City">Oklahoma City</a> that had the highest recorded winds on Earth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> This outbreak <a href="/wiki/List_of_tornadoes_in_the_1999_Oklahoma_tornado_outbreak" title="List of tornadoes in the 1999 Oklahoma tornado outbreak">spawned over 66 tornadoes</a> in <a href="/wiki/Oklahoma" title="Oklahoma">Oklahoma</a> alone. On this day throughout the area of Oklahoma, <a href="/wiki/Kansas" title="Kansas">Kansas</a> and <a href="/wiki/Texas" title="Texas">Texas</a>, over 141 tornadoes were produced. This outbreak resulted in 50 fatalities and 895 injuries.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>A series of tornadoes, which occurred in May 2013, caused severe devastation to Oklahoma City in general. The first tornado outbreaks occurred on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/May_18%E2%80%9321,_2013_tornado_outbreak" title="May 18–21, 2013 tornado outbreak">May 18 to May 21</a> when a series of tornadoes hit. From one of the storms developed <a href="/wiki/2013_Moore_tornado" title="2013 Moore tornado">a tornado</a> which was later rated <a href="/wiki/Enhanced_Fujita_scale" title="Enhanced Fujita scale">EF5</a>, which traveled across parts of the Oklahoma City area, causing a severe amount of disruption. This tornado was first spotted in <a href="/wiki/Newcastle,_Oklahoma" title="Newcastle, Oklahoma">Newcastle</a>. It touched the ground for 39 minutes, crossing through a heavily populated section of Moore.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Winds with this tornado peaked at 210 miles per hour (340 km/h).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> Twenty-three fatalities and 377 injuries were caused by the tornado.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Deaths_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-Deaths-37">[37]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AFP_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-AFP-38">[38]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/List_of_tornadoes_in_the_May_18%E2%80%9321,_2013_tornado_outbreak" title="List of tornadoes in the May 18–21, 2013 tornado outbreak">Sixty-one other tornadoes</a> were confirmed during the storm period. Later on in the same month, on the night of May 31, 2013, another eight deaths were confirmed from what became the <a href="/wiki/2013_El_Reno_tornado" title="2013 El Reno tornado">widest tornado on record</a> which hit El Reno, Oklahoma, one of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/May_26%E2%80%9331,_2013_tornado_outbreak" title="May 26–31, 2013 tornado outbreak">a series of tornadoes</a> and <a href="/wiki/Funnel_cloud" title="Funnel cloud">funnel clouds</a> which hit nearby areas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="South_Africa">South Africa</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Supercell&amp;action=edit&amp;section=26" title="Edit section: South Africa">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>South Africa witnesses several supercell thunderstorms each year with the inclusion of isolated tornadoes. On most occasions these tornadoes occur in open farmlands and rarely cause damage to property, as such many of the tornadoes which do occur in South Africa are not reported. The majority of supercells develop in the central, northern, and north eastern parts of the country. The Free State, Gauteng, and Kwazulu Natal are typically the provinces where these storms are most commonly experienced, though supercell activity is not limited to these provinces. On occasion, hail reaches sizes in excess of <a href="/wiki/Golf_ball" title="Golf ball">golf balls</a>, and tornadoes, though rare, also occur.</p>
<p>On 6 May 2009, a well-defined hook echo was noticed on local South African radars, along with satellite imagery this supported the presence of a strong supercell storm. Reports from the area indicated heavy rains, winds and large hail.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup></p>
<p>On October 2, 2011, two devastating tornadoes tore through two separate parts of South Africa on the same day, hours apart from each other. The first, classified as an EF2 hit Meqheleng, the informal settlement outside Ficksburg, Free State which devastated shacks and homes, uprooted trees, and killed one small child. The second, which hit the informal settlement of Duduza, Nigel in the Gauteng province, also classified as EF2 hit hours apart from the one that struck Ficksburg. This tornado completely devastated parts of the informal settlement and killed two children, destroying shacks and RDP homes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup></p>
