<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Heat burst</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">
					Jump to:					<a href="#mw-head">navigation</a>, 					<a href="#p-search">search</a>
</div>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><p>In <a href="/wiki/Meteorology" title="Meteorology">meteorology</a>, a <b>heat burst</b> is a rare atmospheric phenomenon characterized by gusty winds and a rapid increase in <a href="/wiki/Temperature" title="Temperature">temperature</a> and decrease in <a href="/wiki/Dew_point" title="Dew point">dew point</a> (moisture). Heat bursts typically occur during night-time and are associated with decaying <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thunderstorms" title="Thunderstorms">thunderstorms</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Although this phenomenon is not fully understood, it is theorized that the event is caused when rain evaporates (<a href="/wiki/Virga" title="Virga">virga</a>) into a parcel of cold dry air high in the atmosphere making the air denser than its surroundings.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> The parcel descends rapidly, warming due to compression, overshoots its <a href="/wiki/Equilibrium_level" title="Equilibrium level">equilibrium level</a> and reaches the surface, similar to a <a href="/wiki/Downburst" title="Downburst">downburst</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Recorded temperatures during heat bursts have reached well above 38 <a href="/wiki/Celsius" title="Celsius">°C</a> (100 <a href="/wiki/Fahrenheit" title="Fahrenheit">°F</a>), sometimes rising by 11 °C (20 °F) or more within only a few minutes. More extreme events have also been documented, where temperatures have been reported to exceed 120 °F (49 °C). However, such extreme events have never been officially verified. Heat bursts are also characterized by extremely dry air and are sometimes associated with very strong, even damaging, <a href="/wiki/Wind" title="Wind">winds</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Characteristics">Characteristics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Heat_burst&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Characteristics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In general, heat bursts occur during the late spring and summer seasons. During the late spring and summer seasons, thunderstorms tend to generate day heating and lose their main energy during the evening hours.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Due to a potential temperature increase, heat bursts normally occur at night. In rare cases, heat bursts have been recorded to occur during the daytime as well. Heat bursts have lasted for times spanning from a couple of minutes to several hours. The rare phenomenon is usually accompanied by strong gusty winds, extreme temperature changes, and an extreme decrease in humidity. They occur near the end of a weakening thunderstorm cluster. Dry air and a low-level inversion are also present during the storm.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Causes">Causes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Heat_burst&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Causes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>As the thunderstorm starts to dissipate, the layer of clouds start to rise. After the layer of clouds rise, a rain-cooled layer remains. The cluster shoots a burst of unsaturated air down towards the ground. In doing so, the system loses all of its up-draft related fuel.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> The raindrops begin to evaporate into dry air, which emphasizes the effects of the heat bursts. As the unsaturated air descends, the air pressure increases. The descending air parcel warms at the dry adiabatic lapse rate of approximately 10° Celsius per 1000 meters (5.5° Fahrenheit per 1000 feet) of descent. The warm air from the cluster replaces the cool air on the ground. The effect is similar to someone blowing down on a puddle of water. On 4 March 1990, the <a href="/wiki/National_Weather_Service" title="National Weather Service">National Weather Service</a> in <a href="/wiki/Goodland,_Kansas" title="Goodland, Kansas">Goodland, Kansas</a> detected a system that had weakening, light rain showers and snow showers. It was followed by gusty winds and a temperature increase. A heat burst was being observed. The detection proved that heat bursts can occur in both summer months and winter months. The occurrence also proved that a weakening thunderstorm was not needed in the development of heat bursts.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Forecasting">Forecasting</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Heat_burst&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Forecasting">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The first step of forecasting and preparing for heat bursts is recognizing the events that come before heat bursts occur. Rain from a high <a href="/wiki/Convection" title="Convection">convection</a> cloud falls below cloud level and evaporates, cooling the air. Air parcels that are cooler than the surrounding environment fall. And lastly, temperature conversion mixed with a downdraft momentum continue downward until the air reaches the ground. The air parcels then become warmer than their environment. McPherson, Lane, Crawford, and McPherson Jr. researched the heat burst system at the <a href="/wiki/Oklahoma_Mesonet" title="Oklahoma Mesonet">Oklahoma Mesonet</a>, which is owned by both the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Oklahoma" title="University of Oklahoma">University of Oklahoma</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oklahoma_State_University" title="Oklahoma State University">Oklahoma State University</a>. The purpose of their research was to discover any technological benefits and challenges in detecting heat bursts, document the time of day and year that heat bursts mostly occur, and to research the topography of where heat bursts mostly occur in Oklahoma. Scientists and meteorologists use archived data to manually study data that detected 390 potential heat burst days during a fifteen-year period. In studying the archived data, they observed that 58% of the potential days had dry-line passages, frontal passages or a temperature change. The temperature change was due to increase in solar radiation in the hours of the morning or a daytime precipitation weather system. By studying the archived data, the scientists' have the ability to determine the beginning, peak and end of heat burst conditions. The peak of heat burst conditions is the maximum observed temperature. The beginning of the heat burst occurrence is the time when the air temperature began to increase without decreasing until after the heat burst. The end of the heat burst is when the system ceased to affect the temperature and dew point of the area. In addition to researching the life cycle and characteristics of heat bursts, a group of scientists concluded that the topography of Oklahoma coincided with the change in atmospheric moisture between northwest and southeast Oklahoma. An increase in convection normally occurs over the <b><a href="/wiki/High_Plains_(United_States)" title="High Plains (United States)">United States High Plains</a></b> during the late spring and summer. They also concluded that a higher increase in convection develops if a mid-tropospheric lifting mechanism interacts with an elevated moist layer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Some_documented_cases">Some documented cases</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Heat_burst&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Some documented cases">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Hobart,_Oklahoma" title="Hobart, Oklahoma">Hobart, Oklahoma</a>, 6-7 July 2016: The temperature rose from 80.6 °F (27.0 °C) just before 11:00 pm CDT, July 6 to 105.8 °F (41.0 °C) at 12:15 am CDT, July 7.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Calgary" title="Calgary">Calgary</a>, <a href="/wiki/Alberta" title="Alberta">Alberta</a>, 30 July 2014: Between 10:00 pm, July 29, and 12:00 am, the <a href="/wiki/Dew_point" title="Dew point">dew point</a> fell from 12 °C (54 °F) to 0 °C (32 °F), with southwest wind gusts of 85 kilometres per hour (53 mph) at the airport. Meanwhile, the mercury rose from 26 °C (79 °F) to 29 °C (84 °F). 31 July 2014: A second heat burst began about 9:30 pm; with the wind gusting to 67 kilometres per hour (42 mph), the dew point falling from 10 °C (50 °F) to 0 °C (32 °F), and the temperature climbing from 26 °C (79 °F) to 29 °C (84 °F)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Melbourne" title="Melbourne">Melbourne</a>, <a href="/wiki/Victoria_(Australia)" title="Victoria (Australia)">Victoria</a>, <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a>, 14–15 January 2014: Following a very hot day, decaying thunderstorms produced a heat burst centered over the western suburbs of the city, but affecting most of the urban area. At 10:50 pm <a href="/wiki/Laverton,_Victoria" title="Laverton, Victoria">Laverton</a> recorded a wind gust of 102 km/h (55 kn; 63 mph), followed by a rise in temperature from 29.9 to 38.9 °C (85.8 to 102.0 °F) in just over an hour,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> while <a href="/wiki/HMAS_Cerberus_(naval_base)" title="HMAS Cerberus (naval base)">Cerberus</a> station recorded a rise from 24.2 to 32.5 °C (75.6 to 90.5 °F) in 30 minutes and later recorded a second rise from 26.6 to 33.6 °C (79.9 to 92.5 °F) in 46 minutes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> The main Melbourne weather station recorded a smaller rise from 33.6 to 36.4 °C (92.5 to 97.5 °F) in 90 minutes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Grand_Island,_Nebraska" title="Grand Island, Nebraska">Grand Island, Nebraska</a>, 11 June 2013: Temperature jumped from 74.2 °F (23.4 °C) to 93.7 °F (34.3 °C) in the 15 minutes between 2:57 and 3:12 AM <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Dane_County,_Wisconsin" title="Dane County, Wisconsin">Dane County, Wisconsin</a>, 15 May 2013: The National Weather Service reported nearly a 10 °F (5.3 °C) temperature boost that coincided with sustained winds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/South_Dakota" title="South Dakota">South Dakota</a>, 14 May 2013: Several "heat bursts" or hybrid "heat burst/wake low" induced wind gusts were observed across portions of northeastern South Dakota. Between 7:00 AM CDT and 8:00 AM CDT temperatures rose from 58 °F (14 °C) to 79 °F (26 °C) and strong winds to 57 miles per hour (92 km/h) were reported.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Bussey,_Iowa" title="Bussey, Iowa">Bussey, Iowa</a>, 3 May 2012: The temperatures shot from about 74 °F (23 °C) to about 85 °F (29 °C) degrees while peak wind gusts jumped from around 15 mph to about 60 mph.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Torcy,_Seine-et-Marne" title="Torcy, Seine-et-Marne">Torcy, Seine-et-Marne</a>, 29 April 2012 : while an area of low pressure moved from the southwest of France to the northwest, the wind suddenly increased between 10 pm and midnight in areas to the south of Paris. Sustained winds topped 45 km/h (28 mph) at the station of Torcy (Seine-et-Marne) with gusts of up to 110 km/h (69 mph). At the same time, the temperature rose from 13.4 °C (56.1 °F) at 11 pm to 24 °C (75 °F) at midnight. The vertical temperature profile was similar to that observed during dry downbursts, with a very strong helicity (700 m²/s²) and a strong shear (60 <a href="/wiki/Knot_(unit)" title="Knot (unit)">kn</a>) but with only a weak instability (CAPE levels of 100 to 200 J/Kg). No thunderstorms developed over the region, however light rain was reported (due to evaporation in dry low level boundary layer). Other stations in the area also experienced the phenomenon but not as dramatically as in Torcy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Atlantic,_Iowa" title="Atlantic, Iowa">Atlantic, Iowa</a>, 23 August 2011: The observation at the Atlantic <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/AWOS" title="AWOS">AWOS</a> at 7:25 pm local time had a temperature of 102 °F (39 °C) and a dew point of 7 °F (−14 °C). Three observations prior to this (6:55 pm), the temperature was 88 °F (31 °C) and the dew point was 64 °F (18 °C). The 7 °F (−14 °C) dew point is considered likely to be incorrect, however, as AWOS stations have been known to have issues with dew points in low humidity environments. Scattered wind damage was also reported in association with the heat bursts, with one wind observation as high as 60 mph (97 km/h).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Indianapolis,_Indiana" title="Indianapolis, Indiana">Indianapolis, Indiana</a>, 3 July 2011: Observations around 1:30 am EDT in the area indicated the temperature rose and the dew point dropped nearly 15 °F (8 °C) in less than an hour, causing the relative humidity to drop nearly 40-50 <a href="/wiki/Percentage_point" title="Percentage point">percentage points</a>. Winds increased rapidly, with gusts to near 50 mph (80 km/h). One NWS Indianapolis employee reported that his neighbor's patio furniture ended up in his backyard. The observation site at Eagle Creek Airpark (KEYE) best observed the temperature, dew point, and pressure changes. The site at Indianapolis International Airport (KIND) observed the strongest wind gusts associated with the heat burst.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Wichita,_Kansas" title="Wichita, Kansas">Wichita, Kansas</a>, 9 June 2011: Temperatures rose from 85 to 102 °F (29 to 39 °C) between 12:22 and 12:42 am. The heat burst caused some wind damage (40–50 mph or 64–80 km/h) and local residents reported the phenomenon to area weather stations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Buenos_Aires,_Argentina" title="Buenos Aires, Argentina">Buenos Aires, Argentina</a>, 29 October 2009: After a day with extremely high and unusual temperatures that peaked over 93.9 °F (34.4 °C) (air temperature 101.6 °F (38.7 °C)), at late midnight temperatures rose from 87.8 to 94.2 °F (31.0 to 34.6 °C) in a matter of minutes with wind gusts over 37 miles per hour (60 km/h)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Delmarva_Peninsula" title="Delmarva Peninsula">Delmarva Peninsula</a>, 26 April 2009: Temperatures rose from 68 to 87 °F (20 to 31 °C) between 10:00 pm and 2:00 am following a series of heat bursts across the Eastern Shore. Double-digit temperature increases were reported from 1:00 to 2:00 am at <a href="/wiki/Salisbury,_Maryland" title="Salisbury, Maryland">Salisbury, Maryland</a> (+13), <a href="/wiki/Ocean_City,_Maryland" title="Ocean City, Maryland">Ocean City, Maryland</a> (+11), and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wallops_Island,_Virginia" title="Wallops Island, Virginia">Wallops Island, Virginia</a> (+10).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Edmonton" title="Edmonton">Edmonton</a>, <a href="/wiki/Alberta" title="Alberta">Alberta</a> 18 August 2008: 23:00 (MST)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-heatbustEDM_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-heatbustEDM-29">[29]</a></sup> In the evening temperatures were cooling off after a high of 34.4 °C (93.9 °F). Thunderstorms had formed to the southwest along the <a href="/wiki/Rocky_Mountain_Foothills" title="Rocky Mountain Foothills">foothills</a>, and were moving to the east-northeast.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-stationCCA_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-stationCCA-30">[30]</a></sup> By 22:37 the <a href="/wiki/Edmonton_City_Centre_(Blatchford_Field)_Airport" title="Edmonton City Centre (Blatchford Field) Airport">Edmonton City Centre Airport</a> the temperature was 22 °C (72 °F), with dew point at 16 °C (61 °F), light rain from the thunderstorm passing the city.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-heatbustEDM_29-1"><a href="#cite_note-heatbustEDM-29">[29]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-stationCCA_30-1"><a href="#cite_note-stationCCA-30">[30]</a></sup> Around 23:00 strong gusts of wind from 37 to 57 kilometres per hour (23 to 35 mph) were recorded at the Airport. The temperatures quickly rose to 31 °C (88 °F), and lowered the dew point to 10 °C (50 °F),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-heatbustEDM_29-2"><a href="#cite_note-heatbustEDM-29">[29]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23hobservation_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-23hobservation-31">[31]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tephigram18_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-tephigram18-32">[32]</a></sup> lasting less than an hour. The burst was caused by the thunderstorms dissipating, North and East of the city.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18radar_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-18radar-33">[33]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Sioux_Falls,_South_Dakota" title="Sioux Falls, South Dakota">Sioux Falls, South Dakota</a>, 3 August 2008: Temperatures rose rapidly from the lower 70 to 101 °F (21 to 38 °C) in a matter of minutes. Wind speeds also rose with gusts up to 50–60 mph (80–97 km/h).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Cozad,_Nebraska" title="Cozad, Nebraska">Cozad, Nebraska</a>, 26 June 2008: Wind gusts reached 75 miles per hour (121 km/h), as the temperature rose 20 °F (11 °C)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> in a matter of minutes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Midland,_Texas" title="Midland, Texas">Midland, Texas</a>, 16 June 2008: At 11:25 pm a wind gust of 62 mph (100 km/h) occurred, and the temperature rose from 71 to 97 °F (21.7 to 36.1 °C) in minutes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> (These measurements were taken from miles away, and theories point to 80–100 mph (130–160 km/h) winds in a 2–3-block perimeter.)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Emporia,_Kansas" title="Emporia, Kansas">Emporia, Kansas</a>, 25 May 2008: Reported temperature jumped from 71 to 91 °F (21.7 to 32.8 °C) between 4:44 and 5:11 am (CDT)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> as the result of wind activity from a slow moving thunderstorm some 40 miles (64 km) to the southwest.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Canby,_Minnesota" title="Canby, Minnesota">Canby, Minnesota</a>, 16 July 2006: A heat burst formed in Western <a href="/wiki/Minnesota" title="Minnesota">Minnesota</a>, pushing <a href="/wiki/Canby,_Minnesota" title="Canby, Minnesota">Canby</a>'s temperature to 100 °F (37.8 °C), and causing a wind gust of 63 mph (55 kn; 101 km/h). The <a href="/wiki/Dew_point" title="Dew point">dew point</a> fell from 70 to 32 °F (21 to 0 °C) over the course of one hour.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Hastings,_Nebraska" title="Hastings, Nebraska">Hastings, Nebraska</a>, 20 June 2006: During the early morning the surface temperature abruptly increased from approximately 75 to 94 °F (23.9 to 34.4 °C).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Sheppard_Air_Force_Base" title="Sheppard Air Force Base">Sheppard Air Force Base</a> <a href="/wiki/Wichita_Falls,_Texas" title="Wichita Falls, Texas">Wichita Falls, Texas</a>, 12 June 2004: During late evening the surface temperature abruptly increased from approximately 83 to 94 °F (28.3 to 34.4 °C) and causing a wind gust of 72 mph (63 kn; 116 km/h). The <a href="/wiki/Dew_point" title="Dew point">dew point</a> fell from 70 to 39 °F (21.1 to 3.9 °C)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Minnesota" title="Minnesota">Minnesota</a> and <a href="/wiki/South_Dakota" title="South Dakota">South Dakota</a>, 26 March 1998: A temperature increase of 10–20 °F (6-11 °C) was reported in the towns of <a href="/wiki/Marshall,_Minnesota" title="Marshall, Minnesota">Marshall, Minnesota</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sioux_Falls,_South_Dakota" title="Sioux Falls, South Dakota">Sioux Falls, South Dakota</a>, <a href="/wiki/Brookings,_South_Dakota" title="Brookings, South Dakota">Brookings, South Dakota</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Montrose,_South_Dakota" title="Montrose, South Dakota">Montrose, South Dakota</a> during a two-hour period.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Oklahoma" title="Oklahoma">Oklahoma</a>, 22–23 May 1996: The temperature in the towns of <a href="/wiki/Chickasha,_Oklahoma" title="Chickasha, Oklahoma">Chickasha</a> rose from 87.6 to 101.9 °F (30.9 to 38.8 °C) in just 25 minutes, while the temperature at <a href="/wiki/Ninnekah,_Oklahoma" title="Ninnekah, Oklahoma">Ninnekah</a> rose from 87.9 to 101.4 °F (31 to 39 °C) in 40 minutes. In addition, wind damage was reported as winds gusted to 95 mph (153 km/h) in <a href="/wiki/Lawton,_Oklahoma" title="Lawton, Oklahoma">Lawton</a>, 67 mph (108 km/h) in Ninnekah, and 63 mph (101 km/h) in Chickasha.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Barcelona" title="Barcelona">Barcelona</a>, <a href="/wiki/Catalonia" title="Catalonia">Catalonia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">Spain</a>: (4–5 August 1993 &amp; 2 July 1994) Heat bursts with temperature rises over 13 °C (55 °F) and wind gusts of more than 44 knots (51 mph) at <a href="/wiki/Barcelona%E2%80%93El_Prat_Airport" title="Barcelona–El Prat Airport">Barcelona–El Prat Airport</a>, record maximum temperature at the airport and the spread of forest fires.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Extreme_cases">Extreme cases</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Heat_burst&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Extreme cases">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>These are cases when temperatures over 56.7 °C or 134 °F (the highest officially confirmed in the world, in <a href="/wiki/Death_Valley" title="Death Valley">Death Valley</a>, <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>, 1913) were recorded during heat bursts.</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Cherokee,_Oklahoma" title="Cherokee, Oklahoma">Cherokee, Oklahoma</a>, 11 July 1909: at 3:00 in the morning, a heat burst south of <a href="/wiki/Cherokee,_Oklahoma" title="Cherokee, Oklahoma">Cherokee, Oklahoma</a> reportedly caused the temperature to rise briefly to 57.8 °C (136.0 °F), desiccating crops in the area.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Kopperl,_Texas" title="Kopperl, Texas">Kopperl, Texas</a>, United States, 1960: A heat burst is claimed to have sent the air temperature to near 140 °F (60 °C)<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Accuracy_dispute#Disputed_statement" title="Wikipedia:Accuracy dispute"><span title="The material near this tag is possibly inaccurate or nonfactual. (September 2017)">dubious</span></a> <span class="metadata">– <a href="/wiki/Talk:Heat_burst#Dubious" title="Talk:Heat burst">discuss</a></span></i>]</sup>, supposedly causing cotton crops to become desiccated and drying out vegetation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup></li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lisbon,_Portugal" title="Lisbon, Portugal">Lisbon, Portugal</a>, 6 July 1949: A heat burst reportedly drove the air temperature from 38 to 70 °C (100.4 to 158.0 °F) within two minutes, in the region of Figueira da Foz and Coimbra, in central Portugal.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ExtremeWeather_51-0"><a href="#cite_note-ExtremeWeather-51">[51]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Abadan,_Iran" title="Abadan, Iran">Abadan, Iran</a>, June 1967: An extreme temperature of 86.7 °C (188.1 °F) was recorded during a heat burst.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Accuracy_dispute#Disputed_statement" title="Wikipedia:Accuracy dispute"><span title="The material near this tag is possibly inaccurate or nonfactual. (July 2017)">dubious</span></a> <span class="metadata">– <a href="/wiki/Talk:Heat_burst#Dubious" title="Talk:Heat burst">discuss</a></span></i>]</sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ExtremeWeather_51-1"><a href="#cite_note-ExtremeWeather-51">[51]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
