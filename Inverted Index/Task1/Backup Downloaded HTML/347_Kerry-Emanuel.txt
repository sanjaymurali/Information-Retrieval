<p><b>Kerry Andrew Emanuel</b> (born April 21, 1955) is an <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">American</a> professor of <a href="/wiki/Meteorology" title="Meteorology">meteorology</a> currently working at the <a href="/wiki/Massachusetts_Institute_of_Technology" title="Massachusetts Institute of Technology">Massachusetts Institute of Technology</a> in <a href="/wiki/Cambridge,_Massachusetts" title="Cambridge, Massachusetts">Cambridge</a>. In particular he has specialized in atmospheric <a href="/wiki/Convection" title="Convection">convection</a> and the mechanisms acting to intensify <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone" title="Tropical cyclone">hurricanes</a>. He was named one of the <a href="/wiki/Time_100" title="Time 100"><i>Time</i> 100</a> influential people of 2006.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> In 2007, he was elected as a member of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._National_Academy_of_Sciences" title="U.S. National Academy of Sciences">U.S. National Academy of Sciences</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>He hypothesized in 1994 about a superpowerful type of hurricane which could be formed if average <a href="/wiki/Sea_surface_temperature" title="Sea surface temperature">sea surface temperature</a> increased another 15C more than it's ever been (see "<a href="/wiki/Hypercane" title="Hypercane">hypercane</a>").</p>
<p>In a March 2008 paper published in the <i><a href="/wiki/Bulletin_of_the_American_Meteorological_Society" title="Bulletin of the American Meteorological Society">Bulletin of the American Meteorological Society</a></i>, he put forward the conclusion that <a href="/wiki/Global_warming" title="Global warming">global warming</a> is likely to increase the intensity but decrease the frequency of hurricane and cyclone activity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Gabriel_Vecchi&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Gabriel Vecchi (page does not exist)">Gabriel Vecchi</a>, of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/NOAA" title="NOAA">NOAA</a> said of Emanuel's announcement, "While his results don't rule out the possibility that global warming has contributed to the recent increase in activity in the Atlantic, they suggest that other factors—possibly in addition to global warming—are likely to have been substantial contributors to the observed increase in activity."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2013, with other leading experts, he was co-author of an open letter to policy makers, which stated that "continued opposition to nuclear power threatens humanity's ability to avoid dangerous climate change."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>Along with Daniel H. Rothman, Emanuel co-founded the MIT Lorenz Center, named for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Edward_N._Lorenz" title="Edward N. Lorenz">Edward N. Lorenz</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
