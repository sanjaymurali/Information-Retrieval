<p>The <b>180th meridian</b> or <b>antimeridian</b><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> is the <a href="/wiki/Meridian_(geography)" title="Meridian (geography)">meridian</a> 180° east or west of the <a href="/wiki/Prime_meridian#International_prime_meridian" title="Prime meridian">Prime Meridian</a>, with which it forms a <a href="/wiki/Great_circle" title="Great circle">great circle</a> dividing the earth into the <a href="/wiki/Western_Hemisphere" title="Western Hemisphere">Western</a> and <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Hemisphere" title="Eastern Hemisphere">Eastern Hemispheres</a>. It is common to both east <a href="/wiki/Longitude" title="Longitude">longitude</a> and west longitude. It is used as the basis for the <a href="/wiki/International_Date_Line" title="International Date Line">International Date Line</a> because it mostly passes through the open waters of the <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Ocean" title="Pacific Ocean">Pacific Ocean</a>. However, the meridian passes through <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fiji" title="Fiji">Fiji</a> as well as <a href="/wiki/Antarctica" title="Antarctica">Antarctica</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Software_representation_problems">Software representation problems</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=180th_meridian&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Software representation problems">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Many geographic software libraries or data formats project the world to a rectangle; very often this rectangle is split exactly at the 180th meridian. This often makes it non-trivial to do simple tasks (like representing an area, or a line) over the 180th meridian. Some examples:</p>
<ul>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/GeoJSON" title="GeoJSON">GeoJSON</a> specification strongly suggests splitting geometries so that neither of their parts cross the antimeridian.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></li>
<li>In <a href="/wiki/OpenStreetMap" title="OpenStreetMap">OpenStreetMap</a>, areas (like the boundary of Russia) are split at the 180th meridian.</li>
</ul>
