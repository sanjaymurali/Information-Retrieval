<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Australian region tropical cyclone</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">
					Jump to:					<a href="#mw-head">navigation</a>, 					<a href="#p-search">search</a>
</div>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><p>An <b>Australian tropical cyclone</b> is a non <a href="/wiki/Weather_front" title="Weather front">frontal</a>, <a href="/wiki/Low-pressure_area" title="Low-pressure area">low pressure system</a> that has developed, within an environment of warm <a href="/wiki/Sea_surface_temperature" title="Sea surface temperature">sea surface temperatures</a> and little vertical <a href="/wiki/Wind_shear" title="Wind shear">wind shear</a> aloft in either the Southern <a href="/wiki/Indian_Ocean" title="Indian Ocean">Indian Ocean</a> or the South <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Ocean" title="Pacific Ocean">Pacific Ocean</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SPAC_TCOP_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-SPAC_TCOP-1">[1]</a></sup> Within the Southern Hemisphere there are officially three areas where tropical cyclones develop on a regular basis, these areas are the South-West Indian Ocean between Africa and 90°E, the Australian region between 90°E and 160°E and the South Pacific basin between 160°E and 120°W. The Australian region between 90°E and 160°E is officially monitored by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Australian_Bureau_of_Meteorology" title="Australian Bureau of Meteorology">Australian Bureau of Meteorology</a>, the Papua New Guinea National Weather Service and the Badan Meteorologi Klimatologi dan Geofisika, while others like the <a href="/wiki/Fiji_Meteorological_Service" title="Fiji Meteorological Service">Fiji Meteorological Service</a> and the United States <a href="/wiki/National_Oceanic_and_Atmospheric_Administration" title="National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration">National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration</a> also monitor the basin. Each tropical cyclone year within this basin starts on July 1 and runs throughout the year, encompassing the tropical cyclone season which runs from November 1 and lasts until April 30 each season. Within the basin, most tropical cyclones have their origins within the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/South_Pacific_Convergence_Zone" title="South Pacific Convergence Zone">South Pacific Convergence Zone</a> or within the Northern Australian monsoon trough, both of which form an extensive area of cloudiness and are dominant features of the season. Within this region a tropical disturbance is classified as a tropical cyclone, when it has 10-minute sustained wind speeds of more than 65 km/h (35 mph), that wrap halfway around the low level circulation centre, while a severe tropical cyclone is classified when the maximum 10-minute sustained wind speeds are greater than 120 km/h (75 mph).</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Basin_history">Basin history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Australian_region_tropical_cyclone&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Basin history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>There is a history of tropical cyclones affecting northeastern Australia for over 5000 years; however, <a href="/wiki/Clement_Lindley_Wragge" title="Clement Lindley Wragge">Clement Lindley Wragge</a> was the first person to monitor and name them.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the early history of tropical cyclones in the Australian region, the only evidence of a storm was based on ship reports and observations from land. Later, satellite imagery began in the basin in the 1959/60 season, although it was not continuous until 1970. In Western Australia in particular, the lack of population centers, shipping lanes, radars, and offshore stations meant that storms were tracked infrequently. After the onset of satellite imagery, the <a href="/wiki/Dvorak_technique" title="Dvorak technique">Dvorak technique</a> was used to estimate storm's intensities and locations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
