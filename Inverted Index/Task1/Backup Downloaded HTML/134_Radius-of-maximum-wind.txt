<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Radius of maximum wind</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>

The radius of maximum wind of a tropical cyclone lies just within the eyewall of an intense tropical cyclone, such as <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Isabel" title="Hurricane Isabel">Hurricane Isabel</a> from 2003</div>
</div>

<p>The <b>radius of maximum wind</b> (<b>RMW</b>) is the distance between the center of a <a href="/wiki/Cyclone" title="Cyclone">cyclone</a> and its band of strongest <a href="/wiki/Wind" title="Wind">winds</a>. It is a parameter in atmospheric dynamics and <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone_forecasting" title="Tropical cyclone forecasting">tropical cyclone forecasting</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HSU_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-HSU-1">[1]</a></sup> The highest <a href="/wiki/Rain" title="Rain">rainfall</a> rates occur near the RMW of <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone" title="Tropical cyclone">tropical cyclones</a>. The extent of a cyclone's <a href="/wiki/Storm_surge" title="Storm surge">storm surge</a> and its maximum potential intensity can be determined using the RMW. As <a href="/wiki/Maximum_sustained_wind" title="Maximum sustained wind">maximum sustained winds</a> increase, the RMW decreases. Recently, RMW has been used in descriptions of <a href="/wiki/Tornado" title="Tornado">tornadoes</a>. When designing <a href="/wiki/Building" title="Building">buildings</a> to prevent against failure from atmospheric pressure change, RMW can be used in the calculations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Determination">Determination</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Radius_of_maximum_wind&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Determination">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The RMW is traditionally measured by reconnaissance aircraft in the Atlantic basin.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HSU_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-HSU-1">[1]</a></sup> It can also be determined on <a href="/wiki/Surface_weather_analysis" title="Surface weather analysis">weather maps</a> as the distance between the cyclone center and the system's greatest pressure gradient.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Using <a href="/wiki/Weather_satellite" title="Weather satellite">weather satellite</a> data, the distance between the coldest cloud top temperature and the warmest temperatature within the <a href="/wiki/Eye_(cyclone)" title="Eye (cyclone)">eye</a>, in infrared satellite imagery, is one method of determining RMW. The reason why this method has merit is that the strongest winds within <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone" title="Tropical cyclone">tropical cyclones</a> tend to be located under the deepest <a href="/wiki/Convection" title="Convection">convection</a>, which is seen on satellite imagery as the coldest cloud tops.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HSU_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-HSU-1">[1]</a></sup> Use of velocity data from Doppler <a href="/wiki/Weather_radar" title="Weather radar">weather radar</a> can also be used to determine this quantity, both for tornadoes and tropical cyclones near the coast.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Tornadoes">Tornadoes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Radius_of_maximum_wind&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Tornadoes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:05june-rapiddow-wide.gif"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="832" data-file-width="1914" height="130" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a8/05june-rapiddow-wide.gif/300px-05june-rapiddow-wide.gif" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a8/05june-rapiddow-wide.gif/450px-05june-rapiddow-wide.gif 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a8/05june-rapiddow-wide.gif/600px-05june-rapiddow-wide.gif 2x" width="300"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:05june-rapiddow-wide.gif" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Radar imagery of a tornado and associated mesocyclone in <a href="/wiki/Tornadoes_of_2009#June_5" title="Tornadoes of 2009">Wyoming on June 5, 2009</a>. Reflectivity data on the left show the calm interior of the tornado. Velocity data on the right show where the strongest winds are located.</div>
</div>
</div>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Tornado" title="Tornado">Tornado</a></div>
<p>In the case of tornadoes, knowledge of the RMW is important as atmospheric pressure change (APC) within sealed buildings can cause failure of the structure. Most buildings have openings totaling one square foot per 1,000-cubic-foot (28 m<sup>3</sup>) volume to help equalize air pressure between the inside and outside of the structures. The APC is around one-half of its maximum value at the RMW, which normally ranges between 150 feet (46 m) and 500 feet (150 m) from the center (or eye) of the tornado.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> The widest tornado as measured by actual radar wind measurements was the <a href="/wiki/Mulhall,_Oklahoma" title="Mulhall, Oklahoma">Mulhall</a> tornado in northern Oklahoma, part of the <a href="/wiki/1999_Oklahoma_tornado_outbreak" title="1999 Oklahoma tornado outbreak">1999 Oklahoma tornado outbreak</a>, which had a radius of maximum wind of over 800 metres (2,600 ft).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mulhall_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Mulhall-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Tropical_cyclones">Tropical cyclones</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Radius_of_maximum_wind&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Tropical cyclones">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone" title="Tropical cyclone">Tropical cyclone</a>, <a href="/wiki/Storm_surge" title="Storm surge">Storm surge</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ocean_swell" title="Ocean swell">Ocean swell</a></div>
<p>An average value for the RMW of 47 kilometres (29 mi) was calculated as the mean (or average) of all hurricanes with a lowest central <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_pressure" title="Atmospheric pressure">atmospheric pressure</a> between a pressure of 909 hectopascals (26.8 inHg) and 993 hectopascals (29.3 inHg).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> As tropical cyclones intensify, maximum sustained winds increase as the RMW decreases.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wil_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Wil-7">[7]</a></sup> However, values for RMW produced based on central pressure or maximum wind speed could be substantial scattering around the regression lines.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tak_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Tak-8">[8]</a></sup> The heaviest rainfall within intense tropical cyclones has been observed in the vicinity of the RMW.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>The radius of maximum wind helps determine the direct strikes of tropical cyclones. Tropical cyclones are considered to have made a direct strike to a landmass when a tropical cyclone passes close enough to a landmass that areas inside the radius of maximum wind are experienced on land.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> The radius of maximum wind is used within the maximum potential intensity equation. The Emanuel equation for Maximum Intensity Potential relies upon the winds near the RMW of a tropical cyclone to determine its ultimate potential.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>The highest storm surge is normally coincident with the radius of maximum wind. Because the strongest winds within a tropical cyclone lie at the RMW, this is the region of a tropical cyclone which generates the dominant waves near the storm, and ultimately <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ocean_swell" title="Ocean swell">ocean swell</a> away from the cyclone.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> Tropical cyclones mix the ocean water within a radius three times that of the RMW, which lowers sea surface temperatures due to <a href="/wiki/Upwelling" title="Upwelling">upwelling</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wil_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-Wil-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>Much is still unknown about the radius of maximum wind in tropical cyclones, including whether or not it can be predictable.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
