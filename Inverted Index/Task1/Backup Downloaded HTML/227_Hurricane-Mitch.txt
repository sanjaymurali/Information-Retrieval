<p><b>Hurricane Mitch</b> was the second deadliest <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_hurricane" title="Atlantic hurricane">Atlantic hurricane</a> on record. The storm was the thirteenth tropical storm, ninth hurricane, and third major hurricane of the <a href="/wiki/1998_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="1998 Atlantic hurricane season">1998 hurricane season</a>. Along with <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Georges" title="Hurricane Georges">Hurricane Georges</a>, Mitch was the most notable hurricane in the season. At the time, Hurricane Mitch was the strongest <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/North_Atlantic_tropical_cyclone" title="North Atlantic tropical cyclone">Atlantic hurricane</a> observed in the month of October, though it has since been surpassed by <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Wilma" title="Hurricane Wilma">Hurricane Wilma</a> of the <a href="/wiki/2005_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="2005 Atlantic hurricane season">2005 season</a>. The hurricane matched the fourth most intense Atlantic hurricane on record (it has since dropped to seventh).</p>
<p>Mitch formed in the western <a href="/wiki/Caribbean_Sea" title="Caribbean Sea">Caribbean Sea</a> on October 22, 1998, and after drifting through extremely favorable conditions, it rapidly strengthened to peak at Category 5 status, the highest possible rating on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saffir%E2%80%93Simpson_Hurricane_Scale" title="Saffir–Simpson Hurricane Scale">Saffir–Simpson Hurricane Scale</a>. After drifting southwestward and weakening, the hurricane hit <a href="/wiki/Honduras" title="Honduras">Honduras</a> as a minimal hurricane. It drifted through <a href="/wiki/Central_America" title="Central America">Central America</a>, reformed in the <a href="/wiki/Bay_of_Campeche" title="Bay of Campeche">Bay of Campeche</a>, and ultimately struck <a href="/wiki/Florida" title="Florida">Florida</a> as a strong tropical storm.</p>
<p>Due to its slow motion from October 29 to November 3, Hurricane Mitch dropped historic amounts of rainfall in <a href="/wiki/Honduras" title="Honduras">Honduras</a>, <a href="/wiki/Guatemala" title="Guatemala">Guatemala</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Nicaragua" title="Nicaragua">Nicaragua</a>, with unofficial reports of up to 75 inches (1,900 mm). Deaths due to catastrophic flooding made it the second deadliest Atlantic hurricane in history after the <a href="/wiki/Great_Hurricane_of_1780" title="Great Hurricane of 1780">Great Hurricane of 1780</a>; nearly 11,000 people were killed with over 11,000 left missing by the end of 1998. Additionally, roughly 2.7 million were left homeless as a result of the hurricane. The flooding caused extreme damage, estimated at over $6 billion (1998 <a href="/wiki/United_States_dollar" title="United States dollar">USD</a>).</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Meteorological_history">Meteorological history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hurricane_Mitch&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Meteorological history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Meteorological_history_of_Hurricane_Mitch" title="Meteorological history of Hurricane Mitch">Meteorological history of Hurricane Mitch</a></div>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Mitch_1998_track.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="642" data-file-width="1024" height="138" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d6/Mitch_1998_track.png/220px-Mitch_1998_track.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d6/Mitch_1998_track.png/330px-Mitch_1998_track.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d6/Mitch_1998_track.png/440px-Mitch_1998_track.png 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Mitch_1998_track.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Map plotting the track and intensity of the storm, according to the <a href="/wiki/Saffir%E2%80%93Simpson_scale" title="Saffir–Simpson scale">Saffir–Simpson scale</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Tropical Depression Thirteen formed on October 22 over the southwestern Caribbean Sea, from a <a href="/wiki/Tropical_wave" title="Tropical wave">tropical wave</a> that exited Africa on October 10. It executed a small loop, and while doing so intensified into Tropical Storm Mitch. A weakness in a <a href="/wiki/Ridge_(meteorology)" title="Ridge (meteorology)">ridge</a> allowed the storm to track slowly to the north. After becoming disorganized due to <a href="/wiki/Wind_shear" title="Wind shear">wind shear</a> from an <a href="/wiki/Cold-core_low" title="Cold-core low">upper-level low</a>, Mitch quickly intensified in response to favorable conditions,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nhc_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-nhc-1">[1]</a></sup> including warm waters and good <a href="/wiki/Outflow_(meteorology)" title="Outflow (meteorology)">outflow</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> It became a hurricane on October 24 and developed an <a href="/wiki/Eye_(cyclone)" title="Eye (cyclone)">eye</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> After turning to the west, Mitch <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rapid_deepening" title="Rapid deepening">rapidly intensified</a>, first into a <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone_scales#Atlantic_and_East_Pacific" title="Tropical cyclone scales">major hurricane</a> on October 25 and then into a <a href="/wiki/List_of_Category_5_Atlantic_hurricanes" title="List of Category 5 Atlantic hurricanes">Category 5</a> on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saffir-Simpson_Hurricane_Scale" title="Saffir-Simpson Hurricane Scale">Saffir-Simpson Hurricane Scale</a> the next day.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nhc_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-nhc-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>At peak intensity, Mitch maintained <a href="/wiki/Maximum_sustained_wind" title="Maximum sustained wind">maximum sustained winds</a> of 180 mph (285 km/h) while off the northern coast of Honduras. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hurricane_Hunters" title="Hurricane Hunters">Hurricane Hunters</a> reported a minimum <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_pressure" title="Atmospheric pressure">barometric pressure</a> of 905 mbar (26.7 inHg), which at the time was the lowest in the month of October and tied for the fourth lowest for any <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_hurricane" title="Atlantic hurricane">Atlantic hurricane</a>. Initially, the <a href="/wiki/National_Hurricane_Center" title="National Hurricane Center">National Hurricane Center</a> (NHC) and various <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone_forecast_model" title="Tropical cyclone forecast model">tropical cyclone forecast models</a> anticipated a turn to the north, threatening the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Yucat%C3%A1n_peninsula" title="Yucatán peninsula">Yucatán peninsula</a>. Instead, Mitch turned to the south due to a ridge that was not observed while the storm was active.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nhc_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-nhc-1">[1]</a></sup> Land interaction imparted weakening,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> and the hurricane made <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Landfall_(meteorology)" title="Landfall (meteorology)">landfall</a> on Honduras on October 29 with winds of 80 mph (130 km/h).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nhc_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-nhc-1">[1]</a></sup> Mitch slowly weakened while turning to the west over land, maintaining deep <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Convection_(meteorology)" title="Convection (meteorology)">convection</a> over waters.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> After moving across mountainous terrain in Central America, the surface circulation of Mitch dissipated on November 1. The next day, the remnants reached the Gulf of Mexico, which reorganized into a tropical storm on November 3. Mitch accelerated to the northeast ahead of a <a href="/wiki/Cold_front" title="Cold front">cold front</a>, moving across the Yucatán peninsula before striking near <a href="/wiki/Naples,_Florida" title="Naples, Florida">Naples, Florida</a> on November 5. Shortly thereafter, the storm became an <a href="/wiki/Extratropical_cyclone" title="Extratropical cyclone">extratropical cyclone</a>, which was tracked by the NHC until November 9.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nhc_1-4"><a href="#cite_note-nhc-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<div style="clear:both;"></div>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Preparations">Preparations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hurricane_Mitch&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Preparations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Due to the threat, the government of <a href="/wiki/Honduras" title="Honduras">Honduras</a> evacuated some of the 45,000 citizens on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bay_Islands_(department)" title="Bay Islands (department)">Bay Islands</a> and prepared all air and naval resources. The government of Belize issued a purple alert and asked for citizens on offshore islands to leave for the mainland.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-spare_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-spare-7">[7]</a></sup> Because the hurricane threatened to strike near <a href="/wiki/Belize_City" title="Belize City">Belize City</a> as a Category 4 hurricane, much of the city was evacuated in fear of a repeat of <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Hattie" title="Hurricane Hattie">Hurricane Hattie</a> 37 years earlier.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ncdc_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-ncdc-8">[8]</a></sup> Guatemala issued a purple alert as well, recommending boats to stay in port, telling people to prepare or seek shelter, and warning of potential overflowing rivers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-spare_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-spare-7">[7]</a></sup> By the time hurricane Mitch made landfall, numerous people were evacuated along the western Caribbean coastline, including 100,000 in Honduras, 10,000 in Guatemala, and 20,000 in the Mexican state of <a href="/wiki/Quintana_Roo" title="Quintana Roo">Quintana Roo</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-standstill_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-standstill-9">[9]</a></sup></p>

<p>Hurricane Mitch was the deadliest Atlantic hurricane since the <a href="/wiki/Great_Hurricane_of_1780" title="Great Hurricane of 1780">Great Hurricane of 1780</a>, displacing the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Galveston_Hurricane_of_1900" title="Galveston Hurricane of 1900">Galveston Hurricane of 1900</a> as the second-deadliest on record. Nearly 11,000 people were confirmed dead, and almost as many reported missing. Deaths were mostly from <a href="/wiki/Flood" title="Flood">flooding</a> and <a href="/wiki/Landslide" title="Landslide">mudslides</a> in Central America, where the slow-moving hurricane and then <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tropical_storm" title="Tropical storm">tropical storm</a> dropped nearly 36 inches (900 mm) of rain. The flooding and mudslides damaged or destroyed tens of thousands of homes, with total damage amounting to over $5 billion (1998 USD, $6 billion 2006 USD), most of which was in <a href="/wiki/Honduras" title="Honduras">Honduras</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nicaragua" title="Nicaragua">Nicaragua</a>. Prior to Mitch, the deadliest hurricane in Central America was <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hurricane_Fifi" title="Hurricane Fifi">Hurricane Fifi</a> in 1974, which killed an estimated 8,000–10,000.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nhc_1-7"><a href="#cite_note-nhc-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Honduras">Honduras</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hurricane_Mitch&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Honduras">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Effects_of_Hurricane_Mitch_in_Honduras" title="Effects of Hurricane Mitch in Honduras">Effects of Hurricane Mitch in Honduras</a></div>
<p>While offshore northern Honduras, Hurricane Mitch passed over Guanaja island.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nhc_1-8"><a href="#cite_note-nhc-1">[1]</a></sup> High waves eroded northern coastlines and damaged lagoons.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-eclac_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-eclac-16">[16]</a></sup> Most of the <a href="/wiki/Bay_Islands_Department" title="Bay Islands Department">Bay Islands</a> had damage to their water facilities.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-iadb_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-iadb-17">[17]</a></sup> Two days of winds exceeding 200 km/h (120 mph) destroyed nearly all of the plants and trees on Guanaja, uprooting or knocking down almost the entire <a href="/wiki/Mangrove" title="Mangrove">mangrove</a> forest.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nwrc_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-nwrc-18">[18]</a></sup> It is estimated that the hurricane produced waves of 44 ft (13 m) in height.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ncdc2_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-ncdc2-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Mitch-Tegucigalpa_Damage.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="611" data-file-width="814" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cc/Mitch-Tegucigalpa_Damage.JPG/220px-Mitch-Tegucigalpa_Damage.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cc/Mitch-Tegucigalpa_Damage.JPG/330px-Mitch-Tegucigalpa_Damage.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cc/Mitch-Tegucigalpa_Damage.JPG/440px-Mitch-Tegucigalpa_Damage.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Mitch-Tegucigalpa_Damage.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Damage in <a href="/wiki/Tegucigalpa" title="Tegucigalpa">Tegucigalpa</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>While moving slowly for several days offshore Honduras, Hurricane Mitch drew moisture from the Pacific Ocean and the Caribbean Sea, producing high amounts of rainfall of over 300 mm (12 in) per day.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ncdc_8-9"><a href="#cite_note-ncdc-8">[8]</a></sup> The highest official total was 928 mm (36.5 in) at <a href="/wiki/Choluteca,_Choluteca" title="Choluteca, Choluteca">Choluteca</a>, which represented more than half of the annual precipitation average; at the same location, 466.7 mm (18.37 in) fell in a 24-hour period on October 31, which was more than double of the previous record set in 1985.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-un_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-un-20">[20]</a></sup> There were unofficial rainfall totals in Central America as high as 1,900 mm (75 in); rain gauges in mountainous areas were washed away.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ncdc_8-10"><a href="#cite_note-ncdc-8">[8]</a></sup> The high rainfall caused many rivers in the country to overflow "to an unprecedented extent this century", as described by the United Nations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-un_20-1"><a href="#cite_note-un-20">[20]</a></sup> The rainfall collected in rivers, causing extensive river flooding across the country. The greatest depth recorded was 12.5 m (41 ft) on the <a href="/wiki/Ul%C3%BAa_River" title="Ulúa River">Ulúa River</a> near <a href="/wiki/Chinda" title="Chinda">Chinda</a>, whilst the greatest width recorded was 359 m (1,178 ft) on the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=R%C3%ADo_Lean&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Río Lean (page does not exist)">Río Lean</a> near <a href="/wiki/Arizona,_Atl%C3%A1ntida" title="Arizona, Atlántida">Arizona</a>. The rainfall also caused widespread mudslides across the mountainous country.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-flood_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-flood-21">[21]</a></sup> In the country's interior, particularly the southern portion, the high rainfall caused hundreds of <a href="/wiki/Landslide" title="Landslide">landslides</a>, many of them shallow and about 95% in the form of <a href="/wiki/Debris_flow" title="Debris flow">debris flow</a>. However, two <a href="/wiki/Earthflow" title="Earthflow">earthflows</a> caused significant damage near Tegucigalpa.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-land_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-land-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Mitch-San_Juancito_Mudslide.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="611" data-file-width="393" height="342" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c0/Mitch-San_Juancito_Mudslide.JPG/220px-Mitch-San_Juancito_Mudslide.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c0/Mitch-San_Juancito_Mudslide.JPG/330px-Mitch-San_Juancito_Mudslide.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/c/c0/Mitch-San_Juancito_Mudslide.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Mitch-San_Juancito_Mudslide.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Mudslide in <a href="/wiki/San_Juancito,_Honduras" title="San Juancito, Honduras">San Juancito, Honduras</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Hurricane Mitch wrought significant damage to Honduras, affecting nearly the entire population and causing damage in all 18 <a href="/wiki/Departments_of_Honduras" title="Departments of Honduras">departments</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-un_20-2"><a href="#cite_note-un-20">[20]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_Economic_Commission_for_Latin_America_and_the_Caribbean" title="United Nations Economic Commission for Latin America and the Caribbean">United Nations Economic Commission for Latin America and the Caribbean</a> estimated that Mitch caused the worst floods of the 20th century in the country.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-eclac_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-eclac-16">[16]</a></sup> An estimated 70–80% of transportation network was destroyed, including most bridges and secondary roads,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ncdc_8-11"><a href="#cite_note-ncdc-8">[8]</a></sup> amounting to $236 million in damage. Hurricane Mitch left widespread power outages after damaging more than 385 km (239 mi) of power lines and several power plants.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-eclac_16-2"><a href="#cite_note-eclac-16">[16]</a></sup> About 70% of Honduras lost access to fresh water after the storm, although many rural areas had already been experiencing water shortages.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-un_20-3"><a href="#cite_note-un-20">[20]</a></sup> The combined damage to transportation, communication, utilities, including power and water, was estimated at $665 million.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-iadb_17-1"><a href="#cite_note-iadb-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>High water levels along the <a href="/wiki/Choluteca_River" title="Choluteca River">Choluteca River</a> affected the capital, <a href="/wiki/Tegucigalpa" title="Tegucigalpa">Tegucigalpa</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-iadb_17-2"><a href="#cite_note-iadb-17">[17]</a></sup> reaching levels 10 m (33 ft) above their banks.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-un_20-4"><a href="#cite_note-un-20">[20]</a></sup> The floods damaged about one-third of buildings, including some more than 350 years old.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ncdc_8-12"><a href="#cite_note-ncdc-8">[8]</a></sup> Across Honduras, agriculture sustained serious damage, with initial estimates of 70% of crops destroyed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ncdc_8-13"><a href="#cite_note-ncdc-8">[8]</a></sup> About 50,000 <a href="/wiki/Bovinae" title="Bovinae">bovine</a> were killed, as were 60% of the <a href="/wiki/Fowl" title="Fowl">fowl</a> population.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-iadb_17-3"><a href="#cite_note-iadb-17">[17]</a></sup> Crop and agricultural damage totaled about $1 billion,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-eclac_16-3"><a href="#cite_note-eclac-16">[16]</a></sup> which would take the country years to recover.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ncdc_8-14"><a href="#cite_note-ncdc-8">[8]</a></sup> Honduras's Social Fund for Housing estimated that 35,000 houses nationwide were destroyed with another 50,000 damaged,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-iadb_17-4"><a href="#cite_note-iadb-17">[17]</a></sup> leaving 1.5 million people homeless – about 20% of the total population.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ncdc_8-15"><a href="#cite_note-ncdc-8">[8]</a></sup> This was the highest number of victims from any natural disaster in Honduras's history.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-un_20-5"><a href="#cite_note-un-20">[20]</a></sup> Overall, Hurricane Mitch killed about 7,000 people in Honduras,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-un_20-6"><a href="#cite_note-un-20">[20]</a></sup> and damage was estimated at <a href="/wiki/Honduran_lempira" title="Honduran lempira">L</a>52,345,000,000 ($3.8 billion),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-iadb_17-5"><a href="#cite_note-iadb-17">[17]</a></sup> of which $2.005 billion was from direct damages and the remainder from indirect costs. The overall impact represented about 70% of Honduras's annual <a href="/wiki/Gross_domestic_product" title="Gross domestic product">gross domestic product</a> (GDP).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-eclac_16-4"><a href="#cite_note-eclac-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Nicaragua">Nicaragua</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hurricane_Mitch&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Nicaragua">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Mitch-Flooding_in_Managua.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="168" data-file-width="250" height="148" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b3/Mitch-Flooding_in_Managua.jpg/220px-Mitch-Flooding_in_Managua.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/b/b3/Mitch-Flooding_in_Managua.jpg 1.5x" width="220"/></a>

<p>Though Mitch never entered <a href="/wiki/Nicaragua" title="Nicaragua">Nicaragua</a>, its large circulation caused extensive rainfall, with estimates of over 50 inches (1,300 mm).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ncdc_8-16"><a href="#cite_note-ncdc-8">[8]</a></sup> In some places, as much as 25 inches (630 mm) of rain fell on coastal areas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-reports_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-reports-23">[23]</a></sup> The flank of the <a href="/wiki/Geography_of_Nicaragua" title="Geography of Nicaragua">Casita</a> Volcano failed and turned into a <a href="/wiki/Lahar" title="Lahar">lahar</a> from excessive rain. The resulting <a href="/wiki/Landslide" title="Landslide">mudslide</a> ultimately covered an area 10 miles (16 km) long and 5 miles (8 km) wide.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ncdc_8-17"><a href="#cite_note-ncdc-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>Two million people in Nicaragua were directly affected by the hurricane.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ncdc_8-18"><a href="#cite_note-ncdc-8">[8]</a></sup> Across the country, Mitch's heavy rains damaged 17,600 houses and destroyed 23,900, displacing 368,300 of the population.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-usgs_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-usgs-24">[24]</a></sup> 340 schools and 90 health centers were severely damaged or destroyed. Sewage systems and the electricity subsector were severely damaged, and, combined with property, damage totaled to $300 million (1998 USD).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nicdam_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-nicdam-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Mitch-_Casita_Mudslide.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="594" data-file-width="810" height="161" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1b/Mitch-_Casita_Mudslide.JPG/220px-Mitch-_Casita_Mudslide.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1b/Mitch-_Casita_Mudslide.JPG/330px-Mitch-_Casita_Mudslide.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1b/Mitch-_Casita_Mudslide.JPG/440px-Mitch-_Casita_Mudslide.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Mitch-_Casita_Mudslide.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Casita volcano in western <a href="/wiki/Nicaragua" title="Nicaragua">Nicaragua</a> after deadly mudslide</div>

</div>
<p>Transportation was greatly affected by the hurricane, as well. The rainfall left 70% of the roads unusable or destroyed and greatly damaged 92 bridges.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nic_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-nic-26">[26]</a></sup> Over 1,700 miles (2700 km) of highways or access roads needed replacement subsequent to the storm, especially in the northern part of the country and along portions of the <a href="/wiki/Pan-American_Highway" title="Pan-American Highway">Pan-American Highway</a>. Total transportation damage amounted to $300 million (1998 USD). Agricultural losses were significant, including the deaths of 50,000 animals, mostly bovines. Crops and fisheries were affected greatly as well, and, combined with agricultural losses, damage totaled to $185 million (1998 USD).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nicdam_25-1"><a href="#cite_note-nicdam-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<p>The situation was further compounded by a total of 75,000 live <a href="/wiki/Land_mine" title="Land mine">land mines</a>—left over from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Contra_(guerrillas)" title="Contra (guerrillas)">Contra</a> insurgency of the 1980s—that were calculated to have been uprooted and relocated by the floodwaters.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-landmine_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-landmine-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<p>In all, Hurricane Mitch caused at least 3,800 fatalities in Nicaragua, of which more than 2,000 were killed in the towns of El Provenir and Rolando Rodriguez from the landslide at the Casita volcano. The mudslide buried at least four villages completely in several feet of mud. Throughout the entire country, the hurricane left between 500,000 and 800,000 homeless. In all, damage in Nicaragua is estimated at around $1 billion (1998 USD).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ncdc_8-19"><a href="#cite_note-ncdc-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Caribbean_Sea">Caribbean Sea</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hurricane_Mitch&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Caribbean Sea">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Mitch was also responsible for the loss with all hands of <a href="/wiki/Windjammer_Barefoot_Cruises" title="Windjammer Barefoot Cruises">Windjammer Barefoot Cruises</a>' <a href="/wiki/Schooner" title="Schooner">schooner</a> <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/S/V_Fantome" title="S/V Fantome">Fantome</a></i>. The story was recorded in the book <i>The Ship and The Storm</i> by Jim Carrier. The schooner, which was sailing near the center of the hurricane, experienced over 50 foot (15 m) waves and over 100 mph (160 km/h) winds, causing her to sink off the coast of Honduras.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fantome_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-fantome-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<p>On the south coast of <a href="/wiki/Cuba" title="Cuba">Cuba</a>, the hurricane caused waves of up to 13 feet (4 m) high and winds gusts peaking at 42 mph (67 km/h), causing numerous tourists and workers on the <a href="/wiki/Isla_de_la_Juventud" title="Isla de la Juventud">Isle of Youth</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cayo_Largo_del_Sur" title="Cayo Largo del Sur">Cayo Largo del Sur</a> to leave for safer grounds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mitchcar_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-mitchcar-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Jamaica" title="Jamaica">Jamaica</a>, where officials declared hurricane warnings 12 hours prior to its closest approach,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nhc_1-9"><a href="#cite_note-nhc-1">[1]</a></sup> Mitch caused moderate rainfall and gusty winds for days. Strong waves hit western Jamaica, with wave heights unofficially estimated at nearly 7 feet (2 m) in height. The rainfall in outer rainbands, at times severe, flooded many roads across the island and left them covered with debris. One house in <a href="/wiki/Spanish_Town" title="Spanish Town">Spanish Town</a> collapsed from the flooding, leaving four homeless. Many other homes and buildings were flooded, forcing many to evacuate. A river in northeastern Jamaica overflowed its banks, while heavy rainfall across the mountainous parts of the country caused numerous mudslides.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-unofjam_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-unofjam-30">[30]</a></sup> In all, Mitch killed three people on Jamaica.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ncdc_8-20"><a href="#cite_note-ncdc-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>On the <a href="/wiki/Cayman_Islands" title="Cayman Islands">Cayman Islands</a>, the hurricane caused strong waves, gusty winds, and heavy rainfall at times. Damage was relatively minimal, amounting to blown out windows and beach erosion. Strong waves damaged or destroyed many docks on the south shore of the islands, and also sank one dive ship near <a href="/wiki/Grand_Cayman" title="Grand Cayman">Grand Cayman</a>. In addition, numerous incoming and outgoing flights were cancelled.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-unofcayman_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-unofcayman-31">[31]</a></sup></p>

<p>Due to Mitch's large circulation, it dropped heavy precipitation as far south as <a href="/wiki/Panama" title="Panama">Panama</a>, especially in the <a href="/wiki/Dari%C3%A9n_Province" title="Darién Province">Darién</a> and <a href="/wiki/Chiriqu%C3%AD_Province" title="Chiriquí Province">Chiriquí</a> provinces. The flooding washed away a few roads and bridges, and damaged numerous houses and schools, leaving thousands homeless.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Panama_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-Panama-32">[32]</a></sup> The hurricane left three casualties in Panama.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ncdc_8-21"><a href="#cite_note-ncdc-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Costa_Rica" title="Costa Rica">Costa Rica</a>, Mitch dropped heavy rains, causing flash flooding and mudslides across the country, mostly in the northeastern part of the country.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ncdc_8-22"><a href="#cite_note-ncdc-8">[8]</a></sup> The storm impacted 2,135 homes to some degree, of which 241 were destroyed,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-iadbcr_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-iadbcr-11">[11]</a></sup> leaving 4,000 homeless.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-reports_23-1"><a href="#cite_note-reports-23">[23]</a></sup> Throughout the country, the rainfall and mudslides affected 126 bridges and 800 miles (1,300 km) or roads, mostly on the <a href="/wiki/Pan-American_Highway" title="Pan-American Highway">Inter-American Highway</a> which was affected by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hurricane_Cesar-Douglas" title="Hurricane Cesar-Douglas">Hurricane Cesar</a>, two years prior. Mitch affected 115 sq. miles (300 km<sup>2</sup>) of crop lands, causing damage to both export and domestic crops. In all, Hurricane Mitch caused $92 million in damage (1998 USD)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-iadbcr_11-2"><a href="#cite_note-iadbcr-11">[11]</a></sup> and seven deaths.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ncdc_8-23"><a href="#cite_note-ncdc-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>While drifting through <a href="/wiki/El_Salvador" title="El Salvador">El Salvador</a>, the hurricane dropped immense amounts of precipitation, resulting in flash flooding and mudslides through the country. Multiple rivers, including the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/R%C3%ADo_Grande_de_San_Miguel" title="Río Grande de San Miguel">Río Grande de San Miguel</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Lempa_River" title="Lempa River">Lempa River</a> overflowed, contributing to overall damage. The flooding damaged more than 10,000 houses, leaving around 84,000 homeless<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-iadbes_12-2"><a href="#cite_note-iadbes-12">[12]</a></sup> and forcing 500,000 to evacuate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bbc_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-bbc-33">[33]</a></sup> Crop damage was severe, with serious flooding occurring on 386 sq. miles (1000&amp; km<sup>2</sup>) of pasture or crop land. The flooding destroyed 37% of the bean production, 19% of the corn production, and 20% losses in sugar canes. There were heavy losses in livestock as well, including the deaths of 10,000 cattle. Total agricultural and livestock damaged amounted to $154 million (1998 USD). In addition, the flooding destroyed two bridges and damaged 1,200 mi (1,900 km) of unpaved roads. In all, Mitch caused nearly $400 million in damage (1998 USD) and 240 deaths.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-iadbes_12-3"><a href="#cite_note-iadbes-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>Similar to the rest of Central America, Mitch's heavy rains caused mudslides and severe flooding over <a href="/wiki/Guatemala" title="Guatemala">Guatemala</a>. The flooding destroyed 6,000 houses and damaged 20,000 others, displacing over 730,000 and forcing over 100,000 to evacuate. In addition, the flooding destroyed 27 schools and damaged 286 others, 175 severely. Flooding caused major damage to crops, while landslides destroyed crop land across the country. The most severely affected crops for domestic consumption were tomatoes, bananas, corn, other vegetables, and beans, with damaged totaling to $48 million (1998 USD). Export crops such as bananas or coffee were greatly damaged as well, with damage amounting to $325 million (1998 USD). Damage to plantations and soil totaled to $121 million (1998 USD). The flooding also caused severe damage to the transportation infrastructure, including the loss of 37 bridges. Across the country, flooding damaged or destroyed 840 miles (1350 km) of roads, of which nearly 400 miles (640 km) were sections of major highways. In all, Hurricane Mitch caused $748 million (1998 USD) and 268 deaths in Guatemala.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-iadbg_13-2"><a href="#cite_note-iadbg-13">[13]</a></sup> In addition, Mitch caused 11 indirect deaths when a plane crashed during the storm.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bbc_33-1"><a href="#cite_note-bbc-33">[33]</a></sup></p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Belize" title="Belize">Belize</a>, the hurricane was less severe than initially predicted, though Mitch still caused heavy rainfall across the country.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bbc_33-2"><a href="#cite_note-bbc-33">[33]</a></sup> Numerous rivers exceeded their crests, though the rainfall was beneficial to trees in mountainous areas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-unofbe_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-unofbe-34">[34]</a></sup> The flooding caused extensive crop damage and destroyed many roads. Throughout the country, eleven people died because of the hurricane.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bbc_33-3"><a href="#cite_note-bbc-33">[33]</a></sup></p>

<p>In <a href="/wiki/Mexico" title="Mexico">Mexico</a>, Mitch produced gusty winds and heavy rains on the <a href="/wiki/Yucat%C3%A1n_Peninsula" title="Yucatán Peninsula">Yucatán Peninsula</a>, with <a href="/wiki/Canc%C3%BAn" title="Cancún">Cancún</a> on the <a href="/wiki/Quintana_Roo" title="Quintana Roo">Quintana Roo</a> coast being the worst hit.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bbc_33-4"><a href="#cite_note-bbc-33">[33]</a></sup> Nine people were killed from the flooding, though damage was relatively minimal.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ncdc_8-24"><a href="#cite_note-ncdc-8">[8]</a></sup> The maximum 24-hour rainfall total from Mitch was 13.4 inches (340 mm) in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Campeche,_Campeche" title="Campeche, Campeche">Campeche</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> while the highest rainfall total was 16.85 inches (428.0 mm) in <a href="/wiki/Ciudad_del_Carmen" title="Ciudad del Carmen">Ciudad del Carmen</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rain_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-rain-36">[36]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Florida_and_remnants">Florida and remnants</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hurricane_Mitch&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Florida and remnants">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Then a tropical storm, Mitch caused a storm surge of up to 4 feet (1.2 m) in the lower <a href="/wiki/Florida_Keys" title="Florida Keys">Florida Keys</a> before making landfall on the <a href="/wiki/Florida" title="Florida">Florida</a> west coast. <a href="/wiki/Key_West_International_Airport" title="Key West International Airport">Key West International Airport</a> reported peak wind gusts of 55 mph (89 km/h) and sustained winds of 40 mph (64 km/h), the only report of tropical storm force in the state.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nhc_1-10"><a href="#cite_note-nhc-1">[1]</a></sup> Offshore, the <a href="/wiki/Fowey_Rocks_Light" title="Fowey Rocks Light">Fowey Rocks Light</a> reported a wind gusts of 73 mph (117 km/h).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> In addition, Mitch caused moderate rainfall, peaking at 7 inches (200 mm) in <a href="/wiki/Jupiter,_Florida" title="Jupiter, Florida">Jupiter</a>, though some estimates indicate localized totals of up to 10 inches (250 mm). The storm spawned five tornadoes over the state, the strongest of which was an <a href="/wiki/Fujita_scale" title="Fujita scale">F2</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nhc_1-11"><a href="#cite_note-nhc-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the Florida Keys, multiple buildings that had been damaged by <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Georges" title="Hurricane Georges">Hurricane Georges</a> were leveled by Mitch.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ncdc_8-25"><a href="#cite_note-ncdc-8">[8]</a></sup> Tornadoes from the storm damaged or destroyed 645 houses across the state, in addition to injuring 65 people.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nhc_1-12"><a href="#cite_note-nhc-1">[1]</a></sup> Gusty winds left 100,000 without power during the storm's passage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ncdc_8-26"><a href="#cite_note-ncdc-8">[8]</a></sup> In all, Mitch caused $40 million in damage (1998 USD) in Florida and two deaths from drowning when two boats capsized.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nhc_1-13"><a href="#cite_note-nhc-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>As an extratropical cyclone, Mitch passed west of <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Ireland" title="Republic of Ireland">Ireland</a> and the United Kingdom. In Ireland, the storm produced gusts as high as 90 mph (140 km/h) and 30 ft (9.1 m) waves. The winds knocked down trees and power lines, leaving over 30,000 homes without power. One tree fell onto a car in <a href="/wiki/County_Louth" title="County Louth">Louth</a>, severely injuring the driver. In <a href="/wiki/Dublin" title="Dublin">Dublin</a>, high winds knocked the roof off of a building, and several other buildings nationwide were damaged. The storm caused airports to close and ferry service to be suspended.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Aftermath">Aftermath</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hurricane_Mitch&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Aftermath">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>Because of the hurricane's destruction in <a href="/wiki/Central_America" title="Central America">Central America</a> and elsewhere in North America, the <a href="/wiki/World_Meteorological_Organization" title="World Meteorological Organization">World Meteorological Organization</a> retired the name <i>Mitch</i> in the spring of 1999; it will never again be used for an Atlantic hurricane. The name was replaced with <i><a href="/wiki/Tropical_Storm_Matthew_(2004)" title="Tropical Storm Matthew (2004)">Matthew</a></i> in the <a href="/wiki/2004_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="2004 Atlantic hurricane season">2004 season</a>, which was <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Matthew" title="Hurricane Matthew">itself retired</a> after the <a href="/wiki/2016_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="2016 Atlantic hurricane season">2016 season</a>.</p>
<p>After the disaster caused by Hurricane Mitch, countries around the world donated significant aid, totaling $6.3 billion (1998 USD). Throughout <a href="/wiki/Central_America" title="Central America">Central America</a>, which was recovering from an economic crisis that occurred in 1996, many wished to continue the growth of the infrastructure and economy. In addition, after witnessing the vulnerability to hurricanes, the affected governments endeavored to prevent such a disaster from occurring again.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-iadbca_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-iadbca-39">[39]</a></sup></p>

<p>Hundreds of thousands of people lost their homes, but many took this as an opportunity to rebuild stronger houses. With a new, structurally improved foundation, homes were redesigned to be able to withstand another hurricane. However, lack of arable crop land took away the jobs from many, decreasing an already low income even lower.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-care.org_40-0"><a href="#cite_note-care.org-40">[40]</a></sup></p>
<p>Following the passage of Mitch, disease outbreaks occurred throughout Central America, including <a href="/wiki/Cholera" title="Cholera">cholera</a>, <a href="/wiki/Leptospirosis" title="Leptospirosis">leptospirosis</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Dengue_fever" title="Dengue fever">dengue fever</a>. Over 2,328 cases of cholera were reported, killing 34 people. Guatemala was most affected by the bacterium, where most of the deaths occurred from contaminated food. 450 cases of leptospirosis were reported in Nicaragua, killing seven people. There were over 1,357 cases of dengue reported, though no deaths were reported from the disease.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-paho_41-0"><a href="#cite_note-paho-41">[41]</a></sup></p>
<p>While stalling over the western Caribbean Sea, Mitch's strong winds produced strong waves, damaging local <a href="/wiki/Coral_reef" title="Coral reef">coral reefs</a>. Later, the storm's immense rainfall led to runoff polluted with debris and fresh water. This resulted in diseases occurring within the coral. However, the hurricane's <a href="/wiki/Upwelling" title="Upwelling">upwelling</a> cooled the warm water temperatures, preventing significant bleaching and destruction of the coral reef.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-coral_42-0"><a href="#cite_note-coral-42">[42]</a></sup></p>
<p>Mitch caused such massive and widespread damage that <a href="/wiki/President_of_Honduras" title="President of Honduras">Honduran President</a> <a href="/wiki/Carlos_Roberto_Flores" title="Carlos Roberto Flores">Carlos Roberto Flores</a> claimed it destroyed fifty years of progress in the country.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ncdc_8-27"><a href="#cite_note-ncdc-8">[8]</a></sup> Honduras, the country most affected by the hurricane, received significant aid for the millions impacted by the hurricane. Mexico quickly gave help, sending 700 tons of food, 11 tons of medicine, four rescue planes, rescue personnel, and trained search dogs. Cuba also volunteered, sending a contingent of physicians to the country.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cubmexaid_43-0"><a href="#cite_note-cubmexaid-43">[43]</a></sup> The U.S. administration offered at first troops stationed in Honduras, and then withdrew them a few days after the storm. They also at first offered only $2 million (1998 USD) in aid, which came as a shock to residents, and president Carlos Roberto Flores alike. The U.S. later increased their offer to $70 million (1998 USD).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-aftermath_44-0"><a href="#cite_note-aftermath-44">[44]</a></sup> The Honduran government distributed food, water, and medical services to the hurricane victims, including the more than 4 million without water.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-iadbca_39-1"><a href="#cite_note-iadbca-39">[39]</a></sup> In addition, the country initially experienced a sharp increase in the unemployment rate, largely due to the destruction of crop lands. However, rebuilding provided jobs in the following years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hondam_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-hondam-15">[15]</a></sup> In <a href="/wiki/Costa_Rica" title="Costa Rica">Costa Rica</a>, reconstruction after the hurricane increased the number of jobs by 5.9%, lowering the unemployment rate slightly.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-iadbcr_11-3"><a href="#cite_note-iadbcr-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
