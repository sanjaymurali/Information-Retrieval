<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Contour line</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

<p>A <b>contour line</b> (also <b>isoline</b>, <b>isopleth</b>, or <b>isarithm</b>) of a <a href="/wiki/Function_(mathematics)" title="Function (mathematics)">function</a> of two variables is a <a href="/wiki/Curve" title="Curve">curve</a> along which the function has a constant value, so that the curve joins points of equal value.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hughes_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hughes-2">[2]</a></sup> It is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cross-section_(geometry)#Definition" title="Cross-section (geometry)">plane section</a> of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Graph_of_a_function_of_two_variables" title="Graph of a function of two variables">three-dimensional graph</a> of the function <i>f</i>(<i>x</i>, <i>y</i>) parallel to the <i>x</i>, <i>y</i> plane. In <a href="/wiki/Cartography" title="Cartography">cartography</a>, a contour line (often just called a "contour") joins points of equal <a href="/wiki/Elevation" title="Elevation">elevation</a> (height) above a given level, such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mean_sea_level" title="Mean sea level">mean sea level</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> A <b>contour map</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Map" title="Map">map</a> illustrated with contour lines, for example a <a href="/wiki/Topographic_map" title="Topographic map">topographic map</a>, which thus shows valleys and hills, and the steepness or gentleness of slopes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> The <b>contour interval</b> of a contour map is the difference in elevation between successive contour lines.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>More generally, a contour line for a function of two variables is a curve connecting points where the function has the same particular value.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hughes_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Hughes-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Gradient" title="Gradient">gradient</a> of the function is always perpendicular to the contour lines. When the lines are close together the magnitude of the gradient is large: the variation is steep. A <a href="/wiki/Level_set" title="Level set">level set</a> is a generalization of a contour line for functions of any number of variables.</p>
<p>Contour lines are curved, straight or a mixture of both lines on a <a href="/wiki/Map" title="Map">map</a> describing the intersection of a real or hypothetical surface with one or more horizontal planes. The configuration of these contours allows map readers to infer relative gradient of a parameter and estimate that parameter at specific places. Contour lines may be either traced on a visible three-dimensional model of the <a href="/wiki/Surface_(mathematics)" title="Surface (mathematics)">surface</a>, as when a photogrammetrist viewing a stereo-model plots elevation contours, or interpolated from estimated surface <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Elevations" title="Elevations">elevations</a>, as when a computer program threads contours through a network of observation points of area centroids. In the latter case, the method of <a href="/wiki/Interpolation" title="Interpolation">interpolation</a> affects the reliability of individual isolines and their portrayal of <a href="/wiki/Slope" title="Slope">slope</a>, pits and peaks.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Types">Types</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Contour_line&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Types">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Contour lines are often given specific names beginning "iso-" (<a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek" title="Ancient Greek">Ancient Greek</a>: <span lang="grc" xml:lang="grc">ἴσος</span> <span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="Ancient Greek transliteration"><i>isos</i></span> "equal") according to the nature of the variable being mapped, although in many usages the phrase "contour line" is most commonly used. Specific names are most common in meteorology, where multiple maps with different variables may be viewed simultaneously. The prefix "iso-" can be replaced with "isallo-" to specify a contour line connecting points where a variable changes at the same <i>rate</i> during a given time period.</p>
<p>The words <i>isoline</i> and <i>isarithm</i> (<span lang="grc" xml:lang="grc">ἀριθμός</span> <i><span lang="grc-Latn" xml:lang="grc-Latn">arithmos</span></i> "number") are general terms covering all types of contour line. The word <i>isogram</i> (<span lang="grc" xml:lang="grc">γράμμα</span> <i><span lang="grc-Latn" xml:lang="grc-Latn">gramma</span></i> "writing or drawing") was proposed by <a href="/wiki/Francis_Galton" title="Francis Galton">Francis Galton</a> in 1889 as a convenient generic designation for lines indicating equality of some physical condition or quantity;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> but it commonly refers to a <a href="/wiki/Isogram" title="Isogram">word without a repeated letter</a>.</p>
<p>An <b>isogon</b> (from <span lang="grc" xml:lang="grc">γωνία</span> or <i>gonia</i>, meaning 'angle') is a contour line for a variable which measures direction. In meteorology and in geomagnetics, the term <i>isogon</i> has specific meanings which are described below. An <b><a href="/wiki/Isocline" title="Isocline">isocline</a></b> (from <span lang="grc" xml:lang="grc">κλίνειν</span> or <i>klinein</i>, meaning 'to lean or slope') is a line joining points with equal slope. In population dynamics and in geomagnetics, the terms <i>isocline</i> and <i>isoclinic line</i> have specific meanings which are described below.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Equidistant_points">Equidistant points</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Contour_line&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Equidistant points">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A curve of equidistant points is a set of points all at the same distance from a given <a href="/wiki/Point_(geometry)" title="Point (geometry)">point</a>, <a href="/wiki/Line_(geometry)" title="Line (geometry)">line</a>, or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Polyline" title="Polyline">polyline</a>. In this case the function whose value is being held constant along a contour line is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Distance_function" title="Distance function">distance function</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Isopleths">Isopleths</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Contour_line&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Isopleths">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In geography, the word <i>isopleth</i> (from <span lang="grc" xml:lang="grc">πλῆθος</span> or <i>plethos</i>, meaning 'quantity') is used for contour lines that depict a variable which cannot be measured at a point, but which instead must be calculated from data collected over an area. An example is <a href="/wiki/Population_density" title="Population density">population density</a>, which can be calculated by dividing the population of a <a href="/wiki/Census_tract" title="Census tract">census district</a> by the surface area of that district. Each calculated value is presumed to be the value of the variable at the centre of the area, and isopleths can then be drawn by a process of <a href="/wiki/Interpolation" title="Interpolation">interpolation</a>. The idea of an isopleth map can be compared with that of a <a href="/wiki/Choropleth_map" title="Choropleth map">choropleth map</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>In meteorology, the word <i>isopleth</i> is used for any type of contour line.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>

<p>Meteorological contour lines are based on <a href="/wiki/Interpolation" title="Interpolation">interpolation</a> of the point data received from <a href="/wiki/Weather_station" title="Weather station">weather stations</a> and <a href="/wiki/Weather_satellite" title="Weather satellite">weather satellites</a>. Weather stations are seldom exactly positioned at a contour line (when they are, this indicates a measurement precisely equal to the value of the contour). Instead, lines are drawn to best approximate the locations of exact values, based on the scattered information points available.</p>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Weather_maps" title="Weather maps">Meteorological contour maps</a> may present collected data such as actual air pressure at a given time, or generalized data such as average pressure over a period of time, or forecast data such as predicted air pressure at some point in the future</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Thermodynamic_diagrams" title="Thermodynamic diagrams">Thermodynamic diagrams</a> use multiple overlapping contour sets (including isobars and isotherms) to present a picture of the major thermodynamic factors in a weather system.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Barometric_pressure">Barometric pressure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Contour_line&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Barometric pressure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Loop_isallobaric_tendencies.gif"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="251" data-file-width="300" height="184" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/df/Loop_isallobaric_tendencies.gif/220px-Loop_isallobaric_tendencies.gif" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/d/df/Loop_isallobaric_tendencies.gif 1.5x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Loop_isallobaric_tendencies.gif" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Loop showing the motion of a cold front by the movement of isallobars</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>An <b>isobar</b> (from <span lang="grc" xml:lang="grc"><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%CE%B2%CE%AC%CF%81%CE%BF%CF%82" title="wikt:βάρος">βάρος</a></span> or <i>baros</i>, meaning 'weight') is a line of equal or constant <a href="/wiki/Pressure" title="Pressure">pressure</a> on a graph, plot, or map; an isopleth or contour line of pressure. More accurately, isobars are lines drawn on a map joining places of equal average atmospheric pressure reduced to sea level for a specified period of time. In <a href="/wiki/Meteorology" title="Meteorology">meteorology</a>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Barometric_pressure" title="Barometric pressure">barometric pressures</a> shown are reduced to <a href="/wiki/Sea_level" title="Sea level">sea level</a>, not the surface pressures at the map locations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> The distribution of isobars is closely related to the magnitude and direction of the <a href="/wiki/Wind" title="Wind">wind</a> field, and can be used to predict future weather patterns. Isobars are commonly used in television weather reporting.</p>
<p><b>Isallobars</b> are lines joining points of equal pressure change during a specific time interval.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OMM_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-OMM-13">[13]</a></sup> These can be divided into <i>anallobars</i>, lines joining points of equal pressure increase during a specific time interval,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OMM-1_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-OMM-1-14">[14]</a></sup> and <i>katallobars</i>, lines joining points of equal pressure decrease.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OMM-2_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-OMM-2-15">[15]</a></sup> In general, weather systems move along an axis joining high and low isallobaric centers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> Isallobaric gradients are important components of the wind as they increase or decrease the <a href="/wiki/Geostrophic_wind" title="Geostrophic wind">geostrophic wind</a>.</p>
<p>An <a href="/wiki/Isopycnal" title="Isopycnal">isopycnal</a> is a line of constant density. An <i>isoheight</i> or <i>isohypse</i> is a line of constant <a href="/wiki/Geopotential" title="Geopotential">geopotential</a> height on a constant pressure surface chart. Isohypse and isoheight are simply known as lines showing equal pressure on a map.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Temperature_and_related_subjects">Temperature and related subjects</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Contour_line&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Temperature and related subjects">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="thumb tright">

<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Arctic.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The 10 °C (50 °F) mean isotherm in July, marked by the red line, is commonly used to define the border of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arctic_region" title="Arctic region">Arctic region</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>An <b>isotherm</b> (from <span lang="grc" xml:lang="grc"><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%CE%B8%CE%AD%CF%81%CE%BC%CE%B7" title="wikt:θέρμη">θέρμη</a></span> or <i>thermē</i>, meaning 'heat') is a line that connects points on a map that have the same <a href="/wiki/Temperature" title="Temperature">temperature</a>. Therefore, all points through which an isotherm passes have the same or equal temperatures at the time indicated.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DataAir_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-DataAir-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hughes_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-Hughes-2">[2]</a></sup> An isotherm at 0 °C is called the <a href="/wiki/Freezing_level" title="Freezing level">freezing level</a>. The term was coined by the Prussian geographer and naturalist Alexander von Humboldt, who as part of his research into the geographical distribution of plants published the first map of isotherms in Paris, in 1817.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>An <b>isogeotherm</b> is a line of equal mean annual temperature. An <b>isocheim</b> is a line of equal mean winter temperature, and an <b>isothere</b> is a line of equal mean summer temperature.</p>
<p>An <b>isohel</b> (from <span lang="grc" xml:lang="grc"><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E1%BC%A5%CE%BB%CE%B9%CE%BF%CF%82" title="wikt:ἥλιος">ἥλιος</a></span> or <i>helios</i>, meaning 'Sun') is a line of equal or constant <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solar_radiation" title="Solar radiation">solar radiation</a>.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Rainfall_and_air_moisture">Rainfall and air moisture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Contour_line&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Rainfall and air moisture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>An <b>isohyet</b> or <b>isohyetal line</b> (from <span lang="grc" xml:lang="grc"><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E1%BD%95%CE%B5%CF%84%CE%BF%CF%82" title="wikt:ὕετος">ὕετος</a></span> or <i>huetos</i>, meaning 'rain') is a line joining points of equal rainfall on a <a href="/wiki/Map" title="Map">map</a> in a given period . A map with isohyets is called an <b>isohyetal map</b>.</p>
<p>An <b>isohume</b> is a line of constant relative <a href="/wiki/Humidity" title="Humidity">humidity</a>, while a <b>isodrosotherm</b> (from <span lang="grc" xml:lang="grc"><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%CE%B4%CF%81%CF%8C%CF%83%CE%BF%CF%82" title="wikt:δρόσος">δρόσος</a></span> or <i>drosos</i>, meaning 'dew', and <span lang="grc" xml:lang="grc">θέρμη</span> or <i>therme</i>, meaning 'heat') is a line of equal or constant <a href="/wiki/Dew_point" title="Dew point">dew point</a>.</p>
<p>An <b>isoneph</b> is a line indicating equal <a href="/wiki/Cloud" title="Cloud">cloud</a> cover.</p>
<p>An <b>isochalaz</b> is a line of constant frequency of <a href="/wiki/Hail" title="Hail">hail</a> storms, and an <b>isobront</b> is a line drawn through geographical points at which a given phase of thunderstorm activity occurred simultaneously.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Snow" title="Snow">Snow</a> cover is frequently shown as a contour-line map.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Wind">Wind</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Contour_line&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Wind">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>An <b>isotach</b> (from <span lang="grc" xml:lang="grc"><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%CF%84%CE%B1%CF%87%CF%8D%CF%82" title="wikt:ταχύς">ταχύς</a></span> or <i>tachus</i>, meaning 'fast') is a line joining points with constant <a href="/wiki/Wind" title="Wind">wind</a> speed. In meteorology, the term <b>isogon</b> refers to a line of constant wind direction.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Freeze_and_thaw">Freeze and thaw</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Contour_line&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Freeze and thaw">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>An <b>isopectic</b> line denotes equal dates of <a href="/wiki/Ice" title="Ice">ice</a> formation each winter, and an <b>isotac</b> denotes equal dates of thawing.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Physical_geography_and_oceanography">Physical geography and oceanography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Contour_line&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Physical geography and oceanography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Elevation_and_depth">Elevation and depth</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Contour_line&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Elevation and depth">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Topographic_map_example.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="600" data-file-width="600" height="220" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/79/Topographic_map_example.png/220px-Topographic_map_example.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/79/Topographic_map_example.png/330px-Topographic_map_example.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/79/Topographic_map_example.png/440px-Topographic_map_example.png 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Topographic_map_example.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/Topographic_map" title="Topographic map">Topographic map</a> of <a href="/wiki/Stowe,_Vermont" title="Stowe, Vermont">Stowe</a>, <a href="/wiki/Vermont" title="Vermont">Vermont</a>. The brown contour lines represent the <a href="/wiki/Elevation" title="Elevation">elevation</a>. The contour interval is 20 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Foot_(length)" title="Foot (length)">feet</a>.</div>

<p>Contours are one of several <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cartographic_relief_depiction" title="Cartographic relief depiction">common methods</a> used to denote <a href="/wiki/Elevation" title="Elevation">elevation</a> or <a href="/wiki/Altitude" title="Altitude">altitude</a> and depth on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Maps" title="Maps">maps</a>. From these contours, a sense of the general <a href="/wiki/Terrain" title="Terrain">terrain</a> can be determined. They are used at a variety of scales, from large-scale engineering drawings and architectural plans, through <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Topographic_maps" title="Topographic maps">topographic maps</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bathymetric_charts" title="Bathymetric charts">bathymetric charts</a>, up to continental-scale maps.</p>
<p>"Contour line" is the most common usage in <a href="/wiki/Cartography" title="Cartography">cartography</a>, but <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Isobath" title="Isobath">isobath</a> for underwater depths on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bathymetric" title="Bathymetric">bathymetric</a> maps and <b>isohypse</b> for elevations are also used.</p>
<p>In cartography, the <b>contour interval</b> is the elevation difference between adjacent contour lines. The contour interval should be the same over a single map. When calculated as a ratio against the map scale, a sense of the hilliness of the terrain can be derived.</p>
<h5><span class="mw-headline" id="Interpretation">Interpretation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Contour_line&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Interpretation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h5>
<p>There are several rules to note when interpreting terrain contour lines:</p>
<ul>
<li><b>The rule of Vs</b>: sharp-pointed vees usually are in stream valleys, with the drainage channel passing through the point of the vee, with the vee pointing upstream. This is a consequence of <a href="/wiki/Erosion" title="Erosion">erosion</a>.</li>
<li><b>The rule of Os</b>: closed loops are normally uphill on the inside and downhill on the outside, and the innermost loop is the highest area. If a loop instead represents a depression, some maps note this by short lines radiating from the inside of the loop, called "hachures".</li>
<li><b>Spacing of contours</b>: close contours indicate a steep slope; distant contours a shallow slope. Two or more contour lines merging indicates a cliff. By counting the number of contours that cross a segment of a <a href="/wiki/Stream" title="Stream">stream</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Stream_gradient" title="Stream gradient">stream gradient</a> can be approximated.</li>
</ul>
<p>Of course, to determine differences in elevation between two points, the contour interval, or distance in altitude between two adjacent contour lines, must be known, and this is normally stated in the map key. Usually contour intervals are consistent throughout a map, but there are exceptions. Sometimes intermediate contours are present in flatter areas; these can be dashed or dotted lines at half the noted contour interval. When contours are used with hypsometric tints on a small-scale map that includes mountains and flatter low-lying areas, it is common to have smaller intervals at lower elevations so that detail is shown in all areas. Conversely, for an island which consists of a plateau surrounded by steep cliffs, it is possible to use smaller intervals as the height increases.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Electrostatics">Electrostatics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Contour_line&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Electrostatics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>An <b><a href="/wiki/Isopotential_map" title="Isopotential map">isopotential map</a></b> is a measure of electrostatic potential in space, often depicted in two dimensions with the electostatic charges inducing that <a href="/wiki/Electric_potential" title="Electric potential">electric potential</a>. The term <b><a href="/wiki/Equipotential" title="Equipotential">equipotential</a> line</b> or <b>isopotential line</b> refers to a curve of constant <a href="/wiki/Electric_potential" title="Electric potential">electric potential</a>. Whether crossing an equipotential line represents ascending or descending the potential is inferred from the labels on the charges. In three dimensions, <b><a href="/wiki/Equipotential" title="Equipotential">equipotential</a> surfaces</b> may be depicted with a two dimensional cross-section, showing <a href="/wiki/Equipotential" title="Equipotential">equipotential</a> lines at the intersection of the surfaces and the cross-section.</p>
<p>The general mathematical term <a href="/wiki/Level_set" title="Level set">level set</a> is often used to describe the full collection of points having a particular potential, especially in higher dimensional space.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Magnetism">Magnetism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Contour_line&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Magnetism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:IGRF_2000_magnetic_declination.gif"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="804" data-file-width="1010" height="175" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/68/IGRF_2000_magnetic_declination.gif/220px-IGRF_2000_magnetic_declination.gif" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/68/IGRF_2000_magnetic_declination.gif/330px-IGRF_2000_magnetic_declination.gif 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/68/IGRF_2000_magnetic_declination.gif/440px-IGRF_2000_magnetic_declination.gif 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:IGRF_2000_magnetic_declination.gif" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Isogonic lines for the year 2000. The agonic lines are thicker and labeled with "0".</div>

<p>In the study of the <a href="/wiki/Earth%27s_magnetic_field" title="Earth's magnetic field">Earth's magnetic field</a>, the term <b>isogon</b> or <b>isogonic line</b> refers to a line of constant <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_declination" title="Magnetic declination">magnetic declination</a>, the variation of magnetic north from geographic north. An <b>agonic line</b> is drawn through points of zero magnetic declination. An <b>isoporic line</b> refers to a line of constant annual variation of magnetic declination .<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>An <b>isoclinic line</b> connects points of equal <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_dip" title="Magnetic dip">magnetic dip</a>, and an <b>aclinic line</b> is the isoclinic line of magnetic dip zero.</p>
<p>An <b>isodynamic line</b> (from <span lang="grc" xml:lang="grc">δύναμις</span> or <i>dynamis</i> meaning 'power') connects points with the same intensity of magnetic force.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Oceanography">Oceanography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Contour_line&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Oceanography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Besides ocean depth, <a href="/wiki/Oceanography" title="Oceanography">oceanographers</a> use contour to describe diffuse variable phenomena much as meteorologists do with atmospheric phenomena. In particular, <b>isobathytherms</b> are lines showing depths of water with equal temperature, <b>isohalines</b> show lines of equal ocean salinity, and <b><a href="/wiki/Isopycnal" title="Isopycnal">Isopycnals</a></b> are surfaces of equal water density.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Geology">Geology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Contour_line&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Geology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Various <a href="/wiki/Geology" title="Geology">geological</a> data are rendered as contour maps in <a href="/wiki/Structural_geology" title="Structural geology">structural geology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sedimentology" title="Sedimentology">sedimentology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Stratigraphy" title="Stratigraphy">stratigraphy</a> and <a href="/wiki/Economic_geology" title="Economic geology">economic geology</a>. Contour maps are used to show the below ground surface of geologic <a href="/wiki/Stratum" title="Stratum">strata</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fault_(geology)" title="Fault (geology)">fault</a> surfaces (especially low angle <a href="/wiki/Thrust_fault" title="Thrust fault">thrust faults</a>) and <a href="/wiki/Unconformity" title="Unconformity">unconformities</a>. <a href="/wiki/Isopach_map" title="Isopach map">Isopach maps</a> use <b>isopachs</b> (lines of equal thickness) to illustrate variations in thickness of geologic units.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Environmental_science">Environmental science</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Contour_line&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Environmental science">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In discussing pollution, density maps can be very useful in indicating sources and areas of greatest contamination. Contour maps are especially useful for diffuse forms or scales of pollution. Acid precipitation is indicated on maps with <b>isoplats</b>. Some of the most widespread applications of environmental science contour maps involve mapping of <a href="/wiki/Environmental_noise" title="Environmental noise">environmental noise</a> (where lines of equal sound pressure level are denoted <b>isobels</b><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup>), <a href="/wiki/Air_pollution" title="Air pollution">air pollution</a>, <a href="/wiki/Soil_contamination" title="Soil contamination">soil contamination</a>, <a href="/wiki/Thermal_pollution" title="Thermal pollution">thermal pollution</a> and <a href="/wiki/Groundwater" title="Groundwater">groundwater</a> contamination. By <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Contour_planting" title="Contour planting">contour planting</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Contour_ploughing" title="Contour ploughing">contour ploughing</a>, the rate of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Water_runoff" title="Water runoff">water runoff</a> and thus <a href="/wiki/Soil_erosion" title="Soil erosion">soil erosion</a> can be substantially reduced; this is especially important in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Riparian" title="Riparian">riparian</a> zones.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ecology">Ecology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Contour_line&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Ecology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>An <b>isoflor</b> is an isopleth contour connecting areas of comparable biological diversity. Usually, the variable is the number of species of a given genus or family that occurs in a region. Isoflor maps are thus used to show distribution patterns and trends such as centres of diversity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Specht_1981_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-Specht_1981-22">[22]</a></sup></p>

<p>In <a href="/wiki/Economics" title="Economics">economics</a>, contour lines can be used to describe features which vary quantitatively over space. An <b><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/isochrone" title="wikt:isochrone">isochrone</a></b> shows lines of equivalent drive time or travel time to a given location and is used in the generation of <a href="/wiki/Isochrone_map" title="Isochrone map">isochrone maps</a>. An <b>isotim</b> shows equivalent transport costs from the source of a raw material, and an <b><a href="/wiki/Isodapane" title="Isodapane">isodapane</a></b> shows equivalent cost of travel time.</p>

<p>Contour lines are also used to display non-geographic information in economics. <b><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Indifference_curves" title="Indifference curves">Indifference curves</a></b> (as shown at left) are used to show bundles of goods to which a person would assign equal utility. An <b><a href="/wiki/Isoquant" title="Isoquant">isoquant</a></b> (in the image at right) is a curve of equal production quantity for alternative combinations of <a href="/wiki/Factors_of_production" title="Factors of production">input usages</a>, and an <b><a href="/wiki/Isocost" title="Isocost">isocost curve</a></b> (also in the image at right) shows alternative usages having equal production costs.</p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Political_science" title="Political science">political science</a> an analogous method is used in understanding coalitions (for example the diagram in Laver and Shepsle's work<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup>).</p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Population_dynamics" title="Population dynamics">population dynamics</a>, an <b><a href="/wiki/Isocline" title="Isocline">isocline</a></b> shows the set of population sizes at which the rate of change, or partial derivative, for one population in a pair of interacting populations is zero.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Statistics">Statistics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Contour_line&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Statistics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In statistics, isodensity lines <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fernandez_2011_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-Fernandez_2011-24">[24]</a></sup> or isodensanes are lines that join points with the same value of a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Probability_density" title="Probability density">probability density</a>. Isodensanes are used to display <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bivariate_distribution" title="Bivariate distribution">bivariate distributions</a>. For example, for a bivariate <a href="/wiki/Elliptical_distribution" title="Elliptical distribution">elliptical distribution</a> the isodensity lines are <a href="/wiki/Ellipse" title="Ellipse">ellipses</a>.</p>
<h3><span id="Thermodynamics,_engineering,_and_other_sciences"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Thermodynamics.2C_engineering.2C_and_other_sciences">Thermodynamics, engineering, and other sciences</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Contour_line&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Thermodynamics, engineering, and other sciences">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Various types of graphs in <a href="/wiki/Thermodynamics" title="Thermodynamics">thermodynamics</a>, engineering, and other sciences use isobars (constant pressure), isotherms (constant temperature), isochors (constant specific volume), or other types of isolines, even though these graphs are usually not related to maps. Such isolines are useful for representing more than two dimensions (or quantities) on two-dimensional graphs. Common examples in thermodynamics are some types of <a href="/wiki/Phase_diagram" title="Phase diagram">phase diagrams</a>.</p>
<p><b><a href="/wiki/Isocline" title="Isocline">Isoclines</a></b> are used to solve <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ordinary_differential_equations" title="Ordinary differential equations">ordinary differential equations</a>.</p>
<p>In interpreting <a href="/wiki/Radar" title="Radar">radar</a> images, an <b>isodop</b> is a line of equal <a href="/wiki/Doppler_effect" title="Doppler effect">Doppler</a> velocity, and an <b>isoecho</b> is a line of equal radar reflectivity.</p>

<li>isochasm: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aurora_(astronomy)" title="Aurora (astronomy)">aurora</a> equal occurrence</li>

<li>isophene: biological events occurring with <a href="/wiki/Coincidence" title="Coincidence">coincidence</a> such as plants <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Flowering" title="Flowering">flowering</a></li>
<li>isophote: <a href="/wiki/Illuminance" title="Illuminance">illuminance</a></li>
<li>mobile telephony: <a href="/wiki/Received_signal_strength_indication" title="Received signal strength indication">mobile received power</a> and <a href="/wiki/Coverage_(telecommunication)" title="Coverage (telecommunication)">cell coverage area</a></li>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Contour_line&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The idea of lines that join points of equal value was rediscovered several times. The oldest known <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Isobath" title="Isobath">isobath</a> (contour line of constant depth) is found on a map dated 1584 of the river <a href="/wiki/Spaarne" title="Spaarne">Spaarne</a>, near <a href="/wiki/Haarlem" title="Haarlem">Haarlem</a>, by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dutch_(ethnic_group)" title="Dutch (ethnic group)">Dutchman</a> <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Pieter_Bruinsz&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Pieter Bruinsz (page does not exist)">Pieter Bruinsz</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-:0-25">[25]</a></sup> In 1701, <a href="/wiki/Edmond_Halley" title="Edmond Halley">Edmond Halley</a> used such lines (isogons) on a chart of magnetic variation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> The Dutch engineer <a href="/wiki/Nicolaas_Kruik" title="Nicolaas Kruik">Nicholas Cruquius</a> drew the bed of the river <a href="/wiki/Merwede" title="Merwede">Merwede</a> with lines of equal depth (isobaths) at intervals of 1 <a href="/wiki/Fathom" title="Fathom">fathom</a> in 1727, and <a href="/wiki/Philippe_Buache" title="Philippe Buache">Philippe Buache</a> used them at 10-fathom intervals on a chart of the <a href="/wiki/English_Channel" title="English Channel">English Channel</a> that was prepared in 1737 and published in 1752. Such lines were used to describe a land surface (contour lines) in a map of the Duchy of Modena and Reggio by Domenico Vandelli in 1746, and they were studied theoretically by Ducarla in 1771, and <a href="/wiki/Charles_Hutton" title="Charles Hutton">Charles Hutton</a> used them in the <a href="/wiki/Schiehallion_experiment" title="Schiehallion experiment">Schiehallion experiment</a>. In 1791, a map of <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a> by J. L. Dupain-Triel used contour lines at 20-metre intervals, hachures, spot-heights and a vertical section. In 1801, the chief of the Corps of Engineers, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fran%C3%A7ois_Nicolas_Beno%C3%AEt,_Baron_Haxo" title="François Nicolas Benoît, Baron Haxo">Haxo</a>, used contour lines at the larger scale of 1:500 on a plan of his projects for Rocca d'Aufo.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Skel58_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-Skel58-27">[27]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<p>By around 1843, when the <a href="/wiki/Ordnance_Survey" title="Ordnance Survey">Ordnance Survey</a> started to regularly record contour lines in <a href="/wiki/Great_Britain" title="Great Britain">Great Britain</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ireland" title="Ireland">Ireland</a>, they were already in general use in European countries. Isobaths were not routinely used on <a href="/wiki/Nautical_chart" title="Nautical chart">nautical charts</a> until those of <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russia</a> from 1834, and those of Britain from 1838.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Skel58_27-1"><a href="#cite_note-Skel58-27">[27]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<p>When maps with contour lines became common, the idea spread to other applications. Perhaps the latest to develop are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Air_quality" title="Air quality">air quality</a> and <a href="/wiki/Noise_pollution" title="Noise pollution">noise pollution</a> contour maps, which first appeared in the United States in approximately 1970, largely as a result of national legislation requiring spatial delineation of these parameters. In 2007, <a href="/wiki/Pictometry_International" title="Pictometry International">Pictometry International</a> was the first to allow users to dynamically generate elevation contour lines to be laid over oblique images.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Graphical_design">Graphical design</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Contour_line&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Graphical design">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">For features specific to <a href="/wiki/Topography" title="Topography">topography</a>, see <a href="/wiki/Terrain_cartography#Contour_lines" title="Terrain cartography">Terrain cartography § Contour lines</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Topographic_map#Conventions" title="Topographic map">Topographic map § Conventions</a>.</div>
<p>To maximize readability of contour maps, there are several design choices available to the map creator, principally line weight, line <a href="/wiki/Color" title="Color">color</a>, line type and method of numerical marking.</p>
<p><b>Line weight</b> is simply the darkness or thickness of the line used. This choice is made based upon the least intrusive form of contours that enable the reader to decipher the background information in the map itself. If there is little or no content on the base map, the contour lines may be drawn with relatively heavy thickness. Also, for many forms of contours such as topographic maps, it is common to vary the line weight and/or color, so that a different line characteristic occurs for certain numerical values. For example, in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Topographic" title="Topographic">topographic</a> map above, the even hundred foot elevations are shown in a different weight from the twenty foot intervals.</p>
<p><b>Line color</b> is the choice of any number of <a href="/wiki/Pigment" title="Pigment">pigments</a> that suit the display. Sometimes a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gloss_(paint)" title="Gloss (paint)">sheen or gloss</a> is used as well as color to set the contour lines apart from the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Base_map&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Base map (page does not exist)">base map</a>. Line colour can be varied to show other information.</p>
<p><b>Line type</b> refers to whether the basic contour line is solid, dashed, dotted or broken in some other pattern to create the desired effect. Dotted or dashed lines are often used when the underlying base map conveys very important (or difficult to read) information. Broken line types are used when the location of the contour line is inferred.</p>
<p><b>Numerical marking</b> is the manner of denoting the <a href="/wiki/Arithmetic" title="Arithmetic">arithmetical</a> values of contour lines. This can be done by placing numbers along some of the contour lines, typically using <a href="/wiki/Interpolation" title="Interpolation">interpolation</a> for intervening lines. Alternatively a map key can be produced associating the contours with their values.</p>
<p>If the contour lines are not numerically labeled and adjacent lines have the same style (with the same weight, color and type), then the direction of the gradient cannot be determined from the contour lines alone. However, if the contour lines cycle through three or more styles, then the direction of the gradient can be determined from the lines. The orientation of the numerical text labels is often used to indicate the direction of the slope.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Plan_view_versus_profile_view">Plan view versus profile view</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Contour_line&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: Plan view versus profile view">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Topographic_profile" title="Topographic profile">Topographic profile</a></div>
<p>Most commonly contour lines are drawn in plan view, or as an observer in space would view the Earth's surface: ordinary map form. However, some parameters can often be displayed in profile view showing a vertical profile of the parameter mapped. Some of the most common parameters mapped in profile are <a href="/wiki/Air_pollutant_concentrations" title="Air pollutant concentrations">air pollutant concentrations</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sound_exposure_level" title="Sound exposure level">sound levels</a>. In each of those cases it may be important to analyze (air pollutant concentrations or sound levels) at varying heights so as to determine the air quality or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Noise_health_effects" title="Noise health effects">noise health effects</a> on people at different elevations, for example, living on different floor levels of an urban apartment. In actuality, both plan and profile view contour maps are used in <a href="/wiki/Air_pollution" title="Air pollution">air pollution</a> and <a href="/wiki/Noise_pollution" title="Noise pollution">noise pollution</a> studies.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Labeling_contour_maps">Labeling contour maps</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Contour_line&amp;action=edit&amp;section=26" title="Edit section: Labeling contour maps">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Cntr-map-1.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="680" data-file-width="1035" height="197" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fa/Cntr-map-1.jpg/300px-Cntr-map-1.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fa/Cntr-map-1.jpg/450px-Cntr-map-1.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fa/Cntr-map-1.jpg/600px-Cntr-map-1.jpg 2x" width="300"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Cntr-map-1.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Contour map labeled aesthetically in an "elevation up" manner.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Labeling_(map_design)" title="Labeling (map design)">Labels</a> are a critical component of elevation maps. A properly labeled contour map helps the reader to quickly interpret the shape of the terrain. If numbers are placed close to each other, it means that the terrain is steep. Labels should be placed along a slightly curved line "pointing" to the summit or nadir, from several directions if possible, making the visual identification of the summit or nadir easy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> Contour labels can be oriented so a reader is facing uphill when reading the label.</p>
<p>Manual labeling of contour maps is a time-consuming process, however, there are a few software systems that can do the job automatically and in accordance with cartographic conventions, called <a href="/wiki/Automatic_label_placement" title="Automatic label placement">automatic label placement</a>.</p>
