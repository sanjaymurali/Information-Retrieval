<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">List of retired Pacific typhoon names</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

<p>This is a list of all <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pacific_typhoon" title="Pacific typhoon">Pacific typhoons</a> that have had their names retired by the <a href="/wiki/Japan_Meteorological_Agency" title="Japan Meteorological Agency">Japan Meteorological Agency</a>. A total of 38 <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone" title="Tropical cyclone">typhoon</a> names have been retired since the start of official <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_previous_tropical_cyclone_names" title="List of previous tropical cyclone names">tropical cyclone naming</a> in the western North <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Ocean" title="Pacific Ocean">Pacific Ocean</a> in <a href="/wiki/2000_Pacific_typhoon_season" title="2000 Pacific typhoon season">2000</a>. Tropical cyclone names are retired by the <a href="/wiki/World_Meteorological_Organization" title="World Meteorological Organization">World Meteorological Organization</a> (WMO) in a meeting in January or February. Those typhoons that have their names retired tend to be exceptionally destructive storms. Several names were removed or altered <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_tropical_cyclone_names" title="List of tropical cyclone names">naming list</a> for various reasons other than retirement. Collectively, retired typhoons have caused over $68 billion in damage (2017 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/USD" title="USD">USD</a>), as well as over 12,000 deaths.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Background">Background</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=List_of_retired_Pacific_typhoon_names&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Background">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone_naming" title="Tropical cyclone naming">Tropical cyclone naming</a>, <a href="/wiki/History_of_tropical_cyclone_naming" title="History of tropical cyclone naming">History of tropical cyclone naming</a>, and <a href="/wiki/List_of_historic_tropical_cyclone_names" title="List of historic tropical cyclone names">List of historic tropical cyclone names</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Aerial_view_of_Tacloban_after_Typhoon_Haiyan.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3744" data-file-width="5616" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a0/Aerial_view_of_Tacloban_after_Typhoon_Haiyan.jpg/220px-Aerial_view_of_Tacloban_after_Typhoon_Haiyan.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a0/Aerial_view_of_Tacloban_after_Typhoon_Haiyan.jpg/330px-Aerial_view_of_Tacloban_after_Typhoon_Haiyan.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a0/Aerial_view_of_Tacloban_after_Typhoon_Haiyan.jpg/440px-Aerial_view_of_Tacloban_after_Typhoon_Haiyan.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">

<a href="/wiki/Tacloban" title="Tacloban">Tacloban</a> devastated by <a href="/wiki/Typhoon_Haiyan" title="Typhoon Haiyan">Typhoon Haiyan</a></div>

<p>The practice of using names to identify tropical cyclones goes back several centuries, with systems named after places, saints or things they hit before the formal start of naming in the Western Pacific.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Whats_in_a_name.3F_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Whats_in_a_name.3F-1">[1]</a></sup> These included the <a href="/wiki/Kamikaze_(typhoon)" title="Kamikaze (typhoon)">Kamikaze</a>, <a href="/wiki/1906_Hong_Kong_typhoon" title="1906 Hong Kong typhoon">1906 Hong Kong typhoon</a>, <a href="/wiki/1922_Swatow_typhoon" title="1922 Swatow typhoon">1922 Swatow typhoon</a> and the <a href="/wiki/1934_Muroto_typhoon" title="1934 Muroto typhoon">1934 Muroto typhoon</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TC_FAQ_B1_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-TC_FAQ_B1-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>The practice of retiring significant names was started during 1955 by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_States_Weather_Bureau" title="United States Weather Bureau">United States Weather Bureau</a> in the Northern Atlantic basin, after hurricanes <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Carol" title="Hurricane Carol">Carol</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Edna" title="Hurricane Edna">Edna</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Hazel" title="Hurricane Hazel">Hazel</a> struck the <a href="/wiki/East_Coast_of_the_United_States" title="East Coast of the United States">East Coast of the United States</a> and caused a significant amount of damage in the previous year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mahina_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Mahina-3">[3]</a></sup> Initially the names were only designed to be retired for ten years after which they might be reintroduced; however, it was decided at the 1969 Interdepartmental hurricane conference, that any significant hurricane in the future would have its name permanently retired.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mahina_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-Mahina-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Anna_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Anna-4">[4]</a></sup> The first tropical cyclone name to be removed in the South Pacific, was Rosie after it had impacted Vanuatu and New Caledonia during 1971. Several names have been removed from the Pacific naming lists for various other reasons than causing a significant amount of death/destruction, which include being pronounced in a very similar way to other names and political reasons.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WMO_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-WMO-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HRD_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-HRD-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2000, the <a href="/wiki/Japan_Meteorological_Agency" title="Japan Meteorological Agency">Japan Meteorological Agency</a> (JMA) began naming tropical cyclones from a list of 140 names, submitted by 14 countries. Previously, the JMA labeled storms with numbers, but not names. The JMA has been the official warning agency of the western Pacific Ocean since 1981, though other organizations have also tracked typhoons. The <a href="/wiki/Joint_Typhoon_Warning_Center" title="Joint Typhoon Warning Center">Joint Typhoon Warning Center</a> (JTWC) unofficially named tropical cyclones from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/1940%E2%80%9349_Pacific_typhoon_seasons#1947_Pacific_typhoon_season" title="1940–49 Pacific typhoon seasons">1947</a> to <a href="/wiki/1999_Pacific_typhoon_season" title="1999 Pacific typhoon season">1999</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> During this time period, there were several pre-determined <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_previous_tropical_cyclone_names" title="List of previous tropical cyclone names">tropical cyclone lists</a>, in which many names were removed and replaced with others.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Philippine_Atmospheric,_Geophysical_and_Astronomical_Services_Administration" title="Philippine Atmospheric, Geophysical and Astronomical Services Administration">Philippine Atmospheric, Geophysical and Astronomical Services Administration</a> (PAGASA) names tropical cyclones using a separate list, which is adjusted periodically.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>Several names were removed from the list. In 2002, the name <i>Hanuman</i> was replaced prior to being used, due to objection by the <a href="/wiki/India_Meteorological_Department" title="India Meteorological Department">India Meteorological Department</a> for reason of religion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Additionally, the name <i>Kodo</i> was replaced in 2002 without being used.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2008wmo_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-2008wmo-11">[11]</a></sup> The following year, Koni was replaced by Goni, after an apparent misspelling was made. In 2004, the names <i>Yanyan</i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Typhoon_Tingting" title="Typhoon Tingting">Tingting</a></i> were removed at the request of the <a href="/wiki/Hong_Kong_Observatory" title="Hong Kong Observatory">Hong Kong Observatory</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2008wmo_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-2008wmo-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> A total of nine names on the list had their spellings changed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2008wmo_11-2"><a href="#cite_note-2008wmo-11">[11]</a></sup> In February 2014, the name <i>Sonamu</i> was removed at the request from <a href="/wiki/Malaysia" title="Malaysia">Malaysia</a> due to causing unprecedented panic by the similar pronunciation to <i>tsunami</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> In February 2015 the name <i>Jongdari</i> was chosen as replacement for <i>Sonamu</i>. In the 46th session of the Typhoon Committee, it was noted the name <i>Vicente</i> appears on both the tropical cyclone name lists for the Western North Pacific and Eastern North Pacific. In response to this duplication the name <i>Lan</i> was chosen as replacement for <i>Vicente</i> on the Western North Pacific name list to avoid potential confusion.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="20th_century">20th century</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=List_of_retired_Pacific_typhoon_names&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: 20th century">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>Between 1947 and 2000, eleven names of significant tropical cyclones were retired from the list of names used by the United States <a href="/wiki/Joint_Typhoon_Warning_Center" title="Joint Typhoon Warning Center">Joint Typhoon Warning Center</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Retired_typhoons_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-Retired_typhoons-14">[14]</a></sup> During this time other names were removed from the naming lists, including in 1979 when the lists of names used were revised to include both male and female names.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Retired_typhoons_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-Retired_typhoons-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ATCR_1979_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-ATCR_1979-15">[15]</a></sup> Tropical Storm Lucille was the first name to be retired for its impacts, while Ophelia was retired because of its long 8,045 km (5,000 mi) track.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Retired_typhoons_14-2"><a href="#cite_note-Retired_typhoons-14">[14]</a></sup></p>

<p>So far during the current decade thirteen names have had their names retired by the Typhoon Committee. Collectively, these systems killed at least 9349 people and caused at least <span class="sortkey" style="display:none">7010336163000000000♠</span>$33.6 billion worth of damage. Typhoon Haiyan is currently the strongest and deadliest storm of the decade to have its name retired, while Typhoon Fitow is currently the costliest storm of the decade to have its name retired.</p>
