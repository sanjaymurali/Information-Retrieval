<p>The <b>James River</b> is a river in the <a href="/wiki/U.S._state" title="U.S. state">U.S. state</a> of <a href="/wiki/Virginia" title="Virginia">Virginia</a>. It is 348 miles (560 km) long,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NHD_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-NHD-3">[3]</a></sup> extending to 444 miles (715 km) if one includes the <a href="/wiki/Jackson_River_(Virginia)" title="Jackson River (Virginia)">Jackson River</a>, the longer of its two source tributaries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NHD_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-NHD-3">[3]</a></sup> The James River drains a <a href="/wiki/Drainage_basin" title="Drainage basin">catchment</a> comprising 10,432 square miles (27,020 km<sup>2</sup>). The watershed includes about 4% open water and an area with a population of 2.5 million people (2000). It is the 12th longest river in the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> that remains entirely within a single state.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Course">Course</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=James_River&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Course">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The James River forms in the <a href="/wiki/Appalachian_Mountains" title="Appalachian Mountains">Appalachian Mountains</a>, near <a href="/wiki/Iron_Gate,_Virginia" title="Iron Gate, Virginia">Iron Gate</a> on the border between <a href="/wiki/Alleghany_County,_Virginia" title="Alleghany County, Virginia">Alleghany</a> and <a href="/wiki/Botetourt_County,_Virginia" title="Botetourt County, Virginia">Botetourt</a> counties, from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Confluence_(geography)" title="Confluence (geography)">confluence</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Cowpasture_River" title="Cowpasture River">Cowpasture</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jackson_River_(Virginia)" title="Jackson River (Virginia)">Jackson</a> rivers, and flows into the <a href="/wiki/Chesapeake_Bay" title="Chesapeake Bay">Chesapeake Bay</a> at <a href="/wiki/Hampton_Roads" title="Hampton Roads">Hampton Roads</a>. Tidal waters extend west to <a href="/wiki/Richmond,_Virginia" title="Richmond, Virginia">Richmond</a>, the capital of Virginia, at the river's <a href="/wiki/Fall_line" title="Fall line">fall line</a> (the <a href="/wiki/Head_of_navigation" title="Head of navigation">head of navigation</a>). Larger tributaries draining to the tidal portion include the <a href="/wiki/Appomattox_River" title="Appomattox River">Appomattox River</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chickahominy_River" title="Chickahominy River">Chickahominy River</a>, <a href="/wiki/Warwick_River_(Virginia)" title="Warwick River (Virginia)">Warwick River</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pagan_River" title="Pagan River">Pagan River</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Nansemond_River" title="Nansemond River">Nansemond River</a>.</p>
<p>At its mouth near Newport News Point, the <a href="/wiki/Elizabeth_River_(Virginia)" title="Elizabeth River (Virginia)">Elizabeth River</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Nansemond_River" title="Nansemond River">Nansemond River</a> join the James River to form the harbor area known as <a href="/wiki/Hampton_Roads" title="Hampton Roads">Hampton Roads</a>. Between the tip of the <a href="/wiki/Virginia_Peninsula" title="Virginia Peninsula">Virginia Peninsula</a> near <a href="/wiki/Old_Point_Comfort" title="Old Point Comfort">Old Point Comfort</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Willoughby_Spit" title="Willoughby Spit">Willoughby Spit</a> area of <a href="/wiki/Norfolk,_Virginia" title="Norfolk, Virginia">Norfolk</a> in <a href="/wiki/South_Hampton_Roads" title="South Hampton Roads">South Hampton Roads</a>, a channel leads from Hampton Roads into the southern portion of the <a href="/wiki/Chesapeake_Bay" title="Chesapeake Bay">Chesapeake Bay</a> and out to the <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Ocean" title="Atlantic Ocean">Atlantic Ocean</a> a few miles further east. Many boats pass through this river to import and export Virginia products.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=James_River&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Native_Americans_in_the_United_States" title="Native Americans in the United States">Native Americans</a> who populated the area east of the <a href="/wiki/Fall_line" title="Fall line">fall line</a> in the late 16th and early 17th centuries called the James River the <b>Powhatan River</b>, named for the chief of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Powhatan_Confederacy" title="Powhatan Confederacy">Powhatan Confederacy</a> which extended over most of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tidewater_region_of_Virginia" title="Tidewater region of Virginia">Tidewater region of Virginia</a>. The English colonists named it "James" after King <a href="/wiki/James_VI_and_I" title="James VI and I">James I of England</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> as they also constructed the first permanent <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/English_colonization_of_the_Americas" title="English colonization of the Americas">English settlement in the Americas</a> in 1607 at <a href="/wiki/Jamestown,_Virginia" title="Jamestown, Virginia">Jamestown</a> along the banks of the James River about 35 miles (56 km) upstream from the <a href="/wiki/Chesapeake_Bay" title="Chesapeake Bay">Chesapeake Bay</a>.</p>
<p>The navigable portion of the river was the major highway of the <a href="/wiki/Colony_of_Virginia" title="Colony of Virginia">Colony of Virginia</a> during its first 15 years, facilitating supply ships delivering supplies and more people from England. However, for the first five years, despite many hopes of gold and riches, these ships sent little of monetary value back to the sponsors. In 1612, businessman <a href="/wiki/John_Rolfe" title="John Rolfe">John Rolfe</a> successfully cultivated a non-native strain of <a href="/wiki/Tobacco" title="Tobacco">tobacco</a> which proved popular in England. Soon, the river became the primary means of exporting the large <a href="/wiki/Hogshead" title="Hogshead">hogsheads</a> of this <a href="/wiki/Cash_crop" title="Cash crop">cash crop</a> from an ever-growing number of <a href="/wiki/Plantations_in_the_American_South" title="Plantations in the American South">plantations</a> with wharfs along its banks. This development made the proprietary efforts of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Virginia_Company_of_London" title="Virginia Company of London">Virginia Company of London</a> successful financially, spurring even more development, investments and immigration. Below the falls at Richmond, many <a href="/wiki/List_of_James_River_plantations" title="List of James River plantations">James River plantations</a> had their own wharfs, and additional ports and/or early railheads were located at <a href="/wiki/Warwick,_Virginia_(Chesterfield_County)" title="Warwick, Virginia (Chesterfield County)">Warwick</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bermuda_Hundred,_Virginia" title="Bermuda Hundred, Virginia">Bermuda Hundred</a>, <a href="/wiki/City_Point,_Virginia" title="City Point, Virginia">City Point</a>, <a href="/wiki/Claremont,_Virginia" title="Claremont, Virginia">Claremont</a>, <a href="/wiki/Scotland,_Virginia" title="Scotland, Virginia">Scotland</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Smithfield,_Virginia" title="Smithfield, Virginia">Smithfield</a>, and, during the 17th century, the capital of the Colony at Jamestown.</p>
<p>Navigation of the James River played an important role in early Virginia commerce and the settlement of the interior, although growth of the colony was primarily in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tidewater_region_of_Virginia" title="Tidewater region of Virginia">Tidewater region</a> during the first 75 years. The upper reaches of the river above the head of navigation at the fall line were explored by fur trading parties sent by <a href="/wiki/Abraham_Wood" title="Abraham Wood">Abraham Wood</a> during the late 17th century.</p>
<p>Although ocean-going ships were unable to navigate beyond present-day <a href="/wiki/Richmond,_Virginia" title="Richmond, Virginia">Richmond</a>, portage of products and navigation with smaller craft to transport crops other than tobacco was feasible. Produce from the <a href="/wiki/Piedmont_(United_States)" title="Piedmont (United States)">Piedmont</a> and <a href="/wiki/Great_Appalachian_Valley" title="Great Appalachian Valley">Great Valley</a> regions traveled down the river to seaports at Richmond and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Manchester,_Virginia" title="Manchester, Virginia">Manchester</a> through such port towns as <a href="/wiki/Lynchburg,_Virginia" title="Lynchburg, Virginia">Lynchburg</a>, <a href="/wiki/Scottsville,_Virginia" title="Scottsville, Virginia">Scottsville</a>, <a href="/wiki/Columbia,_Virginia" title="Columbia, Virginia">Columbia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Buchanan,_Virginia" title="Buchanan, Virginia">Buchanan</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="James_River_and_Kanawha_Canal">James River and Kanawha Canal</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=James_River&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: James River and Kanawha Canal">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The James River was considered as a route for transport of produce from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ohio_Valley" title="Ohio Valley">Ohio Valley</a>. The <a href="/wiki/James_River_and_Kanawha_Canal" title="James River and Kanawha Canal">James River and Kanawha Canal</a> was built for this purpose, to provide a navigable portion of the <a href="/wiki/Kanawha_River" title="Kanawha River">Kanawha River</a>, a tributary of the <a href="/wiki/Ohio_River" title="Ohio River">Ohio River</a>. For the most mountainous section between the two points, the <a href="/wiki/James_River_and_Kanawha_Turnpike" title="James River and Kanawha Turnpike">James River and Kanawha Turnpike</a> was built to provide a portage link via wagons and stagecoaches. However, before the canal could be fully completed, in the mid-19th century, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Railroad" title="Railroad">railroads</a> emerged as a more practical technology and eclipsed <a href="/wiki/Canal" title="Canal">canals</a> for economical transportation. The <a href="/wiki/Chesapeake_and_Ohio_Railway" title="Chesapeake and Ohio Railway">Chesapeake and Ohio Railway</a> (C&amp;O) was completed between Richmond and the Ohio River at the new city of <a href="/wiki/Huntington,_West_Virginia" title="Huntington, West Virginia">Huntington, West Virginia</a> by 1873, dooming the canal's economic prospects. In the 1880s, the <a href="/wiki/Richmond_and_Alleghany_Railroad" title="Richmond and Alleghany Railroad">Richmond and Alleghany Railroad</a> was laid along the eastern portion of the canal's towpath, and became part of the C&amp;O within 10 years. In modern times, this rail line is used primarily in transporting <a href="/wiki/West_Virginia" title="West Virginia">West Virginia</a> coal to export <a href="/wiki/Coal_pier" title="Coal pier">coal piers</a> at <a href="/wiki/Newport_News,_Virginia" title="Newport News, Virginia">Newport News</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Recreation">Recreation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=James_River&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Recreation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>The James River contains numerous parks and other recreational attractions. Canoeing, fishing, kayaking, hiking, and swimming are some of the activities that people enjoy along the river during the summer. From the river's start in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Blue_Ridge_mountains" title="Blue Ridge mountains">Blue Ridge mountains</a> to <a href="/wiki/Richmond,_Virginia" title="Richmond, Virginia">Richmond</a>, numerous rapids and pools offer fishing and whitewater rafting. The most intense whitewater stretch is a 2-mile (3 km) segment that ends in downtown Richmond where the river goes over the fall line. This is the only place in the country where extensive class III (class IV with above average river levels) whitewater conditions exist within sight of skyscrapers. The urban river parks provide a great outdoor recreation/adventure opportunity for all the people of Richmond and the surrounding communities.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Below the <a href="/wiki/Fall_line" title="Fall line">fall line</a> east of Richmond, the river is better suited for water skiing and other large boat recreation. Here the river is known for its <a href="/wiki/Blue_catfish" title="Blue catfish">blue catfish</a>, reaching average sizes of 20 to 30 pounds (9.1 to 13.6 kg), with frequent catches exceeding 50 pounds (23 kg). In the Chesapeake watershed, the James River is the last confirmed holdout for the nearly extirpated <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_sturgeon" title="Atlantic sturgeon">Atlantic sturgeon</a>. In May 2007 a survey identified 175 sturgeon remaining in the entire river, with 15 specimens exceeding 5 feet (1.5 m).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Dams">Dams</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=James_River&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Dams">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Due to its potential for generating mechanical power for rotating machinery such as grist mills, hydroelectric power, and as a water route for trade, many dams have been built across the James River since the time of European settlement of the region. While most of these dams have been removed or failed, several dams still exist along the upper course of the river. From the head of the river downstream to Richmond are found the following dams as identified by the current US Army Corps of Engineers National Inventory of Dams:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Cushaw_Hydroelectric_Project" title="Cushaw Hydroelectric Project">Cushaw Hydroelectric Project</a> near <a href="/wiki/Glasgow,_Virginia" title="Glasgow, Virginia">Glasgow</a> and <a href="/wiki/Big_Island,_Virginia" title="Big Island, Virginia">Big Island</a>.</li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Bedford_Hydropower_Project" title="Bedford Hydropower Project">Bedford Hydropower Project</a> near Big Island.</li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Big_Island_Dam" title="Big Island Dam">Big Island Dam</a> near Big Island.</li>
<li>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coleman_falls_dam" title="Coleman falls dam">Coleman Falls Dam</a> in <a href="/wiki/Coleman_Falls,_Virginia" title="Coleman Falls, Virginia">Coleman Falls</a></li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Holcomb_Rock_Dam" title="Holcomb Rock Dam">Holcomb Rock Dam</a> near <a href="/wiki/Lynchburg,_Virginia" title="Lynchburg, Virginia">Lynchburg</a>.</li>
<li>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Reusens_dam" title="Reusens dam">Reusens Dam</a> near Lynchburg.</li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Scotts_Mill_Dam" title="Scotts Mill Dam">Scotts Mill Dam</a> in Lynchburg.</li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Bosher_Dam" title="Bosher Dam">Bosher Dam</a> in <a href="/wiki/Richmond,_Virginia" title="Richmond, Virginia">Richmond</a>.</li>
</ul>
<p>The tallest dam is the Reusens Dam, which also has the greatest hydroelectric nameplate capacity and the greatest <a href="/wiki/Reservoir" title="Reservoir">reservoir</a> capacity. At 1,617 feet, the longest dam is the Cushaw Hydroelectric Project due to the highly angled path the dam takes across the river.</p>
<p>While not identified in the National Inventory of Dams, a very low head weir structure is found below Bosher Dam in Richmond on either side of Williams Island. Known as the "Z-Dam" for its zigzag course on the south side of the island, the current structure was built in 1932 and serves to direct water into Richmond's water treatment facility on the north bank. The less than 5 feet tall dam does not serve any power or navigation purpose.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Highway_bridges_below_Richmond">Highway bridges below Richmond</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=James_River&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Highway bridges below Richmond">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/Hampton_Roads" title="Hampton Roads">Hampton Roads</a> area, the river is as much as 5 miles (8.0 km) wide at points. Due to ocean-going shipping upriver as far as the <a href="/wiki/Port_of_Richmond_(Virginia)" title="Port of Richmond (Virginia)">Port of Richmond</a>, a combination of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ferryboat" title="Ferryboat">ferryboats</a>, high <a href="/wiki/Bridge" title="Bridge">bridges</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bridge-tunnel" title="Bridge-tunnel">bridge-tunnels</a> are used for highway traffic. Crossings east to west include:</p>
<ul>
<li>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hampton_Roads_Bridge-Tunnel" title="Hampton Roads Bridge-Tunnel">Hampton Roads Bridge-Tunnel</a> (<a href="/wiki/Interstate_64_in_Virginia" title="Interstate 64 in Virginia">I-64</a>)</li>
<li>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Monitor-Merrimac_Memorial_Bridge-Tunnel" title="Monitor-Merrimac Memorial Bridge-Tunnel">Monitor-Merrimac Memorial Bridge-Tunnel</a> (<a href="/wiki/Interstate_664" title="Interstate 664">I-664</a>)</li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/James_River_Bridge" title="James River Bridge">James River Bridge</a> (<a href="/wiki/U.S._Route_17" title="U.S. Route 17">US 17</a>/ <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/US_258" title="US 258">US 258</a>/ <a href="/wiki/Virginia_State_Route_32" title="Virginia State Route 32">VA 32</a>)</li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Jamestown_Ferry" title="Jamestown Ferry">Jamestown Ferry</a> (<a href="/wiki/Virginia_State_Route_31" title="Virginia State Route 31">VA 31</a>) (toll-free)</li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Benjamin_Harrison_Memorial_Bridge" title="Benjamin Harrison Memorial Bridge">Benjamin Harrison Memorial Bridge</a> near <a href="/wiki/Hopewell,_Virginia" title="Hopewell, Virginia">Hopewell</a>. This is a <a href="/wiki/Drawbridge" title="Drawbridge">drawbridge</a> on <a href="/wiki/Virginia_State_Route_106" title="Virginia State Route 106">VA 106</a> / <a href="/wiki/Virginia_State_Route_156" title="Virginia State Route 156">VA 156</a> which replaced ferry service in 1966. It was the site of a major collision of a ship in 1977.</li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Varina-Enon_Bridge" title="Varina-Enon Bridge">Varina-Enon Bridge</a> is a high <a href="/wiki/Cable-stayed_bridge" title="Cable-stayed bridge">cable-stayed bridge</a> carrying <a href="/wiki/Interstate_295_(Virginia)" title="Interstate 295 (Virginia)">I-295</a> which was the second of its type in the U.S. when it was completed.</li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Vietnam_Veterans_Memorial_Bridge_(Richmond)" title="Vietnam Veterans Memorial Bridge (Richmond)">Vietnam Veterans Memorial Bridge</a> carries the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pocahontas_Parkway" title="Pocahontas Parkway">Pocahontas Parkway</a> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/State_Route_895_(Virginia)" title="State Route 895 (Virginia)">State Route 895</a>) via a high-level bridge to connect to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/State_Route_150_(Virginia)" title="State Route 150 (Virginia)">State Route 150</a> at <a href="/wiki/Interstate_95_in_Virginia" title="Interstate 95 in Virginia">Interstate 95</a>.</li>
</ul>
<p>The SR 895 high-level crossing is the last bridge east of the Deepwater Port of Richmond and head of ocean-going navigation at the <a href="/wiki/Fall_line" title="Fall line">fall line</a> of the James River. West of this point, potential flooding is more of an engineering concern than clearance for watercraft.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Bicycles">Bicycles</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=James_River&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Bicycles">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="James_River_Reserve_Fleet">James River Reserve Fleet</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=James_River&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: James River Reserve Fleet">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:NDRF_James_River_1990_DN-SC-91-01118.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2014" data-file-width="3000" height="201" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/82/NDRF_James_River_1990_DN-SC-91-01118.JPG/300px-NDRF_James_River_1990_DN-SC-91-01118.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/82/NDRF_James_River_1990_DN-SC-91-01118.JPG/450px-NDRF_James_River_1990_DN-SC-91-01118.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/82/NDRF_James_River_1990_DN-SC-91-01118.JPG/600px-NDRF_James_River_1990_DN-SC-91-01118.JPG 2x" width="300"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:NDRF_James_River_1990_DN-SC-91-01118.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Part of the James River Fleet</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The James River is the anchorage (<span class="plainlinks nourlexpansion"><a class="external text" href="//tools.wmflabs.org/geohack/geohack.php?pagename=James_River&amp;params=37.120393_N_76.646469_W_"><span class="geo-nondefault"><span class="geo-dms" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location"><span class="latitude">37°07′13″N</span> <span class="longitude">76°38′47″W</span></span></span><span class="geo-multi-punct">﻿ / ﻿</span><span class="geo-default"><span class="geo-dec" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location">37.120393°N 76.646469°W</span><span style="display:none">﻿ / <span class="geo">37.120393; -76.646469</span></span></span></a></span>) for a large portion of the <a href="/wiki/National_Defense_Reserve_Fleet" title="National Defense Reserve Fleet">National Defense Reserve Fleet</a>, called the "James River fleet" or the "ghost fleet," consisting of "<a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/mothball#Verb" title="wikt:mothball">mothballed</a>" ships, mostly merchant vessels, that can be activated within 20 to 120 days to provide shipping for the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> during national emergencies, either military or non-military, such as commercial shipping crises.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>The fleet is managed by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._Department_of_Transportation" title="U.S. Department of Transportation">U.S. Department of Transportation</a>'s <a href="/wiki/United_States_Maritime_Administration" title="United States Maritime Administration">Maritime Administration (MARAD)</a>. It is a different entity from the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Navy_reserve_fleets" title="United States Navy reserve fleets">United States Navy reserve fleets</a>, which consist largely of warships.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="James_River_in_music">James River in music</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=James_River&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: James River in music">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A song was recorded by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Country_and_Western" title="Country and Western">country and Western music</a> singer <a href="/wiki/Lefty_Frizzell" title="Lefty Frizzell">Lefty Frizzell</a> about the river, called "James River," released on the <i>Saginaw, Michigan</i> album.</p>
<p>The Americana/bluegrass band <a href="/wiki/Old_Crow_Medicine_Show" title="Old Crow Medicine Show">Old Crow Medicine Show</a>, released "James River Blues," a James River boatman's lament of the arrival of the railroads, on their 2006 album <i><a href="/wiki/Big_Iron_World" title="Big Iron World">Big Iron World</a></i>.</p>
<p>A song recorded by <a href="/wiki/Waylon_Jennings" title="Waylon Jennings">Waylon Jennings</a> titled "The Ghost of General Lee" mentions "the James River water [running] a bloody red" following a battle between the Union and Confederate armies.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Avail" title="Avail">Avail</a>, a music group from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Richmond,_VA" title="Richmond, VA">Richmond</a>, recorded <a href="/wiki/Over_the_James" title="Over the James">Over the James</a>, a 1998 <a href="/wiki/Hardcore_punk" title="Hardcore punk">hardcore punk</a> <a href="/wiki/LP_record" title="LP record">LP record</a> released by <a href="/wiki/Lookout_Records" title="Lookout Records">Lookout Records</a> (and re-released in 2006 by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jade_Tree_Records" title="Jade Tree Records">Jade Tree Records</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup>) named in honor of the river, containing the song "Scuffle Town" whose lyrics also reference the river.</p>
<p>The river is referenced in the <a href="/wiki/Wrinkle_Neck_Mules" title="Wrinkle Neck Mules">Wrinkle Neck Mules</a> song "Banks of the James."</p>
<p>Prior to settling on the title <a href="/wiki/Black_Messiah_(album)" title="Black Messiah (album)">Black Messiah</a>, American soul singer <a href="/wiki/D%27Angelo" title="D'Angelo">D'Angelo</a> was set on titling his third studio album <i>James River</i>.</p>
<p>In her song "<a class="external text" href="https://opinionator.blogs.nytimes.com/2014/02/07/time-travel-and-the-ballad-tradition/?_r=0" rel="nofollow">When the Master Calls the Roll</a>," from her 2014 album "The River &amp; the Thread," American singer/songwriter Roseanne Cash refers to a planned wedding, sometime around the start of the Civil War, "down by the King James River" in Virginia.</p>
