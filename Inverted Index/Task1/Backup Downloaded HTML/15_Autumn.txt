<p><b>Autumn</b>, also known as <b>fall</b> in <a href="/wiki/American_English" title="American English">American</a> and <a href="/wiki/Canadian_English" title="Canadian English">Canadian</a> English,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> is one of the four <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Temperate" title="Temperate">temperate</a> <a href="/wiki/Season" title="Season">seasons</a>. Autumn marks the transition from <a href="/wiki/Summer" title="Summer">summer</a> to <a href="/wiki/Winter" title="Winter">winter</a>, in September (<a href="/wiki/Northern_Hemisphere" title="Northern Hemisphere">Northern Hemisphere</a>) or March (<a href="/wiki/Southern_Hemisphere" title="Southern Hemisphere">Southern Hemisphere</a>), when the duration of <a href="/wiki/Daylight" title="Daylight">daylight</a> becomes noticeably shorter and the temperature cools down considerably. One of its main features is the <a href="/wiki/Leaf#Seasonal_leaf_loss" title="Leaf">shedding of leaves</a> from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Deciduous_trees" title="Deciduous trees">deciduous trees</a>.</p>
<p>Some cultures regard the autumnal <a href="/wiki/Equinox" title="Equinox">equinox</a> as "mid-autumn", while others with a longer <a href="/wiki/Seasonal_lag" title="Seasonal lag">temperature lag</a> treat it as the start of autumn.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> Meteorologists (and most of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Temperate_countries" title="Temperate countries">temperate countries</a> in the southern hemisphere)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> use a definition based on months, with autumn being September, October and November in the northern hemisphere,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> and March, April and May in the southern hemisphere.</p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North America</a>, autumn is usually considered to start with the <a href="/wiki/September_equinox" title="September equinox">September equinox</a> (21 to 24 September)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> and end with the <a href="/wiki/Winter_solstice" title="Winter solstice">winter solstice</a> (21 or 22 December).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Popular culture in North America associates <a href="/wiki/Labor_Day" title="Labor Day">Labor Day</a>, the first Monday in September, as the end of summer and the start of autumn; certain summer traditions, such as wearing white, are discouraged after that date.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> As daytime and nighttime temperatures decrease, trees shed their leaves.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> In traditional East Asian <a href="/wiki/Solar_term" title="Solar term">solar term</a>, autumn starts on or around 8 August and ends on or about 7 November. In <a href="/wiki/Ireland" title="Ireland">Ireland</a>, the autumn months according to the national meteorological service, <a href="/wiki/Met_%C3%89ireann" title="Met Éireann">Met Éireann</a>, are September, October and November.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-met.ie_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-met.ie-9">[9]</a></sup> However, according to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Irish_Calendar" title="Irish Calendar">Irish Calendar</a>, which is based on ancient <a href="/wiki/Gaelic_Ireland" title="Gaelic Ireland">Gaelic</a> traditions, autumn lasts throughout the months of August, September and October, or possibly a few days later, depending on tradition<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>. In <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a> and <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand" title="New Zealand">New Zealand</a>, autumn officially begins on 1 March and ends on 31 May.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Autumn&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:D%C3%BClmen,_Wildpark_--_2014_--_3808_color_balanced.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2978" data-file-width="4608" height="129" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d0/D%C3%BClmen%2C_Wildpark_--_2014_--_3808_color_balanced.jpg/200px-D%C3%BClmen%2C_Wildpark_--_2014_--_3808_color_balanced.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d0/D%C3%BClmen%2C_Wildpark_--_2014_--_3808_color_balanced.jpg/300px-D%C3%BClmen%2C_Wildpark_--_2014_--_3808_color_balanced.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d0/D%C3%BClmen%2C_Wildpark_--_2014_--_3808_color_balanced.jpg/400px-D%C3%BClmen%2C_Wildpark_--_2014_--_3808_color_balanced.jpg 2x" width="200"/></a>

<p>The word <i>autumn</i> comes from the ancient <a href="/wiki/Etruscan_language" title="Etruscan language">Etruscan</a> root <i>autu-</i> and has within it connotations of the passing of the year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> It was borrowed by the neighbouring <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Rome" title="Ancient Rome">Romans</a>, and became the <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> word <i><span lang="la" xml:lang="la">autumnus</span></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> After the Roman era, the word continued to be used as the <a href="/wiki/Old_French" title="Old French">Old French</a> word <i><span lang="fro" xml:lang="fro">autompne</span></i> (<i><span lang="fr" xml:lang="fr">automne</span></i> in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Modern_French" title="Modern French">modern French</a>) or <i><span lang="enm" xml:lang="enm">autumpne</span></i> in Middle English,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> and was later normalised to the original Latin. In the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Medieval" title="Medieval">Medieval</a> period, there are rare examples of its use as early as the 12th century, but by the 16th century, it was in common use.</p>
<p>Before the 16th century, <i><a href="/wiki/Harvest" title="Harvest">harvest</a></i> was the term usually used to refer to the season, as it is common in other <a href="/wiki/West_Germanic_languages" title="West Germanic languages">West Germanic languages</a> to this day (<a href="/wiki/Cf." title="Cf.">cf.</a> Dutch <i><span lang="nl" xml:lang="nl">herfst</span></i>, German <i><span lang="de" xml:lang="de">Herbst</span></i> and Scots <i><span lang="sco" xml:lang="sco">hairst</span></i>). However, as more people gradually moved from working the land to living in towns, the word <i>harvest</i> lost its reference to the time of year and came to refer only to the actual activity of reaping, and <i>autumn</i>, as well as <i>fall</i>, began to replace it as a reference to the season.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>The alternative word <i>fall</i> for the season traces its origins to old <a href="/wiki/Germanic_languages" title="Germanic languages">Germanic languages</a>. The exact derivation is unclear, with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Old_English_language" title="Old English language">Old English</a> <i><span lang="ang" xml:lang="ang">fiæll</span></i> or <i><span lang="ang" xml:lang="ang">feallan</span></i> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Old_Norse_language" title="Old Norse language">Old Norse</a> <i><span lang="non" xml:lang="non">fall</span></i> all being possible candidates. However, these words all have the meaning "to fall from a height" and are clearly derived either from a common root or from each other. The term came to denote the season in <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=16th-century_England&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="16th-century England (page does not exist)">16th-century England</a>, a contraction of <a href="/wiki/Middle_English" title="Middle English">Middle English</a> expressions like "<a href="/wiki/Abscission" title="Abscission">fall of the leaf</a>" and "fall of the year".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>During the 17th century, <a href="/wiki/British_colonization_of_the_Americas" title="British colonization of the Americas">English emigration to the British colonies</a> in North America was at its peak, and the new settlers took the English language with them. While the term <i>fall</i> gradually became obsolete in Britain, it became the more common term in North America.</p>
<p>The name <i>backend</i>, a once common name for the season in <a href="/wiki/Northern_England" title="Northern England">Northern England</a>, has today been largely replaced by the name autumn.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Associations">Associations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Autumn&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Associations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Harvest">Harvest</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Autumn&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Harvest">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Harvest_Straw_Bales_in_Schleswig-Holstein.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2000" data-file-width="3008" height="133" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e3/Harvest_Straw_Bales_in_Schleswig-Holstein.jpg/200px-Harvest_Straw_Bales_in_Schleswig-Holstein.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e3/Harvest_Straw_Bales_in_Schleswig-Holstein.jpg/300px-Harvest_Straw_Bales_in_Schleswig-Holstein.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e3/Harvest_Straw_Bales_in_Schleswig-Holstein.jpg/400px-Harvest_Straw_Bales_in_Schleswig-Holstein.jpg 2x" width="200"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Harvest_Straw_Bales_in_Schleswig-Holstein.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/Harvest" title="Harvest">Harvest</a> straw bales in a field of <a href="/wiki/Schleswig-Holstein" title="Schleswig-Holstein">Schleswig-Holstein</a>, <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a></div>

<p>Association with the transition from warm to cold weather, and its related status as the season of the primary <a href="/wiki/Harvest" title="Harvest">harvest</a>, has dominated its themes and popular images. In Western cultures, personifications of autumn are usually pretty, well-fed females adorned with fruits, vegetables and grains that ripen at this time. Many cultures feature autumnal <a href="/wiki/Harvest_festival" title="Harvest festival">harvest festivals</a>, often the most important on their calendars. Still extant echoes of these celebrations are found in the autumn <a href="/wiki/Thanksgiving" title="Thanksgiving">Thanksgiving</a> holiday of the United States and Canada, and the Jewish <a href="/wiki/Sukkot" title="Sukkot">Sukkot</a> holiday with its roots as a full-moon harvest festival of "tabernacles" (living in outdoor huts around the time of harvest).<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> There are also the many North American Indian festivals tied to harvest of ripe foods gathered in the wild, the Chinese Mid-Autumn or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Moon_festival" title="Moon festival">Moon festival</a>, and many others. The predominant mood of these autumnal celebrations is a gladness for the fruits of the earth mixed with a certain melancholy linked to the imminent arrival of harsh weather.</p>
<p>This view is presented in English poet <a href="/wiki/John_Keats" title="John Keats">John Keats</a>' poem <i><a href="/wiki/To_Autumn" title="To Autumn">To Autumn</a></i>, where he describes the season as a time of bounteous fecundity, a time of 'mellow fruitfulness'.</p>
<p>While most foods are harvested during the autumn, foods particularly associated with the season include <a href="/wiki/Pumpkin" title="Pumpkin">pumpkins</a> (which are integral parts of both Thanksgiving and <a href="/wiki/Halloween" title="Halloween">Halloween</a>) and <a href="/wiki/Apple" title="Apple">apples</a>, which are used to make the seasonal beverage <a href="/wiki/Apple_cider" title="Apple cider">apple cider</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Melancholia">Melancholia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Autumn&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Melancholia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Udusse_mattuv_harilik_vaher,_punaste_s%C3%BCgislehtedega._L%C3%A4%C3%A4nemaa.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3456" data-file-width="4736" height="161" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e9/Udusse_mattuv_harilik_vaher%2C_punaste_s%C3%BCgislehtedega._L%C3%A4%C3%A4nemaa.jpg/220px-Udusse_mattuv_harilik_vaher%2C_punaste_s%C3%BCgislehtedega._L%C3%A4%C3%A4nemaa.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e9/Udusse_mattuv_harilik_vaher%2C_punaste_s%C3%BCgislehtedega._L%C3%A4%C3%A4nemaa.jpg/330px-Udusse_mattuv_harilik_vaher%2C_punaste_s%C3%BCgislehtedega._L%C3%A4%C3%A4nemaa.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e9/Udusse_mattuv_harilik_vaher%2C_punaste_s%C3%BCgislehtedega._L%C3%A4%C3%A4nemaa.jpg/440px-Udusse_mattuv_harilik_vaher%2C_punaste_s%C3%BCgislehtedega._L%C3%A4%C3%A4nemaa.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Udusse_mattuv_harilik_vaher,_punaste_s%C3%BCgislehtedega._L%C3%A4%C3%A4nemaa.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>

<p>Autumn, especially in poetry, has often been associated with <a href="/wiki/Melancholia" title="Melancholia">melancholia</a>. The possibilities of summer are gone, and the chill of winter is on the horizon. Skies turn grey, the amount of usable daylight drops rapidly, and many people turn inward, both physically and mentally.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> It has been referred to as an unhealthy season.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>Similar examples may be found in Irish poet <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/William_Butler_Yeats" title="William Butler Yeats">William Butler Yeats</a>' poem <i><a href="/wiki/The_Wild_Swans_at_Coole" title="The Wild Swans at Coole">The Wild Swans at Coole</a></i> where the maturing season that the poet observes symbolically represents his own ageing self. Like the natural world that he observes, he too has reached his prime and now must look forward to the inevitability of old age and death. French poet <a href="/wiki/Paul_Verlaine" title="Paul Verlaine">Paul Verlaine</a>'s "<i><a href="/wiki/Chanson_d%27automne" title="Chanson d'automne">Chanson d'automne</a></i>" ("Autumn Song") is likewise characterised by strong, painful feelings of sorrow. <a href="/wiki/John_Keats" title="John Keats">Keats</a>' <i><a href="/wiki/To_Autumn" title="To Autumn">To Autumn</a></i>, written in September 1819, echoes this sense of melancholic reflection, but also emphasises the lush abundance of the season.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Halloween">Halloween</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Autumn&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Halloween">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Pumpkins-2009.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="333" data-file-width="500" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9a/Pumpkins-2009.jpg/220px-Pumpkins-2009.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9a/Pumpkins-2009.jpg/330px-Pumpkins-2009.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9a/Pumpkins-2009.jpg/440px-Pumpkins-2009.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">

<a href="/wiki/Halloween" title="Halloween">Halloween</a> <a href="/wiki/Pumpkin" title="Pumpkin">pumpkins</a></div>

<p>Autumn is associated with <a href="/wiki/Halloween" title="Halloween">Halloween</a> (influenced by <a href="/wiki/Samhain" title="Samhain">Samhain</a>, a Celtic autumn festival),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ENC_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-ENC-20">[20]</a></sup> and with it a widespread marketing campaign that promotes it. Halloween is in autumn in the northern hemisphere. The television, film, book, costume, home decoration, and confectionery industries use this time of year to promote products closely associated with such a holiday, with promotions going from early September to 31 October, since their themes rapidly lose strength once the holiday ends, and advertising starts concentrating on Christmas.</p>

<p>Autumn also has a strong association with the end of <a href="/wiki/Summer_vacation" title="Summer vacation">summer holiday</a> and the <a href="/wiki/First_day_of_school" title="First day of school">start of a new school year</a>, particularly for children in primary and secondary education. "<a href="/wiki/Back_to_school_(marketing)" title="Back to school (marketing)">Back to School</a>" advertising and preparations usually occurs in the weeks leading to the beginning of autumn.</p>
<p>Easter is in autumn in the southern hemisphere.</p>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thanksgiving_Day" title="Thanksgiving Day">Thanksgiving Day</a> is a national holiday celebrated in Canada, in the United States, in some of the <a href="/wiki/Caribbean" title="Caribbean">Caribbean islands</a> and in <a href="/wiki/Liberia" title="Liberia">Liberia</a>. Thanksgiving is celebrated on the <a href="/wiki/Thanksgiving_(Canada)" title="Thanksgiving (Canada)">second Monday of October</a> in Canada and on the <a href="/wiki/Thanksgiving_(United_States)" title="Thanksgiving (United States)">fourth Thursday of November</a> in the United States, and around the same part of the year in other places. Similarly named festival holidays occur in <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a> and <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a>.</p>
<p>Television stations and networks, particularly in North America, traditionally begin their regular seasons in autumn, with new series and new episodes of existing series debuting mostly during late September or early October (series that debut outside the fall season are usually known as <a href="/wiki/Mid-season_replacement" title="Mid-season replacement">mid-season replacements</a>). A sweeps period takes place in November to measure <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nielsen_Ratings" title="Nielsen Ratings">Nielsen Ratings</a>.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/American_football" title="American football">American football</a> is played almost exclusively in the autumn months; at the <a href="/wiki/High_school_football" title="High school football">high school level</a>, seasons run through September and October, with some playoff games and <a href="/wiki/American_football_on_Thanksgiving" title="American football on Thanksgiving">holiday rivalry contests</a> being played as late as Thanksgiving. <a href="/wiki/College_football" title="College football">College football</a>'s regular season runs from September through November, while the main <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Professional_football_(gridiron)" title="Professional football (gridiron)">professional</a> circuit, the <a href="/wiki/National_Football_League" title="National Football League">National Football League</a>, plays from September through December. Summer sports, such as <a href="/wiki/Stock_car_racing" title="Stock car racing">stock car racing</a> and <a href="/wiki/Major_League_Baseball" title="Major League Baseball">Major League Baseball</a>, wrap up their seasons in early autumn; MLB's championship <a href="/wiki/World_Series" title="World Series">World Series</a> is known popularly as the "Fall Classic".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> (Amateur baseball is usually finished by August.) Likewise, professional winter sports, such as <a href="/wiki/Professional_ice_hockey" title="Professional ice hockey">professional ice hockey</a>, <a href="/wiki/Basketball" title="Basketball">basketball</a> and most leagues of <a href="/wiki/Association_football" title="Association football">soccer football</a> in Europe, are in the early stages of their seasons during autumn; American <a href="/wiki/College_basketball" title="College basketball">college basketball</a> and <a href="/wiki/College_ice_hockey" title="College ice hockey">college ice hockey</a> play teams outside their <a href="/wiki/Athletic_conference" title="Athletic conference">athletic conferences</a> during the late autumn before their in-conference schedules begin in winter.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Christianity" title="Christianity">Christian</a> religious holidays of <a href="/wiki/All_Saints%27_Day" title="All Saints' Day">All Saints' Day</a> and <a href="/wiki/All_Souls%27_Day" title="All Souls' Day">All Souls' Day</a> are observed in autumn in the Northern hemisphere.</p>
<p>Since 1997, <a href="/wiki/Autumn_(given_name)" title="Autumn (given name)">Autumn</a> has been one of the top 100 names for girls in the United States.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p>In Indian mythology, autumn is considered to be the preferred season for the goddess of learning <a href="/wiki/Saraswati" title="Saraswati">Saraswati</a>, who is also known by the name of "goddess of autumn" (Sharada).</p>
<p>In Asian mysticism, Autumn is associated with the <a href="/wiki/Wu_Xing" title="Wu Xing">element</a> of <a href="/wiki/Metal_(Wu_Xing)" title="Metal (Wu Xing)">metal</a>, and subsequently with the colour <a href="/wiki/White" title="White">white</a>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bai_Hu" title="Bai Hu">White Tiger of the West</a>, and death and mourning.</p>
<p>In the United States, <a href="/wiki/Labor_Day" title="Labor Day">Labor Day</a> is a <a href="/wiki/Public_holidays_in_the_United_States" title="Public holidays in the United States">public holiday</a> celebrated on the first Monday in September.</p>

<p>Although <a href="/wiki/Autumn_leaf_color" title="Autumn leaf color">colour change in leaves</a> occurs wherever <a href="/wiki/Deciduous" title="Deciduous">deciduous</a> trees are found, coloured autumn <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Foliage" title="Foliage">foliage</a> is noted in various regions of the world: most of <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North America</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eastern_Asia" title="Eastern Asia">Eastern Asia</a> (including China, <a href="/wiki/Korea" title="Korea">Korea</a>, and Japan), <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a>, the forest of <a href="/wiki/Patagonia" title="Patagonia">Patagonia</a>, eastern <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a> and <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand" title="New Zealand">New Zealand</a>'s <a href="/wiki/South_Island" title="South Island">South Island</a>.</p>
<p>Eastern <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a> and <a href="/wiki/New_England" title="New England">New England</a> are famous for their autumnal foliage,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> and this attracts major tourism (worth billions of US dollars) for the regions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
