<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Troposphere</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">

<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="thumb tright">

<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Endeavour_silhouette_STS-130.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/Space_Shuttle" title="Space Shuttle">Space Shuttle</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Space_Shuttle_Endeavour" title="Space Shuttle Endeavour">Endeavour</a></i> silhouetted against the atmosphere. The orange layer is the troposphere, the white layer is the <a href="/wiki/Stratosphere" title="Stratosphere">stratosphere</a>, and the blue layer is the <a href="/wiki/Mesosphere" title="Mesosphere">mesosphere</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> (The shuttle is actually orbiting at an altitude of more than 320 km (200 mi), far above all three layers.)</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The <b>troposphere</b> is the lowest portion of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Earth%27s_atmosphere" title="Earth's atmosphere">Earth's atmosphere</a>, and is also where nearly all <a href="/wiki/Weather" title="Weather">weather</a> takes place. It contains approximately 75% of the <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere" title="Atmosphere">atmosphere</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Mass" title="Mass">mass</a> and 99% of the total mass of <a href="/wiki/Water_vapor" title="Water vapor">water vapor</a> and <a href="/wiki/Aerosol" title="Aerosol">aerosols</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> The average depths of the troposphere are 20 km (12 mi) in the <a href="/wiki/Tropics" title="Tropics">tropics</a>, 17 km (11 mi) in the mid latitudes, and 7 km (4.3 mi) in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Polar_regions" title="Polar regions">polar regions</a> in winter. The lowest part of the troposphere, where <a href="/wiki/Friction" title="Friction">friction</a> with the Earth's surface influences air flow, is the <a href="/wiki/Planetary_boundary_layer" title="Planetary boundary layer">planetary boundary layer</a>. This layer is typically a few hundred meters to 2 km (1.2 mi) deep depending on the <a href="/wiki/Landform" title="Landform">landform</a> and time of day. Atop the troposphere is the <a href="/wiki/Tropopause" title="Tropopause">tropopause</a>, which is the border between the troposphere and <a href="/wiki/Stratosphere" title="Stratosphere">stratosphere</a>. The tropopause is an <a href="/wiki/Inversion_(meteorology)" title="Inversion (meteorology)">inversion layer</a>, where the air temperature ceases to decrease with height and remains constant through its thickness.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DLA_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-DLA-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>The word <i>troposphere</i> derives from the <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a>: <span lang="el" xml:lang="el"><i>tropos</i></span> for "turn, turn toward, trope" and "-sphere" (as in, the Earth), reflecting the fact that rotational <a href="/wiki/Turbulence" title="Turbulence">turbulent</a> mixing plays an important role in the troposphere's structure and behaviour. Most of the phenomena associated with day-to-day weather occur in the troposphere.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DLA_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-DLA-3">[3]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Pressure_and_temperature_structure">Pressure and temperature structure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Troposphere&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Pressure and temperature structure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Composition">Composition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Troposphere&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Composition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>By volume, dry air contains 78.08% <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen" title="Nitrogen">nitrogen</a>, 20.95% <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a>, 0.93% <a href="/wiki/Argon" title="Argon">argon</a>, 0.04% <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a>, and small amounts of other gases. Air also contains a variable amount of <a href="/wiki/Water_vapor" title="Water vapor">water vapor</a>. Execpt for the water vapor content, the composition of the troposphere is essentially uniform. The source of water vapor is at the Earth's surface through the process of <a href="/wiki/Evaporation" title="Evaporation">evaporation</a>. The temperature of the troposphere decreases with <a href="/wiki/Altitude" title="Altitude">altitude</a>. And, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saturation_vapor_pressure" title="Saturation vapor pressure">saturation vapor pressure</a> decreases strongly as temperature drops. Hence, the amount of water vapor that can exist in the atmosphere decreases strongly with altitude and the proportion of water vapor is normally greatest near the surface of the Earth.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Pressure">Pressure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Troposphere&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Pressure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The pressure of the atmosphere is maximum at <a href="/wiki/Sea_level" title="Sea level">sea level</a> and decreases with altitude. This is because the atmosphere is very nearly in <a href="/wiki/Hydrostatic_equilibrium" title="Hydrostatic equilibrium">hydrostatic equilibrium</a> so that the pressure is equal to the weight of air above a given point. <p>Since temperature in principle also depends on altitude, one needs a second equation to determine the pressure as a function of altitude as discussed in the next section.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Temperature">Temperature</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Troposphere&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Temperature">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>The <a href="/wiki/Temperature" title="Temperature">temperature</a> of the troposphere generally decreases as altitude increases. The rate at which the temperature decreases, <span class="mwe-math-element"><span class="mwe-math-mathml-inline mwe-math-mathml-a11y" style="display: none;"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">

</mrow>
<mi>d</mi>

</mstyle>
</mrow>
<annotation encoding="application/x-tex">{\displaystyle -dT/dz}</annotation>
</semantics>
</math></span><img alt="-dT/dz" aria-hidden="true" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline" src="https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/fc20cd5e511d209bb75dedb98be3b10e1f203968" style="vertical-align: -0.838ex; width:8.189ex; height:2.843ex;"/></span>, is called the environmental lapse rate (ELR). The ELR is nothing more than the difference in temperature between the surface and the tropopause divided by the height. The ELR assumes that the air is perfectly still, i.e. that there is no mixing of the layers of air from vertical convection, nor winds that would create turbulence and hence mixing of the layers of air. The reason for this temperature difference is that the ground absorbs most of the sun's energy, which then heats the lower levels of the atmosphere with which it is in contact. Meanwhile, the radiation of heat at the top of the atmosphere results in the cooling of that part of the atmosphere.</p>

<p>The ELR assumes the atmosphere is still, but as air is heated it becomes buoyant and rises. The dry adiabatic lapse rate accounts for the effect of the expansion of dry air as it rises in the atmosphere and wet adiabatic lapse rates includes the effect of the condensation of water vapor on the lapse rate.</p>
<p>When a parcel of air rises, it expands, because the pressure is lower at higher altitudes. As the air parcel expands, it pushes the surrounding air outward, transferring energy in the form of <a href="/wiki/Work_(thermodynamics)" title="Work (thermodynamics)">work</a> from that parcel to the atmosphere. As energy transfer to a parcel of air by way of heat is very slow, it is assumed to not exchange energy by way of <a href="/wiki/Heat" title="Heat">heat</a> with the environment. Such a process is called an <a href="/wiki/Adiabatic_process" title="Adiabatic process">adiabatic</a> process (no energy transfer by way of heat). Since the rising parcel of air is losing energy as it does work on the surrounding atmosphere and no energy is transferred into it as heat from the atmosphere to make up for the loss, the parcel of air is losing energy, which manifests itself as a decrease in the temperature of the air parcel. The reverse, of course, will be true for a parcel of air that is sinking and is being compressed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DLA_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-DLA-3">[3]</a></sup></p>

<p>The troposphere is heated from below by <a href="/wiki/Latent_heat" title="Latent heat">latent heat</a>, <a href="/wiki/Outgoing_longwave_radiation" title="Outgoing longwave radiation">longwave radiation</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Sensible_heat" title="Sensible heat">sensible heat</a>. Surplus heating and vertical expansion of the troposphere occurs in the tropics. At middle latitudes, tropospheric temperatures decrease from an average of 15 °C at sea level to about −55 °C at the <a href="/wiki/Tropopause" title="Tropopause">tropopause</a>. At the <a href="/wiki/Geographical_pole" title="Geographical pole">poles</a>, tropospheric temperature only decreases from an average of 0 °C at sea level to about −45 °C at the tropopause. At the <a href="/wiki/Equator" title="Equator">equator</a>, tropospheric temperatures decrease from an average of 20 °C at sea level to about −70 to −75 °C at the tropopause. The troposphere is thinner at the poles and thicker at the equator. The average thickness of the tropical tropopause is roughly 7 kilometers greater than the average tropopause thickness at the poles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ZonalFlowDef_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-ZonalFlowDef-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Tropopause">Tropopause</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Troposphere&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Tropopause">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Tropopause" title="Tropopause">Tropopause</a></div>
<p>The tropopause is the boundary region between the troposphere and the <a href="/wiki/Stratosphere" title="Stratosphere">stratosphere</a>.</p>
<p>Measuring the temperature change with height through the troposphere and the stratosphere identifies the location of the tropopause. In the troposphere, temperature decreases with altitude. In the stratosphere, however, the temperature remains constant for a while and then increases with altitude. The region of the atmosphere where the lapse rate changes from positive (in the troposphere) to negative (in the stratosphere), is defined as the tropopause.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DLA_3-5"><a href="#cite_note-DLA-3">[3]</a></sup> Thus, the tropopause is an <a href="/wiki/Inversion_(meteorology)" title="Inversion (meteorology)">inversion layer</a>, and there is little mixing between the two layers of the atmosphere.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Atmospheric_flow">Atmospheric flow</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Troposphere&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Atmospheric flow">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The flow of the atmosphere generally moves in a west to east direction. This, however, can often become interrupted, creating a more north to south or south to north flow. These scenarios are often described in meteorology as zonal or meridional. These terms, however, tend to be used in reference to localised areas of atmosphere (at a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Synoptic_scale" title="Synoptic scale">synoptic scale</a>). A fuller explanation of the flow of atmosphere around the Earth as a whole can be found in the three-cell model.</p>

<p>A <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Zonal_flow_regime" title="Zonal flow regime">zonal flow regime</a> is the <a href="/wiki/Meteorology" title="Meteorology">meteorological</a> term meaning that the general flow pattern is west to east along the Earth's latitude lines, with weak shortwaves embedded in the flow.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ZonalFlowDef2_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-ZonalFlowDef2-8">[8]</a></sup> The use of the word "zone" refers to the flow being along the Earth's latitudinal "zones". This pattern can buckle and thus become a meridional flow.</p>

</div>
<p>When the zonal flow buckles, the atmosphere can flow in a more longitudinal (or meridional) direction, and thus the term "<a href="/wiki/Meridional_flow" title="Meridional flow">meridional flow</a>" arises. Meridional flow patterns feature strong, amplified troughs of low pressure and ridges of high pressure, with more north-south flow in the general pattern than west-to-east flow.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MeridonialFlow_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-MeridonialFlow-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Three-cell_model">Three-cell model</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Troposphere&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Three-cell model">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_circulation" title="Atmospheric circulation">Atmospheric circulation</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:AtmosphCirc2.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="507" data-file-width="576" height="176" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cd/AtmosphCirc2.png/200px-AtmosphCirc2.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cd/AtmosphCirc2.png/300px-AtmosphCirc2.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cd/AtmosphCirc2.png/400px-AtmosphCirc2.png 2x" width="200"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:AtmosphCirc2.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Atmospheric circulation shown with three large cells.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The three cells model of the atmosphere attempts to describe the actual flow of the Earth's atmosphere as a whole. It divides the Earth into the tropical (<a href="/wiki/Hadley_cell" title="Hadley cell">Hadley cell</a>), mid latitude (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ferrel_cell" title="Ferrel cell">Ferrel cell</a>), and polar (<a href="/wiki/Polar_vortex" title="Polar vortex">polar cell</a>) regions, to describe energy flow and global atmospheric circulation (mass flow). Its fundamental principle is that of balance – the energy that the Earth absorbs from the sun each year is equal to that which it loses to space by radiation. This overall Earth energy balance, however, does not apply in each latitude due to the varying strength of the sun in each "cell" as a result of the tilt of the Earth's axis in relation to its orbit. The result is a circulation of the atmosphere that transports warm air poleward from the tropics and cold air equatorward from the poles. The effect of the three cells is the tendency to even out the heat and moisture in the Earth's atmosphere around the planet<b>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ThreecellEncarta_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-ThreecellEncarta-10">[10]</a></sup></b></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Synoptic_scale_observations_and_concepts">Synoptic scale observations and concepts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Troposphere&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Synoptic scale observations and concepts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Forcing">Forcing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Troposphere&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Forcing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Forcing is a term used by meteorologists to describe the situation where a change or an event in one part of the atmosphere causes a strengthening change in another part of the atmosphere. It is usually used to describe connections between upper, middle or lower levels (such as upper-level divergence causing lower level convergence in cyclone formation), but also be to describe such connections over lateral distance rather than height alone. In some respects, <a href="/wiki/Teleconnection" title="Teleconnection">teleconnections</a> could be considered a type of forcing.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Divergence_and_convergence">Divergence and convergence</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Troposphere&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Divergence and convergence">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>An area of convergence is one in which the total mass of air is increasing with time, resulting in an increase in pressure at locations below the convergence level (recall that atmospheric pressure is just the total weight of air above a given point). Divergence is the opposite of convergence – an area where the total mass of air is decreasing with time, resulting in falling pressure in regions below the area of divergence. Where divergence is occurring in the upper atmosphere, there will be air coming in to try to balance the net loss of mass (this is called the principle of mass conservation), and there is a resulting upward motion (positive vertical velocity). Another way to state this is to say that regions of upper air divergence are conducive to lower level convergence, cyclone formation, and positive vertical velocity. Therefore, identifying regions of upper air divergence is an important step in forecasting the formation of a surface low pressure area.</p>
