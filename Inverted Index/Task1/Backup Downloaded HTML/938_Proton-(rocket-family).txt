<p><b>Proton</b> (Russian: Протон) (formal designation: <b>UR-500</b>) is an <a href="/wiki/Expendable_launch_system" title="Expendable launch system">expendable launch system</a> used for both commercial and Russian government space launches. The first Proton <a href="/wiki/Rocket" title="Rocket">rocket</a> was launched in 1965. Modern versions of the launch system are still in use as of 2017, making it one of the most successful heavy boosters in the history of spaceflight. All Protons are built at the <a href="/wiki/Khrunichev_State_Research_and_Production_Space_Center" title="Khrunichev State Research and Production Space Center">Khrunichev State Research and Production Space Center</a> plant in Moscow, transported to the <a href="/wiki/Baikonur_Cosmodrome" title="Baikonur Cosmodrome">Baikonur Cosmodrome</a>, brought to the launch pad horizontally, and raised into vertical position for launch.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>As with many Soviet rockets, the names of recurring payloads became associated with the Proton. The moniker "Proton" originates from a series of <a href="/wiki/Proton_satellite" title="Proton satellite">similarly named scientific satellites</a>, which were among the rocket's first payloads. During the Cold War, it was designated the <b>D-1</b>/<b>D-1e</b> or <b>SL-12</b>/<b>SL-13</b> by Western intelligence agencies.</p>
<p>Launch capacity to <a href="/wiki/Low_Earth_orbit" title="Low Earth orbit">low Earth orbit</a> is about 22.8 <a href="/wiki/Tonne" title="Tonne">tonnes</a> (50,000 lb).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-khrunichev.ru_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-khrunichev.ru-1">[1]</a></sup> Geostationary transfer capacity is about 6.3 tonnes (14,000 lb).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Private_spaceflight" title="Private spaceflight">Commercial launches</a> are marketed by <a href="/wiki/International_Launch_Services" title="International Launch Services">International Launch Services</a> (ILS).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> The rocket is intended to be retired before 2030.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>In January 2017, the Proton was temporarily grounded due to the manufacturer, <a href="/wiki/Voronezh_Mechanical_Plant" title="Voronezh Mechanical Plant">Voronezh Mechanical Plant</a>, having substituted a heat-resistant alloy in the engines with a cheaper metal.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Proton_(rocket_family)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Proton<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> initially started its life as a "super <a href="/wiki/Heavy_ICBM" title="Heavy ICBM">heavy ICBM</a>". It was designed to launch a 100-<a href="/wiki/TNT_equivalent" title="TNT equivalent">megaton</a> (or larger) <a href="/wiki/Thermonuclear_weapon" title="Thermonuclear weapon">thermonuclear weapon</a> over a distance of 13,000 km. It was hugely oversized for an ICBM and was never deployed in such a capacity. It was eventually used as a space <a href="/wiki/Launch_vehicle" title="Launch vehicle">launch vehicle</a>. It was the brainchild of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vladimir_Chelomei" title="Vladimir Chelomei">Vladimir Chelomei</a>'s design bureau as a foil to <a href="/wiki/Sergei_Korolev" title="Sergei Korolev">Sergei Korolev</a>'s <a href="/wiki/N1_(rocket)" title="N1 (rocket)">N1</a> rocket, whose purpose was to send a two-man <a href="/wiki/Zond_program" title="Zond program">Zond</a> spacecraft around the Moon; Korolev openly opposed Proton and Chelomei's other designs for their use of toxic propellants.</p>
<p>A rushed development program led to dozens of failures between 1965 and 1972. Proton did not complete its State Trials until 1977, at which point it was judged to have a higher than 90% reliability.</p>
<p>Proton's design was kept secret until 1986, with the public being only shown the upper stages in film clips and photographs, and the first time the complete vehicle was shown to the outside world happened during the televised launch of <a href="/wiki/Mir" title="Mir">Mir</a>.</p>
<p>Proton launched the unmanned Soviet circumlunar flights and was intended to have launched the first Soviet <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Circumlunar" title="Circumlunar">circumlunar</a> spaceflights, before the United States flew the <a href="/wiki/Apollo_8" title="Apollo 8">Apollo 8</a> mission. Proton launched the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Salyut" title="Salyut">Salyut</a> space stations, the <a href="/wiki/Mir" title="Mir">Mir</a> core segment and expansion modules, and both the <a href="/wiki/Zarya" title="Zarya">Zarya</a> and <a href="/wiki/Zvezda_(ISS_module)" title="Zvezda (ISS module)">Zvezda</a> modules of the <a href="/wiki/International_Space_Station" title="International Space Station">ISS</a>.</p>
<p>Proton also launches commercial <a href="/wiki/Satellite" title="Satellite">satellites</a>, most of them being managed by <a href="/wiki/International_Launch_Services" title="International Launch Services">International Launch Services</a>. The first ILS Proton launch was on 9 April 1996 with the launch of the <a href="/wiki/SES_S.A." title="SES S.A.">SES</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Astra_1F" title="Astra 1F">Astra 1F</a> <a href="/wiki/Communications_satellite" title="Communications satellite">communications satellite</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ilslaunch_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-ilslaunch-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>Since 1994, Proton has earned $4.3 billion for the Russian space industry, and by 2011 this figure is expected to rise to $6 billion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nesterov_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-nesterov-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Proton_8K82K">Proton 8K82K</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Proton_(rocket_family)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Proton 8K82K">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Proton-K" title="Proton-K">Proton-K</a></div>
<p>The (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/GRAU_index" title="GRAU index">GRAU index</a>) 8K82K version is now usually called "Proton K". It is fuelled by very toxic <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/UDMH" title="UDMH">unsymmetrical dimethyl hydrazine</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nitrogen_tetroxide" title="Nitrogen tetroxide">nitrogen tetroxide</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ilslaunch2_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-ilslaunch2-13">[13]</a></sup> These are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hypergolic" title="Hypergolic">hypergolic</a> fuels which ignite on contact, avoiding the need for an ignition system, and can be stored at ambient temperatures. This avoids the need for components that are tolerant of low temperatures, and allows the rocket to remain on the pad indefinitely (other launchers with such capability include the U.S. <a href="/wiki/Titan_(rocket_family)" title="Titan (rocket family)">Titan II</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Titan_III" title="Titan III">Titan III</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Titan_IV" title="Titan IV">Titan IV</a>, the Chinese <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Long_March_2_rocket_family" title="Long March 2 rocket family">Long March 2 rocket family</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Long_March_4_rocket_family" title="Long March 4 rocket family">Long March 4 rocket family</a>, the Soviet/Ukrainian <a href="/wiki/Tsyklon" title="Tsyklon">Tsyklon</a> launchers, the Soviet/Russian <a href="/wiki/Kosmos-3" title="Kosmos-3">Kosmos-3</a> and <a href="/wiki/Kosmos-3M" title="Kosmos-3M">Kosmos-3M</a> launchers and the European Ariane 1 to <a href="/wiki/Ariane_4" title="Ariane 4">Ariane 4</a> launchers). In contrast, <a href="/wiki/Cryogenic_fuel" title="Cryogenic fuel">cryogenic fuels</a> need periodic replenishment as they boil off.</p>
<p>The fourth stage has multiple variants, depending on the mission. The simplest, <a href="/wiki/Blok_D" title="Blok D">Blok D</a>, was used for interplanetary missions. Blok D had no guidance module, depending on the probe to control flight. Three different Blok DM versions (DM, DM2, and DM-2M) were for high Earth orbits. The Blok D/DM were unusual in that the fuel was stored in a toroidal tank, around the engine and behind the oxidizer tank.</p>
<p>The initial Proton tests in 1965–66 only used the first two stages of the booster, the complete four-stage vehicle being flown for the first time in 1967. When the Soviet space station program began in 1971, Protons began being flown with the Blok D removed for use as a heavy-lift LEO launcher.</p>
<p>Proton-K payloads including all of the Soviet Union's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Salyut_program" title="Salyut program"><i>Salyut</i></a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Space_stations" title="Space stations">space stations</a>, almost all <a href="/wiki/Mir" title="Mir">Mir</a> modules (with the exception of the <a href="/wiki/Mir_Docking_Module" title="Mir Docking Module">Docking Module</a>, which was launched on the United States <a href="/wiki/Space_Shuttle" title="Space Shuttle">Space Shuttle</a>), and the <a href="/wiki/Zarya" title="Zarya">Zarya</a> and <a href="/wiki/Zvezda_(ISS_module)" title="Zvezda (ISS module)">Zvezda</a> modules of the <a href="/wiki/International_Space_Station" title="International Space Station">International Space Station</a>. It was intended to launch the manned <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/TKS_spacecraft" title="TKS spacecraft">TKS spacecraft</a>, prior to the cancellation of that programme, although a few non-manned flights of spacecraft was fulfilled. Also it was intended for proposed in the 1970s <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=LKS_(spaceplane)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="LKS (spaceplane) (page does not exist)">LKS</a> spaceplane that never realised.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Proton-M">Proton-M</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Proton_(rocket_family)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Proton-M">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Proton-M" title="Proton-M">Proton-M</a></div>
<div class="mw-stack mobile-float-reset" style="box-sizing: border-box; float:left;">
<div style="overflow: hidden; margin: 1px;">
<div class="thumb tleft">

<p>The initial version of Proton M, could launch 3–3.2 tonnes (6,600–7,100 lb) into <a href="/wiki/Geostationary_orbit" title="Geostationary orbit">geostationary orbit</a> or 5.5 tonnes (12,000 lb) into a <a href="/wiki/Geostationary_transfer_orbit" title="Geostationary transfer orbit">geostationary transfer orbit</a>. It could place up to 22 tonnes (49,000 lb) in low Earth orbit with a 51.6-degree inclination, the orbit of the International Space Station (ISS).</p>
<p>The Proton M's improvements included lower stage modifications to reduce structural mass, increase thrust, and fully use propellants. Generally a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Briz-M" title="Briz-M">Briz-M</a> (<a href="/wiki/Russian_language" title="Russian language">Russian</a>: <span lang="ru" xml:lang="ru">Бриз</span> meaning <i>Breeze</i>) storable propellant upper stage is used instead of the <a href="/wiki/Blok_D" title="Blok D">Blok D</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Blok_DM" title="Blok DM">Blok DM</a> stage, removing the need for multiple fuel supplies and oxygen top-off due to boiling; the Proton-M also flew with a Blok-DM upper stage. Efforts were also made to reduce dependency on foreign (usually Ukrainian) component suppliers.</p>
<p>Proton launch vehicles and Briz-M upper stages are designed and built by Khrunichev State Research and Production Space Center (Khrunichev) in Moscow, the majority owner of International Launch Services (ILS). The Center is home to all engineering, assembly and test functions of Proton production. With the recent consolidation of the Russian space enterprises, Khrunichev has direct oversight and control of up to 70% of all Proton manufacturing from suppliers to manufacturers. The consolidation directly supports Khrunichev’s ongoing efforts for vertical integration of Proton production.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ilslaunch3_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-ilslaunch3-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>An enhanced variant, the Phase III Proton-M/Briz-M launch vehicle, was flight proven on the Russian Federal dual mission of Express AM-44 and Express MD-1 in February 2009 and performed its first commercial launch in March 2010 with the Echostar XIV satellite. The Proton-M/Briz-M phase III configuration provides 6150 kg of GTO performance, an increase of 1150 kg over the original Proton-M Briz-M, while maintaining the fundamental design configuration.</p>
<p>On 6 August 2012, the Russian Federal Space Agency lost a Russian and an Indonesian communications satellite in an attempt to launch them into orbit on a Proton-M due to technical difficulties with the last stage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-latimes_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-latimes-15">[15]</a></sup> On 2 July 2013, a Proton-M launching three <a href="/wiki/GLONASS" title="GLONASS">GLONASS</a> navigation satellites experienced a failure reminiscent of the 1960s disasters shortly after liftoff when the booster crashed near LC-39 at Baikonour, ending a 30-year unbroken stretch without a first stage failure; all future Proton flights were suspended pending investigation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rswproton_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-rswproton-16">[16]</a></sup> The accident was eventually determined to be caused by the rate gyro package having been installed upside-down. Due to the difficulty of installing the package incorrectly, it was widely suspected that it had been done deliberately by a disgruntled or drunk worker at the Khrunichev plant.</p>
<p>On 15 May 2014, a Proton-M/Briz-M carrying an Ekspress satellite suffered a third stage failure from a bad turbopump bearing. Debris fell in Manchuria. On 21 October, another Ekspress satellite was left in a useless orbit when the Briz stage cut off 24 seconds too early.</p>
<p>On 16 May 2015, a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mexican_Satellite_System_(MEXSAT)" title="Mexican Satellite System (MEXSAT)">MEXSAT</a> communications satellite failed to orbit due to another third stage malfunction, the eighth Proton failure since 2010.</p>
<p>Khrunichev has initiated development of a set of phase IV enhancements in order to keep pace with market demands and the mass growth trends of commercial satellites. The implementation of Phase IV Proton Briz-M enhancements were completed in 2016. The payload mass performance for phase IV has been increased to 6320 kg to a reference GTO orbit with 1500 m/s of residual delta V to GSO.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ilslaunch4_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-ilslaunch4-17">[17]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Future_developments">Future developments</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Proton_(rocket_family)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Future developments">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Significant upgrades were temporarily put on hold following announcement of the new <a href="/wiki/Angara_(rocket_family)" title="Angara (rocket family)">Angara</a> launch vehicle. The single largest upgrade was the KVRB stage. This <a href="/wiki/Cryogenic_fuel" title="Cryogenic fuel">cryogenic</a> stage would have greatly increased capacity. The engine was developed successfully, and the stage as a whole had progressed to hardware. However, as KVRB is noticeably larger than Blok D, the vehicle's aerodynamics, flight control, software, and possibly electronics would have to be reevaluated. In addition, the launch pad can supply existing Protons with common hypergolic fuels from single sources. The upper stages, in particular, are fed by common loading pipes running along the rocket. Switching to a stage with different fuels requires the addition of extra support articles; switching to cryogens requires that such support articles top off the stage periodically.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Heavy variants of Angara will be simpler and cheaper than Proton (and like the <a href="/wiki/Atlas_V" title="Atlas V">Atlas V</a> rocket, will not use hypergolic fuels; instead, it will use the same <a href="/wiki/RP-1" title="RP-1">RP-1</a> fuel as that used on the <a href="/wiki/Soyuz_(rocket_family)" title="Soyuz (rocket family)">Soyuz rocket</a>). They will also be designed from the start to accept a <a href="/wiki/KVTK_(rocket_stage)" title="KVTK (rocket stage)">KVTK</a> stage, and will already have a LOX supply at the pad; only a hydrogen supply will be called upon. However, delays in Angara development mean that Protons will continue to fly for some time.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
