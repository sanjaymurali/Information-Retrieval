<p><b>Typhoon Nina</b>, known in the Philippines as <b>Typhoon Bebeng</b>, was the fourth-deadliest <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone" title="Tropical cyclone">tropical cyclone</a> on record. Approximately 229,000 people died after the <a href="/wiki/Banqiao_Dam" title="Banqiao Dam">Banqiao Dam</a> (in <a href="/wiki/Zhumadian" title="Zhumadian">Zhumadian</a>, <a href="/wiki/Henan" title="Henan">Henan</a> Province, China) collapsed and devastated areas downstream. The collapse of the dam due to heavy floods also caused a string of smaller dams to collapse, adding more damage by the typhoon.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Meteorological_history">Meteorological history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Typhoon_Nina_(1975)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Meteorological history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Nina_1975_track.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1236" data-file-width="2000" height="136" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/70/Nina_1975_track.png/220px-Nina_1975_track.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/70/Nina_1975_track.png/330px-Nina_1975_track.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/70/Nina_1975_track.png/440px-Nina_1975_track.png 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Nina_1975_track.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Map plotting the track and intensity of the storm, according to the <a href="/wiki/Saffir%E2%80%93Simpson_scale" title="Saffir–Simpson scale">Saffir–Simpson scale</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>A well defined trough line extending southeastward into the Philippine Sea spawned a disturbance on July 29. After initial status as a disturbance, Tropical Depression 04W moved southwestward for 36 hours as the structure of the system began to organize. On July 31, the depression slowed down and began to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rapid_deepening" title="Rapid deepening">rapidly intensify</a> to a tropical storm and was named "Nina" then it began to turn to the northwest. A subtropical ridge prevented Nina from turning further north and it began to track west-northwest just before reaching typhoon intensity.</p>
<p>Nina underwent explosive development on the late hours of August 1. Aircraft reconnaissance reported a 65 hPa drop of pressure on the same day as well as August 2 with wind speeds increasing from a mere 65 kn (75 mph, 120 km/h) to 130 kt (150 mph, 240 km/h) during that period and it attained its peak intensity of 135 kn (155 mph, 250 km/h) later that day. The typhoon began to weaken as it approached <a href="/wiki/Taiwan" title="Taiwan">Taiwan</a>, making landfall near the coastal city of <a href="/wiki/Hualien_City" title="Hualien City">Hualien</a> as a Category 3 storm with 100 kn (115 mph, 185 km/h) winds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>The storm began to weaken as it battered across the island's central mountain range, sparing the most populated areas from the eyewall. It entered the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Formosa_Straits" title="Formosa Straits">Formosa Straits</a> as a weak typhoon and the storm came ashore near <a href="/wiki/Jinjiang,_Fujian" title="Jinjiang, Fujian">Jinjiang</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fujian" title="Fujian">Fujian</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/People%27s_Republic_of_China" title="People's Republic of China">China</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> After moving toward the northwest and crossing <a href="/wiki/Jiangxi" title="Jiangxi">Jiangxi</a>, it turned north on the night of August 5 near <a href="/wiki/Changde" title="Changde">Changde</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hunan" title="Hunan">Hunan</a>. A day later, the storm moved over <a href="/wiki/Xinyang" title="Xinyang">Xinyang</a>, <a href="/wiki/Henan" title="Henan">Henan</a>, and later was blocked by a cold front near <a href="/wiki/Zhumadian" title="Zhumadian">Zhumadian</a>, <a href="/wiki/Henan" title="Henan">Henan</a> for three days.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> The stationary thunderstorm system brought heavy rainfall, causing the infamous collapse of the <a href="/wiki/Banqiao_Dam" title="Banqiao Dam">Banqiao Dam</a>. The storm moved southwest on August 8, and dissipated soon afterwards.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Taiwan">Taiwan</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Typhoon_Nina_(1975)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Taiwan">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Upon making landfall in Taiwan, the storm brought winds of 185 km/h (115 mph) to places near the storm's eye.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ATCR_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-ATCR-5">[5]</a></sup> Wind gusts were also measured up to 222 km/h (138 mph).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Widespread heavy rainfall, peaking around 700 mm (28 in),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> from the storm triggered deadly flooding and landslides which killed 29 people and injured 168 others. Reports from the island indicate that 3,000 homes were damaged or destroyed by the typhoon.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ATCR_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-ATCR-5">[5]</a></sup> In the city of <a href="/wiki/Hualien_City" title="Hualien City">Hualien</a> alone, four people were killed, 561 homes were destroyed and 1,831 more were damaged.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Across the island, domestic flights, trains, and bus services were all suspended due to the storm; however, <a href="/wiki/Taipei_Songshan_Airport" title="Taipei Songshan Airport">Taipei Songshan Airport</a> remained open for international flights.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mainland_China">Mainland China</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Typhoon_Nina_(1975)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Mainland China">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Banqiao_Dam#1975_flood" title="Banqiao Dam">Banqiao Dam § 1975 flood</a></div>
<p>Due to the interaction with the mountains of Taiwan, Nina weakened to a tropical storm before making landfall in mainland China. The storm crossed the coastline with winds of 110 km/h (70 mph); however, little damage resulted near where the system struck land.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ATCR_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-ATCR-5">[5]</a></sup> Further inland, the remnants of the storm produced widespread torrential rainfall, with more than 400 mm (16 in) falling across an area of 19,410 km<sup>2</sup> (7,500 mi<sup>2</sup>). The heaviest rainfall was recorded along the <a href="/wiki/Banqiao_Dam" title="Banqiao Dam">Banqiao Dam</a> where 1,631 mm (64.2 in) of rain fell, 830 mm (33 in) of which fell in a six-hour span.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> These rains led to the collapse of the <a href="/wiki/Banqiao_Dam" title="Banqiao Dam">Banqiao Dam</a>, which received 1-in-2000-year flood conditions. In all, 62 dams failed during the disaster, causing large temporary <a href="/wiki/Lake" title="Lake">lakes</a> and $1.2 billion (1975 USD) in damage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
