<p>The <b>joule</b> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="/dʒ/: 'j' in 'jam'">dʒ</span><span title="/uː/: 'oo' in 'goose'">uː</span><span title="'l' in 'lie'">l</span></span>/</a></span></span>); symbol: J), is a <a href="/wiki/SI_derived_unit" title="SI derived unit">derived unit</a> of <a href="/wiki/Energy" title="Energy">energy</a> in the <a href="/wiki/International_System_of_Units" title="International System of Units">International System of Units</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> It is equal to the energy transferred to (or <a href="/wiki/Work_(physics)" title="Work (physics)">work</a> done on) an object when a <a href="/wiki/Force" title="Force">force</a> of one <a href="/wiki/Newton_(unit)" title="Newton (unit)">newton</a> acts on that object in the direction of its motion through a distance of one <a href="/wiki/Metre" title="Metre">metre</a> (1 newton metre or N⋅m). It is also the energy dissipated as heat when an electric <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Current_(electricity)" title="Current (electricity)">current</a> of one <a href="/wiki/Ampere" title="Ampere">ampere</a> passes through a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Resistance_(electricity)" title="Resistance (electricity)">resistance</a> of one <a href="/wiki/Ohm" title="Ohm">ohm</a> for one second. It is named after the English physicist <a href="/wiki/James_Prescott_Joule" title="James Prescott Joule">James Prescott Joule</a> (1818–1889).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>

<p>where kg is the <a href="/wiki/Kilogram" title="Kilogram">kilogram</a>, m is the <a href="/wiki/Metre" title="Metre">metre</a>, s is the <a href="/wiki/Second" title="Second">second</a>, N is the <a href="/wiki/Newton_(unit)" title="Newton (unit)">newton</a>, Pa is the <a href="/wiki/Pascal_(unit)" title="Pascal (unit)">pascal</a>, W is the <a href="/wiki/Watt" title="Watt">watt</a>, C is the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coulomb_(unit)" title="Coulomb (unit)">coulomb</a>, and V is the <a href="/wiki/Volt" title="Volt">volt</a>.</p>
<p>One joule can also be defined as:</p>
<ul>
<li>The work required to move an <a href="/wiki/Electric_charge" title="Electric charge">electric charge</a> of one <a href="/wiki/Coulomb" title="Coulomb">coulomb</a> through an <a href="/wiki/Voltage" title="Voltage">electrical potential difference</a> of one <a href="/wiki/Volt" title="Volt">volt</a>, or one "coulomb-volt" (C⋅V). This relationship can be used to define the volt.</li>
<li>The work required to produce one <a href="/wiki/Watt" title="Watt">watt</a> of <a href="/wiki/Power_(physics)" title="Power (physics)">power</a> for one <a href="/wiki/Second" title="Second">second</a>, or one "watt-second" (W⋅s) (compare <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kilowatt-hour" title="Kilowatt-hour">kilowatt-hour</a> –  3.6 megajoules). This relationship can be used to define the watt.</li>
</ul>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Usage">Usage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Joule&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Usage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>This <a href="/wiki/International_System_of_Units" title="International System of Units">SI</a> unit is named after <a href="/wiki/James_Prescott_Joule" title="James Prescott Joule">James Prescott Joule</a>. As with every International System of Units (SI) unit named for a person, the first letter of its <a href="/wiki/Symbol" title="Symbol">symbol</a> is <a href="/wiki/Letter_case" title="Letter case">upper case</a> (J). However, when an SI unit is spelled out in English, it should always begin with a <a href="/wiki/Letter_case" title="Letter case">lower case</a> letter (<i>joule</i>)—except in a situation where <i>any</i> word in that position would be capitalized, such as at the beginning of a sentence or in material using <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Title_case" title="Title case">title case</a>. Note that "degree <a href="/wiki/Celsius" title="Celsius">Celsius</a>" conforms to this rule because the "d" is lowercase.— Based on <i><a class="external text" href="http://www.bipm.org/en/publications/si-brochure/section5-2.html" rel="nofollow">The International System of Units</a></i>, section 5.2.</p>

<p>A result of this similarity is that the SI unit for torque is the <a href="/wiki/Newton_metre" title="Newton metre">newton metre</a>, which works out <a href="/wiki/Algebra" title="Algebra">algebraically</a> to have the same <a href="/wiki/Dimensional_analysis" title="Dimensional analysis">dimensions</a> as the Joule. But they are not interchangeable. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/CGPM" title="CGPM">CGPM</a> has given the unit of <a href="/wiki/Energy" title="Energy">energy</a> the name <i>Joule</i>, but has not given the unit of torque any special name, hence it is simply the newton metre (N⋅m) – a compound name derived from its constituent parts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BIPM2_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-BIPM2-5">[5]</a></sup> The use of newton metres for torque and joules for energy is helpful to avoid misunderstandings and miscommunications.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BIPM2_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-BIPM2-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>The distinction may be seen also in the fact that energy is a <a href="/wiki/Scalar_(physics)" title="Scalar (physics)">scalar</a> – the <a href="/wiki/Dot_product" title="Dot product">dot product</a> of a <a href="/wiki/Euclidean_vector" title="Euclidean vector">vector</a> force and a vector displacement. By contrast, torque is a vector – the <a href="/wiki/Cross_product" title="Cross product">cross product</a> of a distance vector and a force vector. Torque and energy are related to one another by the equation</p>

<p>where <i>E</i> is energy, <i>τ</i> is (the <a href="/wiki/Magnitude_(mathematics)#Euclidean_vector_space" title="Magnitude (mathematics)">vector magnitude</a> of) torque, and <i>θ</i> is the angle swept (in <a href="/wiki/Radian" title="Radian">radians</a>). Since radians are dimensionless, it follows that torque and energy have the same dimensions.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Practical_examples">Practical examples</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Joule&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Practical examples">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>One joule in everyday life represents approximately:</p>
<ul>
<li>The energy required to lift a medium-size tomato (100 g) 1 m vertically from the surface of the Earth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></li>
<li>The energy released when that same tomato falls back down to the ground.</li>
<li>The energy required to accelerate a 1 kg mass at 1 m⋅s<sup>−2</sup> through a distance of 1 m.</li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Heat" title="Heat">heat</a> required to raise the temperature of 1 g of water by 0.24 °C.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></li>
<li>The typical energy released as heat by a person at rest every 1/60 s (approximately 17 ms).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Kinetic_energy" title="Kinetic energy">kinetic energy</a> of a 50 kg human moving very slowly (0.2 m/s or 0.72 km/h).</li>
<li>The kinetic energy of a 56 g tennis ball moving at 6 m/s (22 km/h).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></li>
<li>The kinetic energy of an object with mass 1 kg moving at √2 ≈ 1.4 m/s.</li>
<li>The amount of electricity required to light a 1 W <a href="/wiki/Light-emitting_diode" title="Light-emitting diode">LED</a> for 1 s.</li>
</ul>
<p>Since the joule is also a watt-second and the common unit for electricity sales to homes is the kW⋅h (kilowatt-hour), a kW⋅h is thus 1000 W × 3600 s = 3.6 MJ (megajoules).</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Zeptojoule">Zeptojoule</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Joule&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Zeptojoule">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The zeptojoule (zJ) is equal to one sextillionth (10<sup>−21</sup>) of one joule. 160 zeptojoules is about one <a href="/wiki/Electronvolt" title="Electronvolt">electronvolt</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Nanojoule">Nanojoule</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Joule&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Nanojoule">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The nanojoule (nJ) is equal to one billionth (10<sup>−9</sup>) of one joule. 160 nanojoules is about the <a href="/wiki/Kinetic_energy" title="Kinetic energy">kinetic energy</a> of a flying mosquito.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Microjoule">Microjoule</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Joule&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Microjoule">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The microjoule (μJ) is equal to one millionth (10<sup>−6</sup>) of one joule. The <a href="/wiki/Large_Hadron_Collider" title="Large Hadron Collider">Large Hadron Collider</a> (LHC) produces collisions of the microjoule order (7 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electron_volt" title="Electron volt">TeV</a>) per particle.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Millijoule">Millijoule</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Joule&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Millijoule">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The millijoule (mJ) is equal to one thousandth (10<sup>−3</sup>) of a joule.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Kilojoule">Kilojoule</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Joule&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Kilojoule">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The kilojoule (kJ) is equal to one thousand (10<sup>3</sup>) joules. Nutritional food labels in most countries express energy in kilojoules (kJ).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cal_vs_kJ_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Cal_vs_kJ-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>One square metre of the <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a> receives about 1.4 kilojoules of <a href="/wiki/Sunlight#Solar_constant" title="Sunlight">solar radiation</a> every second in full daylight.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TSI_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-TSI-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Megajoule">Megajoule</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Joule&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Megajoule">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The megajoule (MJ) is equal to one million (10<sup>6</sup>) joules, or approximately the kinetic energy of a one megagram (tonne) vehicle moving at 161 km/h.</p>
<p>The energy required to heat 10 liters of liquid water at constant pressure from 0 °C (32 °F) to 100 °C (212 °F) is approximately 4.2 MJ.</p>
<p>One <a href="/wiki/Kilowatt_hour" title="Kilowatt hour">kilowatt hour</a> of electricity is 3.6 megajoules.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Gigajoule">Gigajoule</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Joule&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Gigajoule">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The gigajoule (GJ) is equal to one billion (10<sup>9</sup>) joules. 6 GJ is about the <a href="/wiki/Chemical_energy" title="Chemical energy">chemical energy</a> of combusting 1 barrel (160 l) of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Crude_oil" title="Crude oil">crude oil</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability"><span title="The material near this tag failed verification of its source citation(s). (September 2017)">not in citation given</span></a></i>]</sup> 2 GJ is about the <a href="/wiki/Planck_energy" title="Planck energy">Planck energy</a> unit.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Terajoule">Terajoule</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Joule&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Terajoule">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The terajoule (TJ) is equal to one trillion (10<sup>12</sup>) joules; about 0.278 GWh (often used in energy tables). About 63 TJ of energy was released by <a href="/wiki/Little_Boy" title="Little Boy">the atomic bomb that exploded over Hiroshima</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hironaga_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-hironaga-14">[14]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/International_Space_Station" title="International Space Station">International Space Station</a>, with a mass of approximately 450 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Megagram" title="Megagram">megagrams</a> and orbital velocity of 7.7 km/s,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-iss_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-iss-15">[15]</a></sup> has a <a href="/wiki/Kinetic_energy" title="Kinetic energy">kinetic energy</a> of roughly 13 TJ. In 2017 <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Irma" title="Hurricane Irma">Hurricane Irma</a> was estimated to contain 100 TJ of energy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Petajoule">Petajoule</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Joule&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Petajoule">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The petajoule (PJ) is equal to one quadrillion (10<sup>15</sup>) joules. 210 PJ is about 50 megatons of TNT. This is the amount of energy released by the <a href="/wiki/Tsar_Bomba" title="Tsar Bomba">Tsar Bomba</a>, the largest man-made nuclear explosion ever.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Exajoule">Exajoule</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Joule&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Exajoule">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The exajoule (EJ) is equal to 10<sup>18</sup> (one quintillion) joules. The <a href="/wiki/2011_T%C5%8Dhoku_earthquake_and_tsunami" title="2011 Tōhoku earthquake and tsunami">2011 Tōhoku earthquake and tsunami</a> in Japan had 1.41 EJ of energy according to its rating of 9.0 on the <a href="/wiki/Moment_magnitude_scale" title="Moment magnitude scale">moment magnitude scale</a>. Yearly <a href="/wiki/Energy_in_the_United_States" title="Energy in the United States">U.S. energy consumption</a> amounts to roughly 94 EJ.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Zettajoule">Zettajoule</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Joule&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Zettajoule">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The zettajoule (ZJ) is equal to one sextillion (10<sup>21</sup>) joules. The human <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/World_energy_resources_and_consumption" title="World energy resources and consumption">annual global energy consumption</a> is approximately 0.5 ZJ.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Yottajoule">Yottajoule</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Joule&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Yottajoule">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The yottajoule (YJ) is equal to one septillion (10<sup>24</sup>) joules. This is approximately the amount of energy required to heat <a href="/wiki/Water#On_Earth" title="Water">all the water on Earth</a> by 1 °C. The thermal output of the <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">Sun</a> is approximately 400 YJ per second.</p>
