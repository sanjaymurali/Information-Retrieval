<p><b>Baikonur Cosmodrome</b> (<a href="/wiki/Russian_language" title="Russian language">Russian</a>: <span lang="ru" xml:lang="ru">Космодро́м Байкону́р <b>Kosmodrom Baykonur</b></span>; <a href="/wiki/Kazakh_language" title="Kazakh language">Kazakh</a>: <span lang="kk" xml:lang="kk">Байқоңыр ғарыш айлағы <i>Bayqoñır ğarış aylağı</i></span>) is a <a href="/wiki/Spaceport" title="Spaceport">spaceport</a> located in southern <a href="/wiki/Kazakhstan" title="Kazakhstan">Kazakhstan</a>.</p>
<p>Baikonur Cosmodrome is the world's first and largest operational <a href="/wiki/Spaceport" title="Spaceport">space launch facility</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> The spaceport is located in the <a href="/wiki/Kazakh_Steppe" title="Kazakh Steppe">desert steppe</a> of <a href="/wiki/Baikonur" title="Baikonur">Baikonur</a>, about 200 kilometres (124 mi) east of the <a href="/wiki/Aral_Sea" title="Aral Sea">Aral Sea</a> and north of the river <a href="/wiki/Syr_Darya" title="Syr Darya">Syr Darya</a>. It is near the <a href="/wiki/Tyuratam" title="Tyuratam">Tyuratam</a> railway station and is about 90 metres (300 ft) above sea level.</p>
<p>The spaceport is currently leased by the <a href="/wiki/Government_of_Kazakhstan" title="Government of Kazakhstan">Kazakh Government</a> to <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russia</a> until <a href="/wiki/2050" title="2050">2050</a>, the spaceport is managed jointly by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Roscosmos_State_Corporation" title="Roscosmos State Corporation">Roscosmos State Corporation</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Russian_Aerospace_Forces" title="Russian Aerospace Forces">Russian Aerospace Forces</a>.</p>
<p>The shape of the area leased is an <a href="/wiki/Ellipse" title="Ellipse">ellipse</a>, measuring 90 kilometres (56 mi) east–west by 85 kilometres (53 mi) north–south, with the cosmodrome at the centre. It was originally built by the <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet Union</a> in the late 1950s as the base of operations for the <a href="/wiki/Soviet_space_program" title="Soviet space program">Soviet space program</a>. Under the current <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Russian_space_program" title="Russian space program">Russian space program</a>, Baikonur remains a busy spaceport, with numerous commercial, military, and scientific missions being launched annually.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> All crewed Russian <a href="/wiki/Spaceflight" title="Spaceflight">spaceflights</a> are launched from Baikonur.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NASA_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-NASA-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>Both <i><a href="/wiki/Sputnik_1" title="Sputnik 1">Sputnik 1</a></i>, the first artificial satellite, and <a href="/wiki/Vostok_1" title="Vostok 1">Vostok 1</a>, the first <a href="/wiki/Human_spaceflight" title="Human spaceflight">human spaceflight</a>, were launched from Baikonur. The launch pad used for both missions was renamed <a href="/wiki/Gagarin%27s_Start" title="Gagarin's Start">Gagarin's Start</a> in honor of Russian Soviet <a href="/wiki/Astronaut" title="Astronaut">cosmonaut</a> <a href="/wiki/Yuri_Gagarin" title="Yuri Gagarin">Yuri Gagarin</a>, pilot of Vostok 1 and first human in space.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Baikonur_Cosmodrome&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Soviet_era">Soviet era</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Baikonur_Cosmodrome&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Soviet era">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Baikonur_CIA_U-2.gif"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="579" data-file-width="675" height="214" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c5/Baikonur_CIA_U-2.gif/250px-Baikonur_CIA_U-2.gif" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c5/Baikonur_CIA_U-2.gif/375px-Baikonur_CIA_U-2.gif 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c5/Baikonur_CIA_U-2.gif/500px-Baikonur_CIA_U-2.gif 2x" width="250"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Baikonur_CIA_U-2.gif" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/Lockheed_U-2" title="Lockheed U-2">U-2</a> Photograph of R-7 Launch Pad in Tyura-Tam</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The Soviet government issued the decree for Scientific Research Test Range No. 5 (NIIP-5; <a href="/wiki/Russian_language" title="Russian language">Russian</a>: <span lang="ru" xml:lang="ru"><i>Nauchno-Issledovatel’skii Ispytatel’nyi Poligon N.5</i></span>) on 12 February 1955. It was actually founded on 2 June 1955, originally a test center for the world's first <a href="/wiki/Intercontinental_ballistic_missile" title="Intercontinental ballistic missile">intercontinental ballistic missile (ICBM)</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> the <a href="/wiki/R-7_Semyorka" title="R-7 Semyorka">R-7 Semyorka</a>. NIIP-5 was soon expanded to include launch facilities for space flights. The site was selected by a commission led by Gen. Vasily Voznyuk, influenced by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sergey_Korolyov" title="Sergey Korolyov">Sergey Korolyov</a>, the Chief Designer of the R-7 ICBM, and soon the man behind the Soviet space program. It had to be surrounded by plains, as the radio control system of the rocket required (at the time) receiving uninterrupted signals from ground stations hundreds of kilometres away.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-vs_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-vs-6">[6]</a></sup> Additionally, the missile trajectory had to be away from populated areas. Also, it is an advantage to place a space launch site closer to the equator, as the surface of the Earth has higher rotational speed there. Taking these constraints into consideration, the commission chose Tyuratam, a village in the heart of the <a href="/wiki/Kazakh_Steppe" title="Kazakh Steppe">Kazakh Steppe</a>. The expense of constructing the launch facilities and the several hundred kilometres of new road and train lines made the Cosmodrome one of the most costly infrastructure projects the Soviets undertook.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> A supporting town was built around the facility to provide housing, schools and infrastructure for workers. It was raised to city status in 1966 and named <a href="/wiki/Baikonur" title="Baikonur">Leninsk</a>.</p>
<p>The American <a href="/wiki/Lockheed_U-2" title="Lockheed U-2">U-2 high-altitude reconnaissance plane</a> found and photographed the Tyuratam missile test range for the first time on 5 August 1957.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Name">Name</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Baikonur_Cosmodrome&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Name">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>There are conflicting sources about origins of the name <i>Baikonur</i>. Some sources say that it was not until 1961 (i.e. until Gagarin's flight), when the name was deliberately chosen to misdirect<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-vs_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-vs-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-apollo_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-apollo-7">[7]</a></sup> the West to a place about 320 kilometres (199 mi) northeast of the launch center, the small mining town of Baikonur near <a href="/wiki/Jezkazgan" title="Jezkazgan">Jezkazgan</a>.</p>
<p>Other sources state that <i>Baikonur</i> was name of the <a href="/wiki/Tyuratam" title="Tyuratam">Tyuratam</a> region even before the cosmodrome existed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-apollo_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-apollo-7">[7]</a></sup> The main cosmodrome-supporting town Leninsk was renamed to <a href="/wiki/Baikonur" title="Baikonur">Baikonur</a> on 20 December 1995 by <a href="/wiki/Boris_Yeltsin" title="Boris Yeltsin">Boris Yeltsin</a>.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Environmental_impact">Environmental impact</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Baikonur_Cosmodrome&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Environmental impact">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Russian scientist <a href="/wiki/Afanasiy_Ilich_Tobonov" title="Afanasiy Ilich Tobonov">Afanasiy Ilich Tobonov</a> researched mass animal deaths in the 1990s and concluded that the mass deaths of birds and wildlife in the <a href="/wiki/Sakha_Republic" title="Sakha Republic">Sakha Republic</a> were noted only along the flight paths of space rockets launched from the Baikonur cosmodrome.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Dead wildlife and livestock were usually incinerated, and the participants in these incinerations, including Tobonov himself, his brothers and inhabitants of his native village of Eliptyan, commonly died from stroke or cancer. In 1997, the Ministry of Defense of the Russian Federation changed the flight path and removed the ejected rocket stages near <a href="/wiki/Nyurbinsky_District" title="Nyurbinsky District">Nyurbinsky District</a>, Russia.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Scientific literature collected enough data that confirmed adverse effects of rockets on environment and health of the population.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ijbch.kaznu.kz_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-ijbch.kaznu.kz-9">[9]</a></sup> Heptyl, or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/UDMH" title="UDMH">UDMH</a>, a fuel used in Russian rocket engines, is considered to be highly toxic. It is one of the reasons for acid rains and cancers in the local population, near the cosmodrome. Valery Yakovlev, a head of the laboratory of ecosystem research of the State scientific-production union of applied ecology "Kazmechanobr", notes: "Scientists have established the extreme character of destructive influence of "Baikonur" space center on environment and population of the region: 11 000 tons of space scrap metal, polluted by especially toxic heptyl is still laying on the falling grounds." <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Importance">Importance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Baikonur_Cosmodrome&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Importance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Many historic flights lifted off from Baikonur: the first operational <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/ICBM" title="ICBM">ICBM</a>; the first man-made satellite, <a href="/wiki/Sputnik_1" title="Sputnik 1">Sputnik 1</a>, on 4 October 1957; the first spacecraft to travel close to the Moon, <a href="/wiki/Luna_1" title="Luna 1">Luna 1</a>, on 2 January 1959; the first manned and orbital flight by <a href="/wiki/Yuri_Gagarin" title="Yuri Gagarin">Yuri Gagarin</a> on 12 April 1961; and the flight of the first woman in space, <a href="/wiki/Valentina_Tereshkova" title="Valentina Tereshkova">Valentina Tereshkova</a>, in 1963. 14 cosmonauts of 13 other nations, such as <a href="/wiki/Czechoslovakia" title="Czechoslovakia">Czechoslovakia</a>, <a href="/wiki/East_Germany" title="East Germany">East Germany</a>, <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a> and <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a>, started their journeys from here as well under the <a href="/wiki/Interkosmos" title="Interkosmos">Interkosmos</a> program. In 1960, a prototype <a href="/wiki/R-16_(missile)" title="R-16 (missile)">R-16</a> ICBM <a href="/wiki/Nedelin_catastrophe" title="Nedelin catastrophe">exploded before launch</a>, killing over 100 people. Baikounr is also the site where <a href="/wiki/Venera_9" title="Venera 9">Venera 9</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mars_3" title="Mars 3">Mars 3</a> were launched from.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Russian_era">Russian era</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Baikonur_Cosmodrome&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Russian era">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Soyuz_expedition_19_launch_pad.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1965" data-file-width="3000" height="164" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/59/Soyuz_expedition_19_launch_pad.jpg/250px-Soyuz_expedition_19_launch_pad.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/59/Soyuz_expedition_19_launch_pad.jpg/375px-Soyuz_expedition_19_launch_pad.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/59/Soyuz_expedition_19_launch_pad.jpg/500px-Soyuz_expedition_19_launch_pad.jpg 2x" width="250"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Soyuz_expedition_19_launch_pad.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
A Soyuz rocket is erected into position at the Baikonur Cosmodrome's <a href="/wiki/Gagarin%27s_Start" title="Gagarin's Start">Pad 1/5 (Gagarin's Start)</a> on 24 March 2009. The rocket launched the crew of <a href="/wiki/Expedition_19" title="Expedition 19">Expedition 19</a> and a spaceflight participant on 26 March 2009.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Following the <a href="/wiki/Dissolution_of_the_Soviet_Union" title="Dissolution of the Soviet Union">dissolution of the Soviet Union</a> in 1991, the Russian space program continued to operate from Baikonur under the auspices of the <a href="/wiki/Commonwealth_of_Independent_States" title="Commonwealth of Independent States">Commonwealth of Independent States</a>. Russia wanted to sign a 99-year lease for Baikonur, but agreed to a $115 million annual lease of the site for 20 years with an option for a 10-year extension.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> On 8 June 2005, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Federation_Council_of_Russia" title="Federation Council of Russia">Russian Federation Council</a> ratified an agreement between <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Kazakhstan" title="Kazakhstan">Kazakhstan</a> extending Russia's rent term of the spaceport until 2050. The rent price—which remained fixed at <span style="white-space: nowrap">US$115,000,000</span> per year — is the source of a long-running dispute between the two countries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> In an attempt to reduce its dependency on Baikonur, Russia is constructing the <a href="/wiki/Vostochny_Cosmodrome" title="Vostochny Cosmodrome">Vostochny Cosmodrome</a> in <a href="/wiki/Amur_Oblast" title="Amur Oblast">Amur Oblast</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>With the retirement of the American Space Shuttle program, Baikonur has been the only launch site for <a href="/wiki/International_Space_Station" title="International Space Station">International Space Station</a> missions using Russian spacecraft.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NASA_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-NASA-4">[4]</a></sup> Following the conclusion of the <a href="/wiki/Space_Shuttle_program" title="Space Shuttle program">Space Shuttle program</a>, Russian spacecraft are now the only means by which astronauts can travel to the International Space Station, making Baikonur the sole launch site used for manned missions to the ISS.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Features">Features</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Baikonur_Cosmodrome&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Features">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Baikonur is fully equipped with facilities for launching both manned and unmanned <a href="/wiki/Spacecraft" title="Spacecraft">spacecraft</a>. It supports several generations of <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russian</a> spacecraft: <a href="/wiki/Soyuz_(rocket_family)" title="Soyuz (rocket family)">Soyuz</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Proton_(rocket)" title="Proton (rocket)">Proton</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tsyklon" title="Tsyklon">Tsyklon</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dnepr_(rocket)" title="Dnepr (rocket)">Dnepr</a>, <a href="/wiki/Zenit_(rocket_family)" title="Zenit (rocket family)">Zenit</a> and <a href="/wiki/Buran_(spacecraft)" title="Buran (spacecraft)">Buran</a>. During the temporary lapse of the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>' <a href="/wiki/Space_Shuttle" title="Space Shuttle">Space Shuttle</a> program after the <a href="/wiki/Space_Shuttle_Columbia_disaster" title="Space Shuttle Columbia disaster"><i>Columbia</i> Disaster</a> in 2003 it played an essential role in operating and resupplying of the <a href="/wiki/International_Space_Station" title="International Space Station">International Space Station</a> (ISS) with <a href="/wiki/Soyuz_(spacecraft)" title="Soyuz (spacecraft)">Soyuz</a> and <a href="/wiki/Progress_(spacecraft)" title="Progress (spacecraft)">Progress</a> spacecraft. Its high latitude of 46° N required the high orbital inclination of the ISS.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>Downrange from the launchpad, spent launch equipment is dropped directly on the ground where it is salvaged by the workers and the local population.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Buran_facilities">Buran facilities</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Baikonur_Cosmodrome&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Buran facilities">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Buran_program" title="Buran program">Buran program</a></div>
<p>As part of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Buran_program" title="Buran program">Buran program</a>, several facilities were adapted or newly built for the Buran-class space shuttle orbiters:</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Baikonur_Cosmodrome_Site_110" title="Baikonur Cosmodrome Site 110">Site 110</a> — Used for the launch of the Buran-class orbiters. Like the assembly and processing hall at Site 112, the launch complex was originally constructed for the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Soviet_manned_lunar_programs" title="Soviet manned lunar programs">Soviet lunar landing program</a> and later converted for the Energia-Buran program.</li>
<li>Site 112 — Used for orbiter maintenance and to mate the orbiters to their <a href="/wiki/Energia" title="Energia">Energia</a> launchers (thus fulfilling a role similar to the <a href="/wiki/Vehicle_Assembly_Building" title="Vehicle Assembly Building">VAB</a> at <a href="/wiki/Kennedy_Space_Center" title="Kennedy Space Center">KSC</a>). The main hangar at the site, called MIK RN or MIK 112, was originally built for the assembly of the <a href="/wiki/N1_(rocket)" title="N1 (rocket)">N1 moon rocket</a>. After cancellation of the N-1 program in 1974, the facilities at Site 112 were converted for the Energia-Buran program. It was here that <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/1.01_(Buran-class_spacecraft)" title="1.01 (Buran-class spacecraft)">Orbiter K1</a> was stored after the end of the Buran program and was destroyed when the hangar roof collapsed in 2002.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></li>
<li>Site 251 — Used as Buran orbiter landing facility, also known as Yubileyniy Airfield (and fulfilling a role similar to the <a href="/wiki/Shuttle_Landing_Facility" title="Shuttle Landing Facility">SLF</a> at <a href="/wiki/Kennedy_Space_Center" title="Kennedy Space Center">KSC</a>). It features one runway, called 06/24, which is 4,500 metres (4,900 yd) long and 84 metres (92 yd) wide, paved with "Grade 600" high quality reinforced concrete. At the edge of the runway was a special <a href="/wiki/Mate-Demate_Device" title="Mate-Demate Device">mating–demating device</a>, designed to lift an orbiter off its <a href="/wiki/Antonov_An-225_Mriya" title="Antonov An-225 Mriya">Antonov An-225 Mriya</a> carrier aircraft and load it on a transporter, which would carry the orbiter to the processing building at Site 254. A purpose-built orbiter landing control facility, housed in a large multi-store office building, was located near the runway. Yubileyniy Airfield was also used to receive heavy transport planes carrying elements of the Energia-Buran system. After the end of the Buran program, Site 251 was abandoned but later reopened as a commercial cargo airport. Besides serving Baikonur, Kazakh authorities also use it for passenger and charter flights from Russia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></li>
<li>Site 254 — Built to service the Buran-class orbiters between flights (thus fulfilling a role similar to the <a href="/wiki/Orbiter_Processing_Facility" title="Orbiter Processing Facility">OPF</a> at <a href="/wiki/Kennedy_Space_Center" title="Kennedy Space Center">KSC</a>). Constructed in the 1980s as a special four-bay building, it also featured a large processing area flanked by several floors of test rooms. After cancellation of the Buran program it was adapted for pre-launch operations of the <a href="/wiki/Soyuz_(spacecraft)" title="Soyuz (spacecraft)">Soyuz</a> and <a href="/wiki/Progress_(spacecraft)" title="Progress (spacecraft)">Progress</a> spacecraft.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></li>
</ul>

<p>All Baikonur's <a href="/wiki/Logistics" title="Logistics">logistics</a> are based on its own intra-site <a href="/wiki/5_ft_and_1520_mm_gauge_railways" title="5 ft and 1520 mm gauge railways">1,520 mm (5 ft) gauge</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Railway" title="Railway">railway</a> network, which is the largest <a href="/wiki/Industrial_railway" title="Industrial railway">industrial railway</a> on the planet. The railway is used for all stages of launch preparation, and all spacecraft are transported to the launchpads by the special <a href="/wiki/Schnabel_car" title="Schnabel car">Schnabel cars</a>. Once part of the <a href="/wiki/Russian_Railway_Troops" title="Russian Railway Troops">Soviet Railroad Troops</a>, the Baikonur Railway is now served by a dedicated civilian state company. There are several rail links connecting the Baikonur Railway to <a href="/wiki/Kazakhstan_Temir_Zholy" title="Kazakhstan Temir Zholy">the public railway of Kazakhstan</a> and the rest of the world.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Baikonur_airports">Baikonur airports</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Baikonur_Cosmodrome&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Baikonur airports">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Baikonur Cosmodrome has two on-site multi-purpose <a href="/wiki/Airport" title="Airport">airports</a>, serving both the personnel transportation needs and the logistics of space launches (including the delivery of the spacecraft by planes). There are scheduled passenger services from <a href="/wiki/Moscow" title="Moscow">Moscow</a> to the smaller <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Krayniy_Airport&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Krayniy Airport (page does not exist)">Krayniy Airport</a> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/International_Air_Transport_Association_airport_code" title="International Air Transport Association airport code">IATA</a>: <b>BXY</b>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/International_Civil_Aviation_Organization_airport_code" title="International Civil Aviation Organization airport code">ICAO</a>: <b>UAOL</b>), which however are not accessible to the public. The larger Yubileyniy Airport (Юбилейный аэропорт) (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/International_Civil_Aviation_Organization_airport_code" title="International Civil Aviation Organization airport code">ICAO</a>: <b>UAON</b>) was where the <a href="/wiki/Buran_(spacecraft)" title="Buran (spacecraft)">Buran orbiter</a> was transported to Baikonur on the back of the <a href="/wiki/Antonov_An-225_Mriya" title="Antonov An-225 Mriya">Antonov An-225 <i>Mriya</i></a> cargo aircraft.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="ICBM_testing">ICBM testing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Baikonur_Cosmodrome&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: ICBM testing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Although Baikonur has always been known around the world as the launch site of Soviet and Russian space missions, from its outset in 1955 and until the collapse of the USSR in 1991 the primary purpose of this center was to test liquid-fueled <a href="/wiki/Ballistic_missile" title="Ballistic missile">ballistic missiles</a>. The official (and secret) name of the center was State Test Range No. 5 or 5 GIK. It remained under control of the Soviet and <a href="/wiki/Ministry_of_Defence_(Russia)" title="Ministry of Defence (Russia)">Russian Ministry of Defense</a> until the second half of the 1990s, when the Russian civilian space agency and its industrial contractors started taking over individual facilities.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>In 2006, the head of Roskosmos, <a href="/wiki/Anatoly_Perminov" title="Anatoly Perminov">Anatoly Perminov</a>, said that the last Russian military personnel would be removed from the Baikonur facility by 2007. However, on 22 October 2008 an <a href="/wiki/UR-100N" title="UR-100N">SS-19 Stiletto</a> missile was test fired from Baikonur, indicating this may not be the case.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Future_projects">Future projects</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Baikonur_Cosmodrome&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Future projects">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>On 22 December 2004, Kazakhstan and Russia signed a contract establishing the "Russia–Kazakhstan Baiterek JV" joint venture, in which each country holds a 50-percent stake. The goal of the project is the construction of the <a href="/wiki/Baikonur_Cosmodrome_Site_250" title="Baikonur Cosmodrome Site 250">Bayterek</a> ("<a href="/wiki/Populus" title="Populus">poplar</a> tree") space launch complex, to facilitate operations of the Russian <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Angara_(rocket)" title="Angara (rocket)">Angara</a> rocket launcher.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> This will allow launches with a payload of 26 tons to <a href="/wiki/Low_Earth_orbit" title="Low Earth orbit">low Earth orbit</a>, compared to 20 tons using the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Proton_(rocket)" title="Proton (rocket)">Proton</a> system. An additional benefit will be that the Angara uses kerosene as fuel and oxygen as the oxidiser, which is less hazardous to the environment than the toxic fuels used by older boosters. The total expenditure on the Kazakh side will be $223 million over 19 years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> As of 2010, the project was stalling due to insufficient funding. It was thought that the project still had good chances to succeed because it will allow both parties – Russia and Kazakhstan – to continue the joint use of Baikonur even after the <a href="/wiki/Vostochny_Cosmodrome" title="Vostochny Cosmodrome">Vostochny Cosmodrome</a> is commissioned.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-observer_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-observer-26">[26]</a></sup> The first scheduled launch of the Baiterek Rocket and Space Complex is scheduled for 2025.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BRSC25_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-BRSC25-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Baikonur_Museum">Baikonur Museum</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Baikonur_Cosmodrome&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Baikonur Museum">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Baikonur Cosmodrome has a small museum, next to two small cottages, once residences of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sergey_Korolev" title="Sergey Korolev">Sergey Korolev</a> and <a href="/wiki/Yuri_Gagarin" title="Yuri Gagarin">Yuri Gagarin</a>. Both cottages are part of the museum complex and have been preserved. The museum is home to a collection of space artifacts. A restored test artifact from the Soviet <a href="/wiki/Buran_programme" title="Buran programme">Buran programme</a> sits next to the museum entrance. The vehicle that flew a single orbital test mission in 1988 was destroyed in a hangar collapse in 2002;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> For a complete list of Buran artifacts, see <a href="/wiki/Buran_(spacecraft)" title="Buran (spacecraft)">Buran (spacecraft)</a>.</p>
<p>The museum also houses photographs related to the cosmodrome's history, including images of all cosmonauts. Every crew of every expedition launched from Baikonur leaves behind a signed crew photograph that is displayed behind the glass.</p>
<p>Baikonur's museum holds many objects related to Gagarin, including the ground control panel from his flight, his uniforms, and soil from his landing site, preserved in a silver container. One of the museum rooms also holds an older version of the Soyuz descent capsule.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_popular_culture">In popular culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Baikonur_Cosmodrome&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: In popular culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul>
<li>In <i><a href="/wiki/Star_Trek:_The_Next_Generation" title="Star Trek: The Next Generation">Star Trek: The Next Generation</a></i> the Baikonur Cosmodrome was a <a href="/wiki/United_Federation_of_Planets" title="United Federation of Planets">Federation</a> shipyard where the <i>SS Tsiolkovsky</i> was launched.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup></li>
<li>In <i><a href="/wiki/Command_%26_Conquer:_Generals" title="Command &amp; Conquer: Generals">Command &amp; Conquer: Generals</a></i>, US and Chinese forces guard the facility against GLA attack. However, their failure allows the GLA to attack the world using MIRVs launched from this facility.
<ul>
<li>In its expansion pack, <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Command_%26_Conquer:_Generals_Zero_Hour" title="Command &amp; Conquer: Generals Zero Hour">Command &amp; Conquer: Generals Zero Hour</a></i>, US forces destroy the facility after the GLA attack in Northern Europe.</li>
</ul>
</li>
<li>In <i><a href="/wiki/Call_of_Duty:_Black_Ops" title="Call of Duty: Black Ops">Call of Duty: Black Ops</a></i>, this facility is featured as the main location of the mission "Executive Order", involving sabotage of the Soviet space program, and is the location of the multiplayer map "Launch" as well as the zombies map "Ascension".</li>
<li>In <i><a href="/wiki/Jormungand_(manga)" title="Jormungand (manga)">Jormungand</a></i>, in the final episode "Century of Shame", Koko and Valmet are at the cosmodrome with Kasper, Jonah, and Chiquita as Koko launches the final rocket.</li>
<li>Much of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Big_Bang_Theory" title="The Big Bang Theory">The Big Bang Theory</a></i> episode "<a href="/wiki/The_Big_Bang_Theory_(season_5)#Episodes" title="The Big Bang Theory (season 5)">The Countdown Reflection</a>" takes place atop a rocket at the facility.</li>
<li>In <i><a href="/wiki/Destiny_(video_game)" title="Destiny (video game)">Destiny</a></i>, the fictional Russian Federal Bureau of Aeronautics (РФБА) in "Old Russia" is based on the Baikonur Cosmodrome. Also, Old Russia, the first location in-game is based off the Cosmodrome.</li>
<li>In <i><a href="/wiki/Blue_Gender" title="Blue Gender">Blue Gender</a></i>, the Baikonur Cosmodrome is an important plot element, providing transportation back to Second Earth. Over a span of several episodes, the protagonists travel across Asia to Baikonur and attempt to board a space shuttle at the Cosmodrome.</li>
<li>In the book <i><a href="/wiki/Plague_Ship_(Cussler_novel)" title="Plague Ship (Cussler novel)">Plague Ship</a></i> by <a href="/wiki/Clive_Cussler" title="Clive Cussler">Clive Cussler</a>, Juan Cabrillo relates how he had infiltrated the facility to sabotage a fictional <a href="/wiki/Kinetic_bombardment" title="Kinetic bombardment">Orbital Ballistic Projectile Weapon</a> which was being launched on an Energia rocket.</li>
<li>In the book <i>Locked On</i> by Tom Clancy, the Cosmodrome is seized by Dagestani terrorists under orders from rogue elements in the Pakistani ISI, threatening to destroy Moscow with stolen nuclear weapons in order to force the Russian government into granting Dagestan and Chechnya independence.</li>
<li>In the game <i><a href="/wiki/Rust" title="Rust">Rust</a></i>, the monument "Launch Site" is heavily inspired by the Cosmodrome.</li>
</ul>
