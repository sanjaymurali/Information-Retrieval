<p>The <b>effects of Hurricane Isabel in Delaware</b> resulted in one of only thirteen presidential disaster declarations for the state of <a href="/wiki/Delaware" title="Delaware">Delaware</a>. <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Isabel" title="Hurricane Isabel">Hurricane Isabel</a> formed from a <a href="/wiki/Tropical_wave" title="Tropical wave">tropical wave</a> on September 6, 2003 in the tropical <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Ocean" title="Atlantic Ocean">Atlantic Ocean</a>. It moved northwestward, and within an environment of light wind shear and warm waters it steadily strengthened to reach peak winds of 165 mph (265 km/h) on September 11. After fluctuating in intensity for four days, Isabel gradually weakened and made landfall on the <a href="/wiki/Outer_Banks" title="Outer Banks">Outer Banks</a> of <a href="/wiki/North_Carolina" title="North Carolina">North Carolina</a> with winds of 105 mph (165 km/h) on September 18. It quickly weakened over land and became <a href="/wiki/Extratropical_cyclone" title="Extratropical cyclone">extratropical</a> over <a href="/wiki/Pennsylvania" title="Pennsylvania">Pennsylvania</a> the next day. The storm's center remained to the south and west of Delaware, and was about 175 miles (280 km) from Delaware at its closest approach. At that time, Isabel was a strong tropical storm located in central <a href="/wiki/Virginia" title="Virginia">Virginia</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tcr_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-tcr-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>The effects of the hurricane were compounded by flooding caused by the remnants of <a href="/wiki/Tropical_Storm_Henri_(2003)" title="Tropical Storm Henri (2003)">Tropical Storm Henri</a> days before. Moderate winds of up to 62 mph (100 km/h) downed numerous trees, tree limbs, and power lines across the state, leaving at least 15,300 without power. Numerous low-lying areas were flooded due to high surf, strong storm surge, or run-off from flooding further inland. The passage of Hurricane Isabel resulted in $40 million in damage (2003 USD, ($52.1 million 2017 USD)) and no casualties in the state.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Preparations">Preparations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Effects_of_Hurricane_Isabel_in_Delaware&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Preparations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Isabel_2003_track.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="766" data-file-width="1024" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5d/Isabel_2003_track.png/220px-Isabel_2003_track.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5d/Isabel_2003_track.png/330px-Isabel_2003_track.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5d/Isabel_2003_track.png/440px-Isabel_2003_track.png 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Isabel_2003_track.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Track of Hurricane Isabel</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>On September 16, 44 hours before Hurricane Isabel made landfall on the Outer Banks of North Carolina, the <a href="/wiki/National_Hurricane_Center" title="National Hurricane Center">National Hurricane Center</a> issued a <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone_warnings_and_watches" title="Tropical cyclone warnings and watches">tropical storm watch</a> for the entire coastline of Delaware. The National Hurricane Center also briefly issued a hurricane watch for the coastline. On September 17, 26 hours before Isabel made landfall, the watches were changed to tropical storm warnings.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tcr_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-tcr-1">[1]</a></sup> While over the western Atlantic Ocean as a <a href="/wiki/List_of_Category_5_Atlantic_hurricanes" title="List of Category 5 Atlantic hurricanes">Category 5 hurricane</a>, forecasters predicted Isabel would move northwestward and within five days be at a position 155 miles (250 km) south-southeast of <a href="/wiki/Lewes,_Delaware" title="Lewes, Delaware">Lewes</a> as a 115 mph (185 km/h) major hurricane.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lawrence32_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-lawrence32-2">[2]</a></sup> Though located within the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/1-2-3_rule" title="1-2-3 rule">cone of uncertainty</a>, all subsequent forecasts predicted a landfall on North Carolina with the hurricane passing to the west of the state.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-isabelarchive_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-isabelarchive-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Governor_of_Delaware" title="Governor of Delaware">Governor</a> <a href="/wiki/Ruth_Ann_Minner" title="Ruth Ann Minner">Ruth Ann Minner</a> declared a state of emergency prior to the arrival of the hurricane. The declaration also included for the activation of the Delaware <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_States_National_Guard" title="United States National Guard">National Guard</a> to assist in hurricane related duties.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fib_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-fib-4">[4]</a></sup> Minner mandated residents in 13 low-lying areas of <a href="/wiki/Sussex_County,_Delaware" title="Sussex County, Delaware">Sussex County</a> to evacuate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wrap_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-wrap-5">[5]</a></sup> In all, 787 people evacuated across the state to seven emergency shelters set up by the <a href="/wiki/American_Red_Cross" title="American Red Cross">American Red Cross</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-isabel2_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-isabel2-6">[6]</a></sup> Officials began recommending visitors to leave potentially affected areas by three days before Isabel made landfall. Governor Minner mandated all schools to be closed on the day of the hurricane's landfall, and recommended all residents in mobile homes to evacuate and for businesses to close. The <a href="/wiki/University_of_Delaware" title="University of Delaware">University of Delaware</a> preemptively canceled classes. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cape_May-Lewes_Ferry" title="Cape May-Lewes Ferry">Cape May-Lewes Ferry</a> closed for several days in anticipation of the storm.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wrap_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-wrap-5">[5]</a></sup> The Delaware Department of Transportation planned to place restrictions on state roads in the event strong wind gusts were recorded.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-isabel1_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-isabel1-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Impact">Impact</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Effects_of_Hurricane_Isabel_in_Delaware&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Impact">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Isabel_2003_rainfall.gif"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="878" data-file-width="992" height="221" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/92/Isabel_2003_rainfall.gif/250px-Isabel_2003_rainfall.gif" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/92/Isabel_2003_rainfall.gif/375px-Isabel_2003_rainfall.gif 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/92/Isabel_2003_rainfall.gif/500px-Isabel_2003_rainfall.gif 2x" width="250"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Isabel_2003_rainfall.gif" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Rainfall totals from Hurricane Isabel</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Strong swells from the hurricane produced a moderate <a href="/wiki/Storm_surge" title="Storm surge">storm surge</a> which peaked at 8.66 feet (2.64 m) in <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Reedy_Point,_Delaware&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Reedy Point, Delaware (page does not exist)">Reedy Point</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tcr_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-tcr-1">[1]</a></sup> Tides were only slightly above normal, though high surf on top of the storm surge resulted in beach erosion,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ncdc_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-ncdc-8">[8]</a></sup> particularly in Sussex County.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-phil_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-phil-9">[9]</a></sup> Waves of 17 feet (5.18 m) in height were reported near the coastline.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-isabel1_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-isabel1-7">[7]</a></sup> The high waters breached dunes south of <a href="/wiki/Bethany_Beach,_Delaware" title="Bethany Beach, Delaware">Bethany Beach</a>, and several locations along <a href="/wiki/Delaware_Route_1" title="Delaware Route 1">Delaware Route 1</a> were flooded.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ncdc_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-ncdc-8">[8]</a></sup> The large circulation of Isabel produced gusty winds across the state, including a maximum of 70 mph (113 km/h) in <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Delaware_Pilot_Tower&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Delaware Pilot Tower (page does not exist)">Delaware Pilot Tower</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Delaware_Bay" title="Delaware Bay">Delaware Bay</a>. Onshore, gusts peaked at 62 mph (100 km/h) in Lewes,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-phil_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-phil-9">[9]</a></sup> where sustained winds of 53 mph (85 km/h) were also reported.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tcr_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-tcr-1">[1]</a></sup> Precipitation was heavy but sporadic, amounting to a maximum of 1.88 inches (48 mm) in <a href="/wiki/Greenwood,_Delaware" title="Greenwood, Delaware">Greenwood</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ncdc_8-2"><a href="#cite_note-ncdc-8">[8]</a></sup> Heavy rainfall further inland resulted in moderate to severe river flooding. The <a href="/wiki/Christina_River" title="Christina River">Christina River</a> at <a href="/wiki/Cooch%27s_Bridge" title="Cooch's Bridge">Cooch's Bridge</a> crested at 2.38 feet (0.725 m) above flood stage, and the <a href="/wiki/Red_Clay_Creek" title="Red Clay Creek">Red Clay Creek</a> at <a href="/wiki/Wooddale,_Delaware" title="Wooddale, Delaware">Wooddale</a> crested at roughly 4 inches (100 mm) above flood stage. <a href="/wiki/Surface_runoff" title="Surface runoff">Runoff</a> from streams were slowed due to the approaching storm surge from the hurricane.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ncdc_8-3"><a href="#cite_note-ncdc-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>At the <a href="/wiki/Delaware_Breakwater_East_End_Light" title="Delaware Breakwater East End Light">Delaware Breakwater East End Lighthouse</a> in Lewes, strong waves destroyed the lower deck of the lighthouse, while powerful winds blew out the watchroom window. A group of eight volunteers quickly repaired the damage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-digest_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-digest-10">[10]</a></sup> Moderate wind gusts knocked down numerous trees, tree limbs, and power lines, causing widespread power outages in the state. <a href="/wiki/Pepco_Holdings" title="Pepco Holdings">Conectiv Energy</a> reported the power outage associated with the hurricane as one of the worst in its history.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ncdc_8-4"><a href="#cite_note-ncdc-8">[8]</a></sup> At least 15,300 were left without power during the worst of the hurricane,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-isabel1_7-2"><a href="#cite_note-isabel1-7">[7]</a></sup> including 2,500 in the capital city of <a href="/wiki/Dover,_Delaware" title="Dover, Delaware">Dover</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dover_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-dover-11">[11]</a></sup> Due to the power outages, only one traffic light north of the city of <a href="/wiki/Wilmington,_Delaware" title="Wilmington, Delaware">Wilmington</a> was operational.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-isabel4_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-isabel4-12">[12]</a></sup> The strong winds also resulted in the <a href="/wiki/Delaware_River_and_Bay_Authority" title="Delaware River and Bay Authority">Delaware River and Bay Authority</a> to reduce the speed limit on the <a href="/wiki/Delaware_Memorial_Bridge" title="Delaware Memorial Bridge">Delaware Memorial Bridge</a> to 40 mph (65 km/h).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-isabel1_7-3"><a href="#cite_note-isabel1-7">[7]</a></sup> 62 roads throughout the state were initially closed due to flooding, downed trees, or downed power lines.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-isabel4_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-isabel4-12">[12]</a></sup> Eight roads, including <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._Route_13_Alternate_(Delmar,_Delaware)" title="U.S. Route 13 Alternate (Delmar, Delaware)">U.S. Route 13A</a> remained closed for several days, primarily due to flooding.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wrap_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-wrap-5">[5]</a></sup> Flooding affected the cities of <a href="/wiki/Seaford,_Delaware" title="Seaford, Delaware">Seaford</a>, <a href="/wiki/Blades,_Delaware" title="Blades, Delaware">Blades</a>, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Bayview,_Delaware&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Bayview, Delaware (page does not exist)">Bayview</a>, and <a href="/wiki/St._Augustine_Beach,_Florida" title="St. Augustine Beach, Florida">Augustine Beach</a>, with residents in the latter two being forced to evacuate due to severe flooding conditions. Several state parks reported downed trees and damage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-isabel4_12-2"><a href="#cite_note-isabel4-12">[12]</a></sup> Damage in the state totaled $40 million (2003 USD, $44 million 2006 USD), and there were no deaths in the state as a result of the storm.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tcr_1-4"><a href="#cite_note-tcr-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Aftermath">Aftermath</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Effects_of_Hurricane_Isabel_in_Delaware&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Aftermath">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>On September 20, two days after Hurricane Isabel passed the state, Governor Ruth Ann Minner made a formal request for a federal disaster declaration for the state.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wrap_5-3"><a href="#cite_note-wrap-5">[5]</a></sup> Later that day, <a href="/wiki/President_of_the_United_States" title="President of the United States">President</a> <a href="/wiki/George_W._Bush" title="George W. Bush">George W. Bush</a> issued a federal disaster declaration for Delaware, one of twelve disaster declarations for the state. The declaration allowed for the use of federal disaster funds and emergency resources to help families and businesses recover from the effects of Hurricane Isabel.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-delar_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-delar-13">[13]</a></sup> State and federal officials opened a disaster recovery center in <a href="/wiki/Georgetown,_Delaware" title="Georgetown, Delaware">Georgetown</a> and Wilmington to assist individuals who suffered losses from Isabel and the remnants of Tropical Storm Henri just days before.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-drc_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-drc-14">[14]</a></sup> 761 people visited the recovery centers before they closed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-final_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-final-15">[15]</a></sup> One week after the disaster declaration, residents began receiving checks for those who applied for aid.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-check_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-check-16">[16]</a></sup> By about two months after the passage of Hurricane Isabel, 659 residents applied for assistance, with slightly over $1 million (2003 USD, $1.1 million 2006 USD) being distributed to the victims. 141 loan applications were received, as well. FEMA distributed about $2.5 million ($3.25 million 2017 USD) in Small Business Administration loans, and also received 183 public assistance loans for repair or replacement of public facilities.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-final_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-final-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>A total of 35 power crews, along with outside contractors, worked to restore power. By two days after the storm, 2,000 remained without power in scattered areas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wrap_5-4"><a href="#cite_note-wrap-5">[5]</a></sup> Various locations in the city of Dover were without power for about 30 hours. There, the removal of debris exceeded the capacity of the local landfall, resulting in officials temporarily storing it elsewhere.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dover_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-dover-11">[11]</a></sup> Over 200 volunteers donated time, food and money to provide hot meals for individuals and families affected by the storm. Support teams provided by county officials transported over 300 tons of ruined accessories from the storm, such as appliances, carpets, and drywall, to local landfills.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cmbt_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-cmbt-17">[17]</a></sup> Qualifying for two <a href="/wiki/NASCAR" title="NASCAR">NASCAR</a> races in <a href="/wiki/Dover_International_Speedway" title="Dover International Speedway">Dover</a> were cancelled due to the hurricane, although the races went on as scheduled.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nascar_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-nascar-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
