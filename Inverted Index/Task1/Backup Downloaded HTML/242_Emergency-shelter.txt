<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Emergency shelter</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

<p>An <b>emergency shelter</b> is a place for people to live temporarily when they cannot live in their previous residence, similar to <a href="/wiki/Homeless_shelter" title="Homeless shelter">homeless shelters</a>. The main difference is that an emergency shelter typically specializes in people fleeing a specific type of situation, such as natural or man-made <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Disasters" title="Disasters">disasters</a>, <a href="/wiki/Domestic_violence" title="Domestic violence">domestic violence</a>, or victims of <a href="/wiki/Sexual_abuse" title="Sexual abuse">sexual abuse</a>. A more minor difference is that people staying in emergency shelters are more likely to stay all day, except for work, school, or errands, while homeless shelters usually expect people to stay elsewhere during the day, returning only to sleep or eat. Emergency shelters sometimes facilitate <a href="/wiki/Support_group" title="Support group">support groups</a>, and/or provide meals.</p>
<p><b>Post-disaster emergency shelter</b> is often provided by organizations or governmental emergency management departments, in response to natural disasters, such as a flood or earthquake. They tend to use <a href="/wiki/Tent" title="Tent">tents</a> or other temporary structures, or buildings normally used for another purpose, such as a <a href="/wiki/Church_(building)" title="Church (building)">church</a> or school. These settlements may be inhabited for the entire duration of the reconstruction process and should be thought of more as settlements than shelter, and need to be planned with respect to water / sanitation, livelihoods.</p>
<p>A newer category of emergency shelter is the <a href="/wiki/Warming_center" title="Warming center">warming center</a>. Warming centers typically open during particularly cold or rainy nights. They are available to persons who decline to accept homeless shelters, are not allowed to use homeless shelters, or are not homeless but have inadequate or malfunctioning heat in their homes.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Mass_emergency_shelters">Mass emergency shelters</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Emergency_shelter&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Mass emergency shelters">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>One example of a mass emergency shelter is the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Louisiana_Superdome" title="Louisiana Superdome">Louisiana Superdome</a>, which was used as a hurricane shelter during <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Katrina" title="Hurricane Katrina">Hurricane Katrina</a>. More than 20,000 storm refugees crowded into the arena seeking sanctuary from the winds and waters of Katrina, filling seats, ramps, corridors, and the artificial turf field. The refugees were met with a myriad of new challenges, including stifling heat, stench, filth, unsanitary facilities, and a shortage of food and drinking water. Violent assaults and rapes were reported, as well as one unconfirmed suicide.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Sustainable_design">Sustainable design</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Emergency_shelter&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Sustainable design">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Sustainable design is often employed in response to global environmental crises, the rapid growth of economic activity and human population, depletion of natural resources, damage to <a href="/wiki/Ecosystem" title="Ecosystem">ecosystems</a>, and loss of <a href="/wiki/Biodiversity" title="Biodiversity">biodiversity</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-shu-yang_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-shu-yang-3">[3]</a></sup> In 2013, eco architecture writer <a href="/wiki/Bridgette_Meinhold" title="Bridgette Meinhold">Bridgette Meinhold</a> surveyed emergency and long-term sustainable housing projects that were developed in response to these crises in her book <i>Urgent Architecture: 40 Sustainable Housing Solutions for a Changing World</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wwnorton_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-wwnorton-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Featured projects focus on <a href="/wiki/Green_building" title="Green building">green building</a>, sustainable design, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eco-friendly" title="Eco-friendly">eco-friendly</a> materials, <a href="/wiki/Affordable_housing" title="Affordable housing">affordability</a>, <a href="/wiki/Reuse" title="Reuse">material reuse</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Humanitarian_relief" title="Humanitarian relief">humanitarian relief</a>. Construction methods and materials include repurposed <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shipping_containers" title="Shipping containers">shipping containers</a>, <a href="/wiki/Straw-bale_construction" title="Straw-bale construction">straw bale construction</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sandbag" title="Sandbag">sandbag</a> homes, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Floating_home" title="Floating home">floating homes</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
