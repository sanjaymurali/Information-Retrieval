<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Space environment</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">
					Jump to:					<a href="#mw-head">navigation</a>, 					<a href="#p-search">search</a>
</div>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><p><b>Space environment</b> is a branch of <a href="/wiki/Astronautics" title="Astronautics">astronautics</a>, <a href="/wiki/Aerospace_engineering" title="Aerospace engineering">aerospace engineering</a> and <a href="/wiki/Space_physics" title="Space physics">space physics</a> that seeks to understand and address conditions existing in space that affect the design and operation of spacecraft. A related subject, <a href="/wiki/Space_weather" title="Space weather">space weather</a>, deals with dynamic processes in the solar-terrestrial system that can give rise to effects on spacecraft, but that can also affect the atmosphere, <a href="/wiki/Ionosphere" title="Ionosphere">ionosphere</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Geomagnetic_field" title="Geomagnetic field">geomagnetic field</a>, giving rise to several other kinds of effects on human technologies.</p>
<p>Effects on spacecraft can arise from <a href="/wiki/Radiation" title="Radiation">radiation</a>, <a href="/wiki/Space_debris" title="Space debris">space debris</a> and <a href="/wiki/Meteoroid" title="Meteoroid">meteoroid</a> impact, upper <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atmospheric_drag" title="Atmospheric drag">atmospheric drag</a> and <a href="/wiki/Spacecraft_charging" title="Spacecraft charging">spacecraft electrostatic charging</a>.</p>
<p>Radiation in space usually comes from three main sources: (i) the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Van_Allen_radiation_belts" title="Van Allen radiation belts">Van Allen radiation belts</a>; (ii) <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solar_proton_event" title="Solar proton event">solar proton events</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solar_energetic_particle" title="Solar energetic particle">solar energetic particles</a>; and (iii) galactic <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cosmic_rays" title="Cosmic rays">cosmic rays</a>. For long duration missions, the high doses of radiation can damage electronic components and solar cells. A major concern is also radiation-induced "single-event effects" such as <a href="/wiki/Single_event_upset" title="Single event upset">single event upset</a>. Manned missions usually avoid the radiation belts and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/International_space_station" title="International space station">international space station</a> is at an altitude well below the most severe regions of the radiation belts. During solar energetic events (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solar_flares" title="Solar flares">solar flares</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coronal_mass_ejections" title="Coronal mass ejections">coronal mass ejections</a>) particles can be accelerated to very high energies and can reach the Earth in times as short as 30 minutes (but usually take some hours). These particles are mainly protons and heavier ions that can cause radiation damage, disruption to logic circuits and even hazards to astronauts. Manned missions to return to the Moon or to travel to Mars will have to deal with the major problems presented by solar particle events to radiation safety, in addition to the important contribution to doses from the low-level background <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cosmic_rays" title="Cosmic rays">cosmic rays</a>. In near-Earth orbits, the Earth's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Geomagnetic_field" title="Geomagnetic field">geomagnetic field</a> screens spacecraft from a large part of these hazards - a process called <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Geomagnetic_shielding&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Geomagnetic shielding (page does not exist)">geomagnetic shielding</a>. Space debris and meteoroids can impact spacecraft at high speeds, causing mechanical or electrical damage. The average speed of space debris is 10 km/s <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> while the average speed of meteoroids is much greater. For example, the meteoroids associated with the <a href="/wiki/Perseids" title="Perseids">Perseid meteor shower</a> travel at an average speed of 58 km/s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> Mechanical damage from debris impacts have been studied through space missions including <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/LDEF" title="LDEF">LDEF</a>, which had over 20,000 documented impacts through its 5.7 year mission.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Electrical anomalies associated with impact events include <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/ESA" title="ESA">ESA</a>'s Olympus spacecraft, which lost attitude control during the 1993 Perseid meteor shower.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> A similar event occurred with the <a href="/wiki/Landsat_5" title="Landsat 5">Landsat 5</a> spacecraft<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> during the 2009 Perseid meteor shower.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>Spacecraft electrostatic charging is caused by the hot <a href="/wiki/Plasma_(physics)" title="Plasma (physics)">plasma</a> environment around the Earth. The plasma encountered in the region of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Geostationary" title="Geostationary">geostationary</a> orbit becomes heated during geomagnetic <a href="/wiki/Substorm" title="Substorm">substorms</a> caused by disturbances in the solar wind. "Hot" electrons (with energies in the kilo-<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electron_volt" title="Electron volt">electron volt</a> range) collect on surfaces of spacecraft and can establish electrostatic potentials of the order of kilovolts. As a result, discharges can occur and are known to be the source of many spacecraft anomalies.</p>
<p>Solutions devised by scientists and engineers include, but are not limited to, spacecraft shielding, special "hardening" of electronic systems, various collision detection systems. Evaluation of effects during spacecraft design includes application of various models of the environment, including radiation belt models, spacecraft-plasma interaction models and atmospheric models to predict drag effects encountered in lower orbits and during reentery.</p>
<p>The field often overlaps with the disciplines of <a href="/wiki/Astrophysics" title="Astrophysics">astrophysics</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atmospheric_science" title="Atmospheric science">atmospheric science</a>, <a href="/wiki/Space_physics" title="Space physics">space physics</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Geophysics" title="Geophysics">geophysics</a>, albeit usually with an emphasis on application.</p>
<p>The United States government maintains a <a href="/wiki/Space_Weather_Prediction_Center" title="Space Weather Prediction Center">Space Weather Prediction Center</a> at <a href="/wiki/Boulder,_Colorado" title="Boulder, Colorado">Boulder, Colorado</a>. The Space Weather Prediction Center (SWPC) is part of the <a href="/wiki/National_Oceanic_and_Atmospheric_Administration" title="National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration">National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration</a> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/NOAA" title="NOAA">NOAA</a>). SWPC is one of the <a href="/wiki/National_Weather_Service" title="National Weather Service">National Weather Service</a>'s (NWS) <a href="/wiki/National_Centers_for_Environmental_Prediction" title="National Centers for Environmental Prediction">National Centers for Environmental Prediction</a> (NCEP).</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Space_weather" title="Space weather">Space weather</a> effects on Earth can include <a href="/wiki/Ionosphere" title="Ionosphere">ionospheric</a> storms, temporary decreases in <a href="/wiki/Ozone" title="Ozone">ozone</a> densities, disruption to radio communication, to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/GPS" title="GPS">GPS</a> signals and submarine positioning. Some scientists also theorize links between <a href="/wiki/Sunspot" title="Sunspot">sunspot</a> activity and <a href="/wiki/Ice_age" title="Ice age">ice ages</a>. <a class="external autonumber" href="http://ff.org/centers/csspp/library/co2weekly/20060920/20060920_13.html" rel="nofollow">[1]</a></p>
