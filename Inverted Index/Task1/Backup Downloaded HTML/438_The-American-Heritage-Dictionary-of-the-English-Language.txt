<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en"><i>The American Heritage Dictionary of the English Language</i></h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">
					Jump to:					<a href="#mw-head">navigation</a>, 					<a href="#p-search">search</a>

<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note"><span class="plainlinks selfreference noprint">For the Wikipedia template to cite the online edition of <i>The American Heritage Dictionary of the English Language</i>, see <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Template:AHDict" title="Template:AHDict">Template:AHDict</a>.</span></div>
<p><i><b>The American Heritage Dictionary of the English Language</b></i> (<i><b>AHD</b></i>) is an <a href="/wiki/American_English" title="American English">American</a> <a href="/wiki/Dictionary" title="Dictionary">dictionary</a> of English published by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Boston,_Massachusetts" title="Boston, Massachusetts">Boston</a> publisher <a href="/wiki/Houghton_Mifflin_Harcourt" title="Houghton Mifflin Harcourt">Houghton Mifflin</a>, the first edition of which appeared in 1969. Its creation was spurred by the controversy over the <i><a href="/wiki/Webster%27s_Third_New_International_Dictionary" title="Webster's Third New International Dictionary">Webster's Third New International Dictionary</a></i>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_American_Heritage_Dictionary_of_the_English_Language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>James Parton, the publisher (and co-owner) of the history magazine <i><a href="/wiki/American_Heritage_(magazine)" title="American Heritage (magazine)">American Heritage</a></i>, was appalled by the <a href="/wiki/Webster%27s_Third_New_International_Dictionary#Reception_and_criticisms" title="Webster's Third New International Dictionary">permissiveness of <i>Webster's Third</i></a>, published in 1961, and tried to buy the <a href="/wiki/Merriam-Webster" title="Merriam-Webster">G. and C. Merriam</a> Company so he could undo the changes. When that failed, he contracted with Houghton to publish a new dictionary. The <i>AHD</i> was edited by William Morris and relied on a usage panel of 105 writers, speakers, and eminent persons chosen for their well-known conservatism in the use of language.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ottenhoff_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-ottenhoff-1">[1]</a></sup> However, Morris made inconsistent use of the panels, often ignoring their advice and inserting his own opinions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ottenhoff_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-ottenhoff-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Linguistics">Linguistics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_American_Heritage_Dictionary_of_the_English_Language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Linguistics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <i>AHD</i> broke ground among dictionaries by using <a href="/wiki/Corpus_linguistics" title="Corpus linguistics">corpus linguistics</a> for compiling word frequencies and other information. It took the innovative step of combining <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Prescription_and_description" title="Prescription and description">prescriptive</a></i> information (how language should be used) and <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Prescription_and_description" title="Prescription and description">descriptive</a></i> information (how it actually is used). The descriptive information was derived from actual texts.</p>
<p>Citations were based on a million-word, three-line <a href="/wiki/Brown_Corpus" title="Brown Corpus">citation database</a> prepared by <a href="/wiki/Brown_University" title="Brown University">Brown University</a> linguist <a href="/wiki/Henry_Ku%C4%8Dera" title="Henry Kučera">Henry Kučera</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Usage_panel">Usage panel</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_American_Heritage_Dictionary_of_the_English_Language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Usage panel">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>For expert consultation on words or constructions whose usage is controversial or problematic, the American Heritage Dictionary relies on the advice of a usage panel. In its current form, the panel comprises nearly 200 prominent members of professions whose work demands sensitivity to language. Present and former members of the usage panel include novelists (<a href="/wiki/Isaac_Asimov" title="Isaac Asimov">Isaac Asimov</a>, <a href="/wiki/Barbara_Kingsolver" title="Barbara Kingsolver">Barbara Kingsolver</a>, <a href="/wiki/David_Foster_Wallace" title="David Foster Wallace">David Foster Wallace</a> and <a href="/wiki/Eudora_Welty" title="Eudora Welty">Eudora Welty</a>), poets (<a href="/wiki/Rita_Dove" title="Rita Dove">Rita Dove</a>, <a href="/wiki/Galway_Kinnell" title="Galway Kinnell">Galway Kinnell</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mary_Oliver" title="Mary Oliver">Mary Oliver</a> and <a href="/wiki/Robert_Pinsky" title="Robert Pinsky">Robert Pinsky</a>), playwrights (<a href="/wiki/Terrence_McNally" title="Terrence McNally">Terrence McNally</a> and <a href="/wiki/Marsha_Norman" title="Marsha Norman">Marsha Norman</a>), journalists (<a href="/wiki/Liane_Hansen" title="Liane Hansen">Liane Hansen</a> and <a href="/wiki/Susan_Stamberg" title="Susan Stamberg">Susan Stamberg</a>), literary critics (<a href="/wiki/Harold_Bloom" title="Harold Bloom">Harold Bloom</a>), columnists and commentators (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/William_F._Buckley,_Jr." title="William F. Buckley, Jr.">William F. Buckley, Jr.</a> and <a href="/wiki/Robert_J._Samuelson" title="Robert J. Samuelson">Robert J. Samuelson</a>), linguists and cognitive scientists (<a href="/wiki/Anne_Curzan" title="Anne Curzan">Anne Curzan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Steven_Pinker" title="Steven Pinker">Steven Pinker</a> and <a href="/wiki/Calvert_Watkins" title="Calvert Watkins">Calvert Watkins</a>) and humorists (<a href="/wiki/Garrison_Keillor" title="Garrison Keillor">Garrison Keillor</a>, <a href="/wiki/David_Sedaris" title="David Sedaris">David Sedaris</a> and <a href="/wiki/Alison_Bechdel" title="Alison Bechdel">Alison Bechdel</a>). Pinker, author of the <a href="/wiki/Style_guide" title="Style guide">style guide</a> <i><a href="/wiki/The_Sense_of_Style" title="The Sense of Style">The Sense of Style</a></i>, is its current chair, as of 2016.<sup class="plainlinks noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=The_American_Heritage_Dictionary_of_the_English_Language&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>The members of the panel are sent regular ballots asking about matters of usage; the completed ballots are returned and tabulated, and the results form the basis for special usage notes appended to the relevant dictionary entries. In many cases, these notes not only report the percentage of panelists who consider a given usage or construction to be acceptable, but will also report the results from balloting of the same question in past decades, to give a clearer sense of how the language changes over time.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Illustrations">Illustrations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_American_Heritage_Dictionary_of_the_English_Language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Illustrations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <i>AHD</i> is also somewhat innovative in its liberal use of photographic illustrations, which at the time was highly unusual for general reference dictionaries, many of which went largely or completely unillustrated. It also has an unusually large number of biographical entries for notable persons.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="First_edition">First edition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_American_Heritage_Dictionary_of_the_English_Language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: First edition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The first edition appeared in 1969, highly praised for its <a href="/wiki/Indo-European_languages" title="Indo-European languages">Indo-European</a> <a href="/wiki/Etymology" title="Etymology">etymologies</a>. In addition to the normally expected etymologies, which for instance trace the word <i>ambiguous</i> to a <a href="/wiki/Proto-Indo-European_language" title="Proto-Indo-European language">Proto-Indo-European</a> <a href="/wiki/Root" title="Root">root</a> <i>ag-</i>, meaning "to drive," the appendices included a seven-page article by Professor <a href="/wiki/Calvert_Watkins" title="Calvert Watkins">Calvert Watkins</a> entitled "Indo-European and the Indo-Europeans" and "Indo-European Roots", 46 pages of entries that are each organized around one of some thousand Proto-Indo-European roots and the English words of the AHD that are understood to have evolved from them. These entries might be called "reverse etymologies": the <i>ag-</i> entry there, for instance, lists 49 terms derived from it, words as diverse as <i>agent</i>, <i>essay</i>, <i>purge</i>, <i>stratagem</i>, <i>ambassador</i>, <i>axiom</i>, and <i>pellagra</i>, along with information about varying routes through intermediate transformations on the way to the contemporary words.</p>
<p>A compacted <i>American Heritage College Dictionary</i> was first released in 1974.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Second_and_later_editions">Second and later editions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_American_Heritage_Dictionary_of_the_English_Language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Second and later editions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The first edition's concise successor, <i>The American Heritage Dictionary, Second College Edition</i> was published in 1982 (without a larger-format version). It omitted the <a href="/wiki/Indo-European_languages" title="Indo-European languages">Indo-European</a> etymologies, but they were reintroduced in the third full edition, published in 1992. The third edition was also a departure for the publisher because it was developed in a database, which facilitated the use of the linguistic data for other applications, such as electronic dictionaries.</p>
<p>The fourth edition (2000, reissued in 2006) added an appendix of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Semitic_language" title="Semitic language">Semitic language</a> etymological roots, and included color illustrations, and was also available with a <a href="/wiki/CD-ROM" title="CD-ROM">CD-ROM</a> edition in some versions. This revision was larger than a typical desk dictionary but smaller than <i>Webster's Third New International Dictionary</i> or the unabridged <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Random_House_Dictionary_of_the_English_Language" title="Random House Dictionary of the English Language">Random House Dictionary of the English Language</a></i>. A lower-priced college edition, also the fourth, was issued in black-and-white printing and with fewer illustrations, in 2002 (reprinted in 2007 and 2010).</p>
<p>The fifth and most recent full edition was published in November 2011,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AHD_Fifth_Edition.2C_2011_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-AHD_Fifth_Edition.2C_2011-3">[3]</a></sup> with new printings in 2012 and 2016. It is available in hardcover and, with reduced print size and smaller page count, <a href="/wiki/Paperback#Trade" title="Paperback">trade paperback</a> form. It dropped several of the supplementary features of the fourth edition, and is not available with a disc-based electronic version. The university-student version was rebranded <i>The American Heritage College Writer's Dictionary</i> in 2013, and stripped of biographical and geographical entries to make room for more vocabulary while simultaneously reducing the number of pages compared to the fourth college edition.</p>
<p>The <i>AHD</i> inserts minor revisions (such as a biographical entry, with photograph, for each newly elected U.S. President) in successive printings of any given edition.</p>
<p>Supporting volumes have been issued, including <i>The American Heritage Book of English Usage</i>, <i>The American Heritage Dictionary of Indo-European Roots</i>, <i>The American Heritage <a href="/wiki/Abbreviation" title="Abbreviation">Abbreviations</a> Dictionary</i>, <i>The American Heritage Dictionary of <a href="/wiki/Idiom" title="Idiom">Idioms</a></i>, <i>The American Heritage <a href="/wiki/Thesaurus" title="Thesaurus">Thesaurus</a></i> in various sizes; <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Usage_dictionary" title="Usage dictionary">usage dictionaries</a> of special vocabulary such as <i>The American Heritage Science Dictionary</i>, <i>The American Heritage Medical Dictionary</i> and <i>The American Heritage Dictionary of Business Terms</i>; plus special dictionary editions for children, high-school students, and English-language learners. The <i>American Heritage</i> brand is also used for a series of American history books.</p>
