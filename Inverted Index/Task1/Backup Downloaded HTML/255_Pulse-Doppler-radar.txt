<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Pulse-Doppler radar</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">
					Jump to:					<a href="#mw-head">navigation</a>, 					<a href="#p-search">search</a>

<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="thumb tright">

<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:MAKS-2007-Radar.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Airborne pulse-Doppler radar antenna</div>
</div>

<p>A <b>pulse-Doppler radar</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Radar" title="Radar">radar</a> system that determines the range to a target using pulse-timing techniques, and uses the <a href="/wiki/Doppler_effect" title="Doppler effect">Doppler effect</a> of the returned signal to determine the target object's velocity. It combines the features of pulse radars and <a href="/wiki/Continuous-wave_radar" title="Continuous-wave radar">continuous-wave radars</a>, which were formerly separate due to the complexity of the <a href="/wiki/Electronics" title="Electronics">electronics</a>.</p>
<p>Pulse-Doppler systems were first widely used on <a href="/wiki/Fighter_aircraft" title="Fighter aircraft">fighter aircraft</a> starting in the 1960s. Earlier radars had used pulse-timing in order to determine range and the angle of the antenna (or similar means) to determine the bearing. However, this only worked when the radar antenna was not pointed down; in that case the reflection off the ground overwhelmed any returns from other objects. As the ground moves at the same speed but opposite direction of the aircraft, Doppler techniques allow the ground return to be filtered out, revealing aircraft and vehicles. This gives pulse-Doppler radars "<a href="/wiki/Look-down/shoot-down" title="Look-down/shoot-down">look-down/shoot-down</a>" capability. A secondary advantage in military radar is to reduce the transmitted power while achieving acceptable performance for improved safety of stealthy radar.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Pulse-Doppler techniques also find widespread use in <a href="/wiki/Weather_radar" title="Weather radar">meteorological radars</a>, allowing the radar to determine <a href="/wiki/Wind_speed" title="Wind speed">wind speed</a> from the velocity of any precipitation in the air. Pulse-Doppler radar is also the basis of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Synthetic_aperture_radar" title="Synthetic aperture radar">synthetic aperture radar</a> used in <a href="/wiki/Radar_astronomy" title="Radar astronomy">radar astronomy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Remote_sensing" title="Remote sensing">remote sensing</a> and mapping. In air traffic control, they are used for discriminating aircraft from clutter. Besides the above conventional surveillance applications, pulse-Doppler radar has been successfully applied in healthcare, such as fall risk assessment and fall detection, for nursing or clinical purposes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pulse-Doppler_radar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The earliest radar systems failed to operate as expected. The reason was traced to Doppler effects that degrade performance of systems not designed to account for moving objects. Fast-moving objects cause a phase-shift on the transmit pulse that can produce signal cancellation. Doppler has maximum detrimental effect on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Moving_target_indicator" title="Moving target indicator">moving target indicator</a> systems, which must use reverse phase shift for Doppler compensation in the detector.</p>
<p>Doppler weather effects (precipitation) were also found to degrade <a href="/wiki/Radar" title="Radar">conventional radar</a> and moving target indicator radar, which can mask aircraft reflections. This phenomenon was adapted for use with <a href="/wiki/Weather_radar" title="Weather radar">weather radar</a> in the 1950s after declassification of some World War II systems.</p>
<p>Pulse-Doppler radar was developed during World War II to overcome limitations by increasing <a href="/wiki/Pulse_repetition_frequency" title="Pulse repetition frequency">pulse repetition frequency</a>. This required the development of <a href="/wiki/Klystron" title="Klystron">klystron</a>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Traveling_wave_tube" title="Traveling wave tube">traveling wave tube</a>, and solid state devices. Pulse-Doppler is incompatible with other high power microwave amplification devices that are not <a href="/wiki/Coherence_(physics)" title="Coherence (physics)">coherent</a>.</p>
<p>Early examples of military systems include the <a href="/wiki/AN/SPG-51" title="AN/SPG-51">AN/SPG-51</a>B developed during the 1950s specifically for the purpose of operating in hurricane conditions with no performance degradation.</p>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hughes_AN/ASG-18_Fire_Control_System" title="Hughes AN/ASG-18 Fire Control System">Hughes AN/ASG-18 Fire Control System</a> was a prototype airborne radar/combination system for the planned <a href="/wiki/North_American_XF-108_Rapier" title="North American XF-108 Rapier">North American XF-108 Rapier</a> interceptor aircraft for the United States Air Force, and later for the <a href="/wiki/Lockheed_YF-12" title="Lockheed YF-12">Lockheed YF-12</a>. The US's first pulse-Doppler radar,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> the system had <a href="/wiki/Look-down/shoot-down" title="Look-down/shoot-down">look-down/shoot-down</a> capability and could track one target at a time.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Weather" title="Weather">Weather</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chaff_(countermeasure)" title="Chaff (countermeasure)">chaff</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Terrain_mask" title="Terrain mask">terrain</a>, <a href="/wiki/Blue_Angels" title="Blue Angels">flying techniques</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Stealth_aircraft" title="Stealth aircraft">stealth</a> are common tactics used to hide aircraft from radar. Pulse-Doppler radar eliminates these weaknesses.</p>
<p>It became possible to use pulse-Doppler radar on aircraft after digital computers were incorporated in the design. Pulse-Doppler provided <a href="/wiki/Look-down/shoot-down" title="Look-down/shoot-down">look-down/shoot-down</a> capability to support air-to-air missile systems in most modern military aircraft by the mid 1970s.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Range_measurement">Range measurement</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pulse-Doppler_radar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Range measurement">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:306px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Radarops.gif"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="157" data-file-width="304" height="157" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/0/07/Radarops.gif" width="304"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Radarops.gif" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Principle of pulsed radar</div>
</div>

<p>Pulse-Doppler systems measure the range to objects by measuring the elapsed time between sending a pulse of radio energy and receiving a reflection of the object. Radio waves travel at the <a href="/wiki/Speed_of_light" title="Speed of light">speed of light</a>, so the distance to the object is the elapsed time multiplied by the speed of light, divided by two - there and back.</p>

This causes the reflector to produce Doppler modulation on the reflected signal.</p>
<p>Pulse-Doppler radars exploit this phenomenon to improve performance.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Benefits">Benefits</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pulse-Doppler_radar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Benefits">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Rejection speed is selectable on pulse-Doppler aircraft-detection systems so nothing below that speed will be detected. A one degree antenna beam illuminates millions of square feet of terrain at 10 miles (16 km) range, and this produces thousands of detections at or below the horizon if Doppler is not used.</p>
<p>Pulse-Doppler radar uses the following signal processing criteria to exclude unwanted signals from slow-moving objects. This is also known as clutter rejection.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Rejection velocity is usually set just above the prevailing wind speed (10 to 100 mile/hour or 15 to 150 km/hour). The velocity threshold is much lower for <a href="/wiki/Weather_radar" title="Weather radar">weather radar</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>

<p>Surface reflections appear in almost all radar. <b>Ground clutter</b> generally appears in a circular region within a radius of about 25 miles near ground-based radar. This distance extends much further in airborne and space radar. Clutter results from radio energy being reflected from the earth's surface, buildings, and vegetation. Clutter includes weather in radar intended to detect and report aircraft and spacecraft.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>Clutter creates a vulnerability region in <a href="/wiki/Radar_signal_characteristics#The_radar_signal_in_the_time_domain" title="Radar signal characteristics">pulse-amplitude time-domain radar</a>. Non-Doppler radar systems cannot be pointed directly at the ground due to excessive false alarms, which overwhelm computers and operators. Sensitivity must be reduced near clutter to avoid overload. This vulnerability begins in the low-elevation region several beam widths above the horizon, and extends downward. This also exists throughout the volume of moving air associated with weather phenomenon.</p>
<p>Pulse-Doppler radar corrects this as follows.</p>
<ul>
<li>Allows the radar antenna to be pointed directly at the ground without overwhelming the computer and without reducing sensitivity.</li>
<li>Fills in the vulnerability region associated with <a href="/wiki/Radar_signal_characteristics#The_radar_signal_in_the_time_domain" title="Radar signal characteristics">pulse-amplitude time-domain radar</a> for small object detection near terrain and weather.</li>
<li>Increases detection range by 300% or more in comparison to <a href="/wiki/Moving_target_indication" title="Moving target indication">moving target indication</a> (MTI) by improving sub-clutter visibility.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-scv_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-scv-7">[7]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<p><a href="/wiki/Clutter_(radar)" title="Clutter (radar)">Clutter rejection capability</a> of about 60 dB is needed for <a href="/wiki/Look-down/shoot-down" title="Look-down/shoot-down">look-down/shoot-down</a> capability, and pulse-Doppler is the only strategy that can satisfy this requirement. This eliminates vulnerabilities associated with the low-elevation and below-horizon environment.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Pulse_compression" title="Pulse compression">Pulse compression</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Moving_target_indicator" title="Moving target indicator">moving target indicator</a> (MTI) provide up to 25 dB sub-clutter visibility. MTI antenna beam is aimed above horizon to avoid excessive false alarm rate, which renders systems vulnerable. Aircraft and some missiles exploit this weakness using a technique called <b>flying below the radar</b> to avoid detection (<a href="/wiki/Nap-of-the-earth" title="Nap-of-the-earth">Nap-of-the-earth</a>). This flying technique is ineffective against pulse-Doppler radar.</p>
<p>Pulse-Doppler provides an advantage when attempting to detect missiles and <a href="/wiki/Stealth_aircraft" title="Stealth aircraft">low observability aircraft</a> flying near terrain, sea surface, and weather.</p>
<p>Audible Doppler and target size support passive vehicle type classification when <a href="/wiki/Identification_friend_or_foe" title="Identification friend or foe">identification friend or foe</a> is not available from a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Transponder_(aviation)" title="Transponder (aviation)">transponder signal</a>. Medium <a href="/wiki/Pulse_repetition_frequency" title="Pulse repetition frequency">pulse repetition frequency</a> (PRF) reflected microwave signals fall between 1,500 and 15,000 cycle per second, which is audible. This means a <a href="/wiki/Helicopter" title="Helicopter">helicopter</a> sounds like a helicopter, a <a href="/wiki/Jet_aircraft" title="Jet aircraft">jet</a> sounds like a jet, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Propeller_aircraft" title="Propeller aircraft">propeller aircraft</a> sound like propellers. <a href="/wiki/Glider_(aircraft)" title="Glider (aircraft)">Aircraft with no moving parts</a> produce a tone. The actual size of the target can be calculated using the audible signal.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>

<p>Ambiguity processing is required when target range is above the red line in the graphic, which increases scan time.</p>
<p>Scan time is a critical factor for some systems because vehicles moving at or above the speed of sound can travel one mile (1.6 km) every few seconds, like the <a href="/wiki/Exocet" title="Exocet">Exocet</a>, <a href="/wiki/Harpoon_(missile)" title="Harpoon (missile)">Harpoon</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kh-22" title="Kh-22">Kitchen</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Air-to-air_missile" title="Air-to-air missile">Air-to-air missile</a>. The maximum time to scan the entire volume of the sky must be on the order of a dozen seconds or less for systems operating in that environment.</p>
<p>Pulse-Doppler radar by itself can be too slow to cover the entire volume of space above the horizon unless fan beam is used. This approach is used with the AN/SPS 49(V)5 Very Long Range Air Surveillance Radar, which sacrifices elevation measurement to gain speed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>Pulse-Doppler antenna motion must be slow enough so that all the return signals from at least 3 different PRF can be processed out to the maximum anticipated detection range. This is known as <a href="/wiki/Dwell_time_(radar)" title="Dwell time (radar)">dwell time</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Antenna motion for pulse-Doppler must be as slow as radar using <a href="/wiki/Moving_target_indication" title="Moving target indication">MTI</a>.</p>
<p>Search radar that include pulse-Doppler are usually dual mode because best overall performance is achieved when pulse-Doppler is used for areas with high false alarm rates (horizon or below and weather), while conventional radar will scan faster in free-space where false alarm rate is low (above horizon with clear skies).</p>
<p>The antenna type is an important consideration for multi-mode radar because undesirable phase shift introduced by the radar antenna can degrade <a href="/wiki/Performance_measurement" title="Performance measurement">performance measurements</a> for sub-clutter visibility.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Signal_processing">Signal processing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pulse-Doppler_radar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Signal processing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The signal processing enhancement of pulse-Doppler allows small high-speed objects to be detected in close proximity to large slow moving reflectors. To achieve this, the transmitter must be coherent and should produce low <a href="/wiki/Phase_noise" title="Phase noise">phase noise</a> during the detection interval, and the receiver must have large instantaneous <a href="/wiki/Dynamic_range" title="Dynamic range">dynamic range</a>.</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Pulse-Doppler_signal_processing" title="Pulse-Doppler signal processing">Pulse-Doppler signal processing detailed explanation</a></li>
</ul>
<p>Pulse-Doppler signal processing also includes ambiguity resolution to identify true range and velocity.</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Ambiguity_resolution" title="Ambiguity resolution">Ambiguity resolution detailed explanation</a></li>
</ul>
<p>The received signals from multiple PRF are compared to determine true range using the range ambiguity resolution process.</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Range_ambiguity_resolution" title="Range ambiguity resolution">Range ambiguity resolution detailed explanation</a></li>
</ul>
<p>The received signals are also compared using the frequency ambiguity resolution process.</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Frequency_ambiguity_resolution" title="Frequency ambiguity resolution">Frequency ambiguity resolution detailed explanation</a></li>
</ul>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Special_consideration">Special consideration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pulse-Doppler_radar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Special consideration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Pulse-Doppler radar has special requirements that must be satisfied to achieve acceptable performance.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Pulse_repetition_frequency">Pulse repetition frequency</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pulse-Doppler_radar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Pulse repetition frequency">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Pulse_repetition_frequency" title="Pulse repetition frequency">Pulse repetition frequency</a></div>
<p>Pulse-Doppler typically uses <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Medium_pulse_repetition_frequency" title="Medium pulse repetition frequency">medium pulse repetition frequency</a> (PRF) from about 3 kHz to 30 kHz. The range between transmit pulses is 5 km to 50 km.</p>
<p>Range and velocity cannot be measured directly using medium PRF, and ambiguity resolution is required to identify true range and speed. Doppler signals are generally above 1 kHz, which is audible, so audio signals from medium-PRF systems can be used for passive target classification.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Angular_measurement">Angular measurement</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pulse-Doppler_radar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Angular measurement">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Radar systems require angular measurement. Transponders are not normally associated with pulse-Doppler radar, so sidelobe suppression is required for practical operation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>Tracking radar systems use angle error to improve accuracy by producing measurements perpendicular to the radar antenna beam. Angular measurements are averaged over a span of time and combined with radial movement to develop information suitable to predict target position for a short time into the future.</p>
<p>The two angle error techniques used with tracking radar are monopulse and conical scan.</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Monopulse_radar" title="Monopulse radar">Monopulse radar</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Conical_scanning" title="Conical scanning">Conical scanning</a></li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Coherency">Coherency</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pulse-Doppler_radar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Coherency">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Pulse-Doppler radar requires a <a href="/wiki/Coherence_(physics)#Spectral_coherence" title="Coherence (physics)">coherent oscillator</a> with very little noise. <a href="/wiki/Phase_noise" title="Phase noise">Phase noise</a> reduces sub-clutter visibility performance by producing apparent motion on stationary objects.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Cavity_magnetron" title="Cavity magnetron">Cavity magnetron</a> and <a href="/wiki/Crossed-field_amplifier" title="Crossed-field amplifier">crossed-field amplifier</a> are not appropriate because noise introduced by these devices interfere with detection performance. The only amplification devices suitable for pulse-Doppler are <a href="/wiki/Klystron" title="Klystron">klystron</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Traveling_wave_tube" title="Traveling wave tube">traveling wave tube</a>, and solid state devices.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Scalloping">Scalloping</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pulse-Doppler_radar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Scalloping">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Radar_scalloping" title="Radar scalloping">Radar scalloping</a></div>
<p>Pulse-Doppler signal processing introduces a phenomenon called scalloping. The name is associated with a series of holes that are scooped-out of the detection performance.</p>
<p>Scalloping for pulse-Doppler radar involves blind velocities created by the clutter rejection filter. Every volume of space must be scanned using 3 or more different PRF. A two PRF detection scheme will have <b>detection gaps</b> with a pattern of discrete ranges, each of which has a blind velocity.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Windowing">Windowing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pulse-Doppler_radar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Windowing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Ringing_artifacts" title="Ringing artifacts">Ringing artifacts</a> pose a problem with search, detection, and ambiguity resolution in pulse-Doppler radar.</p>
<p>Ringing is reduced in two ways.</p>
<p>First, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Windowing_functions#Tukey_window" title="Windowing functions">shape of the transmit pulse</a> is adjusted to smooth the leading edge and trailing edge so that RF power is increased and decreased without an abrupt change. This creates a transmit pulse with smooth ends instead of a square wave, which reduces ringing phenomenon that is otherwise associated with target reflection.</p>
<p>Second, the shape of the receive pulse is adjusted using a <a href="/wiki/Window_function" title="Window function">window function</a> that minimizes ringing that occurs any time pulses are applied to a filter. In a digital system, this adjusts the phase and/or amplitude of each sample before it is applied to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fast_Fourier_Transform" title="Fast Fourier Transform">Fast Fourier Transform</a>. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Windowing_functions#Dolph.E2.80.93Chebyshev_window" title="Windowing functions">Dolph-Chebyshev window</a> is the most effective because it produces a flat processing floor with no ringing that would otherwise cause false alarms.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dolph_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-dolph-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Antenna">Antenna</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pulse-Doppler_radar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Antenna">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Pulse-Doppler radar is generally limited to mechanically aimed antennas and active phase array.</p>
<p>Mechanical RF components, such as wave-guide, can produce Doppler modulation due to phase shift induced by vibration. This introduces a requirement to perform full spectrum operational tests using shake tables that can produce high power mechanical vibration across all anticipated audio frequencies.</p>
<p>Doppler is incompatible with most electronically steered phase-array antenna. This is because the phase-shifter elements in the antenna are non-reciprocal and the phase shift must be adjusted before and after each transmit pulse. Spurious phase shift is produced by the sudden impulse of the phase shift, and settling during the receive period between transmit pulses places Doppler modulation onto stationary clutter. That receive modulation corrupts the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Measure_of_Performance" title="Measure of Performance">measure of performance</a> for sub-clutter visibility. Phase shifter settling time on the order of 50ns is required. Start of receiver sampling needs to be postponed at least 1 phase-shifter settling time-constant (or more) for each 20 dB of sub-clutter visibility.</p>
<p>Most antenna phase shifters operating at PRF above 1 kHz introduce spurious phase shift unless special provisions are made, such as reducing phase shifter settling time to a few dozen nanoseconds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-phase-shifters_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-phase-shifters-13">[13]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Diffraction">Diffraction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pulse-Doppler_radar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Diffraction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Choppy surfaces, like waves and trees, form a diffraction grating suitable for bending microwave signals. Pulse-Doppler can be so sensitive that <a href="/wiki/Diffraction" title="Diffraction">diffraction</a> from mountains, buildings or wave tops can be used to detect fast moving objects otherwise blocked by solid obstruction along the line of sight. This is a very lossy phenomenon that only becomes possible when radar has significant excess sub-clutter visibility.</p>
<p>Refraction and ducting use transmit frequency at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/L-band" title="L-band">L-band</a> or lower to extend the horizon, which is very different from diffraction. <a href="/wiki/Refraction" title="Refraction">Refraction</a> for <a href="/wiki/Over-the-horizon_radar" title="Over-the-horizon radar">over-the-horizon radar</a> uses variable density in the air column above the surface of the earth to bend RF signals. An inversion layer can produce a transient <a class="external text" href="http://www.amateur-radio-wiki.net/index.php?title=Tropospheric_ducting" rel="nofollow">troposphere duct</a> that traps RF signals in a thin layer of air like a wave-guide.</p>

<li><i>F</i> = <a href="/wiki/Antenna_gain" title="Antenna gain">Antenna pattern propagation factor</a></li>
<li><i>D</i> = Doppler filter size (transmit pulses in each <a href="/wiki/Fast_Fourier_transform" title="Fast Fourier transform">Fast Fourier transform</a>)</li>
<li><i>K</i><sub>b</sub> = <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Boltzmann%27s_constant" title="Boltzmann's constant">Boltzmann's constant</a></li>
<li><i>T</i> = Temperature (kelvin)</li>
<li><i>B</i> = <a href="/wiki/Bandwidth_(signal_processing)" title="Bandwidth (signal processing)">Receiver Bandwidth (band pass filter)</a></li>
<li><i>N</i> = <a href="/wiki/Noise_figure" title="Noise figure">Noise figure</a></li>
</ul>
<p>This equation is derived by combining the <a href="/wiki/Radar#Radar_equation" title="Radar">Radar equation</a> with the <a href="/wiki/Noise_(electronics)" title="Noise (electronics)">Noise equation</a> and accounting for in-band noise distribution across multiple detection filters. The value <i>D</i> is added to the standard radar range equation to account for both <a href="/wiki/Pulse-Doppler_signal_processing" title="Pulse-Doppler signal processing">pulse-Doppler signal processing</a> and <a href="/wiki/Coherence_(physics)" title="Coherence (physics)">transmitter FM noise reduction</a>.</p>
<p>Detection range is increased proportional to the fourth root of the number of filters for a given power consumption. Alternatively, power consumption is reduced by the number of filers for a given detection range.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Pulse-Doppler_signal_processing" title="Pulse-Doppler signal processing">Pulse-Doppler signal processing</a> integrates all of the energy from all of the individual reflected pulses that enter the filter. This means a <a href="/wiki/Pulse-Doppler_signal_processing" title="Pulse-Doppler signal processing">pulse-Doppler signal processing</a> system with 1,024 elements provides 30.103 dB of improvement due to the type of signal processing that must be used with pulse-Doppler radar. The energy of all of the individual pulses from the object are added together by the filtering process.</p>
<p>Signal processing for a 1,024 point filter improves performance by 30.103 dB, assuming compatible transmitter and antenna. This corresponds to the following potential improvements.</p>
<ul>
<li>562% increase in maximum distance.</li>
</ul>
<p>These improvements are the reason pulse-Doppler is essential for military and astronomy.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Aircraft_tracking_uses">Aircraft tracking uses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pulse-Doppler_radar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Aircraft tracking uses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Pulse-Doppler radar for aircraft detection has two modes.</p>
<ul>
<li>Scan</li>
<li>Track</li>
</ul>
<p>Scan mode involves frequency filtering, amplitude thresholding, and ambiguity resolution. Once a reflection has been <a href="/wiki/Constant_false_alarm_rate" title="Constant false alarm rate">detected</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ambiguity_resolution" title="Ambiguity resolution">resolved</a>, the pulse-Doppler radar automatically transitions to tracking mode for the volume of space surrounding the track.</p>
<p>Track mode works like a <a href="/wiki/Phase-locked_loop" title="Phase-locked loop">phase-locked loop</a>, where Doppler velocity is compared with the range movement on successive scans. <b>Lock</b> indicates the difference between the two measurements is below a threshold, which can only occur with an object that satisfies <a href="/wiki/Classical_mechanics" title="Classical mechanics">Newtonian mechanics</a>. Other types of electronic signals cannot produce a lock. Lock exists in no other type of radar.</p>
<p>The <b>lock criteria</b> needs to be satisfied during normal operation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Pulse-Doppler_signal_processing#Lock" title="Pulse-Doppler signal processing">Pulse-Doppler signal processing § Lock</a></li>
</ul>
<p>Lock eliminates the need for human intervention with the exception of helicopters and <a href="/wiki/Radar_jamming_and_deception#Electronic_jamming" title="Radar jamming and deception">electronic jamming</a>.</p>
<p>Weather phenomenon obey <a href="/wiki/Adiabatic_process" title="Adiabatic process">adiabatic process</a> associated with <a href="/wiki/Air_mass" title="Air mass">air mass</a> and not <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Newtonian_mechanics" title="Newtonian mechanics">Newtonian mechanics</a>, so the lock criteria is not normally used for weather radar.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Pulse-Doppler_signal_processing" title="Pulse-Doppler signal processing">Pulse-Doppler signal processing</a> selectively excludes low-velocity reflections so that no detections occurs below a threshold velocity. This eliminates terrain, weather, biologicals, and <a href="/wiki/Radar_jamming_and_deception#Mechanical_jamming" title="Radar jamming and deception">mechanical jamming</a> with the exception of decoy aircraft.</p>
<p>The target Doppler signal from the detection is converted from <a href="/wiki/Frequency_domain" title="Frequency domain">frequency domain</a> back into <a href="/wiki/Time_domain" title="Time domain">time domain</a> sound for the operator in track mode on some radar systems. The operator uses this sound for passive target classification, such as recognizing helicopters and electronic jamming.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Helicopters">Helicopters</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pulse-Doppler_radar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Helicopters">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Special consideration is required for aircraft with large moving parts because pulse-Doppler radar operates like a <a href="/wiki/Phase-locked_loop" title="Phase-locked loop">phase-locked loop</a>. Blade tips moving near the speed of sound produce the only signal that can be detected when a <a href="/wiki/Helicopter" title="Helicopter">helicopter</a> is moving slow near terrain and weather.</p>
<p>Helicopters appears like a rapidly pulsing noise emitter except in a clear environment free from clutter. An audible signal is produced for passive identification of the type of airborne object. Microwave Doppler frequency shift produced by reflector motion falls into the audible sound range for human beings (<span class="nowrap">20 – 20,000 Hz</span>), which is used for target classification in addition to the kinds of conventional <a href="/wiki/Radar_display" title="Radar display">radar display</a> used for that purpose, like A-scope, B-scope, C-scope, and RHI indicator. The human ear may be able to tell the difference better than electronic equipment.</p>
<p>A special mode is required because the Doppler velocity feedback information must be unlinked from radial movement so that the system can transition from scan to track with no lock.</p>
<p>Similar techniques are required to develop track information for jamming signals and interference that cannot satisfy the lock criteria.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Multi-mode">Multi-mode</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pulse-Doppler_radar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Multi-mode">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Pulse-Doppler radar must be multi-mode to handle aircraft turning and crossing trajectory.</p>
<p>Once in track mode, pulse-Doppler radar must include a way to modify Doppler filtering for the volume of space surrounding a track when radial velocity falls below the minimum detection velocity. Doppler filter adjustment must be linked with a <a href="/wiki/Pulse-Doppler_signal_processing#Track" title="Pulse-Doppler signal processing">radar track function</a> to automatically adjust Doppler rejection speed within the volume of space surrounding the track.</p>
<p>Tracking will cease without this feature because the target signal will otherwise be rejected by the Doppler filter when radial velocity approaches zero.</p>
<p>Multi-mode operation may also include continuous wave illumination for <a href="/wiki/Semi-active_radar_homing" title="Semi-active radar homing">semi-active radar homing</a>.</p>
