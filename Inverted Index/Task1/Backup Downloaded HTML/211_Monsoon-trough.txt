<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Monsoon trough</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">
					Jump to:					<a href="#mw-head">navigation</a>, 					<a href="#p-search">search</a>
</div>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Auguststreamlinesnavy.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="473" data-file-width="900" height="105" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5c/Auguststreamlinesnavy.jpg/200px-Auguststreamlinesnavy.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5c/Auguststreamlinesnavy.jpg/300px-Auguststreamlinesnavy.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5c/Auguststreamlinesnavy.jpg/400px-Auguststreamlinesnavy.jpg 2x" width="200"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Auguststreamlinesnavy.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
August position of the ITCZ and monsoon trough in the Pacific Ocean, depicted by area of convergent <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Isobar_(meteorology)" title="Isobar (meteorology)">streamlines</a> in the northern Pacific</div>
</div>

<p>The <b>monsoon trough</b> is a portion of the <a href="/wiki/Intertropical_Convergence_Zone" title="Intertropical Convergence Zone">Intertropical Convergence Zone</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ams_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-ams-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wang_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Wang-2">[2]</a></sup> as depicted by a line on a <a href="/wiki/Weather_map" title="Weather map">weather map</a> showing the locations of minimum sea level pressure,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ams_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-ams-1">[1]</a></sup> and as such, is a <a href="/wiki/Convergence_zone" title="Convergence zone">convergence zone</a> between the wind patterns of the southern and northern hemispheres.</p>
<p>Westerly <a href="/wiki/Monsoon" title="Monsoon">monsoon</a> winds lie in its equatorward portion while easterly <a href="/wiki/Trade_winds" title="Trade winds">trade winds</a> exist poleward of the trough.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Right along its axis, heavy rains can be found which usher in the peak of a location's respective <a href="/wiki/Wet_season" title="Wet season">rainy season</a>. As it passes poleward of a location, hot and dry conditions develop. The monsoon trough plays a role in creating many of the world's <a href="/wiki/Rainforest" title="Rainforest">rainforests</a>.</p>
<p>The term "monsoon trough" is most commonly used in monsoonal regions of the Western Pacific such as Asia and Australia. The migration of the ITCZ/monsoon trough into a landmass heralds the beginning of the annual rainy season during summer months. Depressions and <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone" title="Tropical cyclone">tropical cyclones</a> often form in the vicinity of the monsoon trough, with each capable of producing a year's worth of rainfall in a matter of days.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Movement_and_strength">Movement and strength</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Monsoon_trough&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Movement and strength">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Februarystreamlinesnavy.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="473" data-file-width="900" height="105" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/54/Februarystreamlinesnavy.jpg/200px-Februarystreamlinesnavy.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/54/Februarystreamlinesnavy.jpg/300px-Februarystreamlinesnavy.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/54/Februarystreamlinesnavy.jpg/400px-Februarystreamlinesnavy.jpg 2x" width="200"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Februarystreamlinesnavy.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
February position of the ITCZ and monsoon trough in the Pacific Ocean, depicted by area of convergent streamlines offshore Australia and in the equatorial eastern Pacific</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Monsoon troughing in the western Pacific reaches its zenith in <a href="/wiki/Latitude" title="Latitude">latitude</a> during the late summer when the wintertime surface ridge in the opposite hemisphere is the strongest. It can reach as far as the 40th parallel in East Asia during August and the 20th parallel in Australia during February. Its poleward progression is accelerated by the onset of the summer monsoon which is characterized by the development of lower air pressure over the warmest part of the various continents.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NCFMRF_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-NCFMRF-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> In the <a href="/wiki/Southern_Hemisphere" title="Southern Hemisphere">Southern Hemisphere</a>, the monsoon trough associated with the Australian monsoon reaches its most southerly latitude in February,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NAVY2_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-NAVY2-7">[7]</a></sup> oriented along a west-northwest/east-southeast axis.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Effect_of_wind_surges">Effect of wind surges</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Monsoon_trough&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Effect of wind surges">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Increases in the relative <a href="/wiki/Vorticity" title="Vorticity">vorticity</a>, or spin, with the monsoon trough are normally a product of increased wind convergence within the <a href="/wiki/Convergence_zone" title="Convergence zone">convergence zone</a> of the monsoon trough. Wind surges can lead to this increase in convergence. A strengthening or equatorward movement in the <a href="/wiki/Subtropical_ridge" title="Subtropical ridge">subtropical ridge</a> can cause a strengthening of a monsoon trough as a wind surge moves towards the location of the monsoon trough. As <a href="/wiki/Surface_weather_analysis" title="Surface weather analysis">fronts</a> move through the <a href="/wiki/Subtropics" title="Subtropics">subtropics</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tropics" title="Tropics">tropics</a> of one <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hemisphere_of_the_Earth" title="Hemisphere of the Earth">hemisphere</a> during their winter, normally as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shear_line_(meteorology)" title="Shear line (meteorology)">shear lines</a> when their temperature gradient becomes minimal, wind surges can cross the equator in oceanic regions and enhance a monsoon trough in the other hemisphere's summer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> A key way of detecting whether a wind surge has reached a monsoon trough is the formation of a burst of thunderstorms within the monsoon trough.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NAVY3_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-NAVY3-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Embedded_depressions">Embedded depressions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Monsoon_trough&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Embedded depressions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:192px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:MonsoonDepression2004091600.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="276" data-file-width="279" height="188" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3b/MonsoonDepression2004091600.jpg/190px-MonsoonDepression2004091600.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/3/3b/MonsoonDepression2004091600.jpg 1.5x" width="190"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:MonsoonDepression2004091600.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Monsoon Depression near Bangladesh</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>If a circulation forms within the monsoon trough, it is able to compete with the neighboring <a href="/wiki/Thermal_low" title="Thermal low">thermal low</a> over the continent, and a wind surge will occur at its periphery. Such a circulation which is broad in nature within a monsoon trough is known as a monsoon depression. In the Northern Hemisphere, monsoon depressions are generally asymmetric, and tend to have their strongest winds on their eastern periphery.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NAVY3_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-NAVY3-9">[9]</a></sup> Light and variable winds cover a large area near their center, while bands of showers and thunderstorms develop within their area of circulation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>The presence of an upper level <a href="/wiki/Jet_stream" title="Jet stream">jet stream</a> poleward and west of the system can enhance its development by leading to increased diverging air aloft over the monsoon depression, which leads to a corresponding drop in surface pressure.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> Even though these systems can develop over land, the outer portions of monsoon depressions are similar to tropical cyclones.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> In India, for example, 6 to 7 monsoon depressions move across the country yearly,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NCFMRF_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-NCFMRF-4">[4]</a></sup> and their numbers within the <a href="/wiki/Bay_of_Bengal" title="Bay of Bengal">Bay of Bengal</a> increase during July and August of <a href="/wiki/El_Ni%C3%B1o" title="El Niño">El Niño</a> events.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> Monsoon depressions are efficient rainfall producers, and can cause a year's worth of rainfall when they move through drier areas such as the <a href="/wiki/Outback" title="Outback">outback</a> of Australia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Roles">Roles</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Monsoon_trough&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Roles">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="In_rainy_season">In rainy season</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Monsoon_trough&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: In rainy season">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Central_Calcutta_after_Rain.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="301" data-file-width="450" height="134" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e6/Central_Calcutta_after_Rain.jpg/200px-Central_Calcutta_after_Rain.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e6/Central_Calcutta_after_Rain.jpg/300px-Central_Calcutta_after_Rain.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e6/Central_Calcutta_after_Rain.jpg/400px-Central_Calcutta_after_Rain.jpg 2x" width="200"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Central_Calcutta_after_Rain.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
View of central <a href="/wiki/Kolkata" title="Kolkata">Kolkata</a> after a monsoon rain.</div>
</div>
</div>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Wet_season" title="Wet season">Wet season</a> and <a href="/wiki/Monsoon" title="Monsoon">Monsoon</a></div>
<p>Since the monsoon trough is an area of convergence in the wind pattern, and an elongated area of low pressure at the surface, the trough focuses low level moisture and is defined by one or more elongated <a href="/wiki/Rainband" title="Rainband">bands</a> of <a href="/wiki/Thunderstorm" title="Thunderstorm">thunderstorms</a> when viewing <a href="/wiki/Satellite" title="Satellite">satellite</a> imagery. Its abrupt movement to the north between May and June is coincident with the beginning of the monsoon regime and rainy seasons across South and East Asia. This convergence zone has been linked to prolonged heavy rain events in the <a href="/wiki/Yangtze" title="Yangtze">Yangtze</a> river as well as northern China.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wang_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Wang-2">[2]</a></sup> Its presence has also been linked to the peak of the rainy season in locations within Australia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> As it progresses poleward of a particular location, clear, hot, and dry conditions develop as winds become westerly.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> Many of the world's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rainforests" title="Rainforests">rainforests</a> are associated with these climatological low pressure systems.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="In_tropical_cyclogenesis">In tropical cyclogenesis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Monsoon_trough&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: In tropical cyclogenesis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:WorldwideTCpeaks.gif"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="221" data-file-width="354" height="125" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/22/WorldwideTCpeaks.gif/200px-WorldwideTCpeaks.gif" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/22/WorldwideTCpeaks.gif/300px-WorldwideTCpeaks.gif 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/2/22/WorldwideTCpeaks.gif 2x" width="200"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:WorldwideTCpeaks.gif" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Months of peak tropical cyclone activity worldwide</div>
</div>
</div>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclogenesis" title="Tropical cyclogenesis">Tropical cyclogenesis</a></div>
<p>A monsoon trough is a significant genesis region for <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone" title="Tropical cyclone">tropical cyclones</a>. <a href="/wiki/Vorticity" title="Vorticity">Vorticity</a>-rich low level environments, with significant low level spin, lead to a better than average chance of tropical cyclone formation due to their inherent rotation. This is because a pre-existing near-surface disturbance with sufficient spin and convergence is one of the six requirements for <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclogenesis" title="Tropical cyclogenesis">tropical cyclogenesis</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> There appears to be a 15- to 25-day cycle in thunderstorm activity associated with the monsoon trough, which is roughly half the wavelength of the <a href="/wiki/Madden%E2%80%93Julian_oscillation" title="Madden–Julian oscillation">Madden–Julian oscillation</a>, or MJO.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> This mirrors tropical cyclone genesis near these features, as genesis clusters in 2–3 weeks of activity followed by 2–3 weeks of inactivity. Tropical cyclones can form in outbreaks around these features under special circumstances, tending to follow the next cyclone to its poleward and west.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>Whenever the monsoon trough on the eastern side of the summertime Asian monsoon is in its normal orientation (oriented east-southeast to west-northwest), <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone" title="Tropical cyclone">tropical cyclones</a> along its periphery will move with a westward motion. If it is reverse oriented, or oriented southwest to northeast, tropical cyclones will move more poleward. Tropical cyclone tracks with S shapes tend to be associated with reverse-oriented monsoon troughs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/South_Pacific_convergence_zone" title="South Pacific convergence zone">South Pacific convergence zone</a> and South American convergence zones are generally reverse oriented.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NAVY2_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-NAVY2-7">[7]</a></sup> The failure of the monsoon trough, or ITCZ, to move south of the equator in the eastern Pacific Ocean and Atlantic Ocean during the southern hemisphere summer is considered one of the reasons that tropical cyclones normally do not form in those regions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NAVY3_9-2"><a href="#cite_note-NAVY3-9">[9]</a></sup> It has also been noted that when the monsoon trough lies near <a href="/wiki/20th_parallel_north" title="20th parallel north">20 degrees north latitude</a> in the Pacific, the frequency of tropical cyclones is 2 to 3 times greater than when it lies closer to <a href="/wiki/10th_parallel_north" title="10th parallel north">10 degrees north</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wang_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-Wang-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
