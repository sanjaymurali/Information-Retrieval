<p>The <b>Federal Government of the United States</b> is the <a href="/wiki/Central_government" title="Central government">national government</a> of the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Republic" title="Republic">republic</a> in <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North America</a>, composed of <a href="/wiki/U.S._state" title="U.S. state">50 states</a>, one district, <a href="/wiki/Washington,_D.C." title="Washington, D.C.">Washington, D.C.</a> (the nation's capital), and <a href="/wiki/Territories_of_the_United_States" title="Territories of the United States">several territories</a>. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Federal_government" title="Federal government">federal government</a> is composed of three distinct branches: <a href="/wiki/United_States_Congress" title="United States Congress">legislative</a>, <a href="#Executive_branch">executive</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Federal_judiciary_of_the_United_States" title="Federal judiciary of the United States">judicial</a>, whose powers are vested by the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Constitution" title="United States Constitution">U.S. Constitution</a> in the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Congress" title="United States Congress">Congress</a>, the <a href="/wiki/President_of_the_United_States" title="President of the United States">President</a>, and the federal courts, respectively. The powers and duties of these branches are further defined by acts of Congress, including the creation of executive departments and courts inferior to the <a href="/wiki/Supreme_Court_of_the_United_States" title="Supreme Court of the United States">Supreme Court</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Naming">Naming</span></h2>
<p>The full name of the republic is "United States of America". No other name appears in the Constitution, and this is the name that appears on money, in treaties, and in legal cases to which it is a party (e.g. <i><a href="/wiki/Schenck_v._United_States" title="Schenck v. United States">Charles T. Schenck v. United States</a></i>). The terms "Government of the United States of America" or "United States Government" are often used in official documents to represent the federal government as distinct from the states collectively. In casual conversation or writing, the term "Federal Government" is often used, and the term "National Government" is sometimes used. The terms "Federal" and "National" in government agency or program names generally indicate affiliation with the federal government (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Federal_Bureau_of_Investigation" title="Federal Bureau of Investigation">Federal Bureau of Investigation</a>, <a href="/wiki/National_Oceanic_and_Atmospheric_Administration" title="National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration">National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration</a>, etc.). Because the <a href="/wiki/Seat_of_government" title="Seat of government">seat of government</a> is in Washington, D.C., "Washington" is commonly used as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Metonym" title="Metonym">metonym</a> for the federal government.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span></h2>
<p>The outline of the government of the United States is laid out in the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Constitution" title="United States Constitution">Constitution</a>. The government was formed in 1789, making the United States one of the world's first, if not the first, modern national <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Constitutional_republic" title="Constitutional republic">constitutional republics</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>The United States government is based on the principles of <a href="/wiki/Federalism_in_the_United_States" title="Federalism in the United States">federalism</a> and <a href="/wiki/Republicanism_in_the_United_States" title="Republicanism in the United States">republicanism</a>, in which power is shared between the federal government and <a href="/wiki/State_governments_of_the_United_States" title="State governments of the United States">state governments</a>. The interpretation and execution of these principles, including what powers the federal government should have and how those powers can be exercised, have been debated ever since the adoption of the Constitution. Some make the case for expansive federal powers while others argue for a more limited role for the central government in relation to individuals, the states or other recognized entities.</p>
<p>Since the <a href="/wiki/American_Civil_War" title="American Civil War">American Civil War</a>, the powers of the federal government have generally expanded greatly, although there have been periods since that time of legislative branch dominance (e.g., the decades immediately following the Civil War) or when <a href="/wiki/States%27_rights" title="States' rights">states' rights</a> proponents have succeeded in limiting federal power through legislative action, executive prerogative or by constitutional interpretation by the courts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>One of the theoretical pillars of the United States Constitution is the idea of "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Checks_and_balances" title="Checks and balances">checks and balances</a>" among the powers and responsibilities of the three branches of American government: the executive, the legislative and the judiciary. For example, while the legislative (Congress) has the power to create law, the executive (President) can veto any legislation—an act which, in turn, can be overridden by Congress.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> The President nominates judges to the nation's highest judiciary authority (Supreme Court), but those nominees must be approved by Congress. The Supreme Court, in its turn, has the power to invalidate as "unconstitutional" any law passed by the Congress. These and other examples are examined in more detail in the text below.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Legislative_branch">Legislative branch</span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/United_States_Congress" title="United States Congress">United States Congress</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:182px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Seal_of_the_United_States_Congress.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1052" data-file-width="1055" height="179" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4b/Seal_of_the_United_States_Congress.svg/180px-Seal_of_the_United_States_Congress.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4b/Seal_of_the_United_States_Congress.svg/270px-Seal_of_the_United_States_Congress.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4b/Seal_of_the_United_States_Congress.svg/360px-Seal_of_the_United_States_Congress.svg.png 2x" width="180"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Seal_of_the_United_States_Congress.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Seal of the U.S. Congress</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/United_States_Congress" title="United States Congress">United States Congress</a> is the legislative branch of the federal government. It is <a href="/wiki/Bicameralism" title="Bicameralism">bicameral</a>, comprising the <a href="/wiki/United_States_House_of_Representatives" title="United States House of Representatives">House of Representatives</a> and the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Senate" title="United States Senate">Senate</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Makeup_of_Congress">Makeup of Congress</span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="House_of_Representatives">House of Representatives</span></h4>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:352px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:USHouseStructure2012-2022_SeatsByState.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1053" data-file-width="2000" height="184" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/65/USHouseStructure2012-2022_SeatsByState.png/350px-USHouseStructure2012-2022_SeatsByState.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/65/USHouseStructure2012-2022_SeatsByState.png/525px-USHouseStructure2012-2022_SeatsByState.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/65/USHouseStructure2012-2022_SeatsByState.png/700px-USHouseStructure2012-2022_SeatsByState.png 2x" width="350"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:USHouseStructure2012-2022_SeatsByState.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The 435 seats of the House grouped by state</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The House currently consists of 435 voting members, each of whom represents a <a href="/wiki/Congressional_district" title="Congressional district">congressional district</a>. The number of representatives each state has in the House is based on each state's population as determined in the most recent <a href="/wiki/United_States_Census" title="United States Census">United States Census</a>. All 435 representatives serve a two-year term. Each state receives a minimum of one representative in the House. In order to be elected as a representative, an individual must be at least 25 years of age, must have been a U.S. citizen for at least seven years, and must live in the state that he or she represents. There is no limit on the number of terms a representative may serve. In addition to the 435 voting members, there are six non-voting members, consisting of five delegates and one <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Resident_commissioner" title="Resident commissioner">resident commissioner</a>. There is one delegate each from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/District_of_Columbia" title="District of Columbia">District of Columbia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Guam" title="Guam">Guam</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Virgin_Islands" title="Virgin Islands">Virgin Islands</a>, <a href="/wiki/American_Samoa" title="American Samoa">American Samoa</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Commonwealth_of_the_Northern_Mariana_Islands" title="Commonwealth of the Northern Mariana Islands">Commonwealth of the Northern Mariana Islands</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Resident_Commissioner_of_Puerto_Rico" title="Resident Commissioner of Puerto Rico">resident commissioner</a> from <a href="/wiki/Puerto_Rico" title="Puerto Rico">Puerto Rico</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Senate">Senate</span></h4>
<p>In contrast, the Senate is made up of two senators from each state, regardless of population. There are currently 100 senators (two from each of the 50 states), who each serve six-year terms. Approximately one third of the Senate stands for election every two years.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Different_powers">Different powers</span></h4>
<p>The House and Senate each have particular exclusive powers. For example, the Senate must approve (give "<a href="/wiki/Advice_and_consent" title="Advice and consent">advice and consent</a>" to) many important Presidential appointments, including cabinet officers, federal judges (including nominees to the Supreme Court), department secretaries (heads of federal executive branch departments), U.S. military and naval officers, and ambassadors to foreign countries. All legislative bills for raising revenue must originate in the House of Representatives. The approval of both chambers is required to pass any legislation, which then may only become law by being signed by the President (or, if the President vetoes the bill, both houses of Congress then re-pass the bill, but by a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Super_majority" title="Super majority">two-thirds majority</a> of each chamber, in which case the bill becomes law without the President's signature). The powers of Congress are limited to those enumerated in the Constitution; all other powers are reserved to the states and the people. The Constitution also includes the "<a href="/wiki/Necessary_and_Proper_Clause" title="Necessary and Proper Clause">Necessary and Proper Clause</a>", which grants Congress the power to "make all laws which shall be necessary and proper for carrying into execution the foregoing powers". Members of the House and Senate are elected by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Plurality_voting_system" title="Plurality voting system">first-past-the-post</a> voting in every state except <a href="/wiki/Louisiana" title="Louisiana">Louisiana</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Georgia_(U.S._state)" title="Georgia (U.S. state)">Georgia</a>, which have <a href="/wiki/Two-round_system" title="Two-round system">runoffs</a>.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Impeachment_of_federal_officers">Impeachment of federal officers</span></h4>
<p>Congress has the power to remove the President, federal judges, and other federal officers from office. The House of Representatives and Senate have separate roles in this process. The House must first vote to "impeach" the official. Then, a trial is held in the Senate to decide whether the official should be removed from office. Although two presidents have been impeached by the House of Representatives (<a href="/wiki/Andrew_Johnson" title="Andrew Johnson">Andrew Johnson</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bill_Clinton" title="Bill Clinton">Bill Clinton</a>), neither of them was removed following trial in the Senate.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Congressional_procedures">Congressional procedures</span></h4>
<p>Article I, Section 2, paragraph 2 of the U.S. Constitution gives each chamber the power to "determine the rules of its proceedings". From this provision were created <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_States_Congressional_committee" title="United States Congressional committee">congressional committees</a>, which do the work of drafting legislation and conducting congressional investigations into national matters. The <a href="/wiki/108th_United_States_Congress" title="108th United States Congress">108th Congress</a> (2003–2005) had 19 standing committees in the House and 17 in the Senate, plus four joint permanent committees with members from both houses overseeing the <a href="/wiki/Library_of_Congress" title="Library of Congress">Library of Congress</a>, printing, taxation and the economy. In addition, each house may name special, or select, committees to study specific problems. Today, much of the congressional workload is borne by subcommittees, of which there are some 150.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Powers_of_Congress">Powers of Congress</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Article_One_of_the_United_States_Constitution" title="Article One of the United States Constitution">Article One of the United States Constitution</a></div>

<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:United_States_Capitol_-_west_front.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The <a href="/wiki/United_States_Capitol" title="United States Capitol">United States Capitol</a> is the <a href="/wiki/Seat_of_government" title="Seat of government">seat of government</a> for Congress.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The Constitution grants numerous powers to Congress. Enumerated in Article I, Section 8, these include the powers to levy and collect <a href="/wiki/Tax" title="Tax">taxes</a>; to coin money and regulate its value; provide for punishment for counterfeiting; establish post offices and roads, issue patents, create federal courts inferior to the <a href="/wiki/Supreme_Court_of_the_United_States" title="Supreme Court of the United States">Supreme Court</a>, combat <a href="/wiki/Piracy" title="Piracy">piracies</a> and <a href="/wiki/Felony" title="Felony">felonies</a>, declare <a href="/wiki/War" title="War">war</a>, raise and support <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Military_of_the_United_States" title="Military of the United States">armies</a>, provide and maintain a <a href="/wiki/United_States_Navy" title="United States Navy">navy</a>, make rules for the regulation of land and naval forces, provide for, arm and discipline the <a href="/wiki/Militia_(United_States)" title="Militia (United States)">militia</a>, exercise exclusive legislation in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Washington_D.C" title="Washington D.C">District of Columbia</a>, and to make laws necessary to properly execute powers. Over the two centuries since the United States was formed, many disputes have arisen over the limits on the powers of the federal government. These disputes have often been the subject of lawsuits that have ultimately been decided by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_States_Supreme_Court" title="United States Supreme Court">United States Supreme Court</a>.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Congressional_oversight">Congressional oversight</span></h4>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Congressional_oversight" title="Congressional oversight">Congressional oversight</a></div>
<p>Congressional oversight is intended to prevent waste and fraud, protect <a href="/wiki/Civil_liberties" title="Civil liberties">civil liberties</a> and individual rights, ensure executive compliance with the law, gather information for making laws and educating the public, and evaluate executive performance.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Congressional_oversight_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Congressional_oversight-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>It applies to cabinet departments, executive agencies, regulatory commissions and the presidency.</p>
<p>Congress's oversight function takes many forms:</p>
<ul>
<li>Committee inquiries and hearings</li>
<li>Formal consultations with and reports from the <a href="/wiki/President_of_the_United_States" title="President of the United States">President</a></li>
<li>Senate advice and consent for presidential nominations and for treaties</li>
<li>House <a href="/wiki/Impeachment_in_the_United_States" title="Impeachment in the United States">impeachment</a> proceedings and subsequent Senate trials</li>
<li>House and Senate proceedings under the <a href="/wiki/Twenty-fifth_Amendment_to_the_United_States_Constitution" title="Twenty-fifth Amendment to the United States Constitution">25th Amendment</a> in the event that the President becomes disabled or the office of the <a href="/wiki/Vice_President_of_the_United_States" title="Vice President of the United States">Vice President</a> falls vacant.</li>
<li>Informal meetings between legislators and executive officials</li>
<li>Congressional membership: each state is allocated a number of seats based on its representation (or ostensible representation, in the case of D.C.) in the House of Representatives. Each state is allocated two Senators regardless of its population. As of January 2010, the District of Columbia elects a non-voting representative to the House of Representatives along with American Samoa, the U.S. Virgin Islands, Guam, Puerto Rico and the Northern Mariana Islands.</li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Executive_branch">Executive branch</span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Article_Two_of_the_United_States_Constitution" title="Article Two of the United States Constitution">Article Two of the United States Constitution</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_States_Executive_Orders" title="United States Executive Orders">United States Executive Orders</a></div>
<p>The executive power in the federal government is vested in the President of the United States,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ArtII_wikisource_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-ArtII_wikisource-7">[7]</a></sup> although power is often delegated to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_States_Cabinet" title="United States Cabinet">Cabinet</a> members and other officials.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/President_of_the_United_States" title="President of the United States">President</a> and <a href="/wiki/Vice_President_of_the_United_States" title="Vice President of the United States">Vice President</a> are elected as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Running_mates" title="Running mates">running mates</a> by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_States_Electoral_College" title="United States Electoral College">Electoral College</a>, for which each <a href="/wiki/U.S._state" title="U.S. state">state</a>, as well as the <a href="/wiki/Washington,_D.C." title="Washington, D.C.">District of Columbia</a>, is allocated a number of seats based on its representation (or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ostensible_authority" title="Ostensible authority">ostensible representation</a>, in the case of D.C.) in both houses of Congress.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ArtII_wikisource_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-ArtII_wikisource-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> The President is limited to a maximum of two four-year terms.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> If the President has already served two years or more of a term to which some other person was elected, he may only serve one more additional four-year term.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ArtII_wikisource_7-2"><a href="#cite_note-ArtII_wikisource-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="President">President</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/President_of_the_United_States" title="President of the United States">President of the United States</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:182px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Seal_of_the_President_of_the_United_States.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2425" data-file-width="2424" height="180" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/36/Seal_of_the_President_of_the_United_States.svg/180px-Seal_of_the_President_of_the_United_States.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/36/Seal_of_the_President_of_the_United_States.svg/270px-Seal_of_the_President_of_the_United_States.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/36/Seal_of_the_President_of_the_United_States.svg/360px-Seal_of_the_President_of_the_United_States.svg.png 2x" width="180"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Seal_of_the_President_of_the_United_States.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Seal of the U.S. President</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The executive branch consists of the President and those to whom the President's powers are delegated. The President is both the <a href="/wiki/Head_of_state" title="Head of state">head of state</a> and <a href="/wiki/Head_of_government" title="Head of government">government</a>, as well as the military <a href="/wiki/Commander-in-chief#United_States" title="Commander-in-chief">commander-in-chief</a> and chief <a href="/wiki/Diplomat" title="Diplomat">diplomat</a>. The President, according to the Constitution, must "take care that the laws be faithfully executed", and "preserve, protect and defend the Constitution". The President presides over the executive branch of the federal government, an organization numbering about 5 million people, including 1 million active-duty military personnel and 600,000 postal service employees.</p>
<p>The President may sign legislation passed by Congress into law or may <a href="/wiki/Veto#United_States" title="Veto">veto</a> it, preventing it from becoming law unless two-thirds of both houses of Congress vote to override the veto. The President may unilaterally sign <a href="/wiki/Treaty" title="Treaty">treaties</a> with foreign nations. However, <a href="/wiki/Ratification#Ratification_of_an_international_treaty" title="Ratification">ratification</a> of international treaties requires a two-thirds majority vote in the Senate. The President may be <a href="/wiki/Impeachment_in_the_United_States" title="Impeachment in the United States">impeached</a> by a majority in the House and removed from office by a two-thirds majority in the Senate for "<a href="/wiki/Treason" title="Treason">treason</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bribery#Government" title="Bribery">bribery</a>, or other <a href="/wiki/High_crimes_and_misdemeanors" title="High crimes and misdemeanors">high crimes and misdemeanors</a>". The President may not <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dissolution_of_Parliament" title="Dissolution of Parliament">dissolve Congress</a> or call <a href="/wiki/By-election" title="By-election">special elections</a> but does have the power to <a href="/wiki/Pardon" title="Pardon">pardon</a>, or release, criminals convicted of offenses against the federal government (except in cases of impeachment), enact <a href="/wiki/Executive_order_(United_States)" title="Executive order (United States)">executive orders</a>, and (with the consent of the Senate) appoint Supreme Court justices and federal judges.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Vice_President">Vice President</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Vice_President_of_the_United_States" title="Vice President of the United States">Vice President of the United States</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:182px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Seal_of_the_Vice_President_of_the_United_States.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2437" data-file-width="2437" height="180" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6a/Seal_of_the_Vice_President_of_the_United_States.svg/180px-Seal_of_the_Vice_President_of_the_United_States.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6a/Seal_of_the_Vice_President_of_the_United_States.svg/270px-Seal_of_the_Vice_President_of_the_United_States.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6a/Seal_of_the_Vice_President_of_the_United_States.svg/360px-Seal_of_the_Vice_President_of_the_United_States.svg.png 2x" width="180"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Seal_of_the_Vice_President_of_the_United_States.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Seal of the U.S. Vice President</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The Vice President is the second-highest official in rank of the federal government. The office of the Vice President's duties and powers are established in the legislative branch of the federal government under Article 1, Section 3, Clauses 4 and 5 as the <a href="/wiki/President_of_the_Senate#United_States" title="President of the Senate">President of the Senate</a>. By virtue of this on-going role, he or she is the head of the Senate. In that capacity, the Vice President is allowed to vote in the Senate, but only when necessary to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_States_Vice_Presidents%27_tie-breaking_votes" title="United States Vice Presidents' tie-breaking votes">break a tie vote</a>. Pursuant to the <a href="/wiki/Twelfth_Amendment_to_the_United_States_Constitution" title="Twelfth Amendment to the United States Constitution">Twelfth Amendment</a>, the Vice President presides over the joint session of Congress when it convenes to count the vote of the Electoral College. As first in the <a href="/wiki/United_States_presidential_line_of_succession" title="United States presidential line of succession">U.S. presidential line of succession</a>, the Vice President duties and powers move to the executive branch when becoming President upon the death, resignation, or removal of the President, which has happened <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Us_presidents#About_the_list" title="Us presidents">nine times</a> in U.S. history. Lastly, in the case of a <a href="/wiki/Twenty-fifth_Amendment_to_the_United_States_Constitution" title="Twenty-fifth Amendment to the United States Constitution">Twenty-fifth Amendment</a> succession event, Vice President would become Acting President, assuming all of the powers and duties of President, except being designated as President. Accordingly, by circumstances, the Constitution designates the Vice President as routinely in the legislative branch, or succeeding to the executive branch as President, or possibly being in both as Acting President pursuant to the <a href="/wiki/Twenty-fifth_Amendment_to_the_United_States_Constitution" title="Twenty-fifth Amendment to the United States Constitution">Twenty-fifth Amendment</a>. Because of circumstances, the overlapping nature of the duties and powers attributed to the office, the title of the office and other matters, such has generated a spirited scholarly dispute regarding attaching an exclusive branch designation to the office of Vice President.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Goldstein_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-Goldstein-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span id="Cabinet,_executive_departments,_and_agencies"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Cabinet.2C_executive_departments.2C_and_agencies">Cabinet, executive departments, and agencies</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Cabinet_of_the_United_States" title="Cabinet of the United States">Cabinet of the United States</a>, <a href="/wiki/United_States_federal_executive_departments" title="United States federal executive departments">United States federal executive departments</a>, and <a href="/wiki/List_of_federal_agencies_in_the_United_States" title="List of federal agencies in the United States">List of federal agencies in the United States</a></div>
<p>The day-to-day enforcement and administration of federal laws is in the hands of the various <a href="/wiki/United_States_federal_executive_departments" title="United States federal executive departments">federal executive departments</a>, created by Congress to deal with specific areas of national and international affairs. The heads of the 15 departments, chosen by the President and approved with the "advice and consent" of the U.S. Senate, form a council of advisers generally known as the President's "Cabinet". Once confirmed "cabinet officers" serve at the pleasure of the President. In addition to departments, a number of staff organizations are grouped into the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Executive_Office_of_the_President" title="Executive Office of the President">Executive Office of the President</a>. These include the <a href="/wiki/White_House" title="White House">White House</a> staff, the <a href="/wiki/United_States_National_Security_Council" title="United States National Security Council">National Security Council</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Office_of_Management_and_Budget" title="Office of Management and Budget">Office of Management and Budget</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Council_of_Economic_Advisers" title="Council of Economic Advisers">Council of Economic Advisers</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Council_on_Environmental_Quality" title="Council on Environmental Quality">Council on Environmental Quality</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Office_of_the_United_States_Trade_Representative" title="Office of the United States Trade Representative">Office of the U.S. Trade Representative</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Office_of_National_Drug_Control_Policy" title="Office of National Drug Control Policy">Office of National Drug Control Policy</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Office_of_Science_and_Technology_Policy" title="Office of Science and Technology Policy">Office of Science and Technology Policy</a>. The employees in these United States government agencies are called <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_States_civil_service" title="United States civil service">federal civil servants</a>.</p>
<p>There are also <a href="/wiki/Independent_agencies_of_the_United_States_government" title="Independent agencies of the United States government">independent agencies</a> such as the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Postal_Service" title="United States Postal Service">United States Postal Service</a>, the <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">National Aeronautics and Space Administration</a> (NASA), the <a href="/wiki/Central_Intelligence_Agency" title="Central Intelligence Agency">Central Intelligence Agency</a> (CIA), the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Environmental_Protection_Agency" title="United States Environmental Protection Agency">Environmental Protection Agency</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Agency_for_International_Development" title="United States Agency for International Development">United States Agency for International Development</a>. In addition, there are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Government-owned_corporation" title="Government-owned corporation">government-owned corporations</a> such as the <a href="/wiki/Federal_Deposit_Insurance_Corporation" title="Federal Deposit Insurance Corporation">Federal Deposit Insurance Corporation</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/National_Railroad_Passenger_Corporation" title="National Railroad Passenger Corporation">National Railroad Passenger Corporation</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Judicial_branch">Judicial branch</span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Federal_judiciary_of_the_United_States" title="Federal judiciary of the United States">Federal judiciary of the United States</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_States_federal_courts" title="United States federal courts">United States federal courts</a></div>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Article_Three_of_the_United_States_Constitution" title="Article Three of the United States Constitution">Article Three of the United States Constitution</a></div>
<p>The Judiciary explains and applies the laws. This branch does this by hearing and eventually making decisions on various legal cases.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Overview_of_the_federal_judiciary">Overview of the federal judiciary</span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:182px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Seal_of_the_United_States_Supreme_Court.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="720" data-file-width="720" height="180" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f3/Seal_of_the_United_States_Supreme_Court.svg/180px-Seal_of_the_United_States_Supreme_Court.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f3/Seal_of_the_United_States_Supreme_Court.svg/270px-Seal_of_the_United_States_Supreme_Court.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f3/Seal_of_the_United_States_Supreme_Court.svg/360px-Seal_of_the_United_States_Supreme_Court.svg.png 2x" width="180"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Seal_of_the_United_States_Supreme_Court.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Seal of the U.S. Supreme Court</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Article III section I of the Constitution establishes the <a href="/wiki/Supreme_Court_of_the_United_States" title="Supreme Court of the United States">Supreme Court of the United States</a> and authorizes the United States Congress to establish inferior (i.e., lower) courts as their need shall arise. Section I also establishes a lifetime tenure for all federal judges and states that their compensation may not be diminished during their time in office. Article II section II establishes that all federal judges are to be appointed by the president and confirmed by the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Senate" title="United States Senate">United States Senate</a>.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Judiciary_Act_of_1789" title="Judiciary Act of 1789">Judiciary Act of 1789</a> subdivided the nation jurisdictionally into <a href="/wiki/United_States_federal_judicial_district" title="United States federal judicial district">judicial districts</a> and created federal courts for each district. The three tiered structure of this act established the basic structure of the national judiciary: the Supreme Court, 13 courts of appeals, 94 district courts, and two courts of special jurisdiction. Congress retains the power to re-organize or even abolish federal courts lower than the Supreme Court.</p>
<p>The U.S. Supreme Court adjudicates "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cases_and_controversies" title="Cases and controversies">cases and controversies</a>"—matters pertaining to the federal government, disputes between states, and interpretation of the United States Constitution, and, in general, can declare legislation or executive action made at any level of the government as <a href="/wiki/Judicial_review_in_the_United_States" title="Judicial review in the United States">unconstitutional</a>, nullifying the law and creating <a href="/wiki/Precedent" title="Precedent">precedent</a> for future law and decisions. The United States Constitution does not specifically mention the power of <a href="/wiki/Judicial_review_in_the_United_States" title="Judicial review in the United States">judicial review</a> (the power to declare a law unconstitutional). The power of judicial review was asserted by <a href="/wiki/John_Marshall" title="John Marshall">Chief Justice Marshall</a> in the landmark Supreme Court Case <i><a href="/wiki/Marbury_v._Madison" title="Marbury v. Madison">Marbury v. Madison</a></i> (1803). There have been instances in the past where such declarations have been ignored by the other two branches. Below the U.S. Supreme Court are the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_States_court_of_appeals" title="United States court of appeals">United States Courts of Appeals</a>, and below them in turn are the <a href="/wiki/United_States_district_court" title="United States district court">United States District Courts</a>, which are the general trial courts for federal law, and for certain controversies between litigants who are not deemed citizens of the same state ("<a href="/wiki/Diversity_jurisdiction" title="Diversity jurisdiction">diversity jurisdiction</a>").</p>
<p>There are three levels of federal courts with <i>general jurisdiction</i>, meaning that these courts handle criminal cases and civil lawsuits between individuals. Other courts, such as the <a href="/wiki/United_States_bankruptcy_court" title="United States bankruptcy court">bankruptcy courts</a> and the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Tax_Court" title="United States Tax Court">Tax Court</a>, are specialized courts handling only certain kinds of cases ("<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Subject_matter_jurisdiction" title="Subject matter jurisdiction">subject matter jurisdiction</a>"). The Bankruptcy Courts are "under" the supervision of the district courts, and, as such, are not considered part of the "<a href="/wiki/Article_Three_of_the_United_States_Constitution" title="Article Three of the United States Constitution">Article III</a>" judiciary and also as such their judges do not have lifetime tenure, nor are they Constitutionally exempt from diminution of their remuneration.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> Also the Tax Court is not an Article III court (but is, instead an "Article I Court").<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>The district courts are the trial courts wherein cases that are considered under the Judicial Code (Title 28, United States Code) consistent with the jurisdictional precepts of "<a href="/wiki/Federal_question_jurisdiction" title="Federal question jurisdiction">federal question jurisdiction</a>" and "diversity jurisdiction" and "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pendent_jurisdiction" title="Pendent jurisdiction">pendent jurisdiction</a>" can be filed and decided. The district courts can also hear cases under "<a href="/wiki/Removal_jurisdiction" title="Removal jurisdiction">removal jurisdiction</a>", wherein a case brought in State court meets the requirements for diversity jurisdiction, and one party litigant chooses to "remove" the case from state court to federal court.</p>
<p>The United States Courts of Appeals are appellate courts that hear appeals of cases decided by the district courts, and some direct appeals from administrative agencies, and some interlocutory appeals. The U.S. Supreme Court hears appeals from the decisions of the courts of appeals or state supreme courts, and in addition has <a href="/wiki/Original_jurisdiction" title="Original jurisdiction">original jurisdiction</a> over a few cases.</p>
<p>The judicial power extends to cases arising under the Constitution, an <a href="/wiki/Act_of_Congress" title="Act of Congress">Act of Congress</a>; a U.S. <a href="/wiki/Treaty" title="Treaty">treaty</a>; cases affecting <a href="/wiki/Ambassador" title="Ambassador">ambassadors</a>, <a href="/wiki/Diplomatic_rank" title="Diplomatic rank">ministers</a> and <a href="/wiki/Consul_(representative)" title="Consul (representative)">consuls</a> of foreign countries in the U.S.; cases and controversies to which the federal government is a party; controversies between states (or their citizens) and foreign nations (or their citizens or subjects); and bankruptcy cases (collectively "federal-question jurisdiction"). The <a href="/wiki/Eleventh_Amendment_to_the_United_States_Constitution" title="Eleventh Amendment to the United States Constitution">Eleventh Amendment</a> removed from federal jurisdiction cases in which citizens of one state were the plaintiffs and the government of another state was the defendant. It did not disturb federal jurisdiction in cases in which a state government is a plaintiff and a citizen of another state the defendant.</p>
<p>The power of the federal courts extends both to civil actions for damages and other redress, and to criminal cases arising under federal law. The interplay of the Supremacy Clause and Article III has resulted in a complex set of relationships between state and federal courts. Federal courts can sometimes hear cases arising under state law pursuant to <a href="/wiki/Diversity_jurisdiction" title="Diversity jurisdiction">diversity jurisdiction</a>, state courts can decide certain matters involving federal law, and a handful of federal claims are primarily reserved by federal statute to the state courts (for example, those arising from the <a href="/wiki/Telephone_Consumer_Protection_Act_of_1991" title="Telephone Consumer Protection Act of 1991">Telephone Consumer Protection Act of 1991</a>). Both court systems thus can be said to have <a href="/wiki/Exclusive_jurisdiction" title="Exclusive jurisdiction">exclusive jurisdiction</a> in some areas and <a href="/wiki/Concurrent_jurisdiction" title="Concurrent jurisdiction">concurrent jurisdiction</a> in others.</p>
<p>The U.S. Constitution safeguards judicial independence by providing that federal judges shall hold office "during good behavior"; in practice, this usually means they serve until they die, retire, or resign. A judge who commits an offense while in office may be <a href="/wiki/Impeachment" title="Impeachment">impeached</a> in the same way as the President or other officials of the federal government. U.S. judges are appointed by the President, subject to confirmation by the Senate. Another Constitutional provision prohibits Congress from reducing the pay of any Article III judge (Congress is able to set a lower salary for all future judges that take office after the reduction, but may not decrease the rate of pay for judges already in office).</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Relationships_between_state_and_federal_courts">Relationships between state and federal courts</span></h3>
<p>Separate from, but not entirely independent of, this federal court system are the court systems of each state, each dealing with, in addition to federal law when not deemed preempted, a state's own laws, and having its own court rules and procedures. Although state governments and the federal government are legally <i>dual sovereigns</i>, the Supreme Court of the United States is in many cases the appellate court from the State Supreme Courts (e.g., absent the Court countenancing the applicability of the <i><a href="/wiki/Adequate_and_independent_state_ground" title="Adequate and independent state ground">doctrine of adequate and independent State grounds</a></i>). The <a href="/wiki/State_supreme_court" title="State supreme court">Supreme Courts of each state</a> are by this doctrine the final authority on the interpretation of the applicable state's laws and Constitution. Many state constitution provisions are equal in breadth to those of the U.S. Constitution, but are considered "parallel" (thus, where, for example, the right to privacy pursuant to a state constitution is broader than the federal right to privacy, and the asserted ground is explicitly held to be "independent", the question can be finally decided in a State Supreme Court—the U.S. Supreme Court will decline to take jurisdiction).</p>
<p>A State Supreme Court, other than of its own accord, is bound <i>only</i> by the U.S. Supreme Court's interpretation of federal law, but is <i>not</i> bound by interpretation of federal law by the federal court of appeals for the federal circuit in which the state is included, or even the federal district courts located in the state, a result of the <i>dual sovereigns</i> concept. Conversely, a federal district court hearing a matter involving only a question of state law (usually through <a href="/wiki/Diversity_jurisdiction" title="Diversity jurisdiction">diversity jurisdiction</a>) must apply the substantive law of the state in which the court sits, a result of the application of the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Erie_Doctrine" title="Erie Doctrine">Erie Doctrine</a></i>; however, at the same time, the case is heard under the <a href="/wiki/Federal_Rules_of_Civil_Procedure" title="Federal Rules of Civil Procedure">Federal Rules of Civil Procedure</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Federal_Rules_of_Criminal_Procedure" title="Federal Rules of Criminal Procedure">Federal Rules of Criminal Procedure</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Federal_Rules_of_Evidence" title="Federal Rules of Evidence">Federal Rules of Evidence</a> instead of state procedural rules (that is, the application of the <i>Erie Doctrine</i> only extends to a requirement that a federal court asserting diversity jurisdiction apply <i>substantive</i> state law, but not <i>procedural</i> state law, which may be different). Together, the laws of the federal and state governments form <a href="/wiki/Law_of_the_United_States" title="Law of the United States">U.S. law</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Elections_and_voting">Elections and voting</span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Elections_in_the_United_States" title="Elections in the United States">Elections in the United States</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">

<div class="thumbcaption">

<p><a href="/wiki/Voting_rights_in_the_United_States" title="Voting rights in the United States">Suffrage</a>, commonly known as the ability to vote, has changed significantly over time. In the early years of the United States, voting was considered a matter for state governments, and was commonly restricted to white men who owned land. Direct elections were mostly held only for the U.S. House of Representatives and state legislatures, although what specific bodies were elected by the electorate varied from state to state. Under this original system, both <a href="/wiki/United_States_Senate" title="United States Senate">senators</a> representing each state in the U.S. Senate were chosen by a majority vote of the state legislature. Since the ratification of the <a href="/wiki/Seventeenth_Amendment_to_the_United_States_Constitution" title="Seventeenth Amendment to the United States Constitution">Seventeenth Amendment</a> in 1913, members of both houses of Congress have been directly elected. Today, U.S. citizens have almost <a href="/wiki/Universal_suffrage" title="Universal suffrage">universal suffrage</a> under equal protection of the laws<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> from the age of 18,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> regardless of race,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> gender,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> or wealth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> The only significant exception to this is the <a href="/wiki/Felony_disenfranchisement" title="Felony disenfranchisement">disenfranchisement of convicted felons</a>, and in some states former felons as well.</p>
<p>Under the U.S. Constitution, the national representation of U.S. territories and the federal district of <a href="/wiki/Washington,_D.C." title="Washington, D.C.">Washington, D.C.</a> in Congress is <a href="/wiki/District_of_Columbia_voting_rights" title="District of Columbia voting rights">limited</a>: while residents of the District of Columbia are subject to federal laws and federal taxes, their only congressional representative is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Delegate_(United_States_Congress)" title="Delegate (United States Congress)">non-voting delegate</a>; however, they have been allowed to participate in presidential elections since March 29, 1961.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> Residents of U.S. territories have varying rights; for example, only some residents of <a href="/wiki/Puerto_Rico" title="Puerto Rico">Puerto Rico</a> pay federal income taxes (though all residents must pay all other federal taxes, including import/export taxes, federal commodity taxes and federal <a href="/wiki/Payroll_tax" title="Payroll tax">payroll taxes</a>, including <a href="/wiki/Social_Security_(United_States)" title="Social Security (United States)">Social Security</a> and <a href="/wiki/Medicare_(United_States)" title="Medicare (United States)">Medicare</a>). All federal laws that are "not locally inapplicable" are automatically the law of the land in Puerto Rico but their current representation in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._Congress" title="U.S. Congress">U.S. Congress</a> is in the form of a <a href="/wiki/Resident_Commissioner_of_Puerto_Rico" title="Resident Commissioner of Puerto Rico">Resident Commissioner</a>, a nonvoting delegate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span id="State,_tribal,_and_local_governments"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="State.2C_tribal.2C_and_local_governments">State, tribal, and local governments</span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/State_governments_of_the_United_States" title="State governments of the United States">State governments of the United States</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tribal_sovereignty_in_the_United_States" title="Tribal sovereignty in the United States">Tribal sovereignty in the United States</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Local_government_in_the_United_States" title="Local government in the United States">Local government in the United States</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Map_of_USA_with_county_outlines.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="983" data-file-width="1513" height="143" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f6/Map_of_USA_with_county_outlines.png/220px-Map_of_USA_with_county_outlines.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f6/Map_of_USA_with_county_outlines.png/330px-Map_of_USA_with_county_outlines.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f6/Map_of_USA_with_county_outlines.png/440px-Map_of_USA_with_county_outlines.png 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Map_of_USA_with_county_outlines.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The states of the United States as divided into <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Counties_of_the_United_States" title="Counties of the United States">counties</a> (or, in <a href="/wiki/Louisiana" title="Louisiana">Louisiana</a> and <a href="/wiki/Alaska" title="Alaska">Alaska</a>, <a href="/wiki/List_of_parishes_in_Louisiana" title="List of parishes in Louisiana">parishes</a> and <a href="/wiki/List_of_boroughs_and_census_areas_in_Alaska" title="List of boroughs and census areas in Alaska">boroughs</a>, respectively). Alaska and <a href="/wiki/Hawaii" title="Hawaii">Hawaii</a> are not to scale and the <a href="/wiki/Aleutian_Islands" title="Aleutian Islands">Aleutian</a> and uninhabited <a href="/wiki/Northwestern_Hawaiian_Islands" title="Northwestern Hawaiian Islands">Northwestern Hawaiian Islands</a> have been omitted.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The state governments tend to have the greatest influence over most Americans' daily lives. The <a href="/wiki/Tenth_Amendment_to_the_United_States_Constitution" title="Tenth Amendment to the United States Constitution">Tenth Amendment</a> prohibits the federal government from exercising any power not delegated to it by the States in the Constitution; as a result, states handle the majority of issues most relevant to individuals within their jurisdiction. Because state governments are not authorized to print currency, they generally have to raise revenue through either taxes or bonds. As a result, state governments tend to impose severe budget cuts or raise taxes any time the economy is faltering.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p>Each state has its own written constitution, government and code of laws. The Constitution stipulates only that each state must have, "a Republican Government". Therefore, there are often great differences in law and procedure between individual states, concerning issues such as property, crime, health and education, amongst others. The highest elected official of each state is the <a href="/wiki/Governor#United_States" title="Governor">Governor</a>. Each state also has an elected <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/State_legislature_(US)" title="State legislature (US)">state legislature</a> (<a href="/wiki/Bicameralism" title="Bicameralism">bicameralism</a> is a feature of every state except <a href="/wiki/Nebraska" title="Nebraska">Nebraska</a>), whose members represent the voters of the state. Each state maintains its own <a href="/wiki/State_court_(United_States)" title="State court (United States)">state court</a> system. In some states, supreme and lower court justices are elected by the people; in others, they are appointed, as they are in the federal system.</p>
<p>As a result of the Supreme Court case <i><a href="/wiki/Worcester_v._Georgia" title="Worcester v. Georgia">Worcester v. Georgia</a></i>, <a href="/wiki/Tribal_sovereignty_in_the_United_States" title="Tribal sovereignty in the United States">American Indian tribes</a> are considered "domestic dependent nations" that operate as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tribal_sovereignty" title="Tribal sovereignty">sovereign</a> governments subject to federal authority but, in some cases, outside of the jurisdiction of state governments. Hundreds of laws, executive orders and court cases have modified the governmental status of tribes <a href="/wiki/Face-to-face_(philosophy)" title="Face-to-face (philosophy)">vis-à-vis</a> individual states, but the two have continued to be recognized as separate bodies. Tribal governments vary in robustness, from a simple council used to manage all aspects of tribal affairs, to large and complex bureaucracies with several branches of government. Tribes are currently encouraged to form their own governments, with power resting in elected tribal councils, elected tribal chairpersons, or religiously appointed leaders (as is the case with <a href="/wiki/Pueblo" title="Pueblo">pueblos</a>). Tribal citizenship and voting rights are typically restricted to individuals of native descent, but tribes are free to set whatever citizenship requirements they wish.</p>
<p>The institutions that are responsible for local government within states are typically town, city, or county boards, water management districts, fire management districts, library districts and other similar governmental units which make laws that affect their particular area. These laws concern issues such as traffic, the sale of alcohol and the keeping of animals. The highest elected official of a town or city is usually the <a href="/wiki/Mayor#United_States" title="Mayor">mayor</a>. In <a href="/wiki/New_England" title="New England">New England</a>, towns operate in a <a href="/wiki/Direct_democracy" title="Direct democracy">direct democratic</a> fashion, and in some states, such as <a href="/wiki/Rhode_Island" title="Rhode Island">Rhode Island</a>, <a href="/wiki/Connecticut" title="Connecticut">Connecticut</a>, and some parts of <a href="/wiki/Massachusetts" title="Massachusetts">Massachusetts</a>, counties have little or no power, existing only as geographic distinctions. In other areas, county governments have more power, such as to collect taxes and maintain <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Policing_in_the_United_States" title="Policing in the United States">law enforcement</a> agencies.</p>

of the Interior, Office of Insular Affairs.)</a> <a class="external text" href="https://web.archive.org/web/20120610225702/http://www.doi.gov/oia/Islandpages/prpage.htm" rel="nofollow">Archived</a> June 10, 2012, at the <a href="/wiki/Wayback_Machine" title="Wayback Machine">Wayback Machine</a>., import/export taxes (See <a class="external text" href="https://web.archive.org/web/20100401034052/http://stanford.wellsphere.com/healthcare-industry-policy-article/puerto-rico/267827" rel="nofollow">Archived</a> April 1, 2010, at the <a href="/wiki/Wayback_Machine" title="Wayback Machine">Wayback Machine</a>., federal commodity taxes (See <a class="external text" href="https://www.irs.gov/taxtopics/tc903.html" rel="nofollow">IRS.gov), etc.</a> Residents pay federal <a href="/wiki/Payroll_tax" title="Payroll tax">payroll taxes</a>, such as <a rel="nofollow">IRS.gov)</a> and <a href="/wiki/Medicare_(United_States)" title="Medicare (United States)">Medicare</a> (See <a class="external text" href="https://www.reuters.com/article/healthNews/idUSTRE58N5X320090924" rel="nofollow">Reuters.com)</a>, as well as Commonwealth of Puerto Rico income taxes (See <a class="external text" href="http://www.puertorico-herald.org/issues/2003/vol7n19/USNotInnocent-en.html" rel="nofollow">Puertorico-herald.com</a> and <a class="external text" href="http://www.htrcpa.com/businessinpr1.html" rel="nofollow">HTRCPA.com).</a> <a class="external text" href="https://web.archive.org/web/20110429222842/http://www.htrcpa.com/businessinpr1.html" rel="nofollow">Archived</a> April 29, 2011, at the <a href="/wiki/Wayback_Machine" title="Wayback Machine">Wayback Machine</a>. All federal employees (See <a class="external text" href="http://www.heritage.org/research/taxes/wm2338.cfm" rel="nofollow">Heritage.org)</a> <a class="external text" href="https://web.archive.org/web/20100210124900/http://www.heritage.org/Research/Taxes/wm2338.cfm" rel="nofollow">Archived</a> February 10, 2010, at the <a href="/wiki/Wayback_Machine" title="Wayback Machine">Wayback Machine</a>., those who do business with the federal government (See <a class="external text" href="http://www.mcvpr.com/CM/CurrentEvents/CEOsummitarticle.pdf" rel="nofollow">MCVPR.com)</a> <a class="external text" href="https://www.webcitation.org/5mp67ZoSs?url=http://www.mcvpr.com/CM/CurrentEvents/CEOsummitarticle.pdf" rel="nofollow">Archived</a> January 16, 2010, at <a href="/wiki/WebCite" title="WebCite">WebCite</a>, Puerto Rico-based corporations that intend to send funds to the U.S. (See <a class="external text" href="http://www.jct.gov/x-24-06.pdf" rel="nofollow">Page 9, line 1.)</a>, and some others (For example, Puerto Rican residents that are members of the U.S. military, See <a class="external text" href="http://www.heritage.org/research/taxes/wm2338.cfm;" rel="nofollow">Heritage.org</a> and Puerto Rico residents who earned income from sources outside Puerto Rico, See <a class="external text" href="http://www.jct.gov/x-24-06.pdf," rel="nofollow">pp 14–15.)</a> also pay federal income taxes. In addition, because the cutoff point for income taxation is lower than that of the U.S. IRS code, and because the per-capita income in Puerto Rico is much lower than the average per-capita income on the mainland, more Puerto Rico residents pay income taxes to the local taxation authority than if the IRS code were applied to the island. This occurs because "the Commonwealth of Puerto Rico government has a wider set of responsibilities than do U.S. State and local governments" (See <a class="external text" href="http://www.gao.gov/products/GAO-06-541" rel="nofollow">GAO.gov).</a> As residents of Puerto Rico pay into Social Security, Puerto Ricans are eligible for Social Security benefits upon retirement, but are excluded from the <a href="/wiki/Supplemental_Security_Income" title="Supplemental Security Income">Supplemental Security Income</a> (SSI) (Commonwealth of Puerto Rico residents, unlike residents of the Commonwealth of the Northern Mariana Islands and residents of the 50 States, do not receive the SSI. See <a class="external text" href="http://www.socialsecurity.gov/OP_Home/handbook/handbook.21/handbook-2114.html" rel="nofollow">Socialsecurity.gov)</a>, and the island actually receives less than 15% of the <a href="/wiki/Medicaid" title="Medicaid">Medicaid</a> funding it would normally receive if it were a U.S. state. However, Medicare providers receive less-than-full state-like reimbursements for services rendered to beneficiaries in Puerto Rico, even though the latter paid fully into the system (See <a class="external text" href="http://www.prfaa.com/news/?p=252" rel="nofollow">p. 252).</a> <a class="external text" href="https://web.archive.org/web/20110511054048/http://www.prfaa.com/news/?p=252" rel="nofollow">Archived</a> May 11, 2011, at the <a href="/wiki/Wayback_Machine" title="Wayback Machine">Wayback Machine</a>. It has also been estimated (See <a class="external text" href="http://www.eagleforum.org/column/2007/mar07/07-03-28.html)" rel="nofollow">Eagleforum.org</a> that, because the population of the island is greater than that of 50% of the states, if it were a state, Puerto Rico would have six to eight seats in the House, in addition to the two seats in the Senate.(See <a class="external text" href="http://www.eagleforum.org/column/2007/mar07/07-03-28.html" rel="nofollow">Eagleforum.org,</a> <a class="external text" href="http://www.crf-usa.org/bill-of-rights-in-action/bria-17-4-c.html#" rel="nofollow">CRF-USA.org</a> <a class="external text" href="https://web.archive.org/web/20090610210536/http://www.crf-usa.org/bill-of-rights-in-action/bria-17-4-c.html" rel="nofollow">Archived</a> June 10, 2009, at the <a href="/wiki/Wayback_Machine" title="Wayback Machine">Wayback Machine</a>. and <a class="external text" href="http://www.thomas.gov/cgi-bin/cpquery/?&amp;sid=cp1109rs5H&amp;refer=&amp;r_n=hr597.110" rel="nofollow">Thomas.gov</a> [Note that for the later, the official US Congress database website, you will need to resubmit a query. The document in question is called "House Report 110-597 – <a href="/wiki/Puerto_Rico_Democracy_Act#Puerto_Rico_Democracy_Act_of_2007" title="Puerto Rico Democracy Act">Puerto Rico Democracy Act of 2007</a>". These are the steps to follow: <a class="external text" href="http://www.thomas.gov/" rel="nofollow">Thomas.gov</a> &gt; Committee Reports &gt; 110 &gt; drop down "Word/Phrase" and pick "Report Number" &gt; type "597" next to Report Number. This will provide the document "House Report 110-597 – <a href="/wiki/Puerto_Rico_Democracy_Act#Puerto_Rico_Democracy_Act_of_2007" title="Puerto Rico Democracy Act">Puerto Rico Democracy Act of 2007</a>", then from the Table of Contents choose "Background and need for legislation".]). Another misconception is that the import/export taxes collected by the U.S. on products manufactured in Puerto Rico are all returned to the Puerto Rico Treasury. This is not the case. Such import/export taxes are returned <i>only</i> for rum products, and even then the US Treasury keeps a portion of those taxes (See the "House Report 110-597 – <a href="/wiki/Puerto_Rico_Democracy_Act#Puerto_Rico_Democracy_Act_of_2007" title="Puerto Rico Democracy Act">Puerto Rico Democracy Act of 2007</a>" mentioned above.)</span></li>
