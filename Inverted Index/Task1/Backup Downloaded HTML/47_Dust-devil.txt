<p>A <b>dust devil</b> is a strong, well-formed, and relatively long-lived <a href="/wiki/Whirlwind" title="Whirlwind">whirlwind</a>, ranging from small (half a meter wide and a few meters tall) to large (more than 10 meters wide and more than 1000 meters tall). The primary vertical motion is upward. Dust devils are usually harmless, but can on rare occasions grow large enough to pose a threat to both people and property.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Glossary_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Glossary-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>They are comparable to <a href="/wiki/Tornado" title="Tornado">tornadoes</a> in that both are a weather phenomenon involving a vertically oriented rotating column of wind. Most tornadoes are associated with a larger parent circulation, the <a href="/wiki/Mesocyclone" title="Mesocyclone">mesocyclone</a> on the back of a <a href="/wiki/Supercell" title="Supercell">supercell</a> <a href="/wiki/Thunderstorm" title="Thunderstorm">thunderstorm</a>. Dust devils form as a swirling updraft under sunny conditions during fair weather, rarely coming close to the intensity of a tornado.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Names">Names</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dust_devil&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Names">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In the southwestern United States, a dust devil is sometimes called a "dancing devil". In <a href="/wiki/Death_Valley" title="Death Valley">Death Valley</a>, California, it may be called a "sand auger" or a "dust whirl".<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Accuracy_dispute#Disputed_statement" title="Wikipedia:Accuracy dispute"><span title="The material near this tag is possibly inaccurate or nonfactual. (July 2011)">dubious</span></a> <span class="metadata">– <a href="/wiki/Talk:Dust_devil#Dubious" title="Talk:Dust devil">discuss</a></span></i>]</sup></p>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Navajo_people" title="Navajo people">Navajo</a> refer to them as <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chiindii" title="Chiindii">chiindii</a></i>, ghosts or spirits of dead Navajos. If a chindi spins clockwise, it is said to be a good spirit; if it spins counterclockwise, it is said to be a bad spirit.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Australian_English" title="Australian English">Australian English</a> term "willy-willy" or "whirly-whirly" is thought to derive from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Yindjibarndi_language" title="Yindjibarndi language">Yindjibarndi</a> or a neighbouring language.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> In Aboriginal myths, willy-willies represent spirit forms, parents may warn their children that if they misbehave, a spirit will emerge from the spinning vortex of dirt and chastise them. There is a story of the origin of the <a href="/wiki/Brolga" title="Brolga">brolga</a> in which a bad spirit descends from the sky and captures the young being and abducts her by taking the form of a willy-willy.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Saudi_Arabia" title="Saudi Arabia">Saudi Arabia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kuwait" title="Kuwait">Kuwait</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kazakhstan" title="Kazakhstan">Kazakhstan</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Jordan" title="Jordan">Jordan</a>, dust devils often reach hundreds of meters in height<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2008)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> and are referred to as <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Djin" title="Djin">djin</a></i> ("genies" or "devils").<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Egypt" title="Egypt">Egypt</a> has its <i>fasset el 'afreet</i>, or "ghost's wind". In <a href="/wiki/Iran" title="Iran">Iran</a>, this kind of wind is usually called "Gerd Baad", or "round wind".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Among the <a href="/wiki/Kikuyu_people" title="Kikuyu people">Kikuyu</a> of <a href="/wiki/Kenya" title="Kenya">Kenya</a>, the dust devil is known as <i>ngoma cia aka</i>, meaning "women's devil/demon".<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Brazil" title="Brazil">Brazil</a>, a dust devil is called <i>redemoinho</i> after <i>moinho de vento</i> ("<a href="/wiki/Windmill" title="Windmill">windmill</a>"). In some traditions, it contains a dancing <a href="/wiki/Saci_(Brazilian_folklore)" title="Saci (Brazilian folklore)">Saci</a>. Also, sometimes it is associated to local religion and beliefs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> In <a href="/wiki/Portugal" title="Portugal">Portugal</a> it is known locally as <i>remoinho</i> (translated to <i>continuous rotation</i>).<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>In the US, when they occur in cities or urban scenes, they are sometimes called "Nevada tornadoes" or "Chicago tornadoes" because of Chicago's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Origin_of_Chicago%27s_%22Windy_City%22_nickname" title="Origin of Chicago's &quot;Windy City&quot; nickname">reputation for wind</a>, despite dust devils being rare in Chicago.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Accuracy_dispute#Disputed_statement" title="Wikipedia:Accuracy dispute"><span title="The material near this tag is possibly inaccurate or nonfactual. (June 2014)">dubious</span></a> <span class="metadata">– <a href="/wiki/Talk:Dust_devil#Dubious" title="Talk:Dust devil">discuss</a></span></i>]</sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Formation">Formation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dust_devil&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Formation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Iraqi_Dust_Devil.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="800" data-file-width="600" height="267" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/87/Iraqi_Dust_Devil.jpg/200px-Iraqi_Dust_Devil.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/87/Iraqi_Dust_Devil.jpg/300px-Iraqi_Dust_Devil.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/87/Iraqi_Dust_Devil.jpg/400px-Iraqi_Dust_Devil.jpg 2x" width="200"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Iraqi_Dust_Devil.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
A dust devil in <a href="/wiki/Ramadi" title="Ramadi">Ramadi</a>, <a href="/wiki/Iraq" title="Iraq">Iraq</a>.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Dust devils form when hot air near the surface rises quickly through a small pocket of cooler, low-pressure air above it. If conditions are just right, the air may begin to rotate. As the air rapidly rises, the column of hot air is stretched vertically, thereby moving mass closer to the axis of rotation, which causes intensification of the spinning effect by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Conservation_of_angular_momentum" title="Conservation of angular momentum">conservation of angular momentum</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Secondary_flow#Tornadoes_and_dust_devils" title="Secondary flow">secondary flow</a> in the dust devil causes other hot air to speed horizontally inward to the bottom of the newly forming vortex. As more hot air rushes in toward the developing vortex to replace the air that is rising, the spinning effect becomes further intensified and self-sustaining. A dust devil, fully formed, is a funnel-like chimney through which hot air moves, both upwards and in a circle. As the hot air rises, it cools, loses its buoyancy and eventually ceases to rise. As it rises, it displaces air which descends outside the core of the vortex. This cool air returning acts as a balance against the spinning hot-air outer wall and keeps the system stable.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>The spinning effect, along with surface <a href="/wiki/Friction" title="Friction">friction</a>, usually will produce a forward momentum. The dust devil is able to sustain itself longer by moving over nearby sources of hot surface air.</p>
<p>As available extreme hot air near the surface is channeled up the dust devil, eventually surrounding cooler air will be sucked in. Once this occurs, the effect is dramatic, and the dust devil dissipates in seconds. Usually this occurs when the dust devil is not moving fast enough (depletion) or begins to enter a terrain where the surface temperatures are cooler, causing unbalance.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-death-valley_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-death-valley-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>Certain conditions increase the likelihood of dust devil formation.</p>
<ul>
<li><b>Flat barren terrain, desert or tarmac:</b> Flat conditions increase the likelihood of the hot-air "fuel" being a near constant. Dusty or sandy conditions will cause particles to become caught up in the vortex, making the dust devil easily visible.</li>
<li><b>Clear skies or lightly cloudy conditions:</b> The surface needs to absorb significant amounts of <a href="/wiki/Solar_power" title="Solar power">solar energy</a> to heat the air near the surface and create ideal dust devil conditions.</li>
<li><b>Light or no wind and cool atmospheric temperature:</b> The underlying factor for sustainability of a dust devil is the extreme difference in temperature between the near-surface air and the atmosphere. Windy conditions will destabilize the spinning effect (like a <a href="/wiki/Tornado" title="Tornado">Tornado</a>) of a dust devil.</li>
</ul>

<p>On Earth, most dust devils are very small and weak, often less than 3 feet (0.9 m) in diameter with maximum winds averaging about 45 miles per hour (70 km/h), and they often dissipate less than a minute after forming. On rare occasions, a dust devil can grow very large and intense, sometimes reaching a diameter of up to 300 feet (90 m) with winds in excess of 60 mph (100 km/h+) and can last for upwards of 20 minutes before dissipating.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>Dust devils typically do not cause injuries, but rare, severe dust devils have caused damage and even deaths in the past. One such dust devil struck the <a href="/wiki/Coconino_County,_Arizona" title="Coconino County, Arizona">Coconino County</a> Fairgrounds in <a href="/wiki/Flagstaff,_Arizona" title="Flagstaff, Arizona">Flagstaff, Arizona</a>, on September 14, 2000, causing extensive damage to several temporary tents, stands and booths, as well as some permanent fairgrounds structures. Several injuries were reported, but there were no fatalities. Based on the degree of damage left behind, it is estimated that the dust devil produced winds as high as 75 mph (120 km/h), which is equivalent to an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Enhanced_Fujita_Scale" title="Enhanced Fujita Scale">EF-0</a> tornado.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> On May 19, 2003, a dust devil lifted the roof off a two-story building in <a href="/wiki/Lebanon,_Maine" title="Lebanon, Maine">Lebanon, Maine</a>, causing it to collapse and kill a man inside.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> In East <a href="/wiki/El_Paso,_Texas" title="El Paso, Texas">El Paso, Texas</a> in 2010, three children in an <a href="/wiki/Inflatable_castle" title="Inflatable castle">inflatable jump house</a> were picked up by a dust devil and lifted over 10 feet (3 m), traveling over a fence and landing in a backyard three houses away.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> On December 31, 2014, during the set-up for a cookoff in the <a href="/wiki/Rose_Bowl_Game" title="Rose Bowl Game">Rose Bowl</a> Fan Fest area, a dust devil was caught on video. No one was seriously injured.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Hazards">Hazards</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dust_devil&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Hazards">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Dust devils have been implicated in around 100 aircraft accidents.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> While many incidents have been simple taxiing problems, a few have had fatal consequences. Dust devils are also considered major hazards among <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Skydiving" title="Skydiving">skydivers</a> as they can cause a <a href="/wiki/Parachute" title="Parachute">parachute</a> to collapse with little to no warning, at altitudes considered too low to <a href="/wiki/Cut-away" title="Cut-away">cut away</a>, and contribute the serious injury or death of many parachutists.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Health">Health</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dust_devil&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Health">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>There is an endemic health issue in some arid areas, such as the <a href="/wiki/Southwestern_United_States" title="Southwestern United States">southwestern United States</a>, <a href="/wiki/California" title="California">California</a>, northwestern Mexico, <a href="/wiki/Central_America" title="Central America">Central America</a>, and <a href="/wiki/South_America" title="South America">South America</a>, called <a href="/wiki/Coccidioidomycosis" title="Coccidioidomycosis">Valley fever</a>. “Cocci” fungus grows naturally in alkaline soil and the fungus spores, that lie dormant and can be picked up by dust devils, are blown around. When a person breathes these spores in, they cause <a href="/wiki/Fungal_pneumonia" title="Fungal pneumonia">fungal pneumonia</a>. While usually not a serious threat, the disease is still dangerous to some people.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Electrical_activities">Electrical activities</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dust_devil&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Electrical activities">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Dust devils, even small ones (on Earth), can produce radio noise and electrical fields greater than 10,000 volts per meter.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> A dust devil picks up small dirt and dust particles. As the particles whirl around, they bump and scrape into each other and become electrically charged. The whirling charged particles also create a magnetic field that fluctuates between 3 and 30 times each second.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>These electrical fields assist the vortices in lifting materials off the ground and into the atmosphere. Field experiments indicate that a dust devil can lift 1 gram of dust per second from each square metre (10 lb/s from each acre) of ground it passes over. A large dust devil measuring about 100 metres (330 ft) across at its base can lift about 15 metric tonnes (17 short tons) of dust into the air in 30 minutes. Giant dust storms that sweep across the world's deserts contribute 8% of the mineral dust in the atmosphere each year during the handful of storms that occur. In comparison, the significantly smaller dust devils that twist across the deserts during the summer lift about three times as much dust, thus having a greater combined impact on the dust content of the atmosphere. When this occurs, they are often called <b>sand pillars</b>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Martian_dust_devils">Martian dust devils</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dust_devil&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Martian dust devils">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Dust devils also occur on <a href="/wiki/Mars" title="Mars">Mars</a> (see <a href="/wiki/Dust_devil_tracks" title="Dust devil tracks">dust devil tracks</a>) and were first photographed by the <a href="/wiki/Viking_program" title="Viking program">Viking</a> orbiters in the 1970s. In 1997, the <a href="/wiki/Mars_Pathfinder" title="Mars Pathfinder">Mars Pathfinder</a> lander detected a dust devil passing over it.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> In the image shown here, photographed by the Mars Global Surveyor, the long dark streak is formed by a moving swirling column of Martian atmosphere. The dust devil itself (the black spot) is climbing the crater wall. The streaks on the right are sand dunes on the crater floor.</p>
<p>Martian dust devils can be up to fifty times as wide and ten times as high as terrestrial dust devils, and large ones may pose a threat to terrestrial technology sent to Mars.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p>Mission members monitoring the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spirit_rover" title="Spirit rover">Spirit rover</a> on Mars reported on March 12, 2005, that a lucky encounter with a dust devil had cleaned the solar panels of that robot. Power levels dramatically increased and daily science work was anticipated to be expanded.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> A similar phenomenon (solar panels mysteriously cleaned of accumulated dust) had previously been observed with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Opportunity_rover" title="Opportunity rover">Opportunity rover</a>, and dust devils had also been suspected as the cause.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p>

<p>A <a href="/wiki/Fire_whirl" title="Fire whirl">fire whirl</a> or swirl, sometimes called fire devils or fire tornadoes, can be seen during intense fires in combustible building structures or, more commonly, in forest or bush fires. A fire whirl is a vortex-shaped formation of burning gases being released from the combustible material. The genesis of the vortex is probably similar to that of a dust devil. As distinct from the dust devil, it is improbable that the height reached by the fire gas vortex is greater than the visible height of the vertical flames because of turbulence in the surrounding gases that inhibit creation of a stable boundary layer between the rotating/rising gases relative to the surrounding gases.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<p>Hot cinders underneath freshly deposited ash in recently burned areas may sometimes generate numerous dust devils. The lighter weight and the darker color of the ash may create dust devils that are visible hundreds of feet into the air.</p>
<p>Ash devils form similar to dust devils and are often seen on unstable days in burn scar areas of recent fires.</p>

</div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Steam_devil" title="Steam devil">Steam devils</a> are phenomena often observed in the steam rising from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Power_plant" title="Power plant">power plants</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Handy_Weather_Answer_Book_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-Handy_Weather_Answer_Book-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<p>The same conditions can produce a snow whirlwind, although differential heating is more difficult in snow-covered areas.</p>
<p>Coal devils are common at the coal town of Tsagaan Khad in <a href="/wiki/%C3%96mn%C3%B6govi_Province" title="Ömnögovi Province">South Gobi Province</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mongolia" title="Mongolia">Mongolia</a>. They occur when dust devils pick up large amounts of stockpiled coal. Their dark color makes them resemble some tornados.</p>
