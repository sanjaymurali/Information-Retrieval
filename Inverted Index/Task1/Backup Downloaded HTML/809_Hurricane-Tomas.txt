<p><b>Hurricane Tomas</b> was the latest recorded <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone" title="Tropical cyclone">tropical cyclone</a> on a calendar year to strike the <a href="/wiki/Windward_Islands" title="Windward Islands">Windward Islands</a>. The nineteenth named storm and twelfth hurricane of the <a href="/wiki/2010_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="2010 Atlantic hurricane season">2010 Atlantic hurricane season</a>, Tomas developed from a <a href="/wiki/Tropical_wave" title="Tropical wave">tropical wave</a> east of the Windward Islands on October 29. Quickly intensifying into a hurricane, it moved through the <a href="/wiki/Windward_Islands" title="Windward Islands">Windward Islands</a> and passed over <a href="/wiki/Saint_Lucia" title="Saint Lucia">Saint Lucia</a>. After reaching Category 2 status on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saffir-Simpson_Hurricane_Scale" title="Saffir-Simpson Hurricane Scale">Saffir-Simpson scale</a>, Tomas quickly weakened to a tropical storm in the central <a href="/wiki/Caribbean_Sea" title="Caribbean Sea">Caribbean Sea</a>, due to strong wind shear and dry air. Tomas later regained hurricane status as it reorganized near the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Windward_passage" title="Windward passage">Windward passage</a>.</p>
<p>Throughout the hurricane's path, 71 people are known to have been killed, 14 of whom were in Saint Lucia. Monetary losses throughout the Windward Islands were estimated at US$588 million, mainly in Saint Lucia. In the wake of the storm in <a href="/wiki/Haiti" title="Haiti">Haiti</a>, flooding intensified an <a href="/wiki/2010_Haiti_cholera_outbreak" title="2010 Haiti cholera outbreak">ongoing cholera outbreak</a> indirectly causing more fatalities. However, direct impacts from the hurricane in Haiti were less than anticipated. Overall damage from the storm was at least $741 million.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Meteorological_history">Meteorological history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hurricane_Tomas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Meteorological history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Tomas_Atlantic_2010_track.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="978" data-file-width="1024" height="210" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0a/Tomas_Atlantic_2010_track.png/220px-Tomas_Atlantic_2010_track.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0a/Tomas_Atlantic_2010_track.png/330px-Tomas_Atlantic_2010_track.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0a/Tomas_Atlantic_2010_track.png/440px-Tomas_Atlantic_2010_track.png 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Tomas_Atlantic_2010_track.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Map plotting the track and intensity of the storm, according to the <a href="/wiki/Saffir%E2%80%93Simpson_scale" title="Saffir–Simpson scale">Saffir–Simpson scale</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>A <a href="/wiki/Tropical_wave" title="Tropical wave">tropical wave</a>, or elongated <a href="/wiki/Low-pressure_area" title="Low-pressure area">low-pressure area</a>, exited the western coast of Africa on October 24.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TCR_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-TCR-1">[1]</a></sup> It moved hastily westward across the <a href="/wiki/Tropical_Atlantic" title="Tropical Atlantic">tropical Atlantic</a> at a comparatively low latitude, becoming embedded within the <a href="/wiki/Intertropical_Convergence_Zone" title="Intertropical Convergence Zone">Intertropical Convergence Zone</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TWD-25_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-TWD-25-3">[3]</a></sup> During this time the wave's structure retained a vigorous appearance;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TCR_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-TCR-1">[1]</a></sup> the western portion contained scattered <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Convection_(meteorology)" title="Convection (meteorology)">convection</a>, with strong thunderstorms, as well as a broad area of rotating winds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TWD-25_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-TWD-25-3">[3]</a></sup> On October 27, when the system was about 1,200 miles (1,940 km) east-southeast of the <a href="/wiki/Windward_Islands" title="Windward Islands">Windward Islands</a>, the <a href="/wiki/National_Hurricane_Center" title="National Hurricane Center">National Hurricane Center</a> (NHC) noted favorable atmospheric conditions for <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclogenesis" title="Tropical cyclogenesis">tropical cyclogenesis</a>, namely light <a href="/wiki/Wind_shear" title="Wind shear">wind shear</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> The wave and its expansive convection amplified over the next two days, featuring hints of spiraling <a href="/wiki/Rainband" title="Rainband">rainbands</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TCR_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-TCR-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> A <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hurricane_Hunters" title="Hurricane Hunters">Hurricane Hunters</a> aircraft investigating the disturbance on October 29 observed a developing circulation with storm-force winds at sea level.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> In light of those features, the NHC estimated that the system became a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tropical_depression" title="Tropical depression">tropical depression</a> by 06:00 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/UTC" title="UTC">UTC</a> that day, about 460 mi (740 km) southeast of <a href="/wiki/Barbados" title="Barbados">Barbados</a>, and further upgraded it to Tropical Storm Tomas six hours later.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TCR_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-TCR-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-disc1_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-disc1-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>On its inception, Tomas turned toward the northwest and decelerated<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TCR_1-4"><a href="#cite_note-TCR-1">[1]</a></sup> within a region of low wind shear and high tropical moisture—two of the prime prerequisites of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rapid_deepening" title="Rapid deepening">rapid intensification</a>. A misalignment between the cyclone's low- and upper-level circulations impeded this strengthening potential, however, and at that time the NHC did not expect Tomas to attain hurricane status until 36 to 48 hours later.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-disc1_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-disc1-7">[7]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone#Environmental_steering" title="Tropical cyclone">Steered</a> along <a href="/wiki/High-pressure_area" title="High-pressure area">high pressure</a> to its north, the storm retraced slightly west-northwestward on its approach to the Windward Islands.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TCR_1-5"><a href="#cite_note-TCR-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-disc4_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-disc4-8">[8]</a></sup> Despite the prior forecasts of moderated strengthening, Tomas' winds sharply increased to 60 mph (95 km/h) by late October 29;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> moreover, the <a href="/wiki/Outflow_(meteorology)" title="Outflow (meteorology)">outflow</a> aloft became well established in all quadrants as the convection consolidated into a prominent rain band.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> The center of the intensifying storm crossed southern <a href="/wiki/Barbados" title="Barbados">Barbados</a> the next morning, around 09:00 UTC.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TCR_1-6"><a href="#cite_note-TCR-1">[1]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Weather_radar" title="Weather radar">Radar images</a> from Martinique soon revealed the formation of an <a href="/wiki/Eye_(cyclone)" title="Eye (cyclone)">eye</a>;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-disc4_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-disc4-8">[8]</a></sup> Tomas attained hurricane status a couple of hours later, about 35 miles (55 km) east of <a href="/wiki/Saint_Vincent_and_the_Grenadines" title="Saint Vincent and the Grenadines">Saint Vincent and the Grenadines</a>, after Hurricane Hunters recorded surface winds of 75 mph (120 km/h). By that time, the eye measured 35–46 mi (56–74 km) in diameter.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Tomas_nov_6_2010.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="7369" data-file-width="4377" height="370" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/78/Tomas_nov_6_2010.jpg/220px-Tomas_nov_6_2010.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/78/Tomas_nov_6_2010.jpg/330px-Tomas_nov_6_2010.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/78/Tomas_nov_6_2010.jpg/440px-Tomas_nov_6_2010.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Tomas_nov_6_2010.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
A weakening Tropical Storm Tomas over the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atlantic" title="Atlantic">Atlantic</a> on November 6</div>

<p>Tomas continued to strengthen through October 30 as it moved generally west- to west-northwestward. Around 20:00 UTC, the eye traversed the northern shores of <a href="/wiki/Saint_Vincent_(Antilles)" title="Saint Vincent (Antilles)">Saint Vincent</a>, while the northernmost ring of the severest winds skirted <a href="/wiki/Saint_Lucia" title="Saint Lucia">Saint Lucia</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TCR_1-7"><a href="#cite_note-TCR-1">[1]</a></sup> Subsequent reports from Hurricane Hunters and post-storm reanalysis indicated that Tomas had reached a peak intensity with wind speeds of 100 mph (155 km/h) and a minimum pressure of 982 <a href="/wiki/Bar_(unit)" title="Bar (unit)">mbar</a> (<a href="/wiki/Pascal_(unit)" title="Pascal (unit)">hPa</a>; 29.00 <a href="/wiki/Inch_of_mercury" title="Inch of mercury">inHg</a>) during its trek across the islands, making it a Category 2 on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saffir-Simpson_Hurricane_Scale" title="Saffir-Simpson Hurricane Scale">Saffir–Simpson hurricane scale</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TCR_1-8"><a href="#cite_note-TCR-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Michael_Brennan_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-Michael_Brennan-12">[12]</a></sup> Although the NHC noted the potential for Tomas to become a <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone_scales#Atlantic" title="Tropical cyclone scales">major hurricane</a>, the agency conversely observed that the hurricane had begun to suffer from southwesterly wind shear, which computer models forecast to increase in the long term.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Michael_Brennan_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-Michael_Brennan-12">[12]</a></sup> On October 31, a combination of this shear and pockets of dry air dispersed the convection surrounding the eye, initiating a weakening trend.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> Tomas diminished to a tropical storm early on November 1, when the strongest thunderstorms became dislocated from the low-level wind vortex by more than 115 mi (185 km).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TCR_1-9"><a href="#cite_note-TCR-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> Tomas meandered slightly south of west for a while, passing north of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/ABC_Islands_(Lesser_Antilles)" title="ABC Islands (Lesser Antilles)">ABC Islands</a> overnight.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TCR_1-10"><a href="#cite_note-TCR-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-disc18_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-disc18-15">[15]</a></sup> Although the upper wind regime relaxed into the next day, with an area of deep convection briefly reblossoming near Tomas' center,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-disc18_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-disc18-15">[15]</a></sup> the shear appeared to have already taken its toll on the storm. With an increasingly diffuse and elongated structure, Tomas further weakened to a tropical depression at 00:00 UTC, November 3, about 325 miles (520 km) southeast of <a href="/wiki/Kingston,_Jamaica" title="Kingston, Jamaica">Kingston, Jamaica</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TCR_1-11"><a href="#cite_note-TCR-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-disc21_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-disc21-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>Following its deterioration, Tomas diverged toward the northwest over the central Caribbean.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TCR_1-12"><a href="#cite_note-TCR-1">[1]</a></sup> The circulation lost further definition, adjoining a wide stretch of low pressures spanning the western Caribbean. A Hurricane Hunters mission to determine whether Tomas retained its status as a tropical cyclone found that the mean low-level circulation had reformed to the northeast of the previous center. Tomas reattained tropical storm strength late on November 3,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> and although the low- and mid-level centers were initially nonaligned, slow restrengthening ensued into the next day.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> Tomas turned northward ahead of an unseasonably strong deep-layered trough, circumnavigating the western periphery of the subtropical ridge.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TCR_1-13"><a href="#cite_note-TCR-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> Over a six-hour period, the cyclone's core thunderstorm activity became much more concentrated, with a corresponding quick drop in central pressure.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> While turning northeastward into the <a href="/wiki/Windward_Passage" title="Windward Passage">Windward Passage</a>, Tomas regained hurricane status around 0600 UTC on November 5, just 36 miles (58 km) west-southwest of the western tip of <a href="/wiki/Haiti" title="Haiti">Haiti</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TCR_1-14"><a href="#cite_note-TCR-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p>Over the course of November 5, the hurricane accelerated northeastward in response to the approaching trough, passing right between eastern Cuba and western Haiti. Due to its proximity to land, the convection became disrupted near the center;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-disc30_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-disc30-23">[23]</a></sup> Tomas reweakened to a tropical storm near the <a href="/wiki/Turks_and_Caicos_Islands" title="Turks and Caicos Islands">Turks and Caicos Islands</a> during a spell of moderate shear on November 6.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TCR_1-15"><a href="#cite_note-TCR-1">[1]</a></sup> After moving through that island nation, Tomas briefly became a hurricane for a final time before hostile wind shear from the nearby trough fully began to take effect. In conjunction with increasingly dry air, the cyclone steadily weakened to a tropical storm on November 7. Tomas lost its tropical characteristics the next day, though it retained gale-force winds as an <a href="/wiki/Extratropical_cyclone" title="Extratropical cyclone">extratropical cyclone</a>. It turned sharply eastward before curving northward, accelerated by a broad cyclonic steering flow over the western Atlantic the next couple of days. The Thomas' extratropical remnant subsequently raced off into the north Atlantic, until it was absorbed by another extratropical system a few hundred miles south of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Newfoundland" title="Newfoundland">Newfoundland</a>, Canada, on November 11.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TCR_1-16"><a href="#cite_note-TCR-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Preparations">Preparations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hurricane_Tomas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Preparations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Prior to the development of Tomas, the NHC noted the potential for heavy rainfall and strong wind gusts to spread across the <a href="/wiki/Windward_Islands" title="Windward Islands">Windward Islands</a>, Venezuela, and northern <a href="/wiki/Guyana" title="Guyana">Guyana</a>, due to the tropical wave of which later developed into Hurricane Tomas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> Upon development of Tropical Storm Tomas, most of the <a href="/wiki/Windward_Islands" title="Windward Islands">Windward Islands</a> were put under a <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone_warnings_and_watches" title="Tropical cyclone warnings and watches">tropical storm warning</a>, issued by their respective Governments; a tropical storm watch was also issued for <a href="/wiki/Dominica" title="Dominica">Dominica</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> Six hours later as Tomas was nearly a hurricane, the tropical storm warning was replaced by a hurricane warning in <a href="/wiki/Barbados" title="Barbados">Barbados</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/St._Vincent_and_the_Grenadines" title="St. Vincent and the Grenadines">St. Vincent and the Grenadines</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/St._Lucia" title="St. Lucia">St. Lucia</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Martinique" title="Martinique">Martinique</a>; the tropical storm watch in Dominica was also upgraded to a tropical storm warning.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<p>In Barbados, people evacuated to schools and shelters for safety during the storm.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1030afp_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-1030afp-27">[27]</a></sup> On St. Lucia, the government closed the island's two airports and ordered the closure of all businesses. Additionally, a large <a href="/wiki/Creole_peoples" title="Creole peoples">Creole</a> festival was canceled due to the storm.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ap1030_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-ap1030-28">[28]</a></sup> Officials in Trinidad and Tobago closed beaches across the country.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> In St. Vincent, the National Emergency Management Organization (NEMO) issued advisories that a tropical system was approaching the country. The rapid intensification of Tomas caught many citizens in St. Vincent off guard. Early on October 30, the Prime Minister addressed the nation and informed the country of the situation it was facing.</p>
<p>Following the west-southwesterly motion of the storm, swells generated by Tomas produced breaking waves on coastal sections of the <a href="/wiki/ABC_islands_(Lesser_Antilles)" title="ABC islands (Lesser Antilles)">ABC islands</a> during the morning of November 1. Consequently, a <a href="/wiki/Small_craft_advisory" title="Small craft advisory">small craft advisory</a> was issued at 1415 <a href="/wiki/Coordinated_Universal_Time" title="Coordinated Universal Time">UTC</a> for all three islands.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> In addition, a state of severe weather was declared for <a href="/wiki/Bonaire" title="Bonaire">Bonaire</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cura%C3%A7ao" title="Curaçao">Curaçao</a> during the passage of Tomas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> In <a href="/wiki/Haiti" title="Haiti">Haiti</a>, the country which was devastated earlier in the year from a deadly <a href="/wiki/2010_Haiti_earthquake" title="2010 Haiti earthquake">January 12 earthquake</a>, government officials began preparing for possible impact from Tomas by October 30. One United Nations spokesperson remarked that a hurricane strike would be "the last thing Haiti needs".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1030afp_27-1"><a href="#cite_note-1030afp-27">[27]</a></sup> The Haitian government issued an orange storm alert, one level short of the highest state of alert, and warned for the possibility of winds, thunderstorms, and flooding.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ap1030_28-1"><a href="#cite_note-ap1030-28">[28]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Barbados_and_the_Windward_Islands">Barbados and the Windward Islands</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hurricane_Tomas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Barbados and the Windward Islands">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Hurricane_Tomas_Saint_Lucia_flooding.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="479" data-file-width="639" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/59/Hurricane_Tomas_Saint_Lucia_flooding.jpg/220px-Hurricane_Tomas_Saint_Lucia_flooding.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/59/Hurricane_Tomas_Saint_Lucia_flooding.jpg/330px-Hurricane_Tomas_Saint_Lucia_flooding.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/59/Hurricane_Tomas_Saint_Lucia_flooding.jpg/440px-Hurricane_Tomas_Saint_Lucia_flooding.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Hurricane_Tomas_Saint_Lucia_flooding.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Flood damage along the <a href="/wiki/Cul_de_Sac_River" title="Cul de Sac River">Cul de Sac River</a> in Saint Lucia</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>As Tomas passed 20 miles (32 km) to the south of Barbados, it produced a wind gust of 63 mph (100 km/h), which damaged homes and power lines on the island.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> There were also reports of blown off roofs, impassable roads and uprooted trees.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> Later, a station on <a href="/wiki/Saint_Lucia" title="Saint Lucia">Saint Lucia</a> recorded sustained winds of 48 mph (77 km/h), with gusts to 69 mph (110 km/h).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-public5a_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-public5a-36">[36]</a></sup> There was widespread damage to homes and power lines.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TomasAdv6_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-TomasAdv6-37">[37]</a></sup> The winds destroyed the roof of a hospital and a school, with several trees and power lines blown down.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ap1030_28-2"><a href="#cite_note-ap1030-28">[28]</a></sup> Additionally, a station on <a href="/wiki/Martinique" title="Martinique">Martinique</a> reported sustained winds of 72 mph (115 km/h), with gusts to 108 mph (173 km/h).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup></p>
<p>On <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saint_Vincent_(island)" title="Saint Vincent (island)">Saint Vincent</a>, there were no deaths but two persons sustained serious injuries while trying to effect repairs to house roofs and two persons were reported missing. The two persons reported missing were found on November 1, off the island of Balliceaux. The National Emergency Management Organization (NEMO) declared all areas from Park Hill to Owia on the eastern side and all areas from Belle Isle to Fitz Hughes on the western side disaster zones. It was also reported that the agriculture sector sustained over US$25 million(EC$67 million) worth of damage. Over 1200 people were forced to seek refuge in hurricane shelters across St. Vincent. About 600 houses lost their roofs. A lot of downed power lines, trees and landslides made some roads impassable but NEMO, the Bridges Roads and General Services Authority (BRAGSA) and the St. Vincent Electricity Services Company (VINLEC) were able to clear the main road by the November 1.</p>
<p>Saint Lucia arguably had sustained the worst damage from the storm overall. Throughout Saint Lucia, severe flooding and mudslides resulted in at least 7 fatalities confirmed by the Chief Medical Officer. According to a government minister, several other people were missing and buried in landslides.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> By the morning of November 2, two more fatalities were confirmed on the island.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_Caribbean_Islands">Other Caribbean Islands</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hurricane_Tomas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Other Caribbean Islands">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Tomas_nov_4_2010_1445Z.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1080" data-file-width="1920" height="124" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/06/Tomas_nov_4_2010_1445Z.jpg/220px-Tomas_nov_4_2010_1445Z.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/06/Tomas_nov_4_2010_1445Z.jpg/330px-Tomas_nov_4_2010_1445Z.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/06/Tomas_nov_4_2010_1445Z.jpg/440px-Tomas_nov_4_2010_1445Z.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Tomas_nov_4_2010_1445Z.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Tropical Storm Tomas reorganizing over the Caribbean Sea on November 4</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>While passing the Windward Islands into the Caribbean Sea, Tomas produced strong winds and heavy rainfall on <a href="/wiki/Isla_Aves" title="Isla Aves">Isla Aves</a>, located off the northern coast of Venezuela.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1030afp_27-2"><a href="#cite_note-1030afp-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<p>Although the storm did not directly strike the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/ABC_Islands_(Lesser_Antilles)" title="ABC Islands (Lesser Antilles)">ABC islands</a>, one of its outer rainbands stalled over the region and intensified during the night of November 1 to November 2. <a href="/wiki/Cura%C3%A7ao" title="Curaçao">Curaçao</a> experienced its most extreme rain event in 40 years; as many as 10.4 inches (265 mm) were recorded over a 24-hour period in the eastern part of the island.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-curacao_41-0"><a href="#cite_note-curacao-41">[41]</a></sup> The majority of the rain fell overnight in a heavy downpour, accompanied by a severe thunderstorm that triggered large-scale power, TV and radio outages.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-effects_42-0"><a href="#cite_note-effects-42">[42]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> Lightning strikes sparked three large fires in a major oil refinery in <a href="/wiki/Willemstad" title="Willemstad">Willemstad</a>. The fires inflicted severe damage to several tanks, estimated at US$10 million.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> Flights from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hato_International_Airport" title="Hato International Airport">Curaçao International Airport</a> were delayed due to the hazardous conditions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup></p>
<p>Following hours of heavy rainfall, widespread floods made most roads in the region impassable, with dozens of cars swept away or stranded.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-effects_42-1"><a href="#cite_note-effects-42">[42]</a></sup> The rains filled dams and overwhelmed drains, causing them to overflow and exacerbating the flooding.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup> The towns of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sali%C3%B1a_(Cura%C3%A7ao)" title="Saliña (Curaçao)">Saliña</a>, <a href="/wiki/Brievengat" title="Brievengat">Brievengat</a> and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Mahaai_(Cura%C3%A7ao)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Mahaai (Curaçao) (page does not exist)">Mahaai</a> were among the hardest hit; hundreds of homes, gardens and businesses were inundated.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-effects_42-2"><a href="#cite_note-effects-42">[42]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup> At the height of the rainstorm, a rescue worker assisting in the evacuation of a hospital was killed by the collapse of a wall. An elderly man suffering a heart attack drowned while unable to exit his flooded car.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup> Overall, Curaçao suffered some of its worst flooding in history; insured losses across the island exceeded <a href="/wiki/Netherlands_Antillean_guilder" title="Netherlands Antillean guilder">NAƒ</a>110 million (US$63 million), though total damage costs from Tomas were estimated at NAƒ200 million (US$115 million).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup></p>
<p>Damage on the other two islands was much more limited. Parts of <a href="/wiki/Bonaire" title="Bonaire">Bonaire</a> experienced heavy but brief periods of rain, with a maximum of 3 inches (75 mm) at <a href="/wiki/Flamingo_International_Airport" title="Flamingo International Airport">Flamingo International Airport</a>, causing localized flooding of property. On <a href="/wiki/Aruba" title="Aruba">Aruba</a>, some thunder and moderate rainfall occurred, without significant consequences.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-curacao_41-1"><a href="#cite_note-curacao-41">[41]</a></sup> All schools across the islands were closed on November 2 and 3 as a result of the storm.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Greater_Antilles">Greater Antilles</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hurricane_Tomas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Greater Antilles">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">

<p>After days of anticipation, Tomas arrived just offshore in Haiti on the morning of November 5. The storm intensified while brushing the disaster-weary nation, reattaining hurricane strength near the westernmost tip of Haiti. Torrential rains and tropical storm force winds buffeted the entire region. Within hours of the storm's arrival, flooding began to occur. In earthquake-ravaged <a href="/wiki/Port-au-Prince" title="Port-au-Prince">Port-au-Prince</a>, one of the largest refugee camps set up near the ruins of the capitol building was flooded following heavy rains.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup> By the evening of November 8, 20 people had been confirmed dead, 7 others were listed as missing and more than 30,000 people were in shelters.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup> Health workers also feared damage related to Tomas on the island could exacerbate the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/2010_Haitian_cholera_outbreak" title="2010 Haitian cholera outbreak">ongoing cholera outbreak</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Hurricane_Tomas_2010-11-05_1530Z.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="8400" data-file-width="6400" height="289" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0b/Hurricane_Tomas_2010-11-05_1530Z.jpg/220px-Hurricane_Tomas_2010-11-05_1530Z.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0b/Hurricane_Tomas_2010-11-05_1530Z.jpg/330px-Hurricane_Tomas_2010-11-05_1530Z.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0b/Hurricane_Tomas_2010-11-05_1530Z.jpg/440px-Hurricane_Tomas_2010-11-05_1530Z.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>

</div>
</div>
<p>Tomas passed just to the east of Cuba hours later, but no significant damage was reported on the island.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup> As the outer bands of Hurricane Tomas began to impact Cuba, <a href="/wiki/Aero_Caribbean_Flight_883" title="Aero Caribbean Flight 883">Aero Caribbean Flight 883</a>, an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/ATR-72#ATR_72.E2.80.93210" title="ATR-72">ATR-72-212</a> aircraft, crashed near the town of <a href="/wiki/Guasimal,_Sancti_Sp%C3%ADritus" title="Guasimal, Sancti Spíritus">Guasimal</a> in <a href="/wiki/Sancti_Sp%C3%ADritus" title="Sancti Spíritus">Sancti Spíritus</a> province. All 68 people on board the plane were killed on impact. Although the plane was the last to leave the airport in Santiago de Cuba before it closed due to Tomas, it is unknown what role, if any, the storm played in the crash.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup> Following a review of the crash, it was determined that severe ice accumulation, at an altitude of 20,000 ft (6,100 m), along with errors by the crew caused the incident.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Elsewhere">Elsewhere</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hurricane_Tomas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Elsewhere">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Hurricane Tomas narrowly missed the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Turks_and_Caicos" title="Turks and Caicos">Turks and Caicos</a>, sparing the islands a direct hit and causing only minor flooding.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tcs_60-0"><a href="#cite_note-tcs-60">[60]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Aftermath">Aftermath</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hurricane_Tomas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Aftermath">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Estimates place the cost of damage in Barbados at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Barbados_dollar" title="Barbados dollar">BDS</a>$17 million (~US$8.5 million).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup> Throughout the northern part of St. Vincent, the entire banana crop was lost. Press reports from the Prime Minister revealed that nearly every banana tree had been downed during the storm. The country's banana crop is a major source of income and jobs, accounting for roughly 60% of the workforce and 50% of the merchandise exported.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup></p>
<p>Damage from the storm on Trinidad and Tobago amounted to <a href="/wiki/Trinidad_and_Tobago_dollar" title="Trinidad and Tobago dollar">TT$</a>4 million (US$629,000).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-63"><a href="#cite_note-63">[63]</a></sup> Losses in Barbados reached <a href="/wiki/Barbadian_dollar" title="Barbadian dollar">Bds$</a>17 million (US$8.4 million).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-64"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup> The Government of St. Lucia stated that damage from the storm could exceed US$100 million.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-65"><a href="#cite_note-65">[65]</a></sup> By November 5, the Prime Minister stated that damage from Tomas was roughly EC$1.3 billion ($500 million USD), five times higher than initially stated.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-66"><a href="#cite_note-66">[66]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Retirement">Retirement</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hurricane_Tomas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Retirement">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/List_of_retired_Atlantic_hurricane_names" title="List of retired Atlantic hurricane names">List of retired Atlantic hurricane names</a></div>
<p>Due to the extent of damage across the Caribbean, the name <i>Tomas</i> was retired on March 16, 2011 by the <a href="/wiki/World_Meteorological_Organization" title="World Meteorological Organization">World Meteorological Organization</a> during their 33rd Hurricane Committee, and will never again be used for an Atlantic hurricane. It was replaced with <i>Tobias</i> for the <a href="/wiki/2016_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="2016 Atlantic hurricane season">2016 hurricane season.</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-67"><a href="#cite_note-67">[67]</a></sup> However, the name was unused in 2016 season, so it may be used in the 2022 season.</p>
