<p><b>Thundersnow</b>, also known as a <b>winter thunderstorm</b> or a <b>thundersnowstorm</b>, is an unusual<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> kind of <a href="/wiki/Thunderstorm" title="Thunderstorm">thunderstorm</a> with <a href="/wiki/Snow" title="Snow">snow</a> falling as the primary <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Precipitation_(meteorology)" title="Precipitation (meteorology)">precipitation</a> instead of <a href="/wiki/Rain" title="Rain">rain</a>. It typically falls in regions of strong upward motion within the cold sector of an <a href="/wiki/Extratropical_cyclone" title="Extratropical cyclone">extratropical cyclone</a>. Thermodynamically, it is not different from any other type of thunderstorm, but the top of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cumulonimbus" title="Cumulonimbus">cumulonimbus cloud</a> is usually quite low. In addition to snow, <a href="/wiki/Graupel" title="Graupel">graupel</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Hail" title="Hail">hail</a> may fall.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Occurrence">Occurrence</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Thundersnow&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Occurrence">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Thundersnow, while relatively rare anywhere, is more common with <a href="/wiki/Lake-effect_snow" title="Lake-effect snow">lake-effect snow</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Great_Lakes" title="Great Lakes">Great Lakes</a> area of the United States and Canada, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Midwest" title="Midwest">midwestern</a> United States, and the <a href="/wiki/Great_Salt_Lake" title="Great Salt Lake">Great Salt Lake</a>. Thundersnow also occurs in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Halifax_Regional_Municipality" title="Halifax Regional Municipality">Halifax</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nova_Scotia" title="Nova Scotia">Nova Scotia</a>, sometimes several times per winter season. <a href="/wiki/Bozeman,_Montana" title="Bozeman, Montana">Bozeman, Montana</a> also sees thundersnow more often than average with these storms typically occurring in April or May. In the United States, March is the peak month of formation; on average, only 6.4 events are reported per year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>The British Isles and other parts of northwestern Europe occasionally report thunder and lightning during sleet or (usually wet) snow showers during winter and spring. It is also common around <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kanazawa,_Ishikawa" title="Kanazawa, Ishikawa">Kanazawa</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Sea_of_Japan" title="Sea of Japan">Sea of Japan</a>, and even around <a href="/wiki/Mount_Everest" title="Mount Everest">Mount Everest</a>. Low pressure events in the eastern <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mediterranean" title="Mediterranean">Mediterranean</a> that originate from polar origin cause copious thundersnow occurrences during winter storms, especially over the elevated provinces of <a href="/wiki/Israel" title="Israel">Israel</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jordan" title="Jordan">Jordan</a>, including <a href="/wiki/Amman" title="Amman">Amman</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jerusalem" title="Jerusalem">Jerusalem</a>. When such storms happen at areas intended for skiing, the mountains are often evacuated for safety.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Acoustic_effects">Acoustic effects</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Thundersnow&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Acoustic effects">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>One unique aspect of thundersnow is that the suspended and deposited snowfall act as an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dampening" title="Dampening">acoustic suppressor</a> of the sound of thunder. The thunder from a typical thunderstorm can be heard many miles away, while the thunder from thundersnow can usually only be heard within a 2–3-mile (3.2–4.8 km) radius from the lightning.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Formation">Formation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Thundersnow&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Formation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>There are usually three causes of thundersnow:</p>
<ul>
<li>A normal <a href="/wiki/Thunderstorm" title="Thunderstorm">thunderstorm</a> on the leading edge of a <a href="/wiki/Weather_front" title="Weather front">cold front</a> or <a href="/wiki/Warm_front" title="Warm front">warm front</a> that can either form in a winter environment, or that runs into cool air, and where the precipitation takes the form of snow</li>
<li>A heavy <a href="/wiki/Synoptic_scale_meteorology" title="Synoptic scale meteorology">synoptic</a> snowstorm that sustains strong vertical mixing which allows for favorable conditions for <a href="/wiki/Lightning" title="Lightning">lightning</a> and <a href="/wiki/Thunder" title="Thunder">thunder</a> to occur</li>
<li>A <a href="/wiki/Lake-effect_snow" title="Lake-effect snow">lake effect</a> or ocean effect thunderstorm which is produced by cold air passing over relatively warm water; this effect commonly produces <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Snow_squall" title="Snow squall">snow squalls</a> over the <a href="/wiki/Great_Lakes" title="Great Lakes">Great Lakes</a></li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Lake_effect_precipitation">Lake effect precipitation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Thundersnow&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Lake effect precipitation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:October_12-13_radarloop_kbuf.gif"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="775" data-file-width="820" height="208" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/98/October_12-13_radarloop_kbuf.gif/220px-October_12-13_radarloop_kbuf.gif" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/98/October_12-13_radarloop_kbuf.gif/330px-October_12-13_radarloop_kbuf.gif 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/98/October_12-13_radarloop_kbuf.gif/440px-October_12-13_radarloop_kbuf.gif 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:October_12-13_radarloop_kbuf.gif" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
A large squall producing heavy snow and frequent lightning over Buffalo, NY.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Lake effect thundersnow occurs after a cold front or <a href="/wiki/Shortwave_(meteorology)" title="Shortwave (meteorology)">shortwave</a> aloft passes over a body of water. This steepens the thermal <a href="/wiki/Lapse_rate" title="Lapse rate">lapse rates</a> between the lake temperature and the temperatures aloft. A difference in temperature of 25 °C (45 °F) or more between the lake temperature and the temperature at about 1,500 m (4,900 ft) (the 850 hPa level) usually marks the onset of thundersnow, if surface temperatures are expected to be below freezing. However several factors, including other geographical elements, affect the development of thundersnow.</p>
<p>The primary factor is convective depth. This is the vertical depth in the <a href="/wiki/Troposphere" title="Troposphere">troposphere</a> that a parcel of air will rise from the ground before it reaches the equilibrium (EQL) level and stops rising. A minimum depth of 1,500 m (4,900 ft) is necessary, and an average depth of 3,000 m (9,800 ft) or more is generally accepted as sufficient. <a href="/wiki/Wind_shear" title="Wind shear">Wind shear</a> is also a significant factor. Linear snow squall bands produce more thundersnow than clustered bands; thus a directional wind shear with a change of less than 12 °C (54 °F) between the ground and 2,000 m (6,600 ft) in height must be in place. However, any change in direction greater than 12 °C (54 °F) through that layer will tear the snow squall apart. A bare minimum <a href="/wiki/Fetch_(geography)" title="Fetch (geography)">fetch</a> of 50 km/h (31 mph) is required so that the air passing over the lake or ocean water will become sufficiently saturated with moisture and will acquire thermal energy from the water.</p>
<p>The last component is the echo top or storm top temperature. This must be at least −30 °C (−22 °F). It is generally accepted that at this temperature there is no longer any <a href="/wiki/Supercooling" title="Supercooling">super cooled</a> water vapor present in a cloud, but just ice crystals suspended in the air. This allows for the interaction of the ice cloud and graupel pellets within the storm to generate a charge, resulting in lightning and thunder.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Synoptic_forcing">Synoptic forcing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Thundersnow&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Synoptic forcing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Synoptic snow storms tend to be large and complex, with many possible factors affecting the development of thundersnow. The best location in a storm to find thundersnow is typically in its <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/NorthWest" title="NorthWest">NorthWest</a> <a href="/wiki/Quadrant_(plane_geometry)" title="Quadrant (plane geometry)">quadrant</a> (in the <a href="/wiki/Northern_Hemisphere" title="Northern Hemisphere">Northern Hemisphere</a>, based on observations in the <a href="/wiki/Midwestern_United_States" title="Midwestern United States">Midwestern United States</a>), within what is known as the "comma head" of a mature <a href="/wiki/Extratropical_cyclone" title="Extratropical cyclone">extratropical cyclone</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rauber2014_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Rauber2014-6">[6]</a></sup> Thundersnow can also be located underneath the <a href="/wiki/Occluded_front" title="Occluded front">TROWAL</a>, a trough of warm air aloft which shows up in a <a href="/wiki/Surface_weather_analysis" title="Surface weather analysis">surface weather analysis</a> as an inverted trough extending backward into the cold sector from the main cyclone.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> In extreme cases, thunderstorms along the cold front are transported towards the center of the low pressure system and will have their precipitation change to snow or ice, once the cold front becomes a portion of the occluded front.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rauber2014_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-Rauber2014-6">[6]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/1991_Halloween_blizzard" title="1991 Halloween blizzard">1991 Halloween blizzard</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Superstorm_of_1993" title="Superstorm of 1993">Superstorm of 1993</a>, and <a href="/wiki/White_Juan" title="White Juan">White Juan</a> are examples of such blizzards featuring thundersnow.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Upslope_flow">Upslope flow</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Thundersnow&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Upslope flow">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Similar to the lake effect regime, thundersnow is usually witnessed in terrain in the cold sector of an <a href="/wiki/Extratropical_cyclone" title="Extratropical cyclone">extratropical cyclone</a> when a shortwave aloft moves into the region. The shortwave will steepen the local lapse rates, allowing for a greater possibility of both heavy snow at elevations where it is near or below freezing, and occasionally thundersnow.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Hazards">Hazards</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Thundersnow&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Hazards">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Thundersnow produces heavy snowfall rates in the range of 5 to 10 cm (2 to 4 in) per hour. Snowfall of this intensity may <a href="/wiki/Whiteout_(weather)" title="Whiteout (weather)">limit visibilities severely</a>, even during light wind conditions. However, thundersnow is often a part of a severe <a href="/wiki/Winter_storm" title="Winter storm">winter storm</a> or <a href="/wiki/Blizzard" title="Blizzard">blizzard</a>. Winds of above <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tropical_storm" title="Tropical storm">tropical storm</a> <a href="/wiki/Beaufort_scale" title="Beaufort scale">force</a> are frequent with thundersnow. As a result, visibilities in thundersnow are frequently under 400 m (<span class="frac nowrap"><sup>1</sup>⁄<sub>4</sub></span> mi). Additionally, such wind creates extreme <a href="/wiki/Wind_chill" title="Wind chill">wind chills</a> and may result in <a href="/wiki/Frostbite" title="Frostbite">frostbite</a>. Finally, there is a greater likelihood that thundersnow lightning will have a <a href="/wiki/Lightning#Positive_lightning" title="Lightning">positive</a> <a href="/wiki/Electrical_polarity" title="Electrical polarity">polarity</a> which has greater destructive potential than negatively charged (typical) lightning.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GHCC_primer2_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-GHCC_primer2-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
