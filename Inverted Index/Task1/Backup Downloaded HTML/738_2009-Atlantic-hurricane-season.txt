<p>The <b>2009 Atlantic hurricane season</b> was a below-average <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="Atlantic hurricane season">Atlantic hurricane season</a> that produced eleven <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone" title="Tropical cyclone">tropical cyclones</a>, nine <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone_naming" title="Tropical cyclone naming">named storms</a>, three hurricanes, and two <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Major_hurricane" title="Major hurricane">major hurricanes</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Seasonend_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Seasonend-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[nb 1]</a></sup> It officially began on June 1 and ended on November 30, dates that conventionally delimit the period of each year when most tropical cyclones develop in the Atlantic basin.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[3]</a></sup> The season's first tropical cyclone, <a href="/wiki/Tropical_Depression_One_(2009)" title="Tropical Depression One (2009)">Tropical Depression One</a>, developed on May 28,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OneTCR_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-OneTCR-5">[4]</a></sup> while the final storm, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hurricane_Ida_(2009)" title="Hurricane Ida (2009)">Hurricane Ida</a>, dissipated on November 10.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IdaTCR_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-IdaTCR-6">[5]</a></sup> The most intense hurricane, <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Bill_(2009)" title="Hurricane Bill (2009)">Bill</a>, was a powerful <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cape_Verde-type_hurricane" title="Cape Verde-type hurricane">Cape Verde-type hurricane</a> that affected areas from the <a href="/wiki/Leeward_Islands" title="Leeward Islands">Leeward Islands</a> to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Newfoundland_(Island)" title="Newfoundland (Island)">Newfoundland</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BillTCR_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-BillTCR-7">[6]</a></sup> The season featured the lowest number of tropical cyclones since the <a href="/wiki/1997_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="1997 Atlantic hurricane season">1997 season</a>, and only one system, <a href="/wiki/Tropical_Storm_Claudette_(2009)" title="Tropical Storm Claudette (2009)">Claudette</a>, made <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Landfall_(meteorology)" title="Landfall (meteorology)">landfall</a> in the United States. Forming from the interaction of a <a href="/wiki/Tropical_wave" title="Tropical wave">tropical wave</a> and an <a href="/wiki/Low-pressure_area" title="Low-pressure area">upper level low</a>, Claudette made landfall on the <a href="/wiki/Florida_Panhandle" title="Florida Panhandle">Florida Panhandle</a> with <a href="/wiki/Maximum_sustained_wind" title="Maximum sustained wind">maximum sustained winds</a> of 45 mph (75 km/h) before quickly dissipating over Alabama. The storm killed two people and caused $228,000 (2009 <a href="/wiki/United_States_dollar" title="United States dollar">USD</a>) in damage.</p>
<p>Pre-season forecasts issued by <a href="/wiki/Colorado_State_University" title="Colorado State University">Colorado State University (CSU)</a> called for fourteen named storms and seven hurricanes, of which three were expected to attain major hurricane status.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[nb 2]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/National_Oceanic_and_Atmospheric_Administration" title="National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration">National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration (NOAA)</a> later issued its initial forecast, which predicted nine to fourteen named storms, four to seven hurricanes, and one to three major hurricanes. After several revisions in the projected number of named storms, both agencies lowered their forecasts by the middle of the season.</p>
<p>Several storms made landfall or directly affected land outside of the United States. <a href="/wiki/Tropical_Storm_Ana_(2009)" title="Tropical Storm Ana (2009)">Tropical Storm Ana</a> brought substantial rainfall totals to many of the <a href="/wiki/Caribbean_Sea" title="Caribbean Sea">Caribbean</a> islands, including <a href="/wiki/Puerto_Rico" title="Puerto Rico">Puerto Rico</a>, which led to minor street flooding. Hurricane Bill delivered gusty winds and rain to the island of Newfoundland, while <a href="/wiki/Tropical_Storm_Danny_(2009)" title="Tropical Storm Danny (2009)">Tropical Storm Danny</a> affected the U.S. state of North Carolina, and <a href="/wiki/Tropical_Storm_Erika_(2009)" title="Tropical Storm Erika (2009)">Erika</a> affected the Lesser Antilles as a poorly organized tropical system. <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Fred_(2009)" title="Hurricane Fred (2009)">Hurricane Fred</a> affected the <a href="/wiki/Cape_Verde" title="Cape Verde">Cape Verde Islands</a> as a developing tropical cyclone and <a href="/wiki/Tropical_Storm_Grace_(2009)" title="Tropical Storm Grace (2009)">Tropical Storm Grace</a> briefly impacted the <a href="/wiki/Azores" title="Azores">Azores</a>, becoming the farthest northeast forming storm on record. The season's final storm, Ida, affected portions of <a href="/wiki/Central_America" title="Central America">Central America</a> before bringing significant rainfall to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Southeast_United_States" title="Southeast United States">Southeast United States</a> as an <a href="/wiki/Extratropical_cyclone" title="Extratropical cyclone">extratropical cyclone</a>.</p>

<p><a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone_seasonal_forecasting" title="Tropical cyclone seasonal forecasting">Forecasts of hurricane activity</a> are issued before each hurricane season by noted hurricane experts Philip J. Klotzbach, <a href="/wiki/William_M._Gray" title="William M. Gray">William M. Gray</a>, and their associates at <a href="/wiki/Colorado_State_University" title="Colorado State University">Colorado State University</a>; and separately by <a href="/wiki/National_Oceanic_and_Atmospheric_Administration" title="National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration">NOAA</a> forecasters.</p>
<p>Klotzbach's team (formerly led by Gray) defined the average number of storms per season (1950 to 2000) as 9.6 tropical storms, 5.9 hurricanes, 2.3 major hurricanes (storms reaching at least Category 3 strength in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saffir%E2%80%93Simpson_hurricane_scale" title="Saffir–Simpson hurricane scale">Saffir–Simpson hurricane scale</a>) and ACE Index 96.1.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gray_Dec_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Gray_Dec-9">[7]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/National_Oceanic_and_Atmospheric_Administration" title="National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration">NOAA</a> defines a season as above-normal, near-normal or below-normal by a combination of the number of named storms, the number reaching hurricane strength, the number reaching major hurricane strength and <a href="/wiki/Accumulated_cyclone_energy" title="Accumulated cyclone energy">ACE Index</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Pre-season_forecasts">Pre-season forecasts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=2009_Atlantic_hurricane_season&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Pre-season forecasts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On December 10, 2008, Klotzbach's team issued its first extended-range forecast for the 2009 season, predicting above-average activity (14 named storms, 7 hurricanes, 3 of Category 3 or higher and <a href="/wiki/Accumulated_cyclone_energy" title="Accumulated cyclone energy">ACE Index</a> of 125). On April 7, 2009, Klotzbach's team issued an updated forecast for the 2009 season, predicting near-average activity (12 named storms, 6 hurricanes, 2 of Category 3 or higher and <a href="/wiki/Accumulated_cyclone_energy" title="Accumulated cyclone energy">ACE Index</a> of 100), citing the possible cause as the high probability of a weak <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/El_Ni%C3%B1o-Southern_Oscillation" title="El Niño-Southern Oscillation">El Niño</a> forming during the season.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gray_April_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Gray_April-11">[9]</a></sup> On May 21, 2009, <a href="/wiki/National_Oceanic_and_Atmospheric_Administration" title="National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration">NOAA</a> issued their forecast for the season, predicting near or slightly above average activity, (9 to 14 named storms, 4 to 7 hurricanes, and 1 to 3 of Category 3 or higher).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[10]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Midseason_outlooks">Midseason outlooks</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=2009_Atlantic_hurricane_season&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Midseason outlooks">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On June 2, 2009, Klotzbach's team issued another updated forecast for the 2009 season, predicting slightly below average activity (11 named storms, 5 hurricanes, 2 of Category 3 or higher and <a href="/wiki/Accumulated_cyclone_energy" title="Accumulated cyclone energy">ACE Index</a> of 85). Also on June 2, 2009, the Florida State University Center for Ocean-Atmospheric Prediction Studies (FSU COAPS) issued its first ever Atlantic hurricane season forecast. The FSU COAPS forecast predicted 8 named storms, including 4 hurricanes, and an <a href="/wiki/Accumulated_cyclone_energy" title="Accumulated cyclone energy">ACE Index</a> of 65.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[11]</a></sup> On June 18, 2009, the UK <a href="/wiki/Met_Office" title="Met Office">Met Office</a> (<a href="/wiki/Met_Office" title="Met Office">UKMO</a>) issued a forecast of 6 tropical storms in the July to November period with a 70% chance that the number would be in the range 3 to 9. They also predicted an <a href="/wiki/Accumulated_cyclone_energy" title="Accumulated cyclone energy">ACE Index</a> of 60 with a 70% chance that the index would be in the range 40 to 80.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-UKMO_June_forecast_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-UKMO_June_forecast-14">[12]</a></sup> On August 4, 2009, Klotzbach's team updated their forecast for the 2009 season, again predicting slightly below average activity (10 named storms, 4 hurricanes, and 2 major hurricanes). On August 6, 2009, the NOAA also updated their forecast for the 2009 season, predicting below average activity (7–11 named storms, 3–6 hurricanes, and 1–2 major hurricanes).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[13]</a></sup></p>

<p>During the 2009 season, nine of the eleven tropical cyclones affected land, of which five actually made landfall. The United States experienced one of its quietest years, with no hurricanes making landfall in the country. Throughout the basin, six people were killed in tropical cyclone-related incidents and total losses reached roughly $77 million. Most of the damage resulted from Hurricane Bill, which caused severe beach erosion throughout the east coast of the United States. In the United States, tropical cyclones killed six people and caused roughly $50 million in damage. In the Lesser Antilles, Tropical Storms Ana and Erika brought moderate rainfall to several islands but resulted in little damage. Elsewhere in the Atlantic, the Azores Islands, Atlantic Canada, Bermuda, Cape Verde Islands and Wales were affected by tropical cyclones or their remnants.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Seasonend_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Seasonend-1">[1]</a></sup> In Canada, Hurricane Bill produced widespread moderate rainfall in Nova Scotia and Newfoundland, leaving roughly $10 million in losses.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BillDannyCanada_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-BillDannyCanada-16">[14]</a></sup> The hurricane also produced tropical storm-force winds in Bermuda.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BillTCR_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-BillTCR-7">[6]</a></sup> Hurricane Fred briefly impacted the southern Cape Verde Islands as it bypassed the islands early in its existence.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FredTCR_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-FredTCR-17">[15]</a></sup> The Azores and Wales were also affected by Tropical Storm Grace; however, both areas recorded only minor effects.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GraceTCR_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-GraceTCR-18">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>Overall, the season's activity was reflected with a low cumulative <a href="/wiki/Accumulated_cyclone_energy" title="Accumulated cyclone energy">accumulated cyclone energy</a> (ACE) rating of 53, the lowest since <a href="/wiki/1997_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="1997 Atlantic hurricane season">1997</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[17]</a></sup> Due to the low number of storms in the 2009 season, many of which were short-lived, the overall ACE value was ranked as below-average, totaling under 66.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[18]</a></sup> Hurricane Bill was responsible for the ACE value for August being 30% above average.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AugustMWS_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-AugustMWS-21">[19]</a></sup> ACE is, broadly speaking, a measure of the power of the hurricane multiplied by the length of time it existed, so storms that last a long time, as well as particularly strong hurricanes, have high ACEs. ACE is only calculated for full advisories on tropical systems at or exceeding 34 <a href="/wiki/Knot_(unit)" title="Knot (unit)">knots</a> (39 mph, 63 km/h) or tropical storm strength. <a href="/wiki/Subtropical_cyclone" title="Subtropical cyclone">Subtropical cyclones</a> are excluded from the total.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[20]</a></sup></p>

<p>During mid-May, a <a href="/wiki/Weather_front" title="Weather front">frontal boundary</a> stalled near <a href="/wiki/The_Bahamas" title="The Bahamas">The Bahamas</a> and slowly degenerated. On May 25, 2009, an <a href="/wiki/Low-pressure_area" title="Low-pressure area">area of low pressure</a> developed along the tail-end of a decaying <a href="/wiki/Cold_front" title="Cold front">cold front</a> near the northern <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bahamas" title="Bahamas">Bahamas</a>. Tracking northward, this low gradually developed as it moved within 85 mi (140 km) of <a href="/wiki/North_Carolina" title="North Carolina">North Carolina</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Outer_Banks" title="Outer Banks">Outer Banks</a>. By May 28, deep <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_convection" title="Atmospheric convection">convection</a> developed across a small area over the low pressure system, leading to the National Hurricane Center classifying the system as Tropical Depression One. The depression moved over the warm waters of the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_Stream" title="Gulf Stream">Gulf Stream</a> for the following 24 hours, allowing it to maintain its convection, before moving into a hostile environment characterized by strong <a href="/wiki/Wind_shear" title="Wind shear">wind shear</a> and cooler waters. Late on May 29, the system degenerated into a remnant low. Several hours later, on May 30, about 345 mi (555 km) south-southeast of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Halifax_Regional_Municipality" title="Halifax Regional Municipality">Halifax, Nova Scotia</a>, Tropical Depression One was absorbed by a <a href="/wiki/Warm_front" title="Warm front">warm front</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OneTCR_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-OneTCR-5">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>As a tropical cyclone, the depression had no impact on land. However, the precursor to the system brought scattered rainfall and increased winds to parts of the North Carolina coastline, but no damage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OneTCR_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-OneTCR-5">[4]</a></sup> Upon becoming a tropical depression on May 28, Tropical Depression One became the northernmost forming May tropical cyclone in Atlantic history, though subtropical cyclones formed equally far north in 1972 and 2007. It also marked the third consecutive year with pre-season tropical or <a href="/wiki/Subtropical_cyclone" title="Subtropical cyclone">subtropical</a> cyclones in the basin.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OneTCR_5-3"><a href="#cite_note-OneTCR-5">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HURDAT_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-HURDAT-23">[21]</a></sup></p>

<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Tropical_Storm_Ana_(2009)" title="Tropical Storm Ana (2009)">Tropical Storm Ana (2009)</a></div>
<p>Ana formed out of an <a href="/wiki/Low-pressure_area" title="Low-pressure area">area of low pressure</a> associated with a <a href="/wiki/Tropical_wave" title="Tropical wave">tropical wave</a> on August 11, Ana briefly attained tropical storm intensity on August 12 before weakening back to a depression. The following day, the system degenerated into a non-convective remnant low as it tracked westward. On August 14, the depression regenerated roughly 1,075 mi (1,735 km) east of the <a href="/wiki/Leeward_Islands" title="Leeward Islands">Leeward Islands</a>. Early on August 15, the storm re-attained tropical storm status, at which time it was named Ana. After reaching a peak intensity with winds of 40 mph (65 km/h) and a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Barometric_pressure" title="Barometric pressure">barometric pressure</a> of 1003 mbar (hPa; 29.65 inHg), the storm began to weaken again due to increasing wind shear and the unusually fast movement of Ana. In post-storm analysis, it was discovered that Ana had degenerated into a tropical wave once more on August 16, before reaching any landmasses.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AnaTCR_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-AnaTCR-24">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p>Numerous <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone_warnings_and_watches" title="Tropical cyclone warnings and watches">tropical storm watches</a> were issued for the Lesser Antilles, Puerto Rico and the <a href="/wiki/Dominican_Republic" title="Dominican Republic">Dominican Republic</a> between August 15 and 17.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AnaTCR_24-1"><a href="#cite_note-AnaTCR-24">[22]</a></sup> Several islands took minor precautions for the storm, including <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/St._Croix" title="St. Croix">St. Croix</a> which evacuated 40 residents from flood-prone areas ahead of the storm.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[23]</a></sup> In the Dominican Republic, officials took preparations by setting up relief agencies and setting up shelters.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[24]</a></sup> Impact from Ana was minimal, mainly consisting of light to moderate rainfall.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AnaTCR_24-2"><a href="#cite_note-AnaTCR-24">[22]</a></sup> In Puerto Rico, up to 2.76 in (70 mm),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[25]</a></sup> causing street flooding and forcing the evacuation of three schools. High winds associated with the storm also downed trees and power lines, leaving roughly 6,000 residents without power.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[26]</a></sup></p>

<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Bill_(2009)" title="Hurricane Bill (2009)">Hurricane Bill (2009)</a></div>
<p>As Ana regenerated into a tropical depression,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AnaTCR_24-3"><a href="#cite_note-AnaTCR-24">[22]</a></sup> a new tropical depression developed early on August 15 southwest of the Cape Verde Islands. Light wind shear and warm waters allowed the depression to steadily intensify, becoming <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Bill_(2009)" title="Hurricane Bill (2009)">Tropical Storm Bill</a> later that day. By August 17, Bill attained hurricane-status about midway between the Cape Verde Islands and the Lesser Antilles. Eventually the hurricane attained its peak intensity as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saffir%E2%80%93Simpson_Hurricane_Scale#Category_4" title="Saffir–Simpson Hurricane Scale">Category 4 storm</a> roughly 345 mi (555 km) east-northeast of the Leeward Islands. The storm attained maximum winds of 130 mph (215 km/h), the highest of any storm during the season, before weakening slightly as it turned north. The large storm passed roughly 175 mi (280 km) west of <a href="/wiki/Bermuda" title="Bermuda">Bermuda</a> as a Category 2 hurricane. Further weakening took place as Bill brushed the southern coast of <a href="/wiki/Nova_Scotia" title="Nova Scotia">Nova Scotia</a> the following day. Shortly before making landfall in <a href="/wiki/Newfoundland_(island)" title="Newfoundland (island)">Newfoundland</a>, Bill weakened to a tropical storm and accelerated. The storm eventually transitioned into an extratropical cyclone after moving over the north Atlantic before being absorbed by a larger non-tropical low on August 24.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BillTCR_7-2"><a href="#cite_note-BillTCR-7">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>Two people were killed by the storm's large swells—one in <a href="/wiki/Maine" title="Maine">Maine</a> and another in <a href="/wiki/Florida" title="Florida">Florida</a>. The hurricane came close enough to warrant <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone_watches_and_warnings" title="Tropical cyclone watches and warnings">tropical cyclone watches and warnings</a> in both the US and Canada. Bill was one of three tropical storms active on August 16.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BillTCR_7-3"><a href="#cite_note-BillTCR-7">[6]</a></sup> Large, life-threatening swells produced by the storm impacted north-facing coastlines of <a href="/wiki/Puerto_Rico" title="Puerto Rico">Puerto Rico</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hispaniola" title="Hispaniola">Hispaniola</a> as Hurricane Bill approached Bermuda.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[27]</a></sup> Along the coasts of North Carolina, waves averaging 10 ft (3.0 m) in height impacted beaches. On <a href="/wiki/Long_Island" title="Long Island">Long Island</a>, beach damage was severe; in some areas the damage was worse than <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Gloria" title="Hurricane Gloria">Hurricane Gloria</a> in 1985.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[28]</a></sup> In <a href="/wiki/New_York_(state)" title="New York (state)">New York</a>, severe <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Beach_erosion" title="Beach erosion">beach erosion</a> caused by the storm resulted in over $35.5 million in losses.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[29]</a></sup></p>

<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Tropical_Storm_Claudette_(2009)" title="Tropical Storm Claudette (2009)">Tropical Storm Claudette (2009)</a></div>
<p>Just one day after the formation of Hurricane Bill, the season's third named storm developed on August 16. Forming out of a <a href="/wiki/Tropical_wave" title="Tropical wave">tropical wave</a> and an upper-level <a href="/wiki/Low-pressure_area" title="Low-pressure area">low pressure system</a>, Claudette quickly intensified into a tropical storm offshore south of <a href="/wiki/Tallahassee,_Florida" title="Tallahassee, Florida">Tallahassee, Florida</a>. By the afternoon, the storm had attained winds of 60 mph (95 km/h) and steadily tracked towards the Florida Panhandle. Early on August 17, the center of Claudette made <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Landfall_(meteorology)" title="Landfall (meteorology)">landfall</a> on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Santa_Rosa_Island,_Florida" title="Santa Rosa Island, Florida">Santa Rosa Island</a>. Several hours after landfall, the storm weakened to a tropical depression and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydrometeorological_Prediction_Center" title="Hydrometeorological Prediction Center">Hydrometeorological Prediction Center</a> took over primary responsibility of the storm. The system quickly dissipated and was last noted over <a href="/wiki/Alabama" title="Alabama">Alabama</a> on August 18.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ClaudetteTCR_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-ClaudetteTCR-32">[30]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/National_Hurricane_Center" title="National Hurricane Center">National Hurricane Center</a> issued tropical storm warnings for the Florida coastline and residents in some counties were advised to evacuate <a href="/wiki/Storm_surge" title="Storm surge">storm-surge</a>-prone areas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ClaudetteTCR_32-1"><a href="#cite_note-ClaudetteTCR-32">[30]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[31]</a></sup> Tropical Storm Claudette, produced moderate rainfall across portions of Florida, Georgia, and Alabama between August 16 and 18. Two people were killed offshore amidst rough seas from the storm.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[32]</a></sup> An <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Enhanced_Fujita_Scale" title="Enhanced Fujita Scale">EF-0 tornado</a> spawned by the storm in <a href="/wiki/Cape_Coral,_Florida" title="Cape Coral, Florida">Cape Coral</a> damaged 11 homes, leaving $103,000 in damages. Additional damages to coastal property and beaches amounted to $125,000 as a result of Claudette.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NCDC_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-NCDC-35">[33]</a></sup></p>

<p>Around the same time the remnants of Hurricane Bill dissipated over the northern Atlantic,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BillTCR_7-4"><a href="#cite_note-BillTCR-7">[6]</a></sup> a new tropical storm developed near the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bahamas" title="Bahamas">Bahamas</a> on August 26. <p>On September 1, the season's fifth named storm, <a href="/wiki/Tropical_Storm_Erika_(2009)" title="Tropical Storm Erika (2009)">Tropical Storm Erika</a>, formed east of the Lesser Antilles. Upon forming, the storm had attained its peak intensity with winds of 50 mph (85 km/h). Persistent wind shear prevented the system from intensifying and resulted in the storm's convection being completely displaced from the center of circulation by the time it passed over <a href="/wiki/Guadeloupe" title="Guadeloupe">Guadeloupe</a> on September 2. After entering the Caribbean Sea, Erika briefly regained strength before fully succumbing to strong shear. The system eventually dissipated on September 4, to the south of Puerto Rico.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ErikaTCR_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-ErikaTCR-38">[36]</a></sup> Damages were minor, though one island received several inches of rain.</p>

<p>Several days after Erika dissipated,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ErikaTCR_38-1"><a href="#cite_note-ErikaTCR-38">[36]</a></sup> a new tropical depression formed southeast of the Cape Verde Islands on September 7. This depression <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rapid_deepening" title="Rapid deepening">rapidly intensified</a> within an environment of low wind shear and high <a href="/wiki/Sea_surface_temperature" title="Sea surface temperature">sea surface temperatures</a>. Receiving the name <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Fred_(2009)" title="Hurricane Fred (2009)">Fred</a> on September 8, the storm quickly developed an <a href="/wiki/Eye_(cyclone)" title="Eye (cyclone)">eye feature</a> and was upgraded to a hurricane roughly 24 hours after being named. Within a 12‑hour span, the storm's winds increased by 40 mph (65 km/h) to its peak of 120 mph (195 km/h). Upon reaching this intensity, Fred became the strongest storm on record south of 30°N and east of 35°W in the Atlantic basin. Not long after the intensification ceased, it began to weaken as dry air became entrained within the system. By September 11, the storm nearly stalled northwest of the Cape Verde Islands and weakened to a tropical storm. The following day, Fred degenerated into a remnant low before taking a westward track across the Atlantic. The remnants of Fred persisted for nearly a week, nearly regenerating into a tropical depression several times. The low eventually dissipated on September 19, to the south of Bermuda.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FredTCR_17-1"><a href="#cite_note-FredTCR-17">[15]</a></sup></p>

<p>In late September, a new, well-defined tropical wave moved off the west coast of Africa into the Atlantic Ocean. By September 25, the system had developed sufficient deep convection for the NHC to classify it as Tropical Depression Eight. At this time, the depression attained its peak intensity with winds of 35 mph (55 km/h) and a minimum pressure of 1008 mbar (hPa). Shortly thereafter, wind shear and decreasing sea surface temperatures caused the depression to weaken. The system degenerated into a remnant low on September 26 before degenerating into a <a href="/wiki/Trough_(meteorology)" title="Trough (meteorology)">trough</a> of low pressure.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EightTCR_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-EightTCR-39">[37]</a></sup></p>

<td>65 mph (100 km/h) <small>(1-min)</small>  986 <a href="/wiki/Bar_(unit)" title="Bar (unit)">mbar</a> (<a href="/wiki/Pascal_(unit)" title="Pascal (unit)">hPa</a>)</td>

<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Tropical_Storm_Grace_(2009)" title="Tropical Storm Grace (2009)">Tropical Storm Grace (2009)</a></div>
<p>Originating from an extratropical cyclone east of Newfoundland on September 27, the precursor to <a href="/wiki/Tropical_Storm_Grace_(2009)" title="Tropical Storm Grace (2009)">Tropical Storm Grace</a> tracked southeastward towards the Azores, gaining subtropical characteristics. After executing a counterclockwise loop between October 1 and 3, deep convection wrapped around a small circulation center that had developed within the larger cyclone. On October 4, this smaller low developed into a tropical storm while situated near the Azores Islands, becoming the northeastern-most forming Atlantic tropical cyclone on record. The storm quickly turned northeastward and intensified, developing an eye-like feature as it attained peak winds of 65 mph (100 km/h) and a minimum pressure of 986 mbar (hPa; 29.11 inHg). It weakened over increasingly colder waters and began merging with an approaching frontal boundary. Early on October 6, Grace transitioned into an extratropical cyclone before dissipating later that day near <a href="/wiki/Wales" title="Wales">Wales</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GraceTCR_18-1"><a href="#cite_note-GraceTCR-18">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>Although Grace passed through the Azores Islands, the storm had little known effects there.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GraceTCR_18-2"><a href="#cite_note-GraceTCR-18">[16]</a></sup> In Europe, the system and its remnants brought rain to several countries, including Portugal,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[38]</a></sup> the United Kingdom<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[39]</a></sup> and Belgium.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[40]</a></sup> No fatalities were linked to Grace and overall damage was minimal.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GraceTCR_18-3"><a href="#cite_note-GraceTCR-18">[16]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Tropical_Storm_Henri">Tropical Storm Henri</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=2009_Atlantic_hurricane_season&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Tropical Storm Henri">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<td>50 mph (85 km/h) <small>(1-min)</small>  1005 <a href="/wiki/Bar_(unit)" title="Bar (unit)">mbar</a> (<a href="/wiki/Pascal_(unit)" title="Pascal (unit)">hPa</a>)</td>

<p>A tropical wave left the coast of Africa on October 1, moving westward with intermittent showers and thunderstorms.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HTCR_43-0"><a href="#cite_note-HTCR-43">[41]</a></sup> On October 5, the system became better organized,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[42]</a></sup> and a <a href="/wiki/Low-pressure_area" title="Low-pressure area">low-pressure area</a> formed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[43]</a></sup> Although the thunderstorms were displaced east of the <a href="/wiki/Eye_(cyclone)" title="Eye (cyclone)">center of circulation</a> and the probability for development was never high, the disturbance became a tropical depression around 0000 UTC on October 6 about 775 mi (1,247 km) east of the <a href="/wiki/Lesser_Antilles" title="Lesser Antilles">Lesser Antilles</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HTCR_43-1"><a href="#cite_note-HTCR-43">[41]</a></sup> Operationally, the storm was not designated a tropical cyclone until later on October 6, when it was immediately declared a tropical storm.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-disc1_46-0"><a href="#cite_note-disc1-46">[44]</a></sup></p>
<p>Affected by strong wind shear, Henri remained disorganized with its center located on the western edge of the convection. Moving northwestward, Henri intensified slightly to peak winds of 50 mph (85 km/h) on October 7 after the convection increased. Shortly thereafter, the wind shear grew stronger, and on October 8 the storm weakened to a tropical depression.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HTCR_43-2"><a href="#cite_note-HTCR-43">[41]</a></sup> The structure became further disorganized with several low-level vortices.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[45]</a></sup> Just twelve hours after weakening into a depression, Henri degenerated into a remnant area of low pressure. The remnants continued northwestward before turning to the west-southwest due to a ridge. On October 11, the storm's circulation dissipated near <a href="/wiki/Hispaniola" title="Hispaniola">Hispaniola</a>, having never impacted land.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HTCR_43-3"><a href="#cite_note-HTCR-43">[41]</a></sup></p>

<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Ida" title="Hurricane Ida">Hurricane Ida</a></div>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/November_2009_Mid-Atlantic_nor%27easter" title="November 2009 Mid-Atlantic nor'easter">November 2009 Mid-Atlantic nor'easter</a></div>
<p>The final storm of the 2009 season formed over the southern Caribbean Sea on November 4. The slow moving system quickly developed into <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hurricane_Ida_(2009)" title="Hurricane Ida (2009)">Tropical Storm Ida</a> within a favorable environment as it neared the coastline of <a href="/wiki/Nicaragua" title="Nicaragua">Nicaragua</a>. Several hours before moving over land, Ida attained hurricane-status, with winds reaching 80 mph (130 km/h). Hours after moving inland, Ida weakened to a tropical storm and further to a tropical depression as it turned northward. On November 7, the depression re-entered the Caribbean Sea and quickly intensified. Early on November 8, the system re-attained hurricane intensity as it rapidly intensified over warm waters. Ida attained its peak intensity as a Category 2 hurricane early the next day with winds of 105 mph (165 km/h) as it moved over the <a href="/wiki/Yucat%C3%A1n_Channel" title="Yucatán Channel">Yucatán Channel</a>. Not long after reaching this intensity, Ida quickly weakened to a tropical storm as it entered the Gulf of Mexico. Despite strong wind shear, the storm briefly re-attained hurricane status for a third time near the southeastern <a href="/wiki/Louisiana" title="Louisiana">Louisiana</a> coastline before quickly weakening to a tropical storm. Shortly before moving inland over the southern United States, Ida transitioned into an extratropical cyclone. The remnants of Ida persisted until November 11, at which time the low dissipated. Remnant energy from Ida provided energy for another system which became a powerful <a href="/wiki/Nor%27easter" title="Nor'easter">nor'easter</a>, causing significant damage in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mid-Atlantic_States" title="Mid-Atlantic States">Mid-Atlantic States</a>. The resulting storm came to be known as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nor%27Ida" title="Nor'Ida">Nor'Ida</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IdaTCR_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-IdaTCR-6">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the southern Caribbean, Hurricane Ida caused roughly $2.1 million in damage in Nicaragua after destroying numerous homes and leaving an estimated 40,000 people homeless.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[46]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[47]</a></sup> Ida also produced significant rainfall across portions of western Cuba, with some areas recording up to 12.5 in (320 mm) of rain during the storm's passage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IdaTCR_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-IdaTCR-6">[5]</a></sup> In the United States, the hurricane and the subsequent nor'easter caused substantial damage, mainly in the Mid-Atlantic States.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IdaTCR_6-3"><a href="#cite_note-IdaTCR-6">[5]</a></sup> One person was killed by Ida after drowning in rough seas while six others were killed in various incidents related to the nor'easter. Overall, the two systems caused nearly $300 million in damage throughout the country.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NCDC_35-1"><a href="#cite_note-NCDC-35">[33]</a></sup></p>
