<p><b>Hurricane Catarina</b> (<small>Portuguese pronunciation: </small><span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/Portuguese" title="Help:IPA/Portuguese">[kataˈɾinɐ]</a></span>) was an extremely rare <a href="/wiki/South_Atlantic_tropical_cyclone" title="South Atlantic tropical cyclone">South Atlantic tropical cyclone</a> that hit southeastern <a href="/wiki/Brazil" title="Brazil">Brazil</a> in late March 2004. The storm developed out of a stationary <a href="/wiki/Surface_weather_analysis" title="Surface weather analysis">cold-core</a> upper-level <a href="/wiki/Trough_(meteorology)" title="Trough (meteorology)">trough</a> on March 12. Almost a week later, on March 19, a disturbance developed along the trough and traveled towards the east-southeast until March 22 when a <a href="/wiki/Ridge_(meteorology)" title="Ridge (meteorology)">ridge</a> stopped the forward motion of the disturbance. The disturbance was in an unusually favorable environment with a little bit below-average <a href="/wiki/Wind_shear" title="Wind shear">wind shear</a> and above-average sea surface temperatures. The combination of the two led to a slow transition from an <a href="/wiki/Extratropical_cyclone" title="Extratropical cyclone">extratropical cyclone</a> to a <a href="/wiki/Subtropical_cyclone" title="Subtropical cyclone">subtropical cyclone</a> by March 24. The storm continued to obtain <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclogenesis" title="Tropical cyclogenesis">tropical characteristics</a> and became a <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone" title="Tropical cyclone">tropical storm</a> the next day while the winds steadily increased. The storm attained wind speeds of 75 mph (120 km/h)—equivalent to a low-end Category 1 hurricane on the <a href="/wiki/Saffir%E2%80%93Simpson_scale" title="Saffir–Simpson scale">Saffir–Simpson scale</a>—on March 26. At this time it was unofficially named <i>Catarina</i> and was also the first hurricane-intensity tropical cyclone ever recorded in the Southern Atlantic Ocean. Unusually favorable conditions persisted and Catarina continued to intensify and was estimated to have peaked with winds of 100 mph (155 km/h) on March 28. The center of the storm made landfall later that day at the time between the cities of <a href="/wiki/Passo_de_Torres" title="Passo de Torres">Passo de Torres</a> and <a href="/wiki/Balne%C3%A1rio_Gaivota" title="Balneário Gaivota">Balneário Gaivota</a>, <a href="/wiki/Santa_Catarina_(state)" title="Santa Catarina (state)">Santa Catarina</a>. Catarina rapidly weakened upon landfall and dissipated the next day.</p>
<p>Since Catarina was the first tropical cyclone to make landfall in Brazil since the beginning of reliable records, and hence infrastructure and the population were not specifically prepared for it, the damage was quite severe. Although the storm was an unprecedented event, Brazilian officials took the appropriate actions and warned the public about the approaching storm. Residents heeded the warnings and prepared for the storm by either evacuating or by riding it out in their homes. Catarina ended up destroying 1,500 homes and damaging around 40,000 others. Agricultural products were severely damaged: 85% of the banana crops and 40% of the rice crops were lost in the storm. Three people were confirmed to have perished in the storm and 185 others were injured. Damages from the storm amounted to $350 million (2004 USD, $444 million 2017 USD).</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Meteorological_history">Meteorological history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hurricane_Catarina&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Meteorological history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Catarina_2004_track.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1669" data-file-width="2700" height="136" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/90/Catarina_2004_track.png/220px-Catarina_2004_track.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/90/Catarina_2004_track.png/330px-Catarina_2004_track.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/90/Catarina_2004_track.png/440px-Catarina_2004_track.png 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Catarina_2004_track.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Map plotting the track and intensity of the storm, according to the <a href="/wiki/Saffir%E2%80%93Simpson_scale" title="Saffir–Simpson scale">Saffir–Simpson scale</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>On March 12, a <a href="/wiki/Cold-core_low" title="Cold-core low">cold-core</a> stationary upper-level <a href="/wiki/Trough_(meteorology)" title="Trough (meteorology)">trough</a> became established offshore of southern Brazil. A disturbance formed within the trough on the 19th, and moved east-southeastward until the 22nd, when a <a href="/wiki/Ridge_(meteorology)" title="Ridge (meteorology)">ridge</a> to its southeast kept it stationary. With exceptionally favorable upper level winds and slightly below average,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> if marginally warm, water temperatures from 24 to 26 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/%C2%B0C" title="°C">°C</a> (75 to 79 °F), it gradually developed, resembling a subtropical storm by the 24th. Located 630 mi (1,015 km) east-southeast of <a href="/wiki/Florian%C3%B3polis" title="Florianópolis">Florianópolis</a>, it headed slowly westward, and appeared to become a tropical storm on March 25.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GPTCS_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-GPTCS-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>A compact storm, it continued westward while steadily intensifying. The structure of the storm continued to improve and due to a definite <a href="/wiki/Eye_(cyclone)" title="Eye (cyclone)">eye</a> feature showing on satellites, the storm was determined to have become a hurricane-equivalent cyclone on March 26.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WWCBH_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-WWCBH-4">[4]</a></sup> A Brazilian newspaper had a headline "Furacão Catarina" (i.e. "hurricane [threatening the state of Santa] Catarina"). Partly because of this headline, the storm was unofficially named Catarina. It continued to encounter favorable conditions and reached a peak of 100 mph (155 km/h) estimated sustained winds on the 28th, which made the storm the equivalent of a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saffir-Simpson_Hurricane_Scale#Category_2" title="Saffir-Simpson Hurricane Scale">Category 2 hurricane</a> on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saffir-Simpson_Hurricane_Scale" title="Saffir-Simpson Hurricane Scale">Saffir-Simpson Hurricane Scale</a>. Gusts peaked at around 120 mph (195 km/h). The hurricane hit the southern coast of <a href="/wiki/Santa_Catarina_(state)" title="Santa Catarina (state)">Santa Catarina</a> and northeastern <a href="/wiki/Rio_Grande_do_Sul" title="Rio Grande do Sul">Rio Grande do Sul</a> with winds up to 195 km/h overnight. After making landfall, Catarina rapidly dissipated over land, in the normal manner of a tropical cyclone.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GPTCS_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-GPTCS-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Naming">Naming</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hurricane_Catarina&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Naming">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Cyclone_Catarina_from_the_ISS_on_March_26_2004.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2004" data-file-width="3032" height="145" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/89/Cyclone_Catarina_from_the_ISS_on_March_26_2004.JPG/220px-Cyclone_Catarina_from_the_ISS_on_March_26_2004.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/89/Cyclone_Catarina_from_the_ISS_on_March_26_2004.JPG/330px-Cyclone_Catarina_from_the_ISS_on_March_26_2004.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/89/Cyclone_Catarina_from_the_ISS_on_March_26_2004.JPG/440px-Cyclone_Catarina_from_the_ISS_on_March_26_2004.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Cyclone_Catarina_from_the_ISS_on_March_26_2004.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>

<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone_naming" title="Tropical cyclone naming">Tropical cyclone naming</a></div>
<p>Brazilian meteorologists named the storm Catarina for its proximity to (and eventual <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Landfall_(meteorology)" title="Landfall (meteorology)">landfall</a> near) the state of <a href="/wiki/Santa_Catarina_(state)" title="Santa Catarina (state)">Santa Catarina</a>, although government forecasters initially denied that the storm, which clearly had an open <a href="/wiki/Eye_(cyclone)" title="Eye (cyclone)">eye</a> and various other tropical morphologies, was a hurricane at all. More than a year after the storm made landfall, Brazilian meteorologists finally classified the storm as a <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone" title="Tropical cyclone">tropical cyclone</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Informativo_SBMET_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Informativo_SBMET-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>North American forecasters, however surprised as they were, considered this storm a hurricane immediately upon reviewing the satellite-derived evidence. Since Catarina had a clear eyewall structure bounded by deep convective, dense central overcast, well-defined spiral outer bands and outflow structure, warm water temperatures of 79 °F (26 °C), little shear, a warm core low, overall tropical characteristics, and occurred in March (equivalent to September in the Northern Hemisphere, the peak of hurricane season), it was considered to be a hurricane by the <a href="/wiki/National_Hurricane_Center" title="National Hurricane Center">National Hurricane Center</a> in the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>Though it is most commonly known as Catarina, all names for this storm are "unofficial," in that no <a href="/wiki/World_Meteorological_Organization" title="World Meteorological Organization">World Meteorological Organization</a>-affiliated meteorological agency that monitors hurricanes named it. (Tropical cyclone names are predetermined by an international committee of the <a href="/wiki/World_Meteorological_Organization" title="World Meteorological Organization">World Meteorological Organization</a>.)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> It has also been unofficially called "Aldonça",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> and the advisory names for it were "01T-ALPHA" from the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Met_Office" title="Met Office">Met Office</a>, and "50L-NONAME" from the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>' <a href="/wiki/National_Hurricane_Center" title="National Hurricane Center">National Hurricane Center</a>, which keeps it well outside normal designation, which start at 01L for named storms and use 90L to 99L for <a href="/wiki/Invest_(meteorology)" title="Invest (meteorology)">invests</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GPTCS_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-GPTCS-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Rare_formation">Rare formation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hurricane_Catarina&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Rare formation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Typically, tropical cyclones do not form in the South <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Ocean" title="Atlantic Ocean">Atlantic Ocean</a>, due to strong upper level shear, cool water temperatures, and the lack of a convergence zone of convection. Occasionally though, as seen in <a href="/wiki/South_Atlantic_tropical_cyclone#Angola_Tropical_Storm_of_1991" title="South Atlantic tropical cyclone">1991</a> and <a href="/wiki/South_Atlantic_tropical_cyclone#Tropical_Cyclone_of_January_2004" title="South Atlantic tropical cyclone">early 2004</a>, conditions can become slightly more favorable. For Catarina, it was a combination of climatic and atmospheric anomalies. Water temperatures on Catarina's path ranged from 24 to 25 °C, slightly less than the 26.5 °C temperature of a normal tropical cyclone, but sufficient for a storm of baroclinic origin.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WWCBH_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-WWCBH-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>To this day, Catarina is the only <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saffir%E2%80%93Simpson_hurricane_wind_scale" title="Saffir–Simpson hurricane wind scale">hurricane strength</a> tropical cyclone ever observed in the <a href="/wiki/South_Atlantic_tropical_cyclone" title="South Atlantic tropical cyclone">South</a> <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Ocean" title="Atlantic Ocean">Atlantic Ocean</a> since 1970. Other systems have been observed in this region; however, none have reached hurricane strength, and no <a href="/wiki/Eye_(cyclone)" title="Eye (cyclone)">eyes</a> were seen on satellite imagery or radar.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> While Catarina formed in an unusual area, its relation to <a href="/wiki/Global_warming" title="Global warming">global warming</a> or any other type of global climatic change is still up for debate. The <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Brazilian_Society_of_Meteorology&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Brazilian Society of Meteorology (page does not exist)">Brazilian Society of Meteorology</a><span class="noprint" style="font-size:85%; font-style: normal;"> (<a class="extiw" href="https://pt.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sociedade_Brasileira_de_Meteorologia" title="pt:Sociedade Brasileira de Meteorologia">pt</a>)</span> attributed it to "climatic changes and atmospheric anomalies,"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Informativo_SBMET_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-Informativo_SBMET-5">[5]</a></sup> while other researchers have indicated that it could be the result of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Southern_Annular_Mode" title="Southern Annular Mode">Southern Annular Mode</a> or other seasonal variations in weather within the <a href="/wiki/Southern_Hemisphere" title="Southern Hemisphere">Southern Hemisphere</a>, again linked to global changes in climate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pezza_and_Simmons_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Pezza_and_Simmons-10">[10]</a></sup> However, more research in the area is still needed to make a conclusion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pezza_and_Simmons_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-Pezza_and_Simmons-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Impact">Impact</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hurricane_Catarina&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Impact">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>Like normal tropical cyclones, Catarina brought heavy flooding with it. Despite the uncertainty in the future of the storm, Brazilian officials took the appropriate actions to ensure the safety of the residents who lived along the coastline. A successful evacuation was executed for numerous residents along the coast, though some people decided to ride out the storm in their own homes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WWCBH_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-WWCBH-4">[4]</a></sup> The storm damaged around 40,000 homes and destroyed 1,500; 40% of the rice crop were also lost. Total damages were estimated at <span class="nowrap">$350 million (2004</span> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/USD" title="USD">USD</a>, <span class="nowrap">$444 million 2017 USD)</span>. It also killed at least three and injured at least 75. At least 2,000 people were left homeless following the storm.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>At Passo de Torres, many shipyards were destroyed, as they were not designed to withstand the <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_pressure" title="Atmospheric pressure">pressure</a> differentials caused by Catarina's winds; widespread roof damage was reported at this municipality as well.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Emerson_Marcelino_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-Emerson_Marcelino-12">[12]</a></sup> Near the <a href="/wiki/Mampituba_River" title="Mampituba River">Mampituba River</a>, a house was blown about 50 meters (165 ft) upstream, literally landing in another state: it originally was built in the Torres municipality of <a href="/wiki/Rio_Grande_do_Sul" title="Rio Grande do Sul">Rio Grande do Sul</a>, yet it ended up in Passo de Torres, within Santa Catarina. In rural areas, the <a href="/wiki/Maize" title="Maize">corn</a>, <a href="/wiki/Banana" title="Banana">banana</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Rice" title="Rice">rice</a> fields received the most damage, although rice farmers were able to partially recoup their losses, as they had harvested before Catarina made landfall.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Emerson_Marcelino_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-Emerson_Marcelino-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>Overall, almost 36,000 residences were damaged as a result of Catarina's onslaught; of those, 993 collapsed completely. The commercial sector fared slightly better, as only 2,274 buildings were damaged and 472 collapsed. Finally, 397 public buildings were damaged and three were destroyed. These account for 26% of the total buildings in the region, and property damage of $25.6 million (2004 USD $325 million 2017 USD). Four-fifths of the damaged houses had some sort of roof failure or collapse. Most of the damage was blamed on the low quality of the construction; brick residences typically lacked <a href="/wiki/Plaster" title="Plaster">plaster</a>, <a href="/wiki/Beam_(structure)" title="Beam (structure)">beams</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Column" title="Column">columns</a>, for example. The areas affected the most were those inhabited by low-income families, usually with annual family salaries of less than $400 USD.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Emerson_Marcelino_12-2"><a href="#cite_note-Emerson_Marcelino-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
