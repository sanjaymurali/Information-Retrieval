<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">NOAA Hurricane Hunters</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>

<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:NOAA_logo.svg"><img alt="NOAA logo.svg" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="468" data-file-width="468" height="220" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/79/NOAA_logo.svg/220px-NOAA_logo.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/79/NOAA_logo.svg/330px-NOAA_logo.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/79/NOAA_logo.svg/440px-NOAA_logo.svg.png 2x" width="220"/></a>

<p>The <b>NOAA Hurricane Hunters</b> are a group of aircraft that work under the <a href="/wiki/Aircraft_Operations_Center" title="Aircraft Operations Center">Aircraft Operations Center</a> (AOC), located at <a href="/wiki/Lakeland_Linder_Regional_Airport" title="Lakeland Linder Regional Airport">Lakeland Linder Regional Airport</a> in <a href="/wiki/Lakeland,_Florida" title="Lakeland, Florida">Lakeland, Florida</a>, <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>. The Aircraft Operations Center is under the Office of Marine and Aviation Operations (OMAO), which is a branch of the <a href="/wiki/National_Oceanic_and_Atmospheric_Administration" title="National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration">National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration</a> (NOAA), which is in turn an agency of the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_Commerce" title="United States Department of Commerce">Department of Commerce</a>. The AOC resided at <a href="/wiki/MacDill_Air_Force_Base" title="MacDill Air Force Base">MacDill AFB</a> from January 1993 to June 2017.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>NOAA announced that in 2013, <a href="/wiki/Weather_radar" title="Weather radar">Doppler weather radar</a> data will be transmitted from hurricane hunter aircraft in <a href="/wiki/Real-time_data" title="Real-time data">real time</a> . This should help forecasters more quickly analyze storm conditions and could improve <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/HWRF" title="HWRF">HWRF</a> model forecast accuracy by 10 to 15 percent.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="About">About</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=NOAA_Hurricane_Hunters&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: About">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The NOAA Hurricane Hunters conduct research using special aircraft in order to support NOAA's mission of predicting changes in the Earth's environment and managing coastal and marine resources. AOC crews fly through <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hurricane" title="Hurricane">hurricanes</a> to help forecasters and scientists gather both operational and research data. In addition, the crews conduct a number of other research projects - including ocean wind studies, winter storm research, thunderstorm research, coastal erosion, and air chemistry flights to name a few.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Aircraft">Aircraft</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=NOAA_Hurricane_Hunters&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Aircraft">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/National_Oceanic_and_Atmospheric_Administration" title="National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration">NOAA</a> uses two Lockheed <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/WP-3D_Orion" title="WP-3D Orion">WP-3D Orion</a> turboprops to fly through hurricanes and a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gulfstream_IV-SP" title="Gulfstream IV-SP">Gulfstream IV-SP</a> which flies around the upper fringes of storms to get a read on steering currents.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> The NOAA also uses light aircraft for some of its research. In conjunction with Jim Henson Productions, NOAA's P-3s are nicknamed <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kermit_The_Frog" title="Kermit The Frog">Kermit The Frog</a></i> (N42RF) and <i><a href="/wiki/Miss_Piggy" title="Miss Piggy">Miss Piggy</a></i> (N43RF). The G-IV (N49RF) is nicknamed <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Great_Gonzo" title="The Great Gonzo">Gonzo</a></i>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="WP-3">WP-3</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=NOAA_Hurricane_Hunters&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: WP-3">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Lockheed_WP-3D_Orion.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="240" data-file-width="360" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/69/Lockheed_WP-3D_Orion.jpg/220px-Lockheed_WP-3D_Orion.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/69/Lockheed_WP-3D_Orion.jpg/330px-Lockheed_WP-3D_Orion.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/6/69/Lockheed_WP-3D_Orion.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Lockheed_WP-3D_Orion.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
NOAA's Lockheed WP-3D Orion</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The WP-3 was designed to tolerate large amounts of turbulence since it flies through the eyes of hurricanes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> The WP-3 can be thought of as a flying research lab as it is equipped to take atmospheric measurements. One of the most distinctive parts of the WP-3 is the prominent black, circular belly radome.</p>
<p>Staffed with 18 to 20 crew members, including pilots and scientists, a normal hurricane reconnaissance or research mission can last from 9–10 hours, while a surveillance ("fix" mission) mission will typically last 8 hours, often in rotation with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/WC-130" title="WC-130">WC-130</a> flights from the <a href="/wiki/Air_Force_Reserve_Command" title="Air Force Reserve Command">Air Force Reserve Command</a>'s <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/53rd_Weather_Reconnaissance_Squadron" title="53rd Weather Reconnaissance Squadron">53rd Weather Reconnaissance Squadron</a> (53 WRS) at six-hour intervals. Most often, the scientists and crew aboard the aircraft deploy <a href="/wiki/Dropsonde" title="Dropsonde">dropsondes</a> with <a href="/wiki/Global_Positioning_System" title="Global Positioning System">GPS</a> which collect and transmit data as they descend toward the ocean. Once this data is received and checked by the crew, it is sent to the NOAA <a href="/wiki/National_Hurricane_Center" title="National Hurricane Center">National Hurricane Center</a> for analysis. Other data is collected and transmitted by request. Personnel on board include pilots, navigators, engineers, technicians, and flight <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Meteorologists" title="Meteorologists">meteorologists</a> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Flight_Director" title="Flight Director">Flight Directors</a>).</p>
<p>Past projects use the WP-3 have included <a href="/wiki/Jet_stream#Low_level_jets" title="Jet stream">low level jet</a> observation over South America, a <a href="/wiki/Bow_echo" title="Bow echo">bow echo</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mesoscale_convective_system" title="Mesoscale convective system">mesoscale convective study</a> in the Midwest, and ocean wind satellite verification missions over the Northern Atlantic and Pacific.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="G-IV">G-IV</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=NOAA_Hurricane_Hunters&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: G-IV">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Noaa_g-iv.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1064" data-file-width="1792" height="131" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/27/Noaa_g-iv.jpg/220px-Noaa_g-iv.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/27/Noaa_g-iv.jpg/330px-Noaa_g-iv.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/27/Noaa_g-iv.jpg/440px-Noaa_g-iv.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Noaa_g-iv.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
NOAA's Gulfstream IV-SP</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The G-IV is a high altitude jet that can fly up to 45,000 feet. The G-IV crew is similar to the WP-3 and includes pilots, engineers, technicians, and a <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Flight_meteorologist&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Flight meteorologist (page does not exist)">flight meteorologist</a> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Flight_Director" title="Flight Director">Flight Director</a>)</p>
<p>The G-IV flies around the periphery of both tropical and winter storms, including hurricanes, and drops sondes in order to gather data about the surrounding environment. This information is used to help predict the path of hurricanes. Dropsonde transmissions are collected and checked by an on-board Flight Director, who then transmits the data to the <a href="/wiki/National_Centers_for_Environmental_Prediction" title="National Centers for Environmental Prediction">National Centers for Environmental Prediction</a> (NCEP) and the <a href="/wiki/National_Hurricane_Center" title="National Hurricane Center">National Hurricane Center</a> (NHC) for analysis.</p>
<p>The G-IV serves the NCEP Winter Storm Reconnaissance program in order to better predict the location and intensity of winter storms that affect the United States. The program was started by <a class="external text" href="http://www.ncep.noaa.gov" rel="nofollow">NCEP</a> (National Center for Environmental Prediction). During winter storm reconnaissance, air chemistry measurements are also taken from the upper <a href="/wiki/Troposphere" title="Troposphere">troposphere</a>. These flights are typically flown from <a href="/wiki/Honolulu" title="Honolulu">Honolulu</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anchorage" title="Anchorage">Anchorage</a>, or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Yokota_AB" title="Yokota AB">Yokota AB</a>, <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a>.</p>
