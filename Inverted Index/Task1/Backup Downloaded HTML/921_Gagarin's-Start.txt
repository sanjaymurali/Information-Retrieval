<p><b>Gagarin's Start</b><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> (<a href="/wiki/Russian_language" title="Russian language">Russian</a>: <span lang="ru" xml:lang="ru">Гагаринский старт</span>, <i>Gagarinskij start</i>) is a launch site at <a href="/wiki/Baikonur_Cosmodrome" title="Baikonur Cosmodrome">Baikonur Cosmodrome</a> in <a href="/wiki/Kazakhstan" title="Kazakhstan">Kazakhstan</a>, used for the <a href="/wiki/Soviet_space_program" title="Soviet space program">Soviet space program</a> and now managed by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Roscosmos_State_Corporation" title="Roscosmos State Corporation">Roscosmos State Corporation</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Overview">Overview</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gagarin%27s_Start&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Overview">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The launchpad for the world's first human spaceflight made by <a href="/wiki/Yuri_Gagarin" title="Yuri Gagarin">Yuri Gagarin</a> on <a href="/wiki/Vostok_1" title="Vostok 1">Vostok 1</a> in 1961, the site was referred to as <b>Site No.1</b> (<span lang="ru" xml:lang="ru">Площадка №1</span>, <i>Ploshchadka No. 1</i>) as the first one of its kind. It is also sometimes referred to as <i>NIIP-5 LC1</i>, <i>Baikonur LC1</i> or <i>GIK-5 LC1</i>.</p>
<p>On 17 March 1954 the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Council_of_Ministers_(Soviet_Union)" title="Council of Ministers (Soviet Union)">Council of Ministers</a> ordered several ministries to select a site for a <a href="/wiki/Proving_ground" title="Proving ground">proving ground</a> to test the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/R-7_rocket" title="R-7 rocket">R-7 rocket</a> by 1 January 1955. A special <a href="/wiki/Reconnaissance" title="Reconnaissance">reconnaissance</a> commission considered several possible geographic regions and selected <a href="/wiki/Tyuratam" title="Tyuratam">Tyuratam</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Kazakh_Soviet_Socialist_Republic" title="Kazakh Soviet Socialist Republic">Kazakh SSR</a>. This selection was approved on 12 February 1955 by the Council of Ministers, with a completion of construction targeted for 1958.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RSWB_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-RSWB-2">[2]</a></sup> Work on the construction of Site No.1 began on 20 July 1955 by military engineers. Day and night more than 60 powerful trucks worked at the site; 15,000 cubic metres (20,000 cu yd) of earth were excavated and removed per day, with the total volume estimated to be 750,000 cubic metres (980,000 cu yd). During winter explosives were widely utilized. By the end of October 1956 all primary building and installation of infrastructure for R-7 tests was completed. The Installation and Testing Building (<span lang="ru" xml:lang="ru">Монтажно-испытательный корпус</span>, <i>Montazhno-ispytatel'nyj korpus</i>) named "Site No.2" was built and a special railway completed from there to Site No.1 where the launch pad for the rocket was located.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NKA_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-NKA-3">[3]</a></sup> By April 1957 all remaining work was completed and the site was ready for launches.</p>
<p>The R-7 missile made its maiden voyage from LC-1 on May 15, 1957. On 4 October 1957 the pad was used to launch the world's first artificial satellite, <a href="/wiki/Sputnik_1" title="Sputnik 1">Sputnik 1</a>. Manned spaceflights launched from the site include <a href="/wiki/Yuri_Gagarin" title="Yuri Gagarin">Yuri Gagarin</a>'s flight, <a href="/wiki/Valentina_Tereshkova" title="Valentina Tereshkova">Valentina Tereshkova</a>'s flight, and numerous other <a href="/wiki/Human_spaceflight" title="Human spaceflight">human spaceflight</a> missions, including all <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet</a> and <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russian</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_spacecraft_and_crews_that_visited_Mir" title="List of spacecraft and crews that visited Mir">manned spaceflights to Mir</a>. The pad was also used to launch <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Luna_program" title="Luna program">Luna program</a> spacecraft, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mars_probe_program" title="Mars probe program">Mars probe program</a> spacecraft, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Venera_program" title="Venera program">Venera program</a> spacecraft, many <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cosmos_(satellite)" title="Cosmos (satellite)">Cosmos satellites</a> and others.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RSW_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-RSW-4">[4]</a></sup> From 1957 through 1966 the site hosted ready-to-launch strategic nuclear ICBMs in addition to spacecraft launches;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RSW_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-RSW-4">[4]</a></sup> by the 2000s there were more than 400 launches from the site.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> The 500th launch from this site was of <a href="/wiki/Soyuz_TMA-18M" title="Soyuz TMA-18M">Soyuz TMA-18M</a> on 2 September 2015.</p>
<p>In 1961, the growing launch schedule of the Soviet space program resulted in the opening of a sister pad at Baikonur, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/LC-31/6" title="LC-31/6">LC-31/6</a>. LC-1 has been the primary facility for manned launches, with occasional Soyuz flights from LC-31/6.</p>
<p>LC-1 was damaged several times by booster explosions during the early years. As of 2016, the most recent accident to occur on or around the pad was the attempted launch of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Soyuz_T-10-1" title="Soyuz T-10-1">Soyuz T-10-1</a> in September 1983 ended disastrously when the booster caught fire during prelaunch preparations and exploded, causing severe damage that left LC-1 inoperable for almost a year.</p>
