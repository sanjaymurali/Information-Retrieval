<p><b>Hurricane Otto</b> was the first <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone" title="Tropical cyclone">tropical cyclone</a> since <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Cesar%E2%80%93Douglas" title="Hurricane Cesar–Douglas">Hurricane Cesar–Douglas</a> in <a href="/wiki/1996_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="1996 Atlantic hurricane season">1996</a> to survive the <a href="/wiki/List_of_Atlantic%E2%80%93Pacific_crossover_hurricanes" title="List of Atlantic–Pacific crossover hurricanes">crossover from the Atlantic Ocean to the Pacific Ocean</a>. Forming late on November 20 in the southwestern <a href="/wiki/Caribbean_Sea" title="Caribbean Sea">Caribbean Sea</a>, Otto was the fifteenth and final <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone_naming" title="Tropical cyclone naming">named storm</a>, seventh <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hurricane" title="Hurricane">hurricane</a> and fourth <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Major_hurricane" title="Major hurricane">major hurricane</a> of the <a href="/wiki/2016_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="2016 Atlantic hurricane season">2016 Atlantic hurricane season</a>. It quickly intensified into a strong tropical storm the next day, and on November 23–24, rapidly strengthened into a <a href="/wiki/Saffir%E2%80%93Simpson_scale#Category_3" title="Saffir–Simpson scale">Category 3 major hurricane</a>, the first in the month of November since <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Paloma" title="Hurricane Paloma">Hurricane Paloma</a> in <a href="/wiki/2008_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="2008 Atlantic hurricane season">2008</a>, and the latest date an Atlantic hurricane attained such intensity on record.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TCR_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-TCR-1">[1]</a></sup> Otto made landfall in <a href="/wiki/Nicaragua" title="Nicaragua">Nicaragua</a> at peak intensity on November 24, thus becoming the latest hurricane to make landfall in the Atlantic basin since 1851 when records began.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TCR_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-TCR-1">[1]</a></sup> Traveling along the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nicaragua%E2%80%93Costa_Rica_border" title="Nicaragua–Costa Rica border">Nicaragua–Costa Rica border</a>, the system rapidly weakened to a tropical storm before emerging over the eastern Pacific Ocean, becoming the final storm of the <a href="/wiki/2016_Pacific_hurricane_season" title="2016 Pacific hurricane season">2016 Pacific hurricane season</a> as well. Hostile environmental conditions inhibited reorganization, and Otto subsequently degenerated into an elongated <a href="/wiki/Trough_(meteorology)" title="Trough (meteorology)">trough</a> on November 26.</p>
<p>The hurricane's unusually southern formation in the Caribbean Sea led to impacts in countries rarely affected by tropical cyclones. In particular, <a href="/wiki/Panama" title="Panama">Panama</a> and <a href="/wiki/Costa_Rica" title="Costa Rica">Costa Rica</a> suffered extensive damage. The storm claimed at least 23 lives: 10 in Costa Rica, 9 in Panama, and 4 in Nicaragua. Otto also dropped torrential rainfall across the affected regions; some areas received up towards a month's worth of rain in only a matter of a day, which led to life-threatening flooding, which in turn led to <a href="/wiki/Landslide" title="Landslide">landslides</a>. After the storm had passed, recovery efforts begun with the national mourning of the victims, followed by clean-up efforts. Overall, total economic losses from the hurricane exceeded US$190 million.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Meteorological_history">Meteorological history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hurricane_Otto&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Meteorological history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Otto_2016_track.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1854" data-file-width="3000" height="136" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/74/Otto_2016_track.png/220px-Otto_2016_track.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/74/Otto_2016_track.png/330px-Otto_2016_track.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/74/Otto_2016_track.png/440px-Otto_2016_track.png 2x" width="220"/></a>

<p>On November 12, the <a href="/wiki/National_Hurricane_Center" title="National Hurricane Center">National Hurricane Center</a> (NHC) first noted the potential for a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Low_pressure_area" title="Low pressure area">low pressure area</a> to develop in the southwestern <a href="/wiki/Caribbean_Sea" title="Caribbean Sea">Caribbean Sea</a>, assessing a low probability for <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclogenesis" title="Tropical cyclogenesis">tropical cyclone formation</a> within five days.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> After a convectively coupled <a href="/wiki/Kelvin_wave" title="Kelvin wave">Kelvin wave</a> and several nearby <a href="/wiki/Tropical_wave" title="Tropical wave">tropical waves</a> interacted with each other in the aforementioned area, <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_pressure" title="Atmospheric pressure">atmospheric pressure</a> in that region began falling on November 14,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> resulting in a broad low pressure area forming north of <a href="/wiki/Colombia" title="Colombia">Colombia</a> on November 15.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TCR_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-TCR-1">[1]</a></sup> The low moved slowly and erratically, with a disorganized structure and light winds,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> disrupted by unfavorable upper-level conditions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> On November 19, the convection increased and became better organized, amid marginally favorable conditions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hurricane_Hunters" title="Hurricane Hunters">Hurricane Hunters</a> flew into the system on November 20, observing a well-defined circulation, and the system became a tropical depression at 18:00 UTC that day.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TCR_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-TCR-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tright">

<p>The nascent depression meandered over the southwestern Caribbean Sea, its path to the north blocked by a <a href="/wiki/Ridge_(meteorology)" title="Ridge (meteorology)">ridge</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-d1_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-d1-7">[7]</a></sup> Thunderstorm activity continued to pulse over the center,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> and the inner core of convection became more organized. The depression intensified into a tropical storm by 06:00 UTC on November 21, receiving the name <i>Otto</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TCR_1-4"><a href="#cite_note-TCR-1">[1]</a></sup> The storm had well-established <a href="/wiki/Outflow_(meteorology)" title="Outflow (meteorology)">outflow</a> to the northeast, fueled by warm water temperatures of around 84 °F (29 °C).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> After the convection organized into a <a href="/wiki/Central_dense_overcast" title="Central dense overcast">central dense overcast</a>, Otto became a strong tropical storm with 70 mph (110 km/h) winds, and maintained that intensity for about a day. On November 23, the storm reached hurricane intensity, and began a period of <a href="/wiki/Rapid_intensification" title="Rapid intensification">rapid intensification</a>, reaching Category 2 intensity by 06:00 UTC the following day, with winds of 105 mph (165 km/h).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TCR_1-5"><a href="#cite_note-TCR-1">[1]</a></sup> Six hours later, Otto reached its peak intensity as a Category 3 major hurricane with winds of 115 mph (185 km/h), becoming the latest major hurricane on record in the satellite era.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TCR_1-6"><a href="#cite_note-TCR-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OttoHistoricLandfall_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-OttoHistoricLandfall-10">[10]</a></sup> At 17:30 UTC, Otto made <a href="/wiki/Landfall" title="Landfall">landfall</a> over the <a href="/wiki/Indio_Ma%C3%ADz_Biological_Reserve" title="Indio Maíz Biological Reserve">Indio Maíz Biological Reserve</a> in southeastern <a href="/wiki/Nicaragua" title="Nicaragua">Nicaragua</a>, roughly 12 miles (19 km) northwest of the Nicaragua–<a href="/wiki/Costa_Rica" title="Costa Rica">Costa Rica</a> border at peak intensity. This marked the southernmost landfall of a hurricane in Central America since reliable records began, eclipsing <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Irene%E2%80%93Olivia" title="Hurricane Irene–Olivia">Hurricane Irene–Olivia</a> of 1971 by 30 to 35 miles (50 to 55 km).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TCR_1-7"><a href="#cite_note-TCR-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>After landfall, Otto's structure remained well-organized as it moved roughly parallel to the Costa Rica-Nicaraguan border, although cloud tops warmed, indicating diminishing convection.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DiscoSeventeen_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-DiscoSeventeen-11">[11]</a></sup> Despite moving over land, the hurricane's eye remained well-defined and intact as it traverse Nicaragua and Costa Rica.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TCR_1-8"><a href="#cite_note-TCR-1">[1]</a></sup> Otto was the first Atlantic hurricane on record to have its <a href="/wiki/Eye_(cyclone)" title="Eye (cyclone)">eye</a> cross over Costa Rica,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OttoHistoricLandfall_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-OttoHistoricLandfall-10">[10]</a></sup> and the first hurricane-force system to traverse the nation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TCR_1-9"><a href="#cite_note-TCR-1">[1]</a></sup> Around 03:30 UTC on November 25, the eye of Otto emerged over the Eastern Pacific near the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_of_Papagayo" title="Gulf of Papagayo">Gulf of Papagayo</a>, completing a rare <a href="/wiki/List_of_Atlantic%E2%80%93Pacific_crossover_hurricanes" title="List of Atlantic–Pacific crossover hurricanes">crossover from the Atlantic to Eastern Pacific basin</a>—the first such occurrence in which an Atlantic tropical cyclone retained its name, after a change in NHC naming policy in 2000.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TCR_1-10"><a href="#cite_note-TCR-1">[1]</a></sup> Concurrently, the system weakened to a tropical storm. Continuing to move westwards due to the influence of a <a href="/wiki/Subtropical_ridge" title="Subtropical ridge">subtropical ridge</a> to its north, Otto steadily weakened as it moved away from Central America. Unfavorable environmental conditions hastened the system's demise, and Otto weakened to a tropical depression on November 26. Soon thereafter, the system degraded into a trough roughly 490 mi (790 km) south of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Salina_Cruz,_Mexico" title="Salina Cruz, Mexico">Salina Cruz, Mexico</a>. The remnant system continued to produce scattered convection for a few more days before dissipating entirely.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TCR_1-11"><a href="#cite_note-TCR-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span id="Preparations,_impact_and_aftermath"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Preparations.2C_impact_and_aftermath">Preparations, impact and aftermath</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hurricane_Otto&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Preparations, impact and aftermath">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Panama">Panama</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hurricane_Otto&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Panama">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Otto_2016-11-22_1824Z.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="5608" data-file-width="4272" height="289" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b8/Otto_2016-11-22_1824Z.jpg/220px-Otto_2016-11-22_1824Z.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b8/Otto_2016-11-22_1824Z.jpg/330px-Otto_2016-11-22_1824Z.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b8/Otto_2016-11-22_1824Z.jpg/440px-Otto_2016-11-22_1824Z.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Otto_2016-11-22_1824Z.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Tropical Storm Otto intensifying to the north of <a href="/wiki/Panama" title="Panama">Panama</a> on November 22</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Bocas_Town,_Bocas_del_Toro" title="Bocas Town, Bocas del Toro">Bocas del Toro</a>, preparations were made even though Otto was not expected to directly make landfall in the region. Several patients were evacuated from hospitals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-panamaprep_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-panamaprep-12">[12]</a></sup> The nation was put on "hurricane alert", according to Panamanian president <a href="/wiki/Juan_Carlos_Varela" title="Juan Carlos Varela">Juan Carlos Varela</a>. In a press conference, Varela stated that up to 600 relief units had been prepared in advance. Classes were also suspended in all public and private schools nationwide.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-panamaprep_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-panamaprep-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>The outer bands of Otto caused extensive damage in Panama; at least nine people died in various incidents related to the storm. High winds downed trees in <a href="/wiki/Panama_City" title="Panama City">Panama City</a>, one of which fell on a car killing a child. Landslides claimed three lives. Two people drowned while trying to cross a swollen, swift-moving river on the outskirts of Panama City. Offshore, the ship <i>Jessica</i> sank with six crew; three perished while the other three were rescued after clinging to inflatable rafts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Nicaragua">Nicaragua</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hurricane_Otto&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Nicaragua">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A Hurricane Watch was issued for the coast of Nicaragua on November 22,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> which was upgraded into a warning the next day.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> Schools were closed for the coming days as Otto approached, and about 10,000 were in the process of being evacuated.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-prep_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-prep-16">[16]</a></sup> Officials also feared that Otto could damage the nation's coffee crops, which were close to being able to fully harvest.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-prep_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-prep-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>Rainfall across Nicaragua generally amounted to 3 to 6 in (76 to 152 mm) in southern and southwestern portions of the country; a peak value of 6.35 in (161 mm) was observed in <a href="/wiki/El_Castillo_(village)" title="El Castillo (village)">El Castillo</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TCR_1-12"><a href="#cite_note-TCR-1">[1]</a></sup> Throughout the country, Otto damaged 857 houses, eight schools, and two health facilities. The storm passage caused 11,678 people to stay in 152 temporary shelters. After the storm, 248 people remained in the shelters while their damaged houses were rebuilt. Otto damaged 1,700 m (5,600 ft) of power lines, resulting in power and water outages. Officials deployed 12,000 people to help ensure public safety and rescue efforts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ocha1129_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-ocha1129-17">[17]</a></sup> There were four deaths in Nicaragua related to the hurricane, with five others missing as of November 26.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Costa_Rica">Costa Rica</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hurricane_Otto&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Costa Rica">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Hurricane_Otto_at_landfall_over_Nicaragua.gif"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="480" data-file-width="720" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b4/Hurricane_Otto_at_landfall_over_Nicaragua.gif/220px-Hurricane_Otto_at_landfall_over_Nicaragua.gif" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b4/Hurricane_Otto_at_landfall_over_Nicaragua.gif/330px-Hurricane_Otto_at_landfall_over_Nicaragua.gif 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b4/Hurricane_Otto_at_landfall_over_Nicaragua.gif/440px-Hurricane_Otto_at_landfall_over_Nicaragua.gif 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Hurricane_Otto_at_landfall_over_Nicaragua.gif" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Infrared satellite loop of Hurricane Otto making landfall as Category 3 in Nicaragua on November 24</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>In Costa Rica, the government declared red alert for the northern portion of the national territory and the southern Pacific region. About 6,800 people were evacuated to safer locations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> Costa Rica president <a href="/wiki/Luis_Guillermo_Sol%C3%ADs" title="Luis Guillermo Solís">Luis Guillermo Solís</a> also issued a state of emergency and advised all vehicles to stay off the roads, and that <a href="/wiki/Hospital" title="Hospital">hospitals</a> in the most at-risk areas were having their patients transferred somewhere else.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-prep_16-2"><a href="#cite_note-prep-16">[16]</a></sup> He also advised that the hurricane could damage the agriculture supply in the nation.</p>
<p>Otto was the first hurricane to pass directly over Costa Rica since records began.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TCR_1-13"><a href="#cite_note-TCR-1">[1]</a></sup> In Costa Rica, some areas near the border with Nicaragua observed over a month's worth of rainfall;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> three-day accumulations exceeded 8 in (200 mm) in many areas. The highest recorded total was 12.11 in (308 mm) at the <a href="/wiki/Miravalles_Volcano" title="Miravalles Volcano">Miravalles Volcano</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TCR_1-14"><a href="#cite_note-TCR-1">[1]</a></sup> The National Meteorological Institute of Costa Rica reported that Otto killed at least ten people in the country, mostly from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Flash_flooding" title="Flash flooding">flash flooding</a> and landsides,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TCR_1-15"><a href="#cite_note-TCR-1">[1]</a></sup> including six in <a href="/wiki/Upala" title="Upala">Upala</a>, three in <a href="/wiki/Bagaces" title="Bagaces">Bagaces</a>, and one in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Guayabo" title="Guayabo">Guayabo</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TRT_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-TRT-21">[21]</a></sup> Costa Rican president <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Luis_Guillermo_Solis" title="Luis Guillermo Solis">Luis Guillermo Solis</a> described the situation as "chaotic and unprecedented," with the worst effects in <a href="/wiki/Upala_(canton)" title="Upala (canton)">Upala canton</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Latest_World_News_-_11-28-16_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-Latest_World_News_-_11-28-16-22">[22]</a></sup> Authorities calculated at least ₡106 billion <a href="/wiki/Costa_Rican_col%C3%B3n" title="Costa Rican colón">colónes</a> (US$190 million) in damages across the country, including US$68 million in Upala canton, and another US$34 million in Bagaces.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Otto_damage_total_-_BN_Americas_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-Otto_damage_total_-_BN_Americas-23">[23]</a></sup> The hurricane damaged 14 water systems.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ocha1129_17-1"><a href="#cite_note-ocha1129-17">[17]</a></sup> The president declared three days of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/National_mourning" title="National mourning">national mourning</a> in the wake of the hurricane.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TRT_21-1"><a href="#cite_note-TRT-21">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Latest_World_News_-_11-28-16_22-1"><a href="#cite_note-Latest_World_News_-_11-28-16-22">[22]</a></sup> Officials opened 38 shelters that housed 3,370 people, mostly in Upala. About 10,000 people required humanitarian assistance following the storm. Local Red Cross volunteers, police officers, and firefighters brought food and other supplies to the 42 communities left isolated due to Otto. By November 29, roads were repaired and cleared in most areas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ocha1129_17-2"><a href="#cite_note-ocha1129-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<div style="clear:both;"></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Retirement">Retirement</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hurricane_Otto&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Retirement">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/List_of_retired_Atlantic_hurricane_names" title="List of retired Atlantic hurricane names">List of retired Atlantic hurricane names</a></div>
<p>Due to Otto's significant impacts in Central America, especially in Costa Rica, Panama and Nicaragua, the name <i>Otto</i> was retired at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/World_Meteorological_Organisation" title="World Meteorological Organisation">WMO's</a> Hurricane Committee on March 26, 2017, and replaced with <i>Owen</i> for the 2022 season. The name Otto will never again be used for another <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_hurricane" title="Atlantic hurricane">Atlantic hurricane</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
