<p><b>Severe Tropical Cyclone Olivia</b> was a powerful cyclone that produced the highest non-<a href="/wiki/Tornado" title="Tornado">tornadic</a> winds on record on <a href="/wiki/Barrow_Island_(Western_Australia)" title="Barrow Island (Western Australia)">Barrow Island</a>, 408 kilometres per hour (254 mph), breaking the record of 372 km/h (231 mph) on <a href="/wiki/Mount_Washington_(New_Hampshire)" title="Mount Washington (New Hampshire)">Mount Washington</a> in the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> in April 1934. The 13th <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone_naming" title="Tropical cyclone naming">named storm</a> of the <a href="/wiki/1995%E2%80%9396_Australian_region_cyclone_season" title="1995–96 Australian region cyclone season">1995–96 Australian region cyclone season</a>, Olivia formed on 3 April 1996 to the north of <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Northern_Territory" title="Northern Territory">Northern Territory</a>. The storm moved generally to the southwest, gradually intensifying off <a href="/wiki/Western_Australia" title="Western Australia">Western Australia</a>. On 8 April, Olivia intensified into a <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone_scales#Australia_and_Fiji" title="Tropical cyclone scales">severe tropical cyclone</a> and subsequently turned more to the south, steered by a passing <a href="/wiki/Trough_(meteorology)" title="Trough (meteorology)">trough</a>. On 10 April, Olivia produced the worldwide record strongest gust on Barrow Island, and on the same day the cyclone made <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Landfall_(meteorology)" title="Landfall (meteorology)">landfall</a> near <a href="/wiki/Varanus_Island" title="Varanus Island">Varanus Island</a>. The storm quickly weakened over land, dissipating over the <a href="/wiki/Great_Australian_Bight" title="Great Australian Bight">Great Australian Bight</a> on 12 April.</p>
<p>While in its formative stages, Olivia produced light rainfall in the Northern Territory. While offshore Western Australia, the cyclone forced oil platforms to shut down, and the combination of high winds and waves caused heavy damage to oil facilities. Onshore, Olivia's high winds damaged several small mining towns, halting operations. Every house in <a href="/wiki/Pannawonica,_Western_Australia" title="Pannawonica, Western Australia">Pannawonica</a> sustained some damage. One person in the town was injured by flying glass and had to be flown to receive treatment, and nine others were lightly injured. The cyclone also produced heavy rainfall and a localized <a href="/wiki/Storm_surge" title="Storm surge">storm surge</a>. Damage was estimated "in the millions". While the storm was dissipating, rough seas in <a href="/wiki/South_Australia" title="South Australia">South Australia</a> killed <a href="/wiki/Australian_dollar" title="Australian dollar">A$</a>60 million (US$47.5 million) worth of farm-raised tuna at <a href="/wiki/Port_Lincoln" title="Port Lincoln">Port Lincoln</a>. The name <i>Olivia</i> was <a href="/wiki/List_of_retired_Australian_cyclone_names" title="List of retired Australian cyclone names">retired</a> after the season.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Meteorological_history">Meteorological history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cyclone_Olivia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Meteorological history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Olivia_1996_track.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="724" data-file-width="1024" height="156" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9f/Olivia_1996_track.png/220px-Olivia_1996_track.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9f/Olivia_1996_track.png/330px-Olivia_1996_track.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9f/Olivia_1996_track.png/440px-Olivia_1996_track.png 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Olivia_1996_track.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Map plotting the track and intensity of the storm, according to the <a href="/wiki/Saffir%E2%80%93Simpson_scale" title="Saffir–Simpson scale">Saffir–Simpson scale</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>In early April 1996, a surge in the <a href="/wiki/Trade_winds" title="Trade winds">trade winds</a> interacted with the <a href="/wiki/Monsoon_trough" title="Monsoon trough">monsoon trough</a> to produce an area of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Convection_(meteorology)" title="Convection (meteorology)">convection</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dar_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-dar-1">[1]</a></sup> On 2 April, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bureau_of_Meteorology_(Australia)" title="Bureau of Meteorology (Australia)">Bureau of Meteorology</a> (BoM) first identified the system as a low to mid-level <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Low_pressure_area" title="Low pressure area">low pressure area</a> over <a href="/wiki/Indonesia" title="Indonesia">Indonesia</a> north of <a href="/wiki/Darwin,_Northern_Territory" title="Darwin, Northern Territory">Darwin, Australia</a>. Despite strong <a href="/wiki/Wind_shear" title="Wind shear">wind shear</a>, the system slowly became better organized as it moved southward,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ATCR_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-ATCR-2">[2]</a></sup> aided by improving upper-level <a href="/wiki/Outflow_(meteorology)" title="Outflow (meteorology)">outflow</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dar_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-dar-1">[1]</a></sup> Early on 5 April, the <a href="/wiki/Joint_Typhoon_Warning_Center" title="Joint Typhoon Warning Center">Joint Typhoon Warning Center</a> (JTWC) classified the system as Tropical Cyclone 25S as the system began a westward track,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JTWC_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-JTWC-3">[3]</a></sup> steered by a building mid-level <a href="/wiki/Ridge_(meteorology)" title="Ridge (meteorology)">ridge</a> to the south.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ATCR_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-ATCR-2">[2]</a></sup> Shortly thereafter, the BoM upgraded the system to a <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone_scales#Australia_and_Fiji" title="Tropical cyclone scales">Category 1 cyclone</a>, designating the storm as Tropical Cyclone Olivia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ATCR_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-ATCR-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BoM_TC_Database_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-BoM_TC_Database-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the days following Olivia's development, persistent wind shear prevented convection from developing around the center of circulation. By 8 April, an upper-level <a href="/wiki/Trough_(meteorology)" title="Trough (meteorology)">trough</a> passed to the south of the nascent cyclone, leading to lower shear.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ATCR_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-ATCR-2">[2]</a></sup> Following this, the system had developed sufficiently for the JTWC to upgrade it to a Category 1 equivalent on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saffir%E2%80%93Simpson_Hurricane_Scale" title="Saffir–Simpson Hurricane Scale">Saffir–Simpson Hurricane Scale</a> (SSHS), estimating <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Maximum_sustained_winds" title="Maximum sustained winds">1 minute sustained winds</a> of 120 km/h (75 mph).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JTWC_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-JTWC-3">[3]</a></sup> Around the same time, the BoM upgraded Olivia to a severe tropical cyclone, assessing similar wind speeds but sustained over 10 minutes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ATCR_2-4"><a href="#cite_note-ATCR-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BoM_TC_Database_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-BoM_TC_Database-4">[4]</a></sup> After the storm reached this intensity, the mid-level ridge south of the cyclone began to weaken, turning Olivia toward the southwest. By 9 April, the system attained Category 4 intensity according to the BoM as it continued to strengthen.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ATCR_2-5"><a href="#cite_note-ATCR-2">[2]</a></sup> During the afternoon of 9 April, the BoM estimated that Olivia attained its lowest <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Barometric_pressure" title="Barometric pressure">barometric pressure</a> of 925 <a href="/wiki/Pascal_(unit)" title="Pascal (unit)">hPa</a> (<a href="/wiki/Bar_(unit)" title="Bar (unit)">mbar</a>), along with 10 minute sustained winds of 195 km/h (120 mph).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ATCR_2-6"><a href="#cite_note-ATCR-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BoM_TC_Database_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-BoM_TC_Database-4">[4]</a></sup> Around 00:00 <a href="/wiki/Coordinated_Universal_Time" title="Coordinated Universal Time">UTC</a> on 10 April, the JTWC assessed the cyclone to have attained 1 minute winds of 230 km/h (145 mph), equivalent to a Category 4 on the SSHS.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JTWC_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-JTWC-3">[3]</a></sup> By this time, another trough bypassed the cyclone, causing Olivia to turn to the south and accelerate to the southeast. Early on 10 April, data from a nearby <a href="/wiki/Weather_radar" title="Weather radar">weather radar</a> at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/RAAF_Learmonth" title="RAAF Learmonth">Learmonth Airport</a> near <a href="/wiki/Exmouth,_Western_Australia" title="Exmouth, Western Australia">Exmouth, Western Australia</a>, showed that the storm had developed a 65 km (40 mi) wide <a href="/wiki/Eye_(cyclone)" title="Eye (cyclone)">eye</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ATCR_2-7"><a href="#cite_note-ATCR-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Late on 10 April, the center of Olivia passed near <a href="/wiki/Barrow_Island_(Western_Australia)" title="Barrow Island (Western Australia)">Barrow Island</a> at peak intensity. Shortly thereafter, the storm passed near <a href="/wiki/Varanus_Island" title="Varanus Island">Varanus Island</a> as a high-end Category 4 or low-end Category 5 cyclone.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BoM_TC_Database_4-3"><a href="#cite_note-BoM_TC_Database-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BOMRecords_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-BOMRecords-5">[5]</a></sup> Within several hours of passing by Varanus Island, Olivia made <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Landfall_(meteorology)" title="Landfall (meteorology)">landfall</a> near <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mardie,_Western_Australia" title="Mardie, Western Australia">Mardie</a> at peak intensity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ATCR_2-8"><a href="#cite_note-ATCR-2">[2]</a></sup> Shortly thereafter, the storm began to weaken overland. Accelerating to the southeast, the storm became disorganized and winds decreased below hurricane-force.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BoM_TC_Database_4-4"><a href="#cite_note-BoM_TC_Database-4">[4]</a></sup> During the afternoon of 11 April, Olivia weakened to a tropical low over southern Australia. It moved over the <a href="/wiki/Great_Australian_Bight" title="Great Australian Bight">Great Australian Bight</a> and lost its identity as a gale-force low on 12 April,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ATCR_2-9"><a href="#cite_note-ATCR-2">[2]</a></sup> after it was absorbed by a trough.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wa_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-wa-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Impact_and_aftermath">Impact and aftermath</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cyclone_Olivia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Impact and aftermath">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>As a minimal cyclone in the <a href="/wiki/Timor_Sea" title="Timor Sea">Timor Sea</a>, Olivia brought minor rainfall and gusty winds to parts of the <a href="/wiki/Northern_Territory" title="Northern Territory">Northern Territory</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bom_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-bom-7">[7]</a></sup> On 9 April, the fringes of the storm dropped 84 mm (3.3 in) in the <a href="/wiki/Kimberley_(Western_Australia)" title="Kimberley (Western Australia)">Kimberley</a> region of Western Australia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sw_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-sw-8">[8]</a></sup> An oil rig in the Timor Sea recorded a wind gust of 127 km/h (79 mph) during the storm's passage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bom_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-bom-7">[7]</a></sup> Offshore, Olivia affected the southern portion of Australia's Northwest Shelf, which had 24 oil and gas facilities.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gas_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-gas-9">[9]</a></sup> The storm produced large <a href="/wiki/Swell_(ocean)" title="Swell (ocean)">swells</a> up to 21 m (69 ft), in conjunction with extreme wind gusts 265 km/h (165 mph).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ATCR_2-10"><a href="#cite_note-ATCR-2">[2]</a></sup> Waves toppled an oil drilling structure about 40 km (25 mi) northeast of Barrow Island, the first of its kind to fail since the 1980s. The waves also wrecked several anchors for underwater pipelines, although no lines were ruptured. On Barrow Island, the winds knocked over 30 <a href="/wiki/Pumpjack" title="Pumpjack">pumpjacks</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gas_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-gas-9">[9]</a></sup> and several buildings and instruments were damaged, while high waves incurred beach erosion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sw_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-sw-8">[8]</a></sup> Damage to offshore platforms was estimated in the millions of dollars.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ATCR_2-11"><a href="#cite_note-ATCR-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>A group of 17 people, initially considered missing,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> rode out the storm on the offshore Mackerel Island. Onshore, officials closed a portion of <a href="/wiki/Highway_1_(Western_Australia)" title="Highway 1 (Western Australia)">Highway 1</a> as well as other roads in the area.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> Ahead of the storm, hundreds of residents in older structures and mobile homes, as well as an <a href="/wiki/Aboriginal_Australians" title="Aboriginal Australians">aborigine</a> village near <a href="/wiki/Onslow,_Western_Australia" title="Onslow, Western Australia">Onslow</a>, were ordered to evacuate to safer locations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> Five iron mines were closed during the storm's passage, forcing the 1,500 workers to return home.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dt_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-dt-14">[14]</a></sup> The disruptions from the cyclone affected about 340,000 tonnes of lost production.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> About 800,000 <a href="/wiki/Barrel_(unit)" title="Barrel (unit)">barrels</a> of lost oil production resulted from closed offshore platforms. Oil fields reopened by 14 April.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>Moving ashore Western Australia, Olivia produced peak gusts of 257 km/h (160 mph) in <a href="/wiki/Mardie_Station" title="Mardie Station">Mardie Station</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dt_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-dt-14">[14]</a></sup> the station's second-highest wind gust from a tropical cyclone.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ATCR_2-12"><a href="#cite_note-ATCR-2">[2]</a></sup> Olivia also produced gusts of 267 km/h (166 mph) at Varanus Island, which was the highest wind gust on record in Australia,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ATCR_2-13"><a href="#cite_note-ATCR-2">[2]</a></sup> until the higher reading on Barrow Island during the storm was confirmed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wmo_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-wmo-17">[17]</a></sup> It broke the previous peak of 259 km/h (161 mph) set by <a href="/wiki/1974%E2%80%9375_Australian_region_cyclone_season#Severe_Tropical_Cyclone_Trixie" title="1974–75 Australian region cyclone season">Cyclone Trixie</a> in 1975,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> and was later matched by <a href="/wiki/Cyclone_Vance" title="Cyclone Vance">Cyclone Vance</a> in 1999.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BOMRecords_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-BOMRecords-5">[5]</a></sup> The high winds at Mardie Station damaged the local airport hangar and several windmills.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dt_14-2"><a href="#cite_note-dt-14">[14]</a></sup> Farther inland, Olivia still produced wind gusts of 158 km/h (98 mph) in <a href="/wiki/Pannawonica,_Western_Australia" title="Pannawonica, Western Australia">Pannawonica</a>, which damaged many roofs, trees, and power lines. The town's police station and medical center lost their roofs during the storm,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> and every house sustained some damage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> Of the 82 houses in the small town, 55 lost their roof. It was estimated that Pannawonica would remain without power for three weeks. One person in town was injured by flying broken glass, who had to be airlifted to <a href="/wiki/Karratha,_Western_Australia" title="Karratha, Western Australia">Karratha</a> for medical attention. In nearby <a href="/wiki/Yarraloola" title="Yarraloola">Yarraloola</a>, nearly every building was damaged, and the roofs of several farm buildings were ripped off. Several other small towns in the region sustained damage to roofs, power lines, and trees,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-stoney_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-stoney-21">[21]</a></sup> and a 24 year old <a href="/wiki/List_of_roadhouses_in_Western_Australia" title="List of roadhouses in Western Australia">roadhouse</a> was destroyed along the Fortescue River.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> Olivia also dropped heavy rainfall, peaking at 167 mm (6.6 in) in Red Hill, although flooding was not significant.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wa_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-wa-6">[6]</a></sup> The storm also produced a 2 m (6.6 ft) <a href="/wiki/Storm_surge" title="Storm surge">storm surge</a> in localized areas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bom_7-2"><a href="#cite_note-bom-7">[7]</a></sup> At the port of <a href="/wiki/Dampier,_Western_Australia" title="Dampier, Western Australia">Dampier</a>, the storm sank three boats, although no one was aboard.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-stoney_21-1"><a href="#cite_note-stoney-21">[21]</a></sup> Damage was estimated in the "millions of dollars", according to a local newspaper,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dt_14-3"><a href="#cite_note-dt-14">[14]</a></sup> and overall, 10 people were injured.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p>As the remnants of Olivia moved through Australia, they dropped heavy rainfall and brought gusty winds to <a href="/wiki/South_Australia" title="South Australia">South Australia</a>. <a href="/wiki/Cape_Willoughby" title="Cape Willoughby">Cape Willoughby</a> recorded gusts of 106 km/h (66 mph) on 12 April, strong enough to knock down tree branches on nearby <a href="/wiki/Kangaroo_Island" title="Kangaroo Island">Kangaroo Island</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sw_8-2"><a href="#cite_note-sw-8">[8]</a></sup> At <a href="/wiki/Port_Lincoln" title="Port Lincoln">Port Lincoln</a>, sediment stirred up by Olivia's remnants killed 60,000 farmed tuna, worth about A$60 million (US$47.5 million). The fish were in cages and died due to abnormally high oxygen levels in the water, caused by Olivia's high winds and rough waves.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> The remnants of Olivia later brought rainfall to the states of <a href="/wiki/Victoria_(Australia)" title="Victoria (Australia)">Victoria</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tasmania" title="Tasmania">Tasmania</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sw_8-3"><a href="#cite_note-sw-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>The name "Olivia" was later <a href="/wiki/List_of_retired_Australian_cyclone_names" title="List of retired Australian cyclone names">retired</a> from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_tropical_cyclone_names" title="List of tropical cyclone names">list of tropical cyclone names</a> for the Australian region.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SPAC_TCOP_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-SPAC_TCOP-25">[25]</a></sup> After the storm's passage, the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Australian_Air_Force" title="Royal Australian Air Force">Royal Australian Air Force</a> flew six generators to Pannawonica after the town was out of power for two nights,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> and the Western Australian government sent another 13 generators.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> Residents in the town received counseling to cope with the stress of the storm's aftermath.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-stoney_21-2"><a href="#cite_note-stoney-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Records">Records</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cyclone_Olivia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Records">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>At 10:55 UTC on 10 April 1996 along the offshore Barrow Island, an automatic privately operated <a href="/wiki/Anemometer" title="Anemometer">anemometer</a> recorded a three-second wind gust of 408 km/h (253 mph), at a position 10 m (33 ft) above sea level. The BoM was initially unsure of the veracity of the reading, although a team at the 1999 <a href="/wiki/Offshore_Technology_Conference" title="Offshore Technology Conference">Offshore Technology Conference</a> presented the reading as the highest wind gust on Earth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wmo_17-1"><a href="#cite_note-wmo-17">[17]</a></sup> In 2009, the <a href="/wiki/World_Meteorological_Organization" title="World Meteorological Organization">World Meteorological Organization</a> Commission for Climatology researched whether <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Gustav" title="Hurricane Gustav">Hurricane Gustav</a> in 2008 produced a record 340 km/h (211 mph) gust on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pinar_del_Rio" title="Pinar del Rio">Pinar del Rio</a>, Cuba;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> one committee member recalled the gust set during Olivia, which spurred the investigation. The reading occurred along the western edge of the eyewall, possibly related to <a href="/wiki/Mesovortices" title="Mesovortices">mesovortices</a>. Based on other similarly high wind gusts during Olivia – 369 km/h (229 mph) and 374 km/h (232 mph) observed within five minutes of the record gust – the team confirmed that the instrument was observing properly during the storm. The same anemometer also recorded five-minute sustained winds of 178 km/h (111 mph), causing a much greater than normal ratio of gusts to sustained winds. The team confirmed that the instrument was regularly inspected and calibrated, and that the reading was during the passage of the eyewall.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wmo_17-2"><a href="#cite_note-wmo-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>On 26 January 2010, nearly 14 years later, the World Meteorological Organization announced that the wind gust was the highest recorded worldwide. This gust surpassed the previous non-tornadic wind speed of 372 km/h (231 mph) on <a href="/wiki/Mount_Washington_(New_Hampshire)" title="Mount Washington (New Hampshire)">Mount Washington</a> in the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> in April 1934.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> The long delay was partly due to the anemometer not being owned by the BoM, and as a result the agency did not enact a follow-up investigation. Despite the high winds, the anemometer and a nearby building were not damaged due to the winds occurring over a very short time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wmo_17-3"><a href="#cite_note-wmo-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
