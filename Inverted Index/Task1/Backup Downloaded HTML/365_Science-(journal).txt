<p><i><b>Science</b></i>, also widely referred to as <i><b>Science Magazine</b></i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> is the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Peer-reviewed" title="Peer-reviewed">peer-reviewed</a> <a href="/wiki/Academic_journal" title="Academic journal">academic journal</a> of the <a href="/wiki/American_Association_for_the_Advancement_of_Science" title="American Association for the Advancement of Science">American Association for the Advancement of Science</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> (AAAS) and one of the world's top academic journals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lemonick_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Lemonick-4">[4]</a></sup> It was first published in 1880, is currently circulated weekly and has a print subscriber base of around 130,000. Because institutional subscriptions and online access serve a larger audience, its estimated readership is 570,400 people.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>The major focus of the journal is publishing important original scientific research and research reviews, but <i>Science</i> also publishes science-related news, opinions on <a href="/wiki/Science_policy" title="Science policy">science policy</a> and other matters of interest to scientists and others who are concerned with the wide implications of science and technology. Unlike most <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Scientific_journals" title="Scientific journals">scientific journals</a>, which focus on a specific field, <i>Science</i> and its rival <i><a href="/wiki/Nature_(journal)" title="Nature (journal)">Nature</a></i> cover the full range of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_academic_disciplines#Natural_sciences" title="List of academic disciplines">scientific disciplines</a>. According to the <i><a href="/wiki/Journal_Citation_Reports" title="Journal Citation Reports">Journal Citation Reports</a></i>, <i>Science</i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">'</span>s 2015 <a href="/wiki/Impact_factor" title="Impact factor">impact factor</a> was 34.661.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WoS_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-WoS-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>Although it is the journal of the AAAS, membership in the AAAS is not required to publish in <i>Science</i>. Papers are accepted from authors around the world. Competition to publish in <i>Science</i> is very intense, as an article published in such a highly cited journal can lead to attention and career advancement for the authors. Fewer than 7% of articles submitted are accepted for publication.</p>
<p><i>Science</i> is based in Washington, D.C., United States, with a second office in <a href="/wiki/Cambridge" title="Cambridge">Cambridge</a>, England.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Science_(journal)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Science_1883_Cover.png"><img alt="cover of the first volume of the resurrected journal (February–June 1883)" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1425" data-file-width="1020" height="307" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/21/Science_1883_Cover.png/220px-Science_1883_Cover.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/21/Science_1883_Cover.png/330px-Science_1883_Cover.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/21/Science_1883_Cover.png/440px-Science_1883_Cover.png 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Science_1883_Cover.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Cover of the first volume of the resurrected journal (February–June 1883)</div>
</div>
</div>
<p><i>Science</i> was founded by New York journalist John Michels in 1880 with financial support from <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Edison" title="Thomas Edison">Thomas Edison</a> and later from <a href="/wiki/Alexander_Graham_Bell" title="Alexander Graham Bell">Alexander Graham Bell</a>. However, the journal never gained enough subscribers to succeed and ended publication in March 1882. Entomologist <a href="/wiki/Samuel_Hubbard_Scudder" title="Samuel Hubbard Scudder">Samuel H. Scudder</a> resurrected the journal one year later and had some success while covering the meetings of prominent American scientific societies, including the AAAS.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> However, by 1894, <i>Science</i> was again in financial difficulty and was sold to psychologist <a href="/wiki/James_McKeen_Cattell" title="James McKeen Cattell">James McKeen Cattell</a> for $500.</p>
<p>In an agreement worked out by Cattell and AAAS secretary <a href="/wiki/Leland_Ossian_Howard" title="Leland Ossian Howard">Leland O. Howard</a>, <i>Science</i> became the journal of the American Association for the Advancement of Science in 1900.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> During the early part of the 20th century important articles published in <i>Science</i> included papers on <a href="/wiki/Drosophila_melanogaster" title="Drosophila melanogaster">fruit fly</a> <a href="/wiki/Genetics" title="Genetics">genetics</a> by <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Hunt_Morgan" title="Thomas Hunt Morgan">Thomas Hunt Morgan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gravitational_lens" title="Gravitational lens">gravitational lensing</a> by <a href="/wiki/Albert_Einstein" title="Albert Einstein">Albert Einstein</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spiral_nebulae" title="Spiral nebulae">spiral nebulae</a> by <a href="/wiki/Edwin_Hubble" title="Edwin Hubble">Edwin Hubble</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> After Cattell died in 1944, the ownership of the journal was transferred to the AAAS.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>After Cattell's death in 1944, the journal lacked a consistent editorial presence until Graham DuShane became editor in 1956. In 1958, under DuShane's leadership, <i>Science</i> absorbed <i><a href="/wiki/The_Scientific_Monthly" title="The Scientific Monthly">The Scientific Monthly</a></i>, thus increasing the journal's circulation by over 60% from 38,000 to more than 61,000.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-maturing3_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-maturing3-11">[11]</a></sup> Physicist <a href="/wiki/Philip_Abelson" title="Philip Abelson">Philip Abelson</a>, a co-discoverer of <a href="/wiki/Neptunium" title="Neptunium">neptunium</a>, served as editor from 1962 to 1984. Under Abelson the efficiency of the review process was improved and the publication practices were brought up to date.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-maturing3_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-maturing3-11">[11]</a></sup> During this time, papers on the <a href="/wiki/Apollo_program" title="Apollo program">Apollo program</a> missions and some of the earliest reports on AIDS were published.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-change3_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-change3-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>Biochemist <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Daniel_E._Koshland,_Jr." title="Daniel E. Koshland, Jr.">Daniel E. Koshland, Jr.</a> served as editor from 1985 until 1995. From 1995 until 2000, neuroscientist <a href="/wiki/Floyd_E._Bloom" title="Floyd E. Bloom">Floyd E. Bloom</a> held that position.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-change3_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-change3-12">[12]</a></sup> Biologist <a href="/wiki/Donald_Kennedy" title="Donald Kennedy">Donald Kennedy</a> became the editor of <i>Science</i> in 2000. Biochemist <a href="/wiki/Bruce_Alberts" title="Bruce Alberts">Bruce Alberts</a> took his place in March 2008.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> Geophysicist <a href="/wiki/Marcia_McNutt" title="Marcia McNutt">Marcia McNutt</a> became editor-in-chief in June 2013.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> During her tenure the family of journals expanded to include <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Science_Robotics&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Science Robotics (page does not exist)">Science Robotics</a></i> and <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Science_Immunology&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Science Immunology (page does not exist)">Science Immunology</a></i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Open_access" title="Open access">open access</a> publishing with <i><a href="/wiki/Science_Advances" title="Science Advances">Science Advances</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Jeremy_M._Berg" title="Jeremy M. Berg">Jeremy M. Berg</a> became editor-in-chief on July 1, 2016.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>In February 2001, draft results of the <a href="/wiki/Human_genome" title="Human genome">human genome</a> were simultaneously published by <i>Nature</i> and <i>Science</i> with <i>Science</i> publishing the <a href="/wiki/Celera_Corporation" title="Celera Corporation">Celera Genomics</a> paper and <i>Nature</i> publishing the publicly funded <a href="/wiki/Human_Genome_Project" title="Human Genome Project">Human Genome Project</a>. In 2007 <i>Science</i> (together with <a href="/wiki/Nature_(journal)" title="Nature (journal)"><i>Nature</i></a>) received the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Prince_of_Asturias_Awards" title="Prince of Asturias Awards">Prince of Asturias Award</a> for Communications and Humanity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> In 2015 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rush_D._Holt,_Jr." title="Rush D. Holt, Jr.">Rush D. Holt, Jr.</a>, chief executive officer of the AAAS and executive publisher of <i>Science</i>, stated that the journal was becoming increasingly international: "[I]nternationally co-authored papers are now the norm—they represent almost 60 percent of the papers. In 1992, it was slightly less than 20 percent."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Science_and_Diplomacy._19-0"><a href="#cite_note-Science_and_Diplomacy.-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Family_of_journals">Family of journals</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Science_(journal)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Family of journals">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Science family of journals includes <i>Science</i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Science_Translational_Medicine" title="Science Translational Medicine">Science Translational Medicine</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Science_Signaling" title="Science Signaling">Science Signaling</a></i>, and <i><a href="/wiki/Science_Advances" title="Science Advances">Science Advances</a></i>. In 2015, Holt announced another expansion: <i>Science Robotics</i> and <i>Science Immunology</i> would begin publication in mid-2016.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Availability">Availability</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Science_(journal)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Availability">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The full journal is available online, through the main journal website, only to subscribers, AAAS members, and for delivery to <a href="/wiki/IP_address" title="IP address">IP addresses</a> at institutions that subscribe; students, K–12 teachers, and some others can subscribe at a reduced fee. However, research articles published after 1997 are available free (with online registration) one year after they are published. Significant public-health related articles are also available for free, sometimes immediately after publication. AAAS members may also access the pre-1997 Science archives at the <i>Science</i> website, where it is called "Science Classic". Institutions can opt to add Science Classic to their subscriptions for an additional fee. Some older articles can also be accessed via <a href="/wiki/JSTOR" title="JSTOR">JSTOR</a> and <a href="/wiki/ProQuest" title="ProQuest">ProQuest</a>.</p>
<p>The journal also participates in initiatives that provide free or low-cost access to readers in developing countries, including <a href="/wiki/HINARI" title="HINARI">HINARI</a>, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=OARE&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="OARE (page does not exist)">OARE</a>, <a href="/wiki/AGORA" title="AGORA">AGORA</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Scidev.net" title="Scidev.net">Scidev.net</a>.</p>
<p>Other features of the <i>Science</i> website include the free "ScienceNow" section with "up to the minute news from science",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> and "ScienceCareers", which provides free career resources for scientists and engineers.</p>
