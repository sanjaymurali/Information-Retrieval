<p>The <b>Maya people</b> (sometimes <b>Mayans</b>) are a group of <a href="/wiki/Indigenous_peoples_of_the_Americas" title="Indigenous peoples of the Americas">Indigenous peoples</a> of <a href="/wiki/Mesoamerica" title="Mesoamerica">Mesoamerica</a>. They inhabit southern <a href="/wiki/Mexico" title="Mexico">Mexico</a>, <a href="/wiki/Guatemala" title="Guatemala">Guatemala</a>, <a href="/wiki/Belize" title="Belize">Belize</a>, <a href="/wiki/El_Salvador" title="El Salvador">El Salvador</a> and <a href="/wiki/Honduras" title="Honduras">Honduras</a>. The overarching term "Maya" is a collective designation to include the peoples of the region that share some degree of cultural and linguistic heritage; however, the term embraces many distinct populations, societies, and ethnic groups that each have their own particular traditions, cultures, and historical identity.</p>
<p>The pre-Columbian Maya population was approximately eight million.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> There were an estimated seven million Maya living in this area at the start of the 21st century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-intercontinentalcry.org_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-intercontinentalcry.org-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-2">[2]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Guatemala" title="Guatemala">Guatemala</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mexico" title="Mexico">southern Mexico</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Yucat%C3%A1n_Peninsula" title="Yucatán Peninsula">Yucatán Peninsula</a>, <a href="/wiki/Belize" title="Belize">Belize</a>, <a href="/wiki/El_Salvador" title="El Salvador">El Salvador</a>, and western <a href="/wiki/Honduras" title="Honduras">Honduras</a> have managed to maintain numerous remnants of their ancient cultural heritage. Some are quite integrated into the majority <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hispanicized" title="Hispanicized">hispanicized</a> <a href="/wiki/Mestizo" title="Mestizo">Mestizo</a> cultures of the nations in which they reside, while others continue a more traditional, culturally distinct life, often speaking one of the <a href="/wiki/Mayan_languages" title="Mayan languages">Mayan languages</a> as a primary <a href="/wiki/Language" title="Language">language</a>.</p>
<p>The largest populations of contemporary Maya inhabit Guatemala, Belize, and the western portions of Honduras and El Salvador, as well as large segments of population within the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Political_divisions_of_Mexico" title="Political divisions of Mexico">Mexican states</a> of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Yucat%C3%A1n_(state)" title="Yucatán (state)">Yucatán</a>, <a href="/wiki/Campeche" title="Campeche">Campeche</a>, <a href="/wiki/Quintana_Roo" title="Quintana Roo">Quintana Roo</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tabasco_(state)" title="Tabasco (state)">Tabasco</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Chiapas" title="Chiapas">Chiapas</a>.</p>

<p>One of the largest groups of modern Maya can be found in Mexico's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Yucat%C3%A1n_(state)" title="Yucatán (state)">Yucatán State</a> and the neighboring states of Campeche, Quintana Roo and in Belize. These peoples commonly identify themselves simply as "Maya" with no further ethnic subdivision (unlike in the Highlands of Western Guatemala). They speak the language which anthropologists term "<a href="/wiki/Yucatec_Maya_language" title="Yucatec Maya language">Yucatec Maya</a>", but is identified by speakers and Yucatecos simply as "Maya". Among Maya speakers, Spanish is commonly spoken as a second or first language.</p>
<p>There is a significant amount of confusion as to the correct terminology to use—Maya or Mayan—and the meaning of these words with reference to contemporary or pre-Columbian peoples, to Maya peoples in different parts of Mexico, Guatemala, Belize, and to languages or peoples.</p>

</div>
<cite style="display: block; text-align: right;"><a href="/wiki/Chilam_Balam" title="Chilam Balam">Chilam Balam</a> Chumayel<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTERestall200467_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTERestall200467-4">[4]</a></sup></cite></div>
<p>Linguists refer to the Maya language as Yucatec or Yucatec Maya to distinguish it from other <a href="/wiki/Mayan_languages" title="Mayan languages">Mayan languages</a>. This norm has often been misinterpreted to mean that the people are also called Yucatec Maya; that term refers only to the language, and the correct name for the people is simply Maya (not Mayans). Maya is one language in the Mayan language family. Thus, to refer to Maya as Mayans would be similar to referring to Spanish people as Romantics because they speak a language belonging to the Romance language family.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Confusion of the term Maya/Mayan as an ethnic label occurs because Maya women who use traditional dress identify by the ethnic term <i>mestiza</i> and not Maya.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>Persons use a strategy of ethnic identification that Juan Castillo Cocom refers to as "ethnoexodus"—meaning that ethnic self-identification as Maya is quite variable, situational, and articulated not to processes of producing group identity, but of escaping from discriminatory processes of sociocultural marginalization.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Yucatán's indigenous population was first exposed to Europeans after a party of Spanish shipwreck survivors came ashore in 1511. One of the sailors, <a href="/wiki/Gonzalo_Guerrero" title="Gonzalo Guerrero">Gonzalo Guerrero</a>, is reported to have taken up with a local woman and started a family; he became a counselor among a local polity near present-day <a href="/wiki/Chetumal" title="Chetumal">Chetumal</a>. Later Spanish expeditions to the region were led by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Francisco_Hern%C3%A1ndez_de_C%C3%B3rdoba_(discoverer_of_Yucat%C3%A1n)" title="Francisco Hernández de Córdoba (discoverer of Yucatán)">Córdoba</a> in 1517, <a href="/wiki/Juan_de_Grijalva" title="Juan de Grijalva">Grijalva</a> in 1518, and <a href="/wiki/Hern%C3%A1n_Cort%C3%A9s" title="Hernán Cortés">Cortés</a> in 1519. From 1528 to 1540, several attempts by <a href="/wiki/Francisco_de_Montejo" title="Francisco de Montejo">Francisco Montejo</a> to conquer the Yucatán failed. His son, Francisco de Montejo the Younger, fared almost as badly when he first took over: while desperately holding out at Chichen Itza, he lost 150 men in a single day.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ambconq_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-ambconq-9">[9]</a></sup> European diseases, massive recruitment of native warriors from Campeche and Champoton, and internal hatred between the Xiu Maya and the lords of Cocom eventually turned the tide for Montejo the Younger. <a href="/wiki/Chichen_Itza" title="Chichen Itza">Chichen Itza</a> was conquered by 1570.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ambconq_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-ambconq-9">[9]</a></sup> In 1542, the western Yucatán Peninsula also surrendered to him.</p>

<p>Historically, the population in the eastern half of the peninsula was less affected by and less integrated with Hispanic culture than the western half. In the 21st century in the Yucatán Peninsula (Mexican states of Campeche, Yucatán and Quintana Roo), between 750,000 and 1,200,000 people speak Mayan. However, three times more than that are of Maya origins, hold ancient Maya surnames, and do not speak Mayan languages as their first language.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Matthew_Restall" title="Matthew Restall">Matthew Restall</a>, in his book <i>The Maya Conquistador</i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> mentions a series of letters sent to the King of Spain in the 16th and 17th centuries. The <a href="/wiki/Nobility" title="Nobility">noble</a> Maya families at that time signed documents to the Spanish Royal Family; surnames mentioned in those letters are Pech, Camal, Xiu, Ucan, Canul, Cocom, and Tun, among others.</p>
<p>A large 19th-century revolt by the native Maya people of Yucatán (Mexico), known as the <a href="/wiki/Caste_War_of_Yucat%C3%A1n" title="Caste War of Yucatán">Caste War of Yucatán</a>, was one of the most successful modern Native American revolts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> For a period the Maya state of <a href="/wiki/Chan_Santa_Cruz" title="Chan Santa Cruz">Chan Santa Cruz</a> was recognized as an independent nation by the <a href="/wiki/British_Empire" title="British Empire">British Empire</a>, particularly in terms of trading with British Honduras.</p>

<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Francisco_Epigmenio_Luna_Kan" title="Francisco Epigmenio Luna Kan">Francisco Luna-Kan</a> was elected governor of the state of Yucatán from 1976 to 1982. Luna-Kan was born in <a href="/wiki/M%C3%A9rida,_Yucat%C3%A1n" title="Mérida, Yucatán">Mérida, Yucatán</a>, and he was a Doctor of medicine, then a Professor of Medicine before his political offices. He was first appointed as overseer of the state's rural medical system. He was the first Governor of the modern <a href="/wiki/Yucat%C3%A1n_Peninsula" title="Yucatán Peninsula">Yucatán Peninsula</a> to be of full Maya ancestry. In the early 21st century, dozens of politicians, including Deputies, Majors and Senators, are of full or mixed Maya heritage from the Yucatán Peninsula.</p>

<p>According to the National Institute of Geography and Informatics (Mexico's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/INEGI" title="INEGI">INEGI</a>), in Yucatán State there were 1.2 million Mayan speakers in 2009, representing 59.5% of the inhabitants.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> Due to this, the cultural section of the government of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Yucat%C3%A1n_(state)" title="Yucatán (state)">Yucatán</a> began on-line classes for grammar and proper pronunciation of Maya.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>Maya people from <a href="/wiki/Yucat%C3%A1n_Peninsula" title="Yucatán Peninsula">Yucatán Peninsula</a> living in the United States of America have been organizing Maya language lessons and Maya cooking classes since 2003 in California and other states: clubs of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Yucatec_Maya" title="Yucatec Maya">Yucatec Maya</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-yucatecos_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-yucatecos-14">[14]</a></sup> are registered in <a href="/wiki/Dallas" title="Dallas">Dallas</a> and <a href="/wiki/Irving,_Texas" title="Irving, Texas">Irving, Texas</a>; <a href="/wiki/Salt_Lake_City" title="Salt Lake City">Salt Lake City</a> in Utah; <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Las_Vegas,_Nevada" title="Las Vegas, Nevada">Las Vegas, Nevada</a>; and <a href="/wiki/California" title="California">California</a>, with groups in San Francisco, San Rafael, Chino, Pasadena, Santa Ana, Garden Grove, Inglewood, Los Angeles, Thousand Oaks, Oxnard, San Fernando Valley and Whittier.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-yucatecos_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-yucatecos-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Chiapas">Chiapas</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Maya_peoples&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Chiapas">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Chiapas-Maya.gif"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="416" data-file-width="475" height="193" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/84/Chiapas-Maya.gif/220px-Chiapas-Maya.gif" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/84/Chiapas-Maya.gif/330px-Chiapas-Maya.gif 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/84/Chiapas-Maya.gif/440px-Chiapas-Maya.gif 2x" width="220"/></a>

<p><a href="/wiki/Chiapas" title="Chiapas">Chiapas</a> was for many years one of the regions of Mexico that was least touched by the reforms of the <a href="/wiki/Mexican_Revolution" title="Mexican Revolution">Mexican Revolution</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Zapatista_Army_of_National_Liberation" title="Zapatista Army of National Liberation">Zapatista Army of National Liberation</a>, launched a rebellion against the Mexican state, Chiapas in January 1994, declared itself to be an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Indigenous_people_of_the_Americas" title="Indigenous people of the Americas">indigenous</a> movement and drew its strongest and earliest support from Chiapan Maya. Today its number of supporters is relevant. (see also the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/EZLN" title="EZLN">EZLN</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Chiapas_conflict" title="Chiapas conflict">Chiapas conflict</a>)</p>
<p>Maya groups in Chiapas include the <a href="/wiki/Tzotzil_people" title="Tzotzil people">Tzotzil</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tzeltal_people" title="Tzeltal people">Tzeltal</a>, in the highlands of the state, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tojolabal" title="Tojolabal">Tojolabalis</a> concentrated in the lowlands around <a href="/wiki/Las_Margaritas,_Chiapas" title="Las Margaritas, Chiapas">Las Margaritas</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Ch%27ol_people" title="Ch'ol people">Ch'ol</a> in the jungle. (see map)</p>
<p>The most traditional of Maya groups are the <a href="/wiki/Lacandon_people" title="Lacandon people">Lacandon</a>, a small population avoiding contact with outsiders until the late 20th century by living in small groups in the <a href="/wiki/Lacandon_Jungle" title="Lacandon Jungle">Lacandon Jungle</a>. These Lacandon Maya came from the Campeche/Petén area (north-east of <a href="/wiki/Chiapas" title="Chiapas">Chiapas</a>) and moved into the Lacandon rain-forest at the end of the 18th century.</p>
<p>In the course of the 20th century, and increasingly in the 1950s and 1960s, other people (mainly the Maya and subsistence peasants from the highlands), also entered into the Lacandon region; initially encouraged by the government. This immigration led to land-related conflicts and an increasing pressure on the <a href="/wiki/Rainforest" title="Rainforest">rainforest</a>. To halt the migration, the government decided in 1971 to declare a large part of the forest (614,000 hectares, or 6140 km<sup>2</sup>) a protected area: the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Montes_Azules_Biosphere_Reserve" title="Montes Azules Biosphere Reserve">Montes Azules Biosphere Reserve</a>. They appointed only one small population group (the 66 Lacandon families) as tenants (thus creating the Lacandon Community), thereby displacing 2000 Tzeltal and Ch'ol families from 26 communities, and leaving non-Lacandon communities dependent on the government for granting their rights to land. In the decades that followed the government carried out numerous programs to keep the problems in the region under control, using land distribution as a political tool; as a way of ensuring loyalty from different campesino groups. This strategy of <a href="/wiki/Divide_and_rule" title="Divide and rule">divide and rule</a> led to great disaffection and tensions among population groups in the region.<br/>
(see also the <a href="/wiki/Chiapas_conflict" title="Chiapas conflict">Chiapas conflict</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Lacandon_Jungle" title="Lacandon Jungle">Lacandon Jungle</a>).</p>

<p>The Maya population in <a href="/wiki/Belize" title="Belize">Belize</a> is concentrated in the <a href="/wiki/Corozal_District" title="Corozal District">Corozal</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cayo_District" title="Cayo District">Cayo</a>, <a href="/wiki/Toledo_District" title="Toledo District">Toledo</a> and <a href="/wiki/Orange_Walk_District" title="Orange Walk District">Orange Walk</a> districts, but they are scattered throughout the country. The Maya are thought to have been in Belize and the Yucatán region since the second millennium BC. Much of Belize's original Maya population died as a result of new infectious diseases and conflicts between tribes and with Europeans. They are divided into the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Yucatec" title="Yucatec">Yucatec</a>, <a href="/wiki/Q%27eqchi%27_people" title="Q'eqchi' people">Kekchi</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Mopan_people" title="Mopan people">Mopan</a>. These three Maya groups now inhabit the country.</p>
<p>The Yucatec Maya (many of whom came from Yucatán, Mexico to escape the Caste War of the 1840s) there have been evidence of several Yucatec Maya groups living by the Yalbac area of Belize and in the Orange Walk district near the present day Lamanai at the time the British reach. The Mopan (indigenous to Belize but were forced out by the British; they returned from Guatemala to evade slavery in the 19th century), and Kek'Chi (also fled from slavery in Guatemala in the 19th century). The later groups are chiefly found in the Toledo District.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Guatemala">Guatemala</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Maya_peoples&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Guatemala">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Nebaj.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2048" data-file-width="1536" height="293" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b7/Nebaj.JPG/220px-Nebaj.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b7/Nebaj.JPG/330px-Nebaj.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b7/Nebaj.JPG/440px-Nebaj.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a>

<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Nebaj.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Ixil women in Nebaj, Guatemala.</div>
</div>

<p>In <a href="/wiki/Guatemala" title="Guatemala">Guatemala</a>, indigenous people of Maya descent comprise around 40% of the population.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> The largest and most traditional Maya populations are in the western highlands in the departments of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Baja_Verapaz" title="Baja Verapaz">Baja Verapaz</a>, <a href="/wiki/Quich%C3%A9_Department" title="Quiché Department">Quiché</a>, <a href="/wiki/Totonicap%C3%A1n_Department" title="Totonicapán Department">Totonicapán</a>, <a href="/wiki/Huehuetenango_Department" title="Huehuetenango Department">Huehuetenango</a>, <a href="/wiki/Quetzaltenango_Department" title="Quetzaltenango Department">Quetzaltenango</a>, and <a href="/wiki/San_Marcos_Department" title="San Marcos Department">San Marcos</a>; their inhabitants are mostly Maya.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Maya people of the Guatemala highlands include the <a href="/wiki/Achi_people" title="Achi people">Achi</a>, <a href="/wiki/Akatek_people" title="Akatek people">Akatek</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chuj_people" title="Chuj people">Chuj</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ixil_people" title="Ixil people">Ixil</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jakaltek_people" title="Jakaltek people">Jakaltek</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kaqchikel_people" title="Kaqchikel people">Kaqchikel</a>, <a href="/wiki/K%27iche%27_people" title="K'iche' people">K'iche'</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mam_people" title="Mam people">Mam</a>, <a href="/wiki/Poqomam_people" title="Poqomam people">Poqomam</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Poqomchi%27" title="Poqomchi'">Poqomchi'</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Q%27anjob%27al_people" title="Q'anjob'al people">Q'anjob'al</a>, <a href="/wiki/Q%27eqchi%27_people" title="Q'eqchi' people">Q'eqchi'</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tz%27utujil_people" title="Tz'utujil people">Tz'utujil</a> and <a href="/wiki/Uspantek_people" title="Uspantek people">Uspantek</a>.</p>
<p>The Q'eqchi' live in lowland areas of Alta Vera Paz, Peten, and Western Belize. Over the course of the succeeding centuries a series of land displacements, re-settlements, persecutions and migrations resulted in a wider dispersal of Q'eqchi' communities, into other regions of Guatemala (Izabal, Petén, El Quiché). They are the 2nd largest ethnic Maya group in Guatemala (after the <a href="/wiki/K%27iche%27_people" title="K'iche' people">K'iche'</a>) and one of the largest and most widespread throughout Central America.</p>
<p>In Guatemala, the Spanish colonial pattern of keeping the native population legally separate and subservient continued well into the 20th century.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> This resulted in many traditional customs being retained, as the only other option than traditional Maya life open to most Maya was entering the Hispanic culture at the very bottom rung. Because of this many Guatemalan Maya, especially women, continue to wear traditional clothing, that varies according to their specific local identity.</p>
<p>The southeastern region of Guatemala (bordering with <a href="/wiki/Honduras" title="Honduras">Honduras</a>) includes groups such as the <a href="/wiki/Ch%27orti%27_people" title="Ch'orti' people">Ch'orti'</a>. The northern lowland Petén region includes the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Itza" title="Itza">Itza</a>, whose language is near extinction but whose agro-forestry practices, including use of dietary and medicinal plants may still tell us much about pre-colonial management of the Maya lowlands.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Genocide_in_Guatemala">Genocide in Guatemala</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Maya_peoples&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Genocide in Guatemala">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Guatemalan_genocide" title="Guatemalan genocide">Guatemalan genocide</a></div>
<p>During the 1980s the indigenous Maya community made up about two-thirds of Guatemala’s 10 million people.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> During his presidency (1982-1983), <a href="/wiki/Efra%C3%ADn_R%C3%ADos_Montt" title="Efraín Ríos Montt">Efraín Ríos Montt</a> instituted a campaign of state terror intended to destroy the Mayas in the name of countering “communist subversion” and ridding the country of its indigenous culture.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>In some areas, government forces killed about 40% of the total population; the campaign destroyed at least 440 Mayan villages. The army attacked inhabitants, and burned down their homes and fields.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>The 36 year long <a href="/wiki/Guatemalan_Civil_War" title="Guatemalan Civil War">Guatemalan Civil War</a> left more than 200,000 people dead and at least 100,000 women raped; most of the victims were Maya.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p>On January 26, 2012 former president Ríos Montt was formally indicted in Guatemala and appeared in court for genocide and crimes against humanity<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> for which he was then sentenced to a sentence of 80 years in prison on May 10, 2013<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup>. This ruling was overturned by the constitutional court on May 20, 2013 over alleged irregularities in the handling of the case<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup>. The ex-president appeared in court again on January 5, 2015 amongst protest form his lawyers regarding his health conditions<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> and on August 25, 2015 it was deliberated that a re-trial of the 2013 proceedings could find Ríos Montt guilty or not, but that the sentence would be suspended<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup>.</p>

<p>The Maya people are known for their brightly colored, <a href="/wiki/Yarn" title="Yarn">yarn</a>-based, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Textiles" title="Textiles">textiles</a> that are woven into capes, shirts, blouses, <a href="/wiki/Huipil" title="Huipil">huipiles</a> and dresses. Each village has its own distinctive pattern, making it possible to distinguish a person's home town. Women's clothing consists of a shirt and a long skirt.</p>
<p>The Maya religion is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Roman_Catholicism" title="Roman Catholicism">Roman Catholicism</a> combined with the indigenous <a href="/wiki/Maya_religion" title="Maya religion">Maya religion</a> to form the unique syncretic religion which prevailed throughout the country and still does in the rural regions prior to 2010s of "orthodoxing" the western rural areas by Christian Orthodox missionaries. Beginning from negligible roots prior to 1960s, however, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Protestant" title="Protestant">Protestant</a> <a href="/wiki/Pentecostalism" title="Pentecostalism">Pentecostalism</a> has grown to become the predominant religion of <a href="/wiki/Guatemala_City" title="Guatemala City">Guatemala City</a> and other urban centers, later to 2010s that almost of all Maya of several rural areas of West Guatemala, living rural areas were mostly mass converted from Catholicism or possibly Maya religion due of various reasons to either <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eastern_Orthodoxy" title="Eastern Orthodoxy">Eastern</a> or <a href="/wiki/Oriental_Orthodoxy" title="Oriental Orthodoxy">Oriental Orthodoxy</a> by late Fr. Andres Giron and some other Orthodox missionaries, and also smaller to mid-sized towns also slowly converted as well since 2013.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-theorthodoxchurch.info_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-theorthodoxchurch.info-28">[28]</a></sup> The unique religion is reflected in the local saint, <a href="/wiki/Maxim%C3%B3n" title="Maximón">Maximón</a>, who is associated with the subterranean force of masculine fertility and prostitution. Always depicted in black, he wears a black hat and sits on a chair, often with a cigar placed in his mouth and a gun in his hand, with offerings of tobacco, alcohol, and Coca-Cola at his feet. The locals know him as San Simon of Guatemala.</p>

<p>The <a href="/wiki/Popol_Vuh" title="Popol Vuh">Popol Vuh</a> is the most significant work of Guatemalan literature in the <a href="/wiki/K%27iche%27_language" title="K'iche' language">K'iche' language</a>, and one of the most important of <a href="/wiki/Pre-Columbian_era" title="Pre-Columbian era">Pre-Columbian</a> American literature. It is a compendium of Maya stories and legends, aimed to preserve Maya traditions. The first known version of this text dates from the 16th century and is written in Quiché transcribed in Latin characters. It was translated into Spanish by the <a href="/wiki/Dominican_Order" title="Dominican Order">Dominican</a> priest <a href="/wiki/Francisco_Xim%C3%A9nez" title="Francisco Ximénez">Francisco Ximénez</a> in the beginning of the 18th century. Due to its combination of historical, mythical, and religious elements, it has been called the Maya Bible. It is a vital document for understanding the culture of Pre-Columbian America. The <a href="/wiki/Rabinal_Ach%C3%AD" title="Rabinal Achí">Rabinal Achí</a> is a dramatic work consisting of dance and text that is preserved as it was originally represented. It is thought to date from the 15th century and narrates the mythical and dynastic origins of the Toj K'iche' rulers of Rabinal, and their relationships with neighboring K'iche' of <a href="/wiki/Q%27umarkaj" title="Q'umarkaj">Q'umarkaj</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> The Rabinal Achí is performed during the Rabinal festival of January 25, the day of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saint_Paul" title="Saint Paul">Saint Paul</a>. It was declared a masterpiece of oral tradition of humanity by UNESCO in 2005. The 16th century saw the first native-born Guatemalan writers that wrote in <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">Spanish</a>.</p>

<p>There is an undeniable symbiotic relationship between cultural heritage, tourism, and a national identity. In the case of the Maya, the many national identities have been constructed because of the growing demands placed on them by cultural tourism. By focusing on lifeways through costumes, rituals, diet, handicrafts, language, housing, or other features, the identity of the economy shifts from the sale of labor to that of the sale of culture.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<p>Global tourism is now considered one of the largest scale movement of goods, services, and people in history and a significant catalyst for economic development and sociopolitical change.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> Estimated that between 35 and 40 percent of tourism today is represented by cultural tourism or heritage tourism, this alternative to mass tourism offers opportunities for place-based engagement that frames context for interaction by the lived space and everyday life of other peoples, as well as sites and objects of global historical significance.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> In this production of tourism the use of historic symbols, signs, and topics form a new side that characterizes a nation and can play an active role in nation building.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup></p>
<p>With this type of tourism, people argue that ethno-commerce may open unprecedented opportunities for creating value of various kinds. Tourists travel with cultural expectations, which has created a touristic experience sometimes faced with the need to invent traditions of artificial and contrived attractions, often developed at the expense of local tradition and meanings.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup></p>
<p>An example of this can be seen in “Mayanizing Tourism on Roatan Island, Honduras: Archaeological Perspectives on Heritage, Development, and Indignity.” Alejandro j. Figueroa et al., combine archaeological data and ethnographic insights to explore a highly contested tourism economy in their discussion of how places on Roatan Island, Honduras, have become increasingly “Mayanized” over the past decade. As tour operators and developers continue to invent an idealized Maya past for the island, non-Maya archaeological remains and cultural patrimony are constantly being threatened and destroyed. While heritage tourism provides economic opportunities for some, it can devalue contributions made by less familiar groups.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup></p>
