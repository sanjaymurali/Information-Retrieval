<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Orbital inclination</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

<p><b>Orbital inclination</b> measures the tilt of an object's <a href="/wiki/Orbit" title="Orbit">orbit</a> around a celestial body. It is expressed as the <a href="/wiki/Angle" title="Angle">angle</a> between a <a href="/wiki/Plane_of_reference" title="Plane of reference">reference plane</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Orbital_plane" title="Orbital plane">orbital plane</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Axis_of_rotation" title="Axis of rotation">axis</a> of direction of the orbiting object.</p>
<p>For a satellite orbiting the Earth directly above the equator, the plane of the satellite's orbit is the same as the Earth's equatorial plane, and the satellite's orbital inclination is 0°. The general case is that the satellite's orbit is tilted; it spends half an orbit over the northern hemisphere and half over the southern. If the orbit swung between 20° north <a href="/wiki/Latitude" title="Latitude">latitude</a> and 20° south latitude, then its orbital inclination would be 20°.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Orbits">Orbits</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Orbital_inclination&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Orbits">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The inclination is one of the six <a href="/wiki/Orbital_elements" title="Orbital elements">orbital elements</a> describing the shape and orientation of a celestial <a href="/wiki/Orbit" title="Orbit">orbit</a>. It is the <a href="/wiki/Angle" title="Angle">angle</a> between the orbital plane and the <a href="/wiki/Plane_of_reference" title="Plane of reference">plane of reference</a>, normally stated in <a href="/wiki/Degree_(angle)" title="Degree (angle)">degrees</a>. For a satellite orbiting a <a href="/wiki/Planet" title="Planet">planet</a>, the plane of reference is usually the plane containing the planet's <a href="/wiki/Equator" title="Equator">equator</a>. For planets in the Solar System, the plane of reference is usually the <a href="/wiki/Ecliptic" title="Ecliptic">ecliptic</a>, the plane in which the Earth orbits the Sun.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> This reference plane is most practical for Earth-based observers. Therefore, Earth's inclination is, by definition, zero.</p>
<p>Inclination could instead be measured with respect to another plane, such as the <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">Sun</a>'s equator or the <a href="/wiki/Invariable_plane" title="Invariable plane">invariable plane</a> (the plane that represents the angular momentum of the Solar System, approximately the orbital plane of <a href="/wiki/Jupiter" title="Jupiter">Jupiter</a>).</p>

<p>On the other hand, the <a href="/wiki/Dwarf_planet" title="Dwarf planet">dwarf planets</a> <a href="/wiki/Pluto" title="Pluto">Pluto</a> and <a href="/wiki/Eris_(dwarf_planet)" title="Eris (dwarf planet)">Eris</a> have inclinations to the ecliptic of 17° and 44° respectively, and the large <a href="/wiki/Asteroid" title="Asteroid">asteroid</a> <a href="/wiki/2_Pallas" title="2 Pallas">Pallas</a> is inclined at 34°.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Natural_and_artificial_satellites">Natural and artificial satellites</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Orbital_inclination&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Natural and artificial satellites">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The inclination of orbits of <a href="/wiki/Natural_satellite" title="Natural satellite">natural</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Artificial_satellite" title="Artificial satellite">artificial satellites</a> is measured relative to the equatorial plane of the body they orbit, if they orbit sufficiently closely. The equatorial plane is the plane perpendicular to the axis of rotation of the central body.</p>
<p>An inclination of 30° could also be described using an angle of 150°. The convention is that the normal orbit is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Direct_motion" title="Direct motion">prograde</a>, an orbit in the same direction as the planet rotates. Inclinations greater than 90° describe <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Retrograde_motion" title="Retrograde motion">retrograde orbits</a>. Thus:</p>
<ul>
<li>An inclination of 0° means the orbiting body has a prograde orbit in the planet's equatorial plane.</li>
<li>An inclination greater than 0° and less than 90° also describe prograde orbits.</li>
<li>An inclination of 63.4° is often called a <b>critical inclination</b>, when describing artificial satellites orbiting the Earth, because they have zero apogee drift.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></li>
<li>An inclination of exactly 90° is a polar orbit, in which the spacecraft passes over the north and south poles of the planet.</li>
<li>An inclination greater than 90° and less than 180° is a retrograde orbit.</li>
<li>An inclination of exactly 180° is a retrograde equatorial orbit.</li>
</ul>
<p>For impact-generated moons of <a href="/wiki/Terrestrial_planet" title="Terrestrial planet">terrestrial planets</a> not too far from their star, with a large planet–moon distance, the orbital planes of moons tend to be aligned with the planet's orbit around the star due to tides from the star, but if the planet–moon distance is small, it may be inclined. For <a href="/wiki/Gas_giant" title="Gas giant">gas giants</a>, the orbits of moons tend to be aligned with the giant planet's equator, because these formed in circumplanetary disks.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Exoplanets_and_multiple_star_systems">Exoplanets and multiple star systems</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Orbital_inclination&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Exoplanets and multiple star systems">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The inclination of <a href="/wiki/Exoplanet" title="Exoplanet">exoplanets</a> or members of <a href="/wiki/Star_system" title="Star system">multiple stars</a> is the angle of the plane of the orbit relative to the plane perpendicular to the line-of-sight from Earth to the object.</p>
<ul>
<li>An inclination of 0° is a face-on orbit, meaning the plane of its orbit is parallel to the sky.</li>
<li>An inclination of 90° is an edge-on orbit, meaning the plane of its orbit is perpendicular to the sky.</li>
</ul>
<p>Since the word 'inclination' is used in exoplanet studies for this line-of-sight inclination then the angle between the planet's orbit and the star's rotation must use a different word and is termed the spin-orbit angle or spin-orbit alignment. In most cases the orientation of the star's rotational axis is unknown.</p>
<p>Because the <a href="/wiki/Doppler_spectroscopy" title="Doppler spectroscopy">radial-velocity method</a> more easily finds planets with orbits closer to edge-on, most exoplanets found by this method have inclinations between 45° and 135°, although in most cases the inclination is not known. Consequently, most exoplanets found by radial velocity have <a href="/wiki/Binary_mass_function" title="Binary mass function">true masses</a> no more than 40% greater than their <a href="/wiki/Minimum_mass" title="Minimum mass">minimum masses</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> If the orbit is almost face-on, especially for superjovians detected by radial velocity, then those objects may actually be <a href="/wiki/Brown_dwarf" title="Brown dwarf">brown dwarfs</a> or even <a href="/wiki/Red_dwarf" title="Red dwarf">red dwarfs</a>. One particular example is <a href="/wiki/HD_33636" title="HD 33636">HD 33636</a> B, which has true mass 142 M<sub>J</sub>, corresponding to an M6V star, while its minimum mass was 9.28 M<sub>J</sub>.</p>
<p>If the orbit is almost edge-on, then the planet can be seen <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Transit_method" title="Transit method">transiting</a> its star.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_meaning">Other meaning</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Orbital_inclination&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Other meaning">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>For planets and other rotating celestial bodies, the angle of the equatorial plane relative to the orbital plane — such as the tilt of the Earth's poles toward or away from the Sun — is sometimes also called inclination, but less ambiguous terms are <a href="/wiki/Axial_tilt" title="Axial tilt">axial tilt</a> or obliquity.</p>

<p>where <i>h<sub>z</sub></i> is the <i>z</i>-component of <b>h</b>.</p>
<p>Mutual inclination of two orbits may be calculated from their inclinations to another plane using <a href="/wiki/Spherical_law_of_cosines" title="Spherical law of cosines">cosine rule for angles</a>.</p>
