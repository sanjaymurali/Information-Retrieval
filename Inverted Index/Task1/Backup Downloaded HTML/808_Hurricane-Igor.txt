<p><b>Hurricane Igor</b> was the most destructive <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone" title="Tropical cyclone">tropical cyclone</a> on record to strike the <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canadian</a> island of <a href="/wiki/Newfoundland_(island)" title="Newfoundland (island)">Newfoundland</a>, and the strongest hurricane of the <a href="/wiki/2010_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="2010 Atlantic hurricane season">2010 Atlantic hurricane season</a>. Igor originated from a broad <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Low_pressure_area" title="Low pressure area">area of low pressure</a> that moved off the <a href="/wiki/Cape_Verde" title="Cape Verde">Cape Verde islands</a> on the west coast of <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a> on September 6, 2010. Tracking slowly westward, it developed into a tropical depression on September 8 and strengthened into a tropical storm shortly thereafter. Higher <a href="/wiki/Wind_shear" title="Wind shear">wind shear</a> temporarily halted intensification over the following days. On September 12, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rapid_deepening#Explosive_intensification" title="Rapid deepening">explosive intensification</a> took place, and Igor reached Category 4 status on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saffir%E2%80%93Simpson_Hurricane_Scale" title="Saffir–Simpson Hurricane Scale">Saffir–Simpson Hurricane Wind Scale</a>. By this time, Igor had already begun a prolonged turn around the western periphery of the <a href="/wiki/Subtropical_ridge" title="Subtropical ridge">subtropical ridge</a>. Peaking with winds of 155 mph (250 km/h), the cyclone began to enter an area unfavorable for continued strengthening, and Igor gradually weakened before brushing <a href="/wiki/Bermuda" title="Bermuda">Bermuda</a> as a minimal hurricane on September 20. After turning northeastward, the system began an <a href="/wiki/Extratropical_cyclone#Extratropical_transition" title="Extratropical cyclone">extratropical transition</a>, which it completed shortly after striking southern Newfoundland. The remnants of Igor were later absorbed by another extratropical cyclone over the <a href="/wiki/Labrador_Sea" title="Labrador Sea">Labrador Sea</a> on September 23.</p>
<p>While the hurricane was over the open ocean, it produced large <a href="/wiki/Swell_(ocean)" title="Swell (ocean)">swells</a> that caused the deaths of three people — two in the <a href="/wiki/Caribbean" title="Caribbean">Caribbean</a> and one in the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>. As it passed west of Bermuda as a minimal hurricane, damage was limited primarily to trees and power lines, with roughly 27,500 residences losing electricity. Total losses in the territory were less than <a href="/wiki/United_States_dollar" title="United States dollar">US$</a>500,000.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[nb 1]</a></sup> However, in Newfoundland, Igor wrought extreme damage, claimed to be the worst ever seen in some areas. Large stretches of roadways were completely washed out by flooding, including a portion of the <a href="/wiki/Newfoundland_and_Labrador_Route_1" title="Newfoundland and Labrador Route 1">Trans-Canada Highway</a>, isolating approximately 150 communities. Throughout the region, one person was killed and damage costs amounted to a record <a href="/wiki/Canadian_dollar" title="Canadian dollar">C$</a>200 million. In the storm's wake, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Land_Forces_Atlantic_Area" title="Land Forces Atlantic Area">military personnel</a> were deployed to assist in recovery efforts and aid distribution.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Meteorological_history">Meteorological history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hurricane_Igor&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Meteorological history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Igor_2010_track.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1024" data-file-width="1024" height="220" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/56/Igor_2010_track.png/220px-Igor_2010_track.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/56/Igor_2010_track.png/330px-Igor_2010_track.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/56/Igor_2010_track.png/440px-Igor_2010_track.png 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Igor_2010_track.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Map plotting the track and intensity of the storm, according to the <a href="/wiki/Saffir%E2%80%93Simpson_scale" title="Saffir–Simpson scale">Saffir–Simpson scale</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Hurricane Igor was first identified as a broad <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Low_pressure_area" title="Low pressure area">area of low pressure</a> accompanying a <a href="/wiki/Tropical_wave" title="Tropical wave">tropical wave</a> over western <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a> in early September 2010. Tracking nearly due west, the system emerged into the eastern <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Ocean" title="Atlantic Ocean">Atlantic Ocean</a> on September 6. Gradual <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclogenesis" title="Tropical cyclogenesis">development</a> took place as <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_convection" title="Atmospheric convection">convection</a> – <a href="/wiki/Thunderstorm" title="Thunderstorm">thunderstorm</a> activity – consolidated around its center. At 0600 <a href="/wiki/Coordinated_Universal_Time" title="Coordinated Universal Time">UTC</a> on September 8, the low was deemed sufficiently organized to be classified as a tropical depression while situated roughly 90 mi (140 km) southeast of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cape_Verde_Islands" title="Cape Verde Islands">Cape Verde Islands</a>. Attaining gale-force winds six hours later, the depression intensified into a tropical storm and was subsequently named <i>Igor</i> by the <a href="/wiki/National_Hurricane_Center" title="National Hurricane Center">National Hurricane Center (NHC)</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TCR_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-TCR-2">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Development of Igor quickly ceased once it was named as a nearby disturbance produced moderate <a href="/wiki/Wind_shear" title="Wind shear">wind shear</a> over the storm, displacing convection from its center. Embedded within a <a href="/wiki/Monsoon_trough" title="Monsoon trough">monsoon trough</a> over the eastern Atlantic, the system maintained a slow westward track as it weakened to a tropical depression on September 9. Intensification resumed the following day once shear lessened and the dominant steering factor shifted to a mid-tropospheric <a href="/wiki/Ridge_(meteorology)" title="Ridge (meteorology)">ridge</a> north of Igor; it would remain so throughout the remainder of the cyclone's existence. Contrary to its earlier movement, the storm's forward motion markedly increased.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TCR_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-TCR-2">[1]</a></sup> Following the development of an intermittent <a href="/wiki/Eye_(cyclone)" title="Eye (cyclone)">eye feature</a> and steady convection around its center,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[2]</a></sup> Igor strengthened into a hurricane around 0000 UTC on September 12.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TCR_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-TCR-2">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Once classified a hurricane, Igor underwent <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rapid_deepening" title="Rapid deepening">explosive intensification</a> over the following 24 hours. During this time, satellite observations indicated that the storm's winds increased from 75 mph (120 km/h) to 150 mph (240 km/h) and its <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Barometric_pressure" title="Barometric pressure">barometric pressure</a> decreased by 52 <a href="/wiki/Bar_(unit)" title="Bar (unit)">mbar</a> (<a href="/wiki/Pascal_(unit)" title="Pascal (unit)">hPa</a>; 1.53 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Inches_of_Mercury" title="Inches of Mercury">inHg</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TCR_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-TCR-2">[1]</a></sup> Near the end of this phase, forecasters at the NHC predicted that Igor would attain <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/SSHS#Category_5" title="SSHS">Category 5</a> status, the highest classification on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saffir%E2%80%93Simpson_Hurricane_Scale" title="Saffir–Simpson Hurricane Scale">Saffir–Simpson Hurricane Wind Scale</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[3]</a></sup> With a symmetrical 17 mi (27 km) wide eye, deep convection and <a href="/wiki/Rainband" title="Rainband">spiral banding</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[4]</a></sup> Igor maintained <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/SSHS#Category_4" title="SSHS">Category 4</a> intensity for nearly five days. Minor fluctuations took place during this period as multiple <a href="/wiki/Eyewall_replacement_cycle" title="Eyewall replacement cycle">eyewall replacement cycles</a> occurred. After the first of these cycles, the hurricane is estimated to have reached its peak strength around 0000 UTC on September 15, with sustained winds of 155 mph (250 km/h) and an estimated minimum pressure of 924 mbar (hPa; 27.29 inHg). By this time, Igor began a northwestward track as it executed a prolonged "C-shaped" curve over the Atlantic Ocean.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TCR_2-4"><a href="#cite_note-TCR-2">[1]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tright">

<p>Passing roughly 345 mi (555 km) northeast of the <a href="/wiki/Leeward_Islands" title="Leeward Islands">Leeward Islands</a> on September 17, Igor gradually weakened as it experienced increased wind shear and dry air intrusion. Over the following several days, the storm became exceedingly large,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TCR_2-5"><a href="#cite_note-TCR-2">[1]</a></sup> with tropical storm-force winds covering an area about 865 mi (1,390 km) wide.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[5]</a></sup> Due to an overestimated bias in Igor's intensity, forecasts from the NHC showed that the storm would strike <a href="/wiki/Bermuda" title="Bermuda">Bermuda</a> as a major hurricane. However, weakening was more marked than anticipated and by the time its center neared the territory on September 20, winds decreased to 75 mph (120 km/h). Igor made its closest approach to Bermuda around 0230 UTC, passing roughly 40 mi (65 km) to the west-northwest.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TCR_2-6"><a href="#cite_note-TCR-2">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Once north of Bermuda, Igor began to undergo an <a href="/wiki/Extratropical_cyclone" title="Extratropical cyclone">extratropical transition</a> as it turned northeastward. Though deep convection was no longer consistently over its center, data from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hurricane_Hunters" title="Hurricane Hunters">Hurricane Hunters</a> continued to support hurricane intensity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[6]</a></sup> Accelerating along the leading edge of a <a href="/wiki/Trough_(meteorology)" title="Trough (meteorology)">trough</a> over the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Canadian_Maritimes" title="Canadian Maritimes">Canadian Maritimes</a>, the storm intensified within a <a href="/wiki/Weather_front" title="Weather front">baroclinic zone</a> as it neared <a href="/wiki/Newfoundland_(island)" title="Newfoundland (island)">Newfoundland</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TCR_2-7"><a href="#cite_note-TCR-2">[1]</a></sup> The interaction between these two systems allowed the hurricane to strengthen despite moving over decreasing <a href="/wiki/Sea_surface_temperature" title="Sea surface temperature">sea surface temperatures</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CHCbulletin_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-CHCbulletin-8">[7]</a></sup> Around 1500 UTC on September 21, Igor made <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Landfall_(meteorology)" title="Landfall (meteorology)">landfall</a> near <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cape_Race,_Newfoundland" title="Cape Race, Newfoundland">Cape Race</a>, with winds of 85 mph (140 km/h), slightly higher than several hours earlier. Shortly thereafter, the storm completed its transition into an extratropical cyclone, as it became fully embedded within the baroclinic zone. After turning northwestward between <a href="/wiki/Labrador" title="Labrador">Labrador</a> and <a href="/wiki/Greenland" title="Greenland">Greenland</a>, the remnants of Igor were absorbed by another extratropical cyclone on September 23, within the <a href="/wiki/Labrador_Sea" title="Labrador Sea">Labrador Sea</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TCR_2-8"><a href="#cite_note-TCR-2">[1]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Preparations_and_impact">Preparations and impact</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hurricane_Igor&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Preparations and impact">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cape_Verde_and_Leeward_Islands">Cape Verde and Leeward Islands</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hurricane_Igor&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Cape Verde and Leeward Islands">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Forming near the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cape_Verde_Islands" title="Cape Verde Islands">Cape Verde Islands</a>, Igor prompted the issuance of <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone_warnings_and_watches" title="Tropical cyclone warnings and watches">tropical storm watches</a> for the southern islands on September 8. Though the storm passed relatively close to the region, only minimal effects were recorded. Once the cyclone tracked away from Cape Verde, the watches were discontinued on September 9.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TCR_2-9"><a href="#cite_note-TCR-2">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Although several hundred miles from the <a href="/wiki/Leeward_Islands" title="Leeward Islands">Leeward Islands</a>, Igor produced large <a href="/wiki/Swell_(ocean)" title="Swell (ocean)">swells</a> averaging 9 to 13 ft (2.7 to 4.0 m) in height, between September 16 and 21. There were also large breaking waves of 15 to 20 ft (4.6 to 6.1 m) or higher. The prolonged period of this event resulted in minor coastal flooding in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/St._Croix" title="St. Croix">St. Croix</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[8]</a></sup> One person drowned near Carambola Beach Resort after being overcome by large swells.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[9]</a></sup> Similar conditions affected <a href="/wiki/Puerto_Rico" title="Puerto Rico">Puerto Rico</a> where another person drowned.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[10]</a></sup> On several occasions, <a href="/wiki/Luquillo,_Puerto_Rico" title="Luquillo, Puerto Rico">Luquillo</a> was flooded by the surf, though no damage took place.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[11]</a></sup> In nearby <a href="/wiki/Haiti" title="Haiti">Haiti</a>, still reeling from a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/January_2010_Haiti_earthquake" title="January 2010 Haiti earthquake">devastating earthquake in January</a>, officials warned residents of possible relocation from "tent cities" to safer areas. An orange alert was declared across the country, indicating that heavy rains could result in flooding.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[12]</a></sup> Portions of the <a href="/wiki/Greater_Antilles" title="Greater Antilles">Greater Antilles</a> were affected by large swells and rip currents for several days as Igor approached Bermuda.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[13]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="United_States">United States</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hurricane_Igor&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: United States">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Though Igor's closest approach to the contiguous United States only brought it within 600 mi (970 km) of land, its large circulation produced significant swells along the entire <a href="/wiki/East_Coast_of_the_United_States" title="East Coast of the United States">East Coast</a>. High surf advisories were issued in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Long_Island,_New_York" title="Long Island, New York">Long Island, New York</a> as waves of 6 to 10 ft (1.8 to 3.0 m) affected the area.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[14]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Rip_current" title="Rip current">Rip currents</a> in Florida pulled two people out to sea who were later rescued.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[15]</a></sup> One person drowned in <a href="/wiki/Surf_City,_North_Carolina" title="Surf City, North Carolina">Surf City, North Carolina</a> after being overwhelmed by rough surf.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[16]</a></sup> Along the <a href="/wiki/New_Jersey" title="New Jersey">New Jersey</a> coastline, waves averaging between 6 and 9 ft (1.8 and 2.7 m) and dangerous rip currents were reported.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[17]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Bermuda">Bermuda</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hurricane_Igor&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Bermuda">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Large_waves_in_Bermuda_from_Hurricane_Igor.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3426" data-file-width="5139" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/82/Large_waves_in_Bermuda_from_Hurricane_Igor.jpg/220px-Large_waves_in_Bermuda_from_Hurricane_Igor.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/82/Large_waves_in_Bermuda_from_Hurricane_Igor.jpg/330px-Large_waves_in_Bermuda_from_Hurricane_Igor.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/82/Large_waves_in_Bermuda_from_Hurricane_Igor.jpg/440px-Large_waves_in_Bermuda_from_Hurricane_Igor.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">

Large <a href="/wiki/Wind_wave" title="Wind wave">waves</a> impacted the coast of Bermuda for several days prior to and following Igor's passage, some of which were up to 15 feet (4.6 m) high.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15ftBMwaves_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-15ftBMwaves-19">[18]</a></sup></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Long seen to be within Igor's track, Bermuda was finally placed under a hurricane watch on September 17 as the threat became imminent. Later that day, the watch was upgraded to a warning as hurricane-force winds were anticipated to impact the islands within the following 24 hours. This remained in place for nearly three days while Igor battered Bermuda. Following the passage of hurricane winds, the advisory was changed to a tropical storm warning early on September 20 before being discontinued later that day.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TCR_2-10"><a href="#cite_note-TCR-2">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Bermudian government closed its schools and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bermuda_International_Airport" title="Bermuda International Airport">Bermuda International Airport</a> on September 20 and 21 in anticipation of Igor.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Prestorm_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-Prestorm-20">[19]</a></sup> No evacuation plans were put in place, although a local high school was converted into a shelter for residents who felt unsafe in their homes. Residents boarded up structures with plywood in order to protect windows. Tourists on the island wanting to escape the storm left more than a week before Igor's arrival. Additionally, a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/British_Royal_Navy" title="British Royal Navy">British Royal Navy</a> vessel and helicopter were stationed offshore to assist with recovery efforts once the storm passed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[20]</a></sup> Prior to Igor's arrival, there were fears that the enormous hurricane would be worse than <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Fabian" title="Hurricane Fabian">Hurricane Fabian</a> in <a href="/wiki/2003_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="2003 Atlantic hurricane season">2003</a> and could "flatten" the territory.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AFP1_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-AFP1-22">[21]</a></sup> These fears resulted from forecasts from the National Hurricane Center which indicated the storm would strike Bermuda as a Category 3 hurricane. However, following post-storm analysis, it was found that the forecast model consensus overestimated the storm's future intensity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TCR_2-11"><a href="#cite_note-TCR-2">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Although effects from Igor were observed in Bermuda for several days, relatively little rain fell, with the highest amount being 3.19 in (81 mm). Winds proved to be the most significant factor; sustained winds reached 91 mph (146 km/h) and gusts peaked at 117 mph (188 km/h) at an unofficial AWOS station on St. David's Lighthouse. Additionally, a <a href="/wiki/Storm_surge" title="Storm surge">storm surge</a> of 1.75 ft (0.53 m) took place in <a href="/wiki/St._George%27s,_Bermuda" title="St. George's, Bermuda">St. George's</a>, this combined with tides to produce a storm tide of over 4 ft (1.22 m).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TCR_2-12"><a href="#cite_note-TCR-2">[1]</a></sup> As the storm arrived, the island's airport was shut down earlier than originally planned due to the threat of <a href="/wiki/Tornado" title="Tornado">tornadoes</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Prestorm_20-1"><a href="#cite_note-Prestorm-20">[19]</a></sup> Against initial fears, Igor left relatively little damage across Bermuda. The most significant impact was from downed trees and powerlines which cut power to 27,500 residents. A few emergency rescues had to be made during the storm but no injuries took place.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AFP1_22-1"><a href="#cite_note-AFP1-22">[21]</a></sup> The causeway-bridge connecting St. George’s Islands to the main island of Bermuda sustained minor damage, leaving one lane closed for several days.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TCR_2-13"><a href="#cite_note-TCR-2">[1]</a></sup> Waves over 15 ft (4.6 m) battered the coast,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15ftBMwaves_19-1"><a href="#cite_note-15ftBMwaves-19">[18]</a></sup> breaking several ships from their moorings and tossing them into rocks.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AFP1_22-2"><a href="#cite_note-AFP1-22">[21]</a></sup> Officials in Bermuda stated that the biggest loss from Igor would be lessened tourism revenue following a mass exodus prior to the hurricane's arrival.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AFP1_22-3"><a href="#cite_note-AFP1-22">[21]</a></sup> Throughout the islands, damage from the storm was less than $500,000.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TCR_2-14"><a href="#cite_note-TCR-2">[1]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Canada">Canada</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hurricane_Igor&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Canada">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Igor_2010_rainfall.gif"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="859" data-file-width="680" height="278" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/88/Igor_2010_rainfall.gif/220px-Igor_2010_rainfall.gif" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/88/Igor_2010_rainfall.gif/330px-Igor_2010_rainfall.gif 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/88/Igor_2010_rainfall.gif/440px-Igor_2010_rainfall.gif 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Igor_2010_rainfall.gif" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Rainfall from Hurricane Igor in Newfoundland</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>On September 20, roughly one day prior to Igor's arrival in <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Canada" title="Atlantic Canada">Atlantic Canada</a>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Canadian_Hurricane_Center" title="Canadian Hurricane Center">Canadian Hurricane Center (CHC)</a> issued <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone_warnings_and_watches" title="Tropical cyclone warnings and watches">tropical storm watches and warnings</a> for southern <a href="/wiki/Newfoundland_(island)" title="Newfoundland (island)">Newfoundland</a> and the <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">French</a> territory of <a href="/wiki/Saint_Pierre_and_Miquelon" title="Saint Pierre and Miquelon">Saint Pierre and Miquelon</a>. The following day the CHC issued a hurricane watch for the eastern and northern coasts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TCR_2-15"><a href="#cite_note-TCR-2">[1]</a></sup> Although sustained hurricane-force winds were recorded across parts of the island, warnings were not issued due to stronger-than-expected re-intensification of the storm as it transitioned into an extratropical cyclone.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CanadaWMO_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-CanadaWMO-23">[22]</a></sup> Early on September 22, all advisories were discontinued as Igor moved away from the region.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TCR_2-16"><a href="#cite_note-TCR-2">[1]</a></sup> In response to the storm's arrival, schools were closed and several flights were delayed or canceled at <a href="/wiki/St._John%27s_International_Airport" title="St. John's International Airport">St. John's International Airport</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MSNBC1_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-MSNBC1-24">[23]</a></sup> Offshore, an oil rig with 110 personnel was mostly evacuated near the coast of Newfoundland on September 19.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[24]</a></sup> In comparison to <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Earl_(2010)" title="Hurricane Earl (2010)">Hurricane Earl</a> two weeks earlier, there was substantially less media attention given to Igor, attributed to the possibility of it going out to sea.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CanadaWMO_23-1"><a href="#cite_note-CanadaWMO-23">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p>The combination of a <a href="/wiki/Stationary_front" title="Stationary front">stationary front</a> and significant moisture from Hurricane Igor resulted in unprecedented rainfall across parts of eastern Newfoundland, leading to widespread flooding.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CHCbulletin_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-CHCbulletin-8">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HPC_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-HPC-26">[25]</a></sup> In <a href="/wiki/Bonavista,_Newfoundland_and_Labrador" title="Bonavista, Newfoundland and Labrador">Bonavista</a>, more than 10 in (250 mm) was estimated to have fallen between September 20 and 21.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TCR_2-17"><a href="#cite_note-TCR-2">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CanadaWMO_23-2"><a href="#cite_note-CanadaWMO-23">[22]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RainEstimate_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-RainEstimate-27">[nb 2]</a></sup> In <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/St._Lawrence,_Newfoundland" title="St. Lawrence, Newfoundland">St. Lawrence</a>, a confirmed 9.37 in (238 mm) of rain fell,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TCR_2-19"><a href="#cite_note-TCR-2">[1]</a></sup> ranking Igor as the third-wettest tropical cyclone in Canadian history.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HPC_26-1"><a href="#cite_note-HPC-26">[25]</a></sup> The widespread nature of heavy rains ranked the storm as a <a href="/wiki/Return_period" title="Return period">1-in-100 year event</a>. Similar to the rainfall, winds across eastern Newfoundland were exacerbated by the interaction of the front and Igor.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CHCbulletin_8-2"><a href="#cite_note-CHCbulletin-8">[7]</a></sup> In <a href="/wiki/Cape_Pine" title="Cape Pine">Cape Pine</a>, near where the center of Igor tracked, sustained winds of 80 mph (130 km/h) and gusts to 107 mph (172 km/h) battered the area.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TCR_2-20"><a href="#cite_note-TCR-2">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SustainedWinds_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-SustainedWinds-28">[nb 3]</a></sup> No records for sustained winds or gusts were broken; however, it was noted as a 1-in-50 year event even with powerful <a href="/wiki/Winter_storm" title="Winter storm">winter storms</a> taken into account.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CHCbulletin_8-3"><a href="#cite_note-CHCbulletin-8">[7]</a></sup> Along the coast, Igor produced a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Storm_tide" title="Storm tide">storm tide</a> of 3.5 ft (1.1 m).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TCR_2-21"><a href="#cite_note-TCR-2">[1]</a></sup> Additionally, offshore waves reached immense heights, measured up to 83.6 ft (25.5 m); one buoy reported a wave of 92 ft (28 m) but data is subject to further analysis to verify it.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CanadaWMO_23-5"><a href="#cite_note-CanadaWMO-23">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p>The most significant impacts from Igor were attributed to torrential rains, which led to excessive <a href="/wiki/Surface_runoff" title="Surface runoff">runoff</a> and flash flooding. Several rivers rose to record levels across the <a href="/wiki/Bonavista_Peninsula" title="Bonavista Peninsula">Bonavista</a> and <a href="/wiki/Burin_Peninsula" title="Burin Peninsula">Burin Peninsulas</a> where many roads were washed out. Entire bridges, homes and portions of roads were destroyed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CanadaWMO_23-6"><a href="#cite_note-CanadaWMO-23">[22]</a></sup> In some instances, flood waters were higher than entire homes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MSNBC1_24-1"><a href="#cite_note-MSNBC1-24">[23]</a></sup> In response to the widespread floods, <a href="/wiki/Newfoundland_Power_Inc." title="Newfoundland Power Inc.">Newfoundland Power Inc.</a> warned residents who still had power by the afternoon of September 21 to turn off their <a href="/wiki/Distribution_board" title="Distribution board">main electrical panel</a> if water enters their basement.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[26]</a></sup> Parts of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/St._Bernard%27s_%E2%80%93_Jacques_Fontaine" title="St. Bernard's – Jacques Fontaine">St. Bernard's – Jacques Fontaine</a> had to be evacuated by boat during the storm as rising water flooded some areas of the small town.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IgorAttack_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-IgorAttack-30">[27]</a></sup> Portions of <a href="/wiki/Clarenville" title="Clarenville">Clarenville</a> had to be evacuated under similar circumstances after a state of emergency was declared for the town.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MSNBC1_24-2"><a href="#cite_note-MSNBC1-24">[23]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IgorAttack_30-1"><a href="#cite_note-IgorAttack-30">[27]</a></sup> Water and sewage lines in <a href="/wiki/Sunnyside,_Newfoundland_and_Labrador" title="Sunnyside, Newfoundland and Labrador">Sunnyside</a> broke due to flooding. In <a href="/wiki/Glovertown" title="Glovertown">Glovertown</a>, downed power lines sparked two fires during the storm, both of which were put out by firefighters without injuries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IgorAttack_30-2"><a href="#cite_note-IgorAttack-30">[27]</a></sup></p>
<p>Roughly 150 communities were temporarily isolated as all roads leading to them were severely damaged or washed out.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CanadaWMO_23-7"><a href="#cite_note-CanadaWMO-23">[22]</a></sup> Overall damage to roadways was regarded as "colossal" by Tom Hedderson, the minister for emergency preparedness.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TWFP1_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-TWFP1-31">[28]</a></sup> A 100 ft (30 m) section of the <a href="/wiki/Newfoundland_and_Labrador_Route_1" title="Newfoundland and Labrador Route 1">Trans-Canada Highway</a> in <a href="/wiki/Terra_Nova_National_Park" title="Terra Nova National Park">Terra Nova National Park</a> was severely eroded, leaving a large ravine behind and disconnecting the main population of Newfoundland from the rest of the island, while a bridge washout on the Burin Peninsula left 20,000 people cut off from the rest of the island.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CanadaWMO_23-8"><a href="#cite_note-CanadaWMO-23">[22]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MSNBC1_24-3"><a href="#cite_note-MSNBC1-24">[23]</a></sup> Public infrastructure losses were estimated in excess of $100 million, mainly attributed to roadways.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[29]</a></sup> On <a href="/wiki/Random_Island" title="Random Island">Random Island</a>, a man was killed when his driveway collapsed from flooding and he was swept out to sea.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MSNBC1_24-4"><a href="#cite_note-MSNBC1-24">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p>In addition to flood damage, hurricane-force winds downed trees and power lines over large areas and many homes were damaged to varying degrees.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CanadaWMO_23-9"><a href="#cite_note-CanadaWMO-23">[22]</a></sup> An estimated 50,000 residences were left without electricity in the region.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TorontoSun1_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-TorontoSun1-33">[30]</a></sup> The small coastal community of South East Bight sustained some of the most significant wind damage where entire fishing sheds were hurled into the air.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TS1_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-TS1-34">[31]</a></sup> Along the East Coast hiking trail, an estimated 5,000 trees were downed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[32]</a></sup> In all, losses from the hurricane were placed at $200 million, ranking it as the costliest cyclone in Newfoundland history.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TCR_2-22"><a href="#cite_note-TCR-2">[1]</a></sup> In terms of its overall impact, Igor was regarded as the worst storm of tropical origin to hit Newfoundland since <a href="/wiki/1935_Atlantic_hurricane_season#Hurricane_Two" title="1935 Atlantic hurricane season">1935</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Government_of_Canada_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-Government_of_Canada-36">[33]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Aftermath">Aftermath</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hurricane_Igor&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Aftermath">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:282px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Igor_sept_21_2010_1445Z.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1080" data-file-width="1920" height="158" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cb/Igor_sept_21_2010_1445Z.jpg/280px-Igor_sept_21_2010_1445Z.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cb/Igor_sept_21_2010_1445Z.jpg/420px-Igor_sept_21_2010_1445Z.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cb/Igor_sept_21_2010_1445Z.jpg/560px-Igor_sept_21_2010_1445Z.jpg 2x" width="280"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Igor_sept_21_2010_1445Z.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Satellite image of Hurricane Igor on September 21 as it made landfall on Newfoundland</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>In Igor's wake, a <a href="/wiki/State_of_emergency" title="State of emergency">state of emergency</a> was declared for 30 communities in Newfoundland.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TS1_34-1"><a href="#cite_note-TS1-34">[31]</a></sup> The mayor of one of the towns affected by Igor, Sam Synard, remarked that "We've never seen such a violent storm before."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MSNBC1_24-5"><a href="#cite_note-MSNBC1-24">[23]</a></sup> More than 50 families involved in the storm-induced destruction were relocated to evacuation shelters.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TS1_34-2"><a href="#cite_note-TS1-34">[31]</a></sup> Electricity was gradually restored to residents; by six days after the hurricane's passage, a few hundred had yet to regain power.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TorontoSun1_33-1"><a href="#cite_note-TorontoSun1-33">[30]</a></sup></p>
<p>In light of the widespread damage, 1,000 personnel from the <a href="/wiki/Land_Force_Atlantic_Area" title="Land Force Atlantic Area">Canadian military</a> were brought in for recovery efforts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CanadaWMO_23-10"><a href="#cite_note-CanadaWMO-23">[22]</a></sup> The military operation, known as <i>Operation Lama</i>, was to last for the duration of the emergency phase in the immediate aftermath. An initial deployment of 120 soldiers and 40 vehicles arrived on September 25 from the <a href="/wiki/CFB_Gagetown" title="CFB Gagetown">Canadian Forces Base Gagetown</a> in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oromocto,_New_Brunswick" title="Oromocto, New Brunswick">Oromocto, New Brunswick</a> arrived in Newfoundland.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OpLama_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-OpLama-37">[34]</a></sup> Three ships and a fleet of helicopters were called in for the distribution of emergency aid and the military set up temporary bridges while long-term plans for reconstruction were made.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TorontoSun1_33-2"><a href="#cite_note-TorontoSun1-33">[30]</a></sup> <i>Operation Lama</i> also enabled partial repairs of several buildings, and simultaneously, emergency personnel surveyed roughly 500 mi (900 km) of roads in Newfoundland.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OpLama_37-1"><a href="#cite_note-OpLama-37">[34]</a></sup> On September 27, government officials stated that it would take months for cleanup efforts to finish.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TorontoSun1_33-3"><a href="#cite_note-TorontoSun1-33">[30]</a></sup> Ten days after the storm, six towns were still isolated as progress on temporary road reconstruction was being made only slowly.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[35]</a></sup> Allocation of military aid continued through October 6, though the number of on-scene personnel began to decrease two days earlier.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[36]</a></sup></p>
<p>Roughly a month after the storm, a benefit concert was held in <a href="/wiki/St._John%27s,_Newfoundland_and_Labrador" title="St. John's, Newfoundland and Labrador">St. John's</a>. The concert raised about $400,000 (2010 <a href="/wiki/Canadian_dollar" title="Canadian dollar">CAD</a>) toward storm relief in one night.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[37]</a></sup> In late October, the Government of Newfoundland and Labrador allocated $275,000 in funds for 200 workers who lost their jobs after two fish processing plants were damaged in <a href="/wiki/Marystown" title="Marystown">Marystown</a> and <a href="/wiki/Port_Union,_Newfoundland_and_Labrador" title="Port Union, Newfoundland and Labrador">Port Union</a>. However, union leaders claimed that the assistance was insufficient and accounted for just 420 of 600 work hours.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[38]</a></sup> Following more than a year of discussions, the plant's parent company, Ocean Choice International, decided to permanently shut down operations on December 2, 2011.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[39]</a></sup> Numerous insurance claims, reaching $65 million by early November, were made following Hurricane Igor's landfall<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[40]</a></sup> Over the course of the recovery phase, several complaints, including concerns lodged by church ministers, that relief funds for victims of the storm were not being distributed fast enough and the overall process was taking too long.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[41]</a></sup> Roughly ten months after the hurricane, work on permanent reconstruction of roadways began in several areas across the Burin Peninsula.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[42]</a></sup> By January 24, 2011, <a href="/wiki/The_Salvation_Army" title="The Salvation Army">The Salvation Army</a> received about $1.6 million worth of donations for residents affected by Hurricane Igor, roughly half of which had been distributed at that point. An additional $200,000 had been pledged by donors.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[43]</a></sup></p>

<p>In June 2011, a federal document detailing the actions made by province officials was released to be public and incited further outcry from residents affected by the storm. The main complaint raised was related to the delay in accepting federal aid despite the severity of the damage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[44]</a></sup> In an email sent on September 21, 2010, Denys Doiron, the nation's Emergency Preparedness and Response Officer, relayed that local emergency services reported Igor to be the worst disaster they have had to deal with. Doiron also stated that no requests for federal aid were made.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[45]</a></sup></p>
<p>Nearly a year after the passage of Igor, a <a href="/wiki/Weather_buoy" title="Weather buoy">weather buoy</a> from St. John's was spotted near the <a href="/wiki/Shetland" title="Shetland">Shetland Islands</a> north of <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a> on September 12, 2011.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[46]</a></sup> In early October, Newfoundland was again struck by a tropical cyclone – <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Ophelia_(2011)" title="Hurricane Ophelia (2011)">Hurricane Ophelia</a>. Though a weaker storm, Ophelia caused more damage than expected as it destroyed infrastructure repaired in the wake of Igor. The mayor of Marystown criticized government officials for not increasing the size of repairs, especially to culverts, to account for flooding.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[47]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Retirement">Retirement</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hurricane_Igor&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Retirement">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/List_of_retired_Atlantic_hurricane_names" title="List of retired Atlantic hurricane names">List of retired Atlantic hurricane names</a></div>
<p>Due to the extensive damage in Newfoundland, the name <i>Igor</i> was <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone_naming#Retirement" title="Tropical cyclone naming">retired</a> in the spring of 2011 by request of the Meteorological Service of Canada, and will never again be used for an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/North_Atlantic_tropical_cyclone" title="North Atlantic tropical cyclone">Atlantic hurricane</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[48]</a></sup> In Canada's report to the World Meteorological Organization, it was stated to be a storm without parallel in the island's history. This was only the second time that the Meteorological Service of Canada had requested that a hurricane name be retired (the first was <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Juan" title="Hurricane Juan">Hurricane Juan</a> in 2003).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[49]</a></sup> The hurricane is also considered a probable benchmark for future cyclones in the region, having illustrated to residents that they are not immune from hurricanes and their impacts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CanadaWMO_23-11"><a href="#cite_note-CanadaWMO-23">[22]</a></sup> The name was replaced by <i>Ian</i> and was first used during the <a href="/wiki/2016_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="2016 Atlantic hurricane season">2016 season</a>.</p>
