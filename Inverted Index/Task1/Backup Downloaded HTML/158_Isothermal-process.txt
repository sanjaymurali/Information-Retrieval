<p>An <b>isothermal process</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Thermodynamic_process" title="Thermodynamic process">change</a> of a <i>system</i>, in which the <a href="/wiki/Temperature" title="Temperature">temperature</a> remains constant: Δ<i>T</i> = 0. This typically occurs when a system is in contact with an outside thermal reservoir (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heat_bath" title="Heat bath">heat bath</a>), and the change will occur slowly enough to allow the system to continually adjust to the temperature of the reservoir through <a href="/wiki/Heat" title="Heat">heat</a> exchange. In contrast, an <i><a href="/wiki/Adiabatic_process" title="Adiabatic process">adiabatic process</a></i> is where a system exchanges no heat with its surroundings (<i>Q</i> = 0). In other words, in an isothermal process, the value Δ<i>T</i> = 0 and therefore Δ<i>U</i> = 0 (only for an ideal gas) but Q ≠ 0, while in an adiabatic process, Δ<i>T</i> ≠ 0 but Q = 0.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Examples">Examples</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Isothermal_process&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Examples">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Isothermal processes can occur in any kind of system that has some means of regulating the temperature, including highly structured <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Machines" title="Machines">machines</a>, and even <a href="/wiki/Life" title="Life">living</a> cells. Some parts of the cycles of some <a href="/wiki/Heat_engine" title="Heat engine">heat engines</a> are carried out isothermally (for example, in the <a href="/wiki/Carnot_cycle" title="Carnot cycle">Carnot cycle</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> In the thermodynamic analysis of <a href="/wiki/Chemical_thermodynamics" title="Chemical thermodynamics">chemical reactions</a>, it is usual to first analyze what happens under isothermal conditions and then consider the effect of temperature.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:Rock_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-:Rock-2">[2]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Phase_changes" title="Phase changes">Phase changes</a>, such as <a href="/wiki/Melting" title="Melting">melting</a> or <a href="/wiki/Evaporation" title="Evaporation">evaporation</a>, are also isothermal processes when, as is usually the case, they occur at constant pressure.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Petrucci_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Petrucci-3">[3]</a></sup> Isothermal processes are often used and a starting point in analyzing more complex, non-isothermal processes.</p>
<p>Isothermal processes are of special interest for ideal gases. This is a consequence of <a href="/wiki/Joule_effect#Joule.27s_second_law" title="Joule effect">Joule's second law</a> which states that the <a href="/wiki/Internal_energy" title="Internal energy">internal energy</a> of a fixed amount of an ideal gas depends only on its temperature.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Klotz_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Klotz-4">[4]</a></sup> Thus, in an isothermal process the internal energy of an ideal gas is constant. This is a result of the fact that in an ideal gas there are no <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Intermolecular_forces" title="Intermolecular forces">intermolecular forces</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Klotz_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-Klotz-4">[4]</a></sup> Note that this is true only for ideal gases; the internal energy depends on pressure as well as on temperature for liquids, solids, and real gases.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the isothermal compression of a gas there is work done on the system to decrease the volume and increase the pressure.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Klotz_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-Klotz-4">[4]</a></sup> Doing work on the gas increases the internal energy and will tend to increase the temperature. To maintain the constant temperature energy must leave the system as heat and enter the environment. If the gas is ideal, the amount of energy entering the environment is equal to the work done on the gas, because internal energy does not change. For details of the calculations, see <a href="/wiki/Isothermal_process#Calculation_of_work" title="Isothermal process">calculation of work</a>.</p>
<p>For an <a href="/wiki/Adiabatic_process" title="Adiabatic process">adiabatic process</a>, in which no heat flows into or out of the gas because its container is well insulated, <i>Q</i> = 0. If there is also no work done, i.e. a <a href="/wiki/Joule_expansion" title="Joule expansion">free expansion</a>, there is no change in internal energy. For an ideal gas, this means that the process is also isothermal.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Klotz_4-3"><a href="#cite_note-Klotz-4">[4]</a></sup> Thus, specifying that a process is isothermal is not sufficient to specify a unique process.</p>

<p>By convention, work is defined as the work on the system by its surroundings. If, for example, the system is compressed, then the work is positive and the internal energy of the system increases. Conversely, if the system expands, it does work on the surroundings and the internal energy of the system decreases.</p>
<p>It is also worth noting that for ideal gases, if the temperature is held constant, the internal energy of the system also is constant, and so Δ<i>U</i> = 0. Since the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/First_Law_of_Thermodynamics" title="First Law of Thermodynamics">First Law of Thermodynamics</a> states that Δ<i>U</i> = <i>Q</i> + <i>W</i> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/IUPAC" title="IUPAC">IUPAC</a> convention), it follows that <i>Q</i> = −<i>W</i> for the isothermal compression or expansion of ideal gases.</p>

<p>Once obtained, these formulas can be applied to an <a href="/wiki/Irreversible_process" title="Irreversible process">irreversible process</a>, such as the <a href="/wiki/Joule_expansion" title="Joule expansion">free expansion</a> of an ideal gas. Such an expansion is also isothermal and may have the same initial and final states as in the reversible expansion. Since entropy is a <a href="/wiki/State_function" title="State function">state function</a>, the change in entropy of the system is the same as in the reversible process and is given by the formulas above. Note that the result <i>Q</i> = 0 for the free expansion can not be used in the formula for the entropy change since the process is not reversible.</p>
<p>The difference between the reversible and free expansions is found in the entropy of the surroundings. In both cases, the surroundings are at a constant temperature, <i>T</i>, so that Δ<i>S</i><sub>sur</sub> = −<span class="sfrac nowrap" style="display:inline-block; vertical-align:-0.5em; font-size:85%; text-align:center;"><span style="display:block; line-height:1em; margin:0 0.1em;"><i>Q</i></span><span class="visualhide">/</span><span style="display:block; line-height:1em; margin:0 0.1em; border-top:1px solid;"><i>T</i></span></span>; the minus sign is used since the heat transferred to the surroundings is equal in magnitude and opposite in sign to the heat, <i>Q</i>, transferred to the system. In the reversible case, the change in entropy of the surroundings is equal and opposite to the change in the system, so the change in entropy of the universe is zero. In the free expansion, <i>Q</i> = 0, so the entropy of the surroundings does not change and the change in entropy of the universe is equal to Δ<i>S</i> for the system.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Isothermal_process&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
