<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Dropsonde</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"><span class="mw-redirectedfrom">  (Redirected from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/w/index.php?title=GPS_dropsonde&amp;redirect=no" title="GPS dropsonde">GPS dropsonde</a>)</span></div>

<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">This article is about the weather reconnaissance device. <div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Dropsonde_delivery_system.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="280" data-file-width="427" height="144" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/06/Dropsonde_delivery_system.jpg/220px-Dropsonde_delivery_system.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/06/Dropsonde_delivery_system.jpg/330px-Dropsonde_delivery_system.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/0/06/Dropsonde_delivery_system.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Dropsonde_delivery_system.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Dropsonde delivery system on a NOAA P-3 Hurricane Hunter.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>A <b>dropsonde</b> is an expendable <a href="/wiki/Weather" title="Weather">weather</a> reconnaissance device created by the <a href="/wiki/National_Center_for_Atmospheric_Research" title="National Center for Atmospheric Research">National Center for Atmospheric Research</a> (NCAR), designed to be dropped from an <a href="/wiki/Aircraft" title="Aircraft">aircraft</a> at altitude to more accurately measure (and therefore track) tropical storm conditions as the device falls to the surface. The sonde contains a <a href="/wiki/Global_Positioning_System" title="Global Positioning System">GPS</a> receiver, along with <a href="/wiki/Pressure" title="Pressure">pressure</a>, <a href="/wiki/Temperature" title="Temperature">temperature</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Humidity" title="Humidity">humidity</a> (PTH) <a href="/wiki/Sensor" title="Sensor">sensors</a> to capture <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere" title="Atmosphere">atmospheric</a> profiles and <a href="/wiki/Thermodynamics" title="Thermodynamics">thermodynamic</a> <a href="/wiki/Data" title="Data">data</a>. It typically relays these data to a computer in the aircraft by radio transmission.</p>
<p>The device's descent is slowed and stabilized by a small square-cone parachute, allowing for multiple readings to be taken before it reaches the ocean surface. The sonde is a lightweight system designed to be operated by one person and is launched through a chute installed in the measuring aircraft, with immediate deployment of the parachute to reduce or eliminate any <a href="/wiki/Pendulum" title="Pendulum">pendulum</a> effect, and typically drops from three to five minutes. The sonde has a casing of stiff cardboard. During a typical hurricane season, the <a href="/wiki/53d_Weather_Reconnaissance_Squadron" title="53d Weather Reconnaissance Squadron">53d Weather Reconnaissance Squadron</a> Hurricane Hunters deploys 1000 to 1500 sondes on training and storm missions.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dropsonde&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:282px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Dropsonde.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="777" data-file-width="640" height="340" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/24/Dropsonde.png/280px-Dropsonde.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/24/Dropsonde.png/420px-Dropsonde.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/24/Dropsonde.png/560px-Dropsonde.png 2x" width="280"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Dropsonde.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
A diagram of an NCAR GPS Dropsonde</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Dropsondes are commonly used by <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_hunters" title="Hurricane hunters">hurricane hunters</a> to obtain meteorological data on <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone" title="Tropical cyclone">hurricanes</a>, and these data are then fed into supercomputers for <a href="/wiki/Numerical_weather_prediction" title="Numerical weather prediction">numerical weather prediction</a>, enabling forecasters to track and predict what will happen to the hurricane. To obtain the data, an aircraft, either operated by NOAA or the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._Air_Force" title="U.S. Air Force">U.S. Air Force</a> flies into the hurricane. The dropsonde is released when the plane reaches the eye (center) of the hurricane, normally at around 10,000 feet (approx. 3,000 meters). The dropsonde sends back coded data, which includes:</p>
<ol>
<li>The date and time of the drop. Time is always in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/UTC" title="UTC">UTC</a>.</li>
<li>Location of the drop, indicated by the <a href="/wiki/Latitude" title="Latitude">latitude</a>, <a href="/wiki/Longitude" title="Longitude">longitude</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Marsden_square" title="Marsden square">Marsden square</a>.</li>
<li>Standard isobaric surfaces: the height, temperature, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dewpoint_depression" title="Dewpoint depression">dewpoint depression</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wind_speed" title="Wind speed">wind speed</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Wind_direction" title="Wind direction">wind direction</a> at the surface, and at where the following air pressures are found as the dropsonde is descending: 1000, 925, 850, 700, 500, 400, 300, 250 <a href="/wiki/Pascal_(unit)#Hectopascal_and_millibar_units" title="Pascal (unit)">hectopascals</a> (hPa).</li>
<li>Significant isobaric surfaces: the temperature and dewpoint depression of other levels of atmospheric pressure deemed significant</li>
<li>Air pressure, temperature, dewpoint depression, wind speed and wind direction of the <a href="/wiki/Tropopause" title="Tropopause">tropopause</a>.</li>
</ol>
<p>Also included in the report is information on the aircraft, the mission, the dropsonde itself, and other remarks.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Driftsondes">Driftsondes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dropsonde&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Driftsondes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Dropsonde_U.S._Air_Force.jpeg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1233" data-file-width="1127" height="241" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3b/Dropsonde_U.S._Air_Force.jpeg/220px-Dropsonde_U.S._Air_Force.jpeg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3b/Dropsonde_U.S._Air_Force.jpeg/330px-Dropsonde_U.S._Air_Force.jpeg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3b/Dropsonde_U.S._Air_Force.jpeg/440px-Dropsonde_U.S._Air_Force.jpeg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Dropsonde_U.S._Air_Force.jpeg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Dropsonde (source <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/US_Air_Force" title="US Air Force">US Air Force</a>)</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>A driftsonde is a high altitude, durable <a href="/wiki/Weather_balloon" title="Weather balloon">weather balloon</a> holding a transmitter and a bank (35 in the first models) of miniature dropsonde capsules. These water-bottle-sized transmitters have enough power to send information to the balloon during their parachute-controlled fall. The balloon carries a transmitter powerful enough to relay readings to a satellite. The single-use sensor packages cost US$300 to $400 each.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SciDay_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-SciDay-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>After being introduced in April 2007, around a thousand a year are expected to be used to track winds in hurricane breeding grounds off of West Africa, which are outside the operating areas of Hurricane Hunter planes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SciDay_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-SciDay-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
