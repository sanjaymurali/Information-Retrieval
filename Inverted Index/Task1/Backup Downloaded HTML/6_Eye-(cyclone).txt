<p>The <b>eye</b> is a region of mostly calm weather at the center of strong <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone" title="Tropical cyclone">tropical cyclones</a>. The eye of a storm is a roughly circular area, typically 30–65 km (20–40 miles) in diameter. It is surrounded by the <b>eyewall</b>, a ring of towering <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thunderstorms" title="Thunderstorms">thunderstorms</a> where the most severe weather and highest winds occur. The cyclone's lowest <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Barometric_pressure" title="Barometric pressure">barometric pressure</a> occurs in the eye and can be as much as 15 percent lower than the pressure outside the storm.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FAQ_eye_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-FAQ_eye-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>In strong tropical cyclones, the eye is characterized by light winds and clear skies, surrounded on all sides by a towering, symmetric eyewall. In weaker tropical cyclones, the eye is less well defined and can be covered by the <a href="/wiki/Central_dense_overcast" title="Central dense overcast">central dense overcast</a>, an area of high, thick clouds that show up brightly on <a href="/wiki/Weather_satellite" title="Weather satellite">satellite imagery</a>. Weaker or disorganized storms may also feature an eyewall that does not completely encircle the eye or have an eye that features heavy rain. In all storms, however, the eye is the location of the storm's minimum barometric pressure - where the atmospheric pressure at sea level is the lowest.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FAQ_eye_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-FAQ_eye-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FAQ_CDO_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-FAQ_CDO-2">[2]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Structure">Structure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eye_(cyclone)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Structure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:402px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Hurricane-en.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="512" data-file-width="1173" height="175" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4f/Hurricane-en.svg/400px-Hurricane-en.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4f/Hurricane-en.svg/600px-Hurricane-en.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4f/Hurricane-en.svg/800px-Hurricane-en.svg.png 2x" width="400"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Hurricane-en.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
A <a href="/wiki/Cross_section_(geometry)" title="Cross section (geometry)">cross section</a> diagram of a mature tropical cyclone, with arrows indicating air flow in and around the eye</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>A typical <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone" title="Tropical cyclone">tropical cyclone</a> will have an eye of approximately 30–65 km (20–40 mi) across, usually situated at the geometric center of the storm. The eye may be clear or have spotty low clouds (a <i>clear eye</i>), it may be filled with <a href="/wiki/Cloud#Middle_clouds_.28Family_B.29" title="Cloud">low- and mid-level clouds</a> (a <i>filled eye</i>), or it may be obscured by the central dense overcast. There is, however, very little wind and rain, especially near the center. This is in stark contrast to conditions in the eyewall, which contains the storm's strongest winds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JetStream_structure_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-JetStream_structure-3">[3]</a></sup> Due to the <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone#Mechanics" title="Tropical cyclone">mechanics of a tropical cyclone</a>, the eye and the air directly above it are warmer than their surroundings.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AOML_FAQ_A7_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-AOML_FAQ_A7-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>While normally quite symmetric, eyes can be oblong and irregular, especially in weakening storms. A large <i>ragged eye</i> is a non-circular eye which appears fragmented, and is an indicator of a weak or weakening tropical cyclone. An <i>open eye</i> is an eye which can be circular, but the eyewall does not completely encircle the eye, also indicating a weakening, moisture-deprived cyclone. Both of these observations are used to estimate the intensity of tropical cyclones via <a href="/wiki/Dvorak_technique" title="Dvorak technique">Dvorak analysis</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ODT_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-ODT-5">[5]</a></sup> Eyewalls are typically circular; however, distinctly polygonal shapes ranging from triangles to hexagons occasionally occur.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-polygonal_eyewalls_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-polygonal_eyewalls-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>While typical mature storms have eyes that are a few dozen miles across, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rapid_deepening" title="Rapid deepening">rapidly intensifying</a> storms can develop an extremely small, clear, and circular eye, sometimes referred to as a <i>pinhole eye</i>. Storms with pinhole eyes are prone to large fluctuations in intensity, and provide difficulties and frustrations for forecasters.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>Small/minuscule eyes—those less than 10 <a href="/wiki/Nautical_mile" title="Nautical mile">nmi</a> (19 km, 12 mi) across—often trigger <a href="#Eyewall_replacement_cycles">eyewall replacement cycles</a>, where a new eyewall begins to form outside the original eyewall. This can take place anywhere from fifteen to hundreds of kilometers (ten to a few hundred miles) outside the inner eye. The storm then develops two <i>concentric eyewalls</i>, or an "eye within an eye". In most cases, the outer eyewall begins to contract soon after its formation, which chokes off the inner eye and leaves a much larger but more stable eye. While the replacement cycle tends to weaken storms as it occurs, the new eyewall can contract fairly quickly after the old eyewall dissipates, allowing the storm to re-strengthen. This may trigger another re-strengthen cycle of eyewall replacement.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AOML_FAQ_D8_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-AOML_FAQ_D8-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>Eyes can range in size from 370 km (230 mi) (<a href="/wiki/1960_Pacific_typhoon_season#Typhoon_Carmen" title="1960 Pacific typhoon season">Typhoon Carmen</a>)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> to a mere 3.7 km (2.3 mi) (<a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Wilma" title="Hurricane Wilma">Hurricane Wilma</a>) across.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> While it is uncommon for storms with large eyes to become very intense, it does occur, especially in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Annular_hurricane" title="Annular hurricane">annular hurricanes</a>. <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Isabel" title="Hurricane Isabel">Hurricane Isabel</a> was the eleventh most powerful <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/North_Atlantic_hurricane" title="North Atlantic hurricane">North Atlantic hurricane</a> in <a href="/wiki/Recorded_history" title="Recorded history">recorded history</a>, and sustained a large, 65–80 km (40–50 mi)-wide eye for a period of several days.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Isabel_TCR_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Isabel_TCR-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Formation_and_detection">Formation and detection</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eye_(cyclone)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Formation and detection">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclogenesis" title="Tropical cyclogenesis">Tropical cyclogenesis</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">

<p>Tropical cyclones typically form from large, disorganized areas of disturbed weather in tropical regions. As more thunderstorms form and gather, the storm develops <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone#Physical_structure" title="Tropical cyclone">rainbands</a> which start rotating around a common center. As the storm gains strength, a ring of stronger <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_convection" title="Atmospheric convection">convection</a> forms at a certain distance from the rotational center of the developing storm. Since stronger thunderstorms and heavier rain mark areas of stronger <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Updraft" title="Updraft">updrafts</a>, the barometric pressure at the surface begins to drop, and air begins to build up in the upper levels of the cyclone.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-eye_formation_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-eye_formation-12">[12]</a></sup> This results in the formation of an upper level <a href="/wiki/Anticyclone" title="Anticyclone">anticyclone</a>, or an area of high atmospheric pressure above the central dense overcast. Consequently, most of this built up air flows outward anticyclonically above the tropical cyclone. Outside the forming eye, the anticyclone at the upper levels of the atmosphere enhances the flow towards the center of the cyclone, pushing air towards the eyewall and causing a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Positive_feedback_loop" title="Positive feedback loop">positive feedback loop</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-eye_formation_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-eye_formation-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>However, a small portion of the built-up air, instead of flowing outward, flows inward towards the center of the storm. This causes air pressure to build even further, to the point where the weight of the air counteracts the strength of the updrafts in the center of the storm. Air begins to descend in the center of the storm, creating a mostly rain-free area—a newly formed eye.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-eye_formation_12-2"><a href="#cite_note-eye_formation-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>There are many aspects of this process which remain a mystery. Scientists do not know why a ring of convection forms around the center of circulation instead of on top of it, or why the upper-level anticyclone only ejects a portion of the excess air above the storm. Many theories exist as to the exact process by which the eye forms: all that is known for sure is that the eye is necessary for tropical cyclones to achieve high wind speeds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-eye_formation_12-3"><a href="#cite_note-eye_formation-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>The formation of an eye is almost always an indicator of increasing tropical cyclone organisation and strength. Because of this, forecasters watch developing storms closely for signs of eye formation.</p>
<p>For storms with a clear eye, detection of the eye is as simple as looking at pictures from a <a href="/wiki/Weather_satellite" title="Weather satellite">weather satellite</a>. However, for storms with a filled eye, or an eye completely covered by the central dense overcast, other detection methods must be used. Observations from ships and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hurricane_Hunters" title="Hurricane Hunters">Hurricane Hunters</a> can pinpoint an eye visually, by looking for a drop in wind speed or lack of rainfall in the storm's center. In the United States, South Korea, and a few other countries, a network of <a href="/wiki/NEXRAD" title="NEXRAD">NEXRAD</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Doppler_weather_radar" title="Doppler weather radar">Doppler weather radar</a> stations can detect eyes near the coast. Weather satellites also carry equipment for measuring atmospheric <a href="/wiki/Water_vapor#Radar_and_satellite_imaging" title="Water vapor">water vapor</a> and cloud temperatures, which can be used to spot a forming eye. In addition, scientists have recently discovered that the amount of ozone in the eye is much higher than the amount in the eyewall, due to air sinking from the ozone-rich stratosphere. Instruments sensitive to ozone perform measurements, which are used to observe rising and sinking columns of air, and provide indication of the formation of an eye, even before satellite imagery can determine its formation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Eyewall_replacement_cycles">Eyewall replacement cycles</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eye_(cyclone)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Eyewall replacement cycles">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Eyewall_replacement_cycle" title="Eyewall replacement cycle">Eyewall replacement cycle</a></div>
<p><i>Eyewall replacement cycles</i>, also called <i>concentric eyewall cycles</i>, naturally occur in intense tropical cyclones, generally with winds greater than 185 km/h (115 mph), or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Major_hurricane" title="Major hurricane">major hurricanes</a> (Category 3 or higher on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saffir%E2%80%93Simpson_hurricane_scale" title="Saffir–Simpson hurricane scale">Saffir–Simpson hurricane scale</a>). When tropical cyclones reach this intensity, and the eyewall contracts or is already sufficiently small (see <a href="#Structure">above</a>), some of the outer rainbands may strengthen and organize into a ring of thunderstorms—an outer eyewall—that slowly moves inward and robs the inner eyewall of its needed moisture and <a href="/wiki/Angular_momentum" title="Angular momentum">angular momentum</a>. Since the strongest winds are located in a cyclone's eyewall, the tropical cyclone usually weakens during this phase, as the inner wall is "choked" by the outer wall. Eventually the outer eyewall replaces the inner one completely, and the storm can re-intensify.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AOML_FAQ_D8_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-AOML_FAQ_D8-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>The discovery of this process was partially responsible for the end of the U.S. government's hurricane modification experiment <a href="/wiki/Project_Stormfury" title="Project Stormfury">Project Stormfury</a>. This project set out to <a href="/wiki/Cloud_seeding" title="Cloud seeding">seed clouds</a> outside the eyewall, causing a new eyewall to form and weakening the storm. When it was discovered that this was a natural process due to hurricane dynamics, the project was quickly abandoned.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AOML_FAQ_D8_8-2"><a href="#cite_note-AOML_FAQ_D8-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>Almost every intense hurricane undergoes at least one of these cycles during its existence. <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Allen" title="Hurricane Allen">Hurricane Allen</a> in 1980 went through repeated eyewall replacement cycles, fluctuating between Category 5 and Category 3 status on the Saffir-Simpson scale several times. <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Juliette_(2001)" title="Hurricane Juliette (2001)">Hurricane Juliette</a> was a rare documented case of triple eyewalls.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Triple_Eyewalls_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-Triple_Eyewalls-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Moats">Moats</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eye_(cyclone)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Moats">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A <i>moat</i> in a tropical cyclone is a clear ring outside the eyewall, or between concentric eyewalls, characterized by <a href="/wiki/Subsidence_(atmosphere)" title="Subsidence (atmosphere)">subsidence</a>—slowly sinking air—and little or no precipitation. The air flow in the moat is dominated by the cumulative effects of <a href="/wiki/Deformation_(mechanics)#Normal_strain" title="Deformation (mechanics)">stretching</a> and <a href="/wiki/Deformation_(mechanics)#Shear_strain" title="Deformation (mechanics)">shearing</a>. The moat between eyewalls is an area in the storm where the rotational speed of the air changes greatly in proportion to the distance from the storm's center; these areas are also known as <i>rapid filamentation zones</i>. Such areas can potentially be found near any <a href="/wiki/Vortex" title="Vortex">vortex</a> of sufficient strength, but are most pronounced in strong tropical cyclones.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rapid_Filamentation_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-Rapid_Filamentation-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Eyewall_mesovortices">Eyewall mesovortices</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eye_(cyclone)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Eyewall mesovortices">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><i>Eyewall <a href="/wiki/Mesovortices" title="Mesovortices">mesovortices</a></i> are small scale rotational features found in the eyewalls of intense tropical cyclones. They are similar, in principle, to small "suction vortices" often observed in <a href="/wiki/Multiple-vortex_tornado" title="Multiple-vortex tornado">multiple-vortex tornadoes</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Montgomery_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-Montgomery-16">[16]</a></sup> In these vortices, wind speeds may be greater than anywhere else in the eyewall.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ARecordWind_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-ARecordWind-17">[17]</a></sup> Eyewall mesovortices are most common during periods of intensification in tropical cyclones.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Montgomery_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-Montgomery-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>Eyewall mesovortices often exhibit unusual behavior in tropical cyclones. They usually rotate around the low pressure center, but sometimes they remain stationary. Eyewall mesovortices have even been documented to cross the eye of a storm. These phenomena have been documented observationally,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Vortical_Swirls_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-Vortical_Swirls-18">[18]</a></sup> experimentally,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Montgomery_16-2"><a href="#cite_note-Montgomery-16">[16]</a></sup> and theoretically.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mesovortices_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-Mesovortices-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>Eyewall mesovortices are a significant factor in the formation of <a href="/wiki/Tornado" title="Tornado">tornadoes</a> after tropical cyclone landfall. Mesovortices can spawn rotation in individual thunderstorms (a <a href="/wiki/Mesocyclone" title="Mesocyclone">mesocyclone</a>), which leads to tornadic activity. At landfall, friction is generated between the circulation of the tropical cyclone and land. This can allow the mesovortices to descend to the surface, causing tornadoes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WSR88DMeso_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-WSR88DMeso-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Stadium_effect">Stadium effect</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eye_(cyclone)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Stadium effect">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

View of <a href="/wiki/Typhoon_Maysak_(2015)" title="Typhoon Maysak (2015)">Typhoon Maysak's</a> eye from the <a href="/wiki/International_Space_Station" title="International Space Station">International Space Station</a> on March 31, 2015, displaying a pronounced stadium effect.</div>

<p>The <i>stadium effect</i> is a phenomenon observed in strong tropical cyclones. It is a fairly common event, where the clouds of the eyewall curve outward from the surface with height. This gives the eye an appearance resembling an open dome from the air, akin to a <a href="/wiki/Stadium" title="Stadium">sports stadium</a>. An eye is always larger at the top of the storm, and smallest at the bottom of the storm because the rising air in the eyewall follows <a href="/wiki/Contour_line" title="Contour line">isolines</a> of equal <a href="/wiki/Angular_momentum" title="Angular momentum">angular momentum</a>, which also slope outward with height.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ang_mom_0_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-ang_mom_0-21">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ang_mom_1_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-ang_mom_1-22">[22]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ang_mom_2_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-ang_mom_2-23">[23]</a></sup> In tropical cyclones with very small eyes, the sloping phenomenon is much more pronounced.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Eye-like_features">Eye-like features</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eye_(cyclone)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Eye-like features">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>An eye-like structure is often found in intensifying tropical cyclones. Similar to the eye seen in hurricanes or typhoons, it is a circular area at the circulation center of the storm in which convection is absent. These eye-like features are most normally found in intensifying tropical storms and hurricanes of Category 1 strength on the Saffir-Simpson scale. For example, an eye-like feature was found in <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Beta" title="Hurricane Beta">Hurricane Beta</a> when the storm had maximum wind speeds of only 80 km/h (50 mph), well below hurricane force.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> The features are typically not visible on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Visible_light" title="Visible light">visible wavelengths</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Infrared_light" title="Infrared light">infrared wavelengths</a> from space, although they are easily seen on <a href="/wiki/Microwave" title="Microwave">microwave</a> satellite imagery.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TRMM_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-TRMM-25">[25]</a></sup> Their development at the middle levels of the atmosphere is similar to the formation of a complete eye, but the features might be horizontally displaced due to vertical wind shear.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p>

<p>Though the eye is by far the calmest part of the storm, with no wind at the center and typically clear skies, over the ocean it is possibly the most hazardous area. In the eyewall, wind-driven waves all travel in the same direction. In the center of the eye, however, the waves converge from all directions, creating erratic crests that can build on each other to become <a href="/wiki/Rogue_wave" title="Rogue wave">rogue waves</a>. The maximum height of hurricane waves is unknown, but measurements during <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Ivan" title="Hurricane Ivan">Hurricane Ivan</a> when it was a Category 4 hurricane estimated that waves near the eyewall exceeded 40 m (130 ft) from peak to trough.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<p>A common mistake, especially in areas where hurricanes are uncommon, is for residents to exit their homes to inspect the damage while the calm eye passes over, only to be caught off guard by the violent winds in the opposite eyewall.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup></p>

<p>Though only tropical cyclones have structures officially termed "eyes", there are other weather systems that can exhibit eye-like features.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FAQ_eye_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-FAQ_eye-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AMS_glossary_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-AMS_glossary-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Polar_lows">Polar lows</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eye_(cyclone)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Polar lows">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Polar_low" title="Polar low">Polar lows</a> are <a href="/wiki/Mesoscale_meteorology" title="Mesoscale meteorology">mesoscale</a> weather systems, typically smaller than 1,000 km (600 mi) across, found near the <a href="/wiki/Geographical_pole" title="Geographical pole">poles</a>. Like tropical cyclones, they form over relatively warm water and can feature deep convection and winds of <a href="/wiki/Beaufort_scale" title="Beaufort scale">gale force</a> or greater. Unlike storms of tropical nature, however, they thrive in much colder temperatures and at much higher latitudes. They are also smaller and last for shorter durations, with few lasting longer than a day or so. Despite these differences, they can be very similar in structure to tropical cyclones, featuring a clear eye surrounded by an eyewall and bands of rain and snow.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Extratropical_cyclones">Extratropical cyclones</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eye_(cyclone)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Extratropical cyclones">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Extratropical_cyclone" title="Extratropical cyclone">Extratropical cyclones</a> are areas of low pressure which exist at the boundary of different <a href="/wiki/Air_mass" title="Air mass">air masses</a>. Almost all storms found at mid-latitudes are extratropical in nature, including classic North American <a href="/wiki/Nor%27easter" title="Nor'easter">nor'easters</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/European_windstorms" title="European windstorms">European windstorms</a>. The most severe of these can have a clear "eye" at the site of lowest barometric pressure, though it is usually surrounded by lower, non-convective clouds and is found near the back end of the storm.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WarmSeclusionCycMet_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-WarmSeclusionCycMet-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Subtropical_cyclones">Subtropical cyclones</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eye_(cyclone)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Subtropical cyclones">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Subtropical_cyclone" title="Subtropical cyclone">Subtropical cyclones</a> are low-pressure systems with some extratropical characteristics and some tropical characteristics. As such, they may have an eye while not being truly tropical in nature. Subtropical cyclones can be very hazardous, generating high winds and seas, and often evolve into fully tropical cyclones. For this reason, the <a href="/wiki/National_Hurricane_Center" title="National Hurricane Center">National Hurricane Center</a> began including subtropical storms in its naming scheme in 2002.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-subtrop_1_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-subtrop_1-33">[33]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Tornadoes">Tornadoes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eye_(cyclone)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Tornadoes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Tornado" title="Tornado">Tornadoes</a> are destructive, small-scale storms, which produce the fastest winds on earth. There are two main types—single-vortex tornadoes, which consist of a single spinning column of air, and multiple-vortex tornadoes, which consist of small "suction vortices," resembling mini-tornadoes themselves, all rotating around a common center. Both of these types of tornadoes are theorized to have calm eyes. These theories are supported by doppler velocity observations by weather radar and eyewitness accounts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tornado_1_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-tornado_1-34">[34]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tornado_2_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-tornado_2-35">[35]</a></sup></p>

</div>

<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Extraterrestrial_vortex" title="Extraterrestrial vortex">Extraterrestrial vortex</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">NASA</a> reported in November 2006 that the <a href="/wiki/Cassini%E2%80%93Huygens" title="Cassini–Huygens">Cassini spacecraft</a> observed a "hurricane-like" storm locked to the south pole of <a href="/wiki/Saturn" title="Saturn">Saturn</a> with a clearly defined eyewall. The observation was particularly notable as eyewall clouds had not previously been seen on any planet other than Earth (including a failure to observe an eyewall in the <a href="/wiki/Great_Red_Spot" title="Great Red Spot">Great Red Spot</a> of Jupiter by the <a href="/wiki/Galileo_(spacecraft)" title="Galileo (spacecraft)">Galileo</a> spacecraft).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> In 2007, very large vortices on both poles of <a href="/wiki/Venus" title="Venus">Venus</a> were observed by the <a href="/wiki/Venus_Express" title="Venus Express">Venus Express</a> mission of the <a href="/wiki/European_Space_Agency" title="European Space Agency">European Space Agency</a> to have a dipole eye structure.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Venus_Express_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-Venus_Express-37">[37]</a></sup></p>
