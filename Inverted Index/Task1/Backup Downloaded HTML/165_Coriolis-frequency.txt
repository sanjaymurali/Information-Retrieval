<p>The <b>Coriolis frequency</b> <i>ƒ</i>, also called the <b>Coriolis parameter</b> or <b>Coriolis coefficient</b>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> is equal to twice the rotation rate <i>Ω</i> of the Earth multiplied by the <a href="/wiki/Sine" title="Sine">sine</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Latitude" title="Latitude">latitude</a> <i>φ</i>.</p>

<p>The <a href="/wiki/Earth%27s_rotation#Rotation_period" title="Earth's rotation">rotation rate of the Earth</a> (<i>Ω</i> = 7.2921 × 10<sup>−5</sup> rad/s) can be calculated as 2<i>π</i> / <i>T</i> radians per second, where <i>T</i> is the rotation <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Period_(physics)" title="Period (physics)">period</a> of the Earth which is one <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sidereal_day" title="Sidereal day"><i>sidereal</i> day</a> (23 hr 56 m 4.1 s). In the midlatitudes, the typical value for <span class="mwe-math-element"><span class="mwe-math-mathml-inline mwe-math-mathml-a11y" style="display: none;"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">

These <a href="/wiki/Oscillation" title="Oscillation">oscillations</a> are the result of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coriolis_effect" title="Coriolis effect">Coriolis effect</a>.</p>

If the Coriolis parameter is large, the effect of the earth's rotation on the body is significant since it will need a larger angular frequency to stay in equilibrium with the Coriolis forces.
