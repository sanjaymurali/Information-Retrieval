<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Typhoon</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">

<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">This article is about Pacific tropical cyclones. For other uses, see <a class="mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Typhoon_(disambiguation)" title="Typhoon (disambiguation)">Typhoon (disambiguation)</a>.</div>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">This article is about cyclones in the Western Pacific basin (Northern Hemisphere) only, which is the only place they are called typhoons. For other areas they occur, see <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone_basins" title="Tropical cyclone basins">Tropical cyclone basins</a>.</div>

<p>A <b>typhoon</b> is a mature <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone" title="Tropical cyclone">tropical cyclone</a> that develops in the western part of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/North_Pacific_Ocean" title="North Pacific Ocean">North Pacific Ocean</a> between <a href="/wiki/180th_meridian" title="180th meridian">180°</a> and <a href="/wiki/100th_meridian_east" title="100th meridian east">100°E</a>. This region is referred to as the <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone_basins#Northwestern_Pacific_Ocean" title="Tropical cyclone basins">Northwestern Pacific Basin</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> and is the most active tropical cyclone basin on <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a>, accounting for almost one-third of the world's annual tropical cyclones. For organizational purposes, the northern Pacific Ocean is divided into three regions: the eastern (North America to <a href="/wiki/140th_meridian_west" title="140th meridian west">140°W</a>), central (140° to 180°W), and western (180° to 100°E). The <a href="/wiki/Regional_Specialized_Meteorological_Center" title="Regional Specialized Meteorological Center">Regional Specialized Meteorological Center</a> (RSMC) for tropical cyclone forecasts is in <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a>, with other tropical cyclone warning centers for the northwest Pacific in <a href="/wiki/Hawaii" title="Hawaii">Hawaii</a> (the <a href="/wiki/Joint_Typhoon_Warning_Center" title="Joint Typhoon Warning Center">Joint Typhoon Warning Center</a>), the <a href="/wiki/Philippines" title="Philippines">Philippines</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hong_Kong" title="Hong Kong">Hong Kong</a>. While the RSMC names each system, the main name list itself is coordinated among 18 countries that have territories threatened by typhoons each year. Only the Philippines use their own naming list for systems approaching the country.</p>
<p>A typhoon differs from a cyclone or hurricane only on the basis of location.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HCT_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-HCT-2">[2]</a></sup> A <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_hurricane" title="Atlantic hurricane">hurricane</a> is a <a href="/wiki/Storm" title="Storm">storm</a> that occurs in the <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Ocean" title="Atlantic Ocean">Atlantic Ocean</a> and northeastern <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Ocean" title="Pacific Ocean">Pacific Ocean</a>, a typhoon occurs in the northwestern Pacific Ocean, and a <a href="/wiki/Cyclone" title="Cyclone">cyclone</a> occurs in the south Pacific or <a href="/wiki/Indian_Ocean" title="Indian Ocean">Indian Ocean</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HCT_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-HCT-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Within the northwestern Pacific there are no official typhoon seasons as tropical cyclones form throughout the year. Like any tropical cyclone, there are six main requirements for typhoon formation and development: sufficiently warm <a href="/wiki/Sea_surface_temperature" title="Sea surface temperature">sea surface temperatures</a>, atmospheric instability, high <a href="/wiki/Relative_humidity" title="Relative humidity">humidity</a> in the lower to middle levels of the troposphere, enough <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coriolis_effect" title="Coriolis effect">Coriolis force</a> to develop a <a href="/wiki/Low-pressure_area" title="Low-pressure area">low pressure center</a>, a pre-existing low level focus or disturbance, and low vertical <a href="/wiki/Wind_shear" title="Wind shear">wind shear</a>. While the majority of storms form between June and November, a few storms do occur between December and May (although tropical cyclone formation is at a minimum during that time). On average, the northwestern Pacific features the most numerous and intense tropical cyclones globally. Like other basins, they are steered by the <a href="/wiki/Subtropical_ridge" title="Subtropical ridge">subtropical ridge</a> towards the west or northwest, with some systems recurving near and east of <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a>. The Philippines receive the brunt of the landfalls, with <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a> and Japan being impacted slightly less. Some of the deadliest typhoons in history have struck China. Southern China has the longest record of typhoon impacts for the region, with a thousand-year sample via documents within their archives. <a href="/wiki/Taiwan" title="Taiwan">Taiwan</a> has received the wettest known typhoon on record for the northwest Pacific <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone_basin" title="Tropical cyclone basin">tropical cyclone basins</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Nomenclature">Nomenclature</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Typhoon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Nomenclature">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology_and_usage">Etymology and usage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Typhoon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Etymology and usage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The term <i>typhoon</i> is the regional name in the northwest Pacific for a severe (or mature) <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone" title="Tropical cyclone">tropical cyclone</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> whereas <i>hurricane</i> is the regional term in the northeast Pacific and northern Atlantic.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Elsewhere this is called a <i>tropical cyclone</i>, <i>severe tropical cyclone</i>, or <i>severe cyclonic storm</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <i><a href="/wiki/Oxford_English_Dictionary" title="Oxford English Dictionary">Oxford English Dictionary</a></i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-oed_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-oed-6">[6]</a></sup> cites <a href="/wiki/Urdu" title="Urdu">Urdu</a> <i>ṭūfān</i> and <a href="/wiki/Chinese_language" title="Chinese language">Chinese</a> <i>tai fung</i> giving rise to several early forms in English. The earliest forms -- "touffon", later "tufan", "tuffon", and others -- derive from Urdu <i>ṭūfān</i>, with citations as early as 1588. From 1699 appears "tuffoon", later "tiffoon", derived from Chinese with spelling influenced by the older Urdu-derived forms. The modern spelling "typhoon" dates to 1820, preceded by "tay-fun" in 1771 and "ty-foong", all derived from the Chinese <i>tai fung</i>.</p>
<p>The Urdu source word توفان <i>ṭūfān</i> ("violent storm"; cognate to <a href="/wiki/Hindi" title="Hindi">Hindi</a> तूफ़ान (<i>tūfān</i>))<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-online_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-online-7">[7]</a></sup> comes via Persian from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arabic_language" title="Arabic language">Arabic</a> طوفان (<i>ṭūfān</i>), which may derive from the verb <i>tūfīdan</i> (<a href="/wiki/Persian_language" title="Persian language">Persian</a>: <span dir="rtl" lang="fa" xml:lang="fa">توفیدن/طوفیدن</span>‎‎, "to roar, to blow furiously")<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> or Arabic <i>ṭāfa</i>, to turn round.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-oed_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-oed-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Chinese source is the word <i>tai fung</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-online_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-online-7">[7]</a></sup> (<a href="/wiki/Simplified_Chinese_characters" title="Simplified Chinese characters">simplified Chinese</a>: <span lang="zh-Hans" xml:lang="zh-Hans"><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E5%8F%B0%E9%A3%8E" title="wikt:台风">台风</a></span>; <a href="/wiki/Traditional_Chinese_characters" title="Traditional Chinese characters">traditional Chinese</a>: <span lang="zh-Hant" xml:lang="zh-Hant"><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E9%A2%B1%E9%A2%A8" title="wikt:颱風">颱風</a></span>; <a href="/wiki/Pinyin" title="Pinyin">pinyin</a>: <i><span lang="zh-Latn-pinyin" xml:lang="zh-Latn-pinyin">táifēng</span></i>), cited as a common dialect form of <a href="/wiki/Standard_Chinese" title="Standard Chinese">Mandarin</a> <i>dà</i> "big" and <i>fēng</i> "wind".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-oed_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-oed-6">[6]</a></sup> In Mandarin the word for the windstorm is <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E5%A4%A7%E9%A3%8E" title="wikt:大风">大风</a> (<i>dàfēng</i>, "big wind") and in <a href="/wiki/Cantonese" title="Cantonese">Cantonese</a> <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E5%A4%A7%E9%A2%A8" title="wikt:大風">大風</a> (<i>daai<sup>6</sup> fung<sup>1</sup></i>, "big wind"). The modern <a href="/wiki/Japanese_language" title="Japanese language">Japanese</a> word is also derived from Chinese: 台風 (たいふう, <i>taifuu</i>). The first character in Japanese has a usual modern meaning of "pedestal" or "stand", but it is actually a simplification of an older <a href="/wiki/Kanji" title="Kanji">kanji</a> 颱 which had a meaning of "typhoon" by itself. So the Japanese characters originally had a meaning of "typhoon wind".</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek" title="Ancient Greek">Ancient Greek</a> Τυφῶν (<i>Tuphôn</i>, <a href="/wiki/Typhon" title="Typhon">Typhon</a>) is not unrelated and has secondarily contaminated the word.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-online_7-2"><a href="#cite_note-online-7">[7]</a></sup> The Persian and Chinese terms may have been originated by the Greek word in the first place.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-oed_6-3"><a href="#cite_note-oed-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>

<p>A <i>tropical depression</i> is the lowest category that the <a href="/wiki/Japan_Meteorological_Agency" title="Japan Meteorological Agency">Japan Meteorological Agency</a> uses and is the term used for a tropical system that has wind speeds not exceeding 33 knots (38 mph; 61 km/h).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TCOM_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-TCOM-9">[9]</a></sup> A tropical depression is upgraded to a <i>tropical storm</i> should its <a href="/wiki/Maximum_sustained_wind" title="Maximum sustained wind">sustained wind speeds</a> exceed 34 knots (39 mph; 63 km/h). Tropical storms also receive official names from RSMC Tokyo.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TCOM_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-TCOM-9">[9]</a></sup> Should the storm intensify further and reach sustained wind speeds of 48 knots (55 mph; 89 km/h) then it will be classified as a <i>severe tropical storm</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TCOM_9-2"><a href="#cite_note-TCOM-9">[9]</a></sup> Once the system's maximum sustained winds reach wind speeds of 64 knots (74 mph; 119 km/h), the JMA will designate the tropical cyclone as a <i>typhoon</i>—the highest category on its scale.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TCOM_9-3"><a href="#cite_note-TCOM-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>From 2009 the <a href="/wiki/Hong_Kong_Observatory" title="Hong Kong Observatory">Hong Kong Observatory</a> started to further divide typhoons into three different classifications: <i>typhoon</i>, <i>severe typhoon</i> and <i>super typhoon</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HKO_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-HKO-10">[10]</a></sup> A <i>typhoon</i> has wind speed of 64-79 knots (73-91 mph; 118–149 km/h), a severe typhoon has winds of at least 80 knots (92 mph; 150 km/h), and a super typhoon has winds of at least 100 knots (120 mph; 190 km/h).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HKO_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-HKO-10">[10]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>' <a href="/wiki/Joint_Typhoon_Warning_Center" title="Joint Typhoon Warning Center">Joint Typhoon Warning Center</a> (JTWC) unofficially classifies typhoons with wind speeds of at least 130 knots (67 m/s; 150 mph; 241 km/h)—the equivalent of a strong Category 4 storm in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saffir-Simpson_Hurricane_Scale" title="Saffir-Simpson Hurricane Scale">Saffir-Simpson scale</a>—as <i>super typhoons</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> However, the maximum sustained wind speed measurements that the JTWC uses are based on a 1-minute averaging period, akin to the U.S.' <a href="/wiki/National_Hurricane_Center" title="National Hurricane Center">National Hurricane Center</a> and <a href="/wiki/Central_Pacific_Hurricane_Center" title="Central Pacific Hurricane Center">Central Pacific Hurricane Center</a>. As a result, the JTWC's wind reports are higher than JMA's measurements, as the latter are based on a 10-minute averaging interval.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JTWC_FAQ_fcstdiff_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-JTWC_FAQ_fcstdiff-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Genesis">Genesis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Typhoon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Genesis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclogenesis" title="Tropical cyclogenesis">Tropical cyclogenesis</a></div>
<p>There are six main requirements for tropical cyclogenesis: sufficiently warm sea surface temperatures, atmospheric instability, high <a href="/wiki/Humidity" title="Humidity">humidity</a> in the lower to middle levels of the <a href="/wiki/Troposphere" title="Troposphere">troposphere</a>, enough <a href="/wiki/Coriolis_force" title="Coriolis force">Coriolis force</a> to develop a low pressure center, a pre-existing low level focus or disturbance, and low vertical wind shear. While these conditions are necessary for tropical cyclone formation, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Necessary_but_not_sufficient" title="Necessary but not sufficient">they do not guarantee</a> that a tropical cyclone will form. Normally, an ocean temperature of 26.5 °C (79.7 °F) spanning through a depth of at least 50 metres (160 ft) is considered the minimum to maintain the special <a href="/wiki/Mesocyclone" title="Mesocyclone">mesocyclone</a> that is the tropical cyclone.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> These warm waters are needed to maintain the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Warm_core" title="Warm core">warm core</a> that fuels tropical systems. A minimum distance of 500 km (300 mi) from the <a href="/wiki/Equator" title="Equator">equator</a> is normally needed for tropical cyclogenesis.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-A15_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-A15-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>Whether it be a depression in the <a href="/wiki/Intertropical_Convergence_Zone" title="Intertropical Convergence Zone">Intertropical Convergence Zone</a> (ITCZ) or <a href="/wiki/Monsoon_trough" title="Monsoon trough">monsoon trough</a>, a broad <a href="/wiki/Surface_weather_analysis" title="Surface weather analysis">surface front</a>, or an <a href="/wiki/Outflow_boundary" title="Outflow boundary">outflow boundary</a>, a low level feature with sufficient <a href="/wiki/Vorticity" title="Vorticity">vorticity</a> and convergence is required to begin tropical cyclogenesis. About 85 to 90 percent of Pacific typhoons form within the monsoon trough.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-KingTUTT_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-KingTUTT-14">[14]</a></sup> Even with perfect upper level conditions and the required atmospheric instability, the lack of a surface focus will prevent the development of organized convection and a surface low. Vertical wind shear of less than 10 m/s (20 kn, 33 ft/s) between the ocean surface and the <a href="/wiki/Tropopause" title="Tropopause">tropopause</a> is required for tropical cyclone development.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-A15_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-A15-13">[13]</a></sup> Typically with Pacific typhoons, there are two <a href="/wiki/Outflow_(meteorology)" title="Outflow (meteorology)">outflow</a> <a href="/wiki/Jet_stream" title="Jet stream">jets</a>: one to the north ahead of an upper trough in the <a href="/wiki/Westerlies" title="Westerlies">Westerlies</a>, and a second towards the equator.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-KingTUTT_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-KingTUTT-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>In general, westerly wind increases associated with the <a href="/wiki/Madden%E2%80%93Julian_oscillation" title="Madden–Julian oscillation">Madden–Julian oscillation</a> lead to increased tropical cyclogenesis in all <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone_basins" title="Tropical cyclone basins">tropical cyclone basins</a>. As the oscillation propagates from west to east, it leads to an eastward march in tropical cyclogenesis with time during that hemisphere's summer season.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AMS2_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-AMS2-15">[15]</a></sup> On average, twice per year twin tropical cyclones will form in the western Pacific Ocean, near the <a href="/wiki/5th_parallel_north" title="5th parallel north">5th parallel north</a> and the <a href="/wiki/5th_parallel_south" title="5th parallel south">5th parallel south</a>, along the same meridian, or line of longitude.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> There is an inverse relationship between tropical cyclone activity in the western Pacific basin and the north Atlantic basin, however. When one basin is active, the other is normally quiet, and vice versa. The main reason for this appears to be the phase of the Madden–Julian oscillation, or MJO, which is normally in opposite modes between the two basins at any given time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mad-Jul_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-Mad-Jul-17">[17]</a></sup></p>

<p>Nearly one-third of the world's tropical cyclones form within the western Pacific. This makes this basin the most active on Earth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-enso_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-enso-19">[19]</a></sup> Pacific typhoons have formed year round, with peak months from August to October. The peak months correspond to that of the <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="Atlantic hurricane season">Atlantic hurricane seasons</a>. Along with a high storm frequency, this basin also features the most globally <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_notable_tropical_cyclones#Major_records" title="List of notable tropical cyclones">intense storms</a> on record. One of the most recent busy seasons was <a href="/wiki/2013_Pacific_typhoon_season" title="2013 Pacific typhoon season">2013</a>. Tropical cyclones form in any month of the year across the northwest Pacific Ocean, and concentrate around June and November in the northern Indian Ocean. The area just northeast of the Philippines is the most active place on Earth for tropical cyclones to exist. Across the Philippines themselves, activity reaches a minimum in February, before increasing steadily through June, and spiking from July through October, with September being the most active month for tropical cyclones across the <a href="/wiki/Archipelago" title="Archipelago">archipelago</a>. Activity falls off significantly in November, although <a href="/wiki/Typhoon_Haiyan" title="Typhoon Haiyan">Typhoon Haiyan</a>, the strongest Philippine typhoon on record, was a November typhoon.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> The most frequently impacted areas of the Philippines by tropical cyclones are northern and central <a href="/wiki/Luzon" title="Luzon">Luzon</a> and eastern <a href="/wiki/Visayas" title="Visayas">Visayas</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> A ten-year average of satellite determined precipitation showed that at least 30 percent of the annual rainfall in the northern Philippines could be traced to tropical cyclones, while the southern islands receive less than 10 percent of their annual rainfall from tropical cyclones.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> The genesis and intensity of typhoons are also modulated by slow variation of the sea surface temperature and circulation features following a near-10-year frequency.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Paths">Paths</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Typhoon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Paths">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>Most tropical cyclones form on the side of the subtropical ridge closer to the equator, then move poleward past the ridge axis before recurving north and northeast into the main belt of the <a href="/wiki/Westerlies" title="Westerlies">Westerlies</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> When the subtropical ridge shifts due to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/El_Ni%C3%B1o-Southern_Oscillation" title="El Niño-Southern Oscillation">El Niño</a>, so will the preferred tropical cyclone tracks. Areas west of Japan and <a href="/wiki/Korea" title="Korea">Korea</a> tend to experience many fewer September–November tropical cyclone impacts during El Niño and neutral years. During El Niño years, the break in the subtropical ridge tends to lie near <a href="/wiki/130th_meridian_east" title="130th meridian east">130°E</a>, which would favor the Japanese archipelago.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-China_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-China-25">[25]</a></sup> During La Niña years, the formation of tropical cyclones, and the subtropical ridge position, shift westward across the western Pacific Ocean, which increases the landfall threat to <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a> and greater intensity to <a href="/wiki/Philippines" title="Philippines">Philippines</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-China_25-1"><a href="#cite_note-China-25">[25]</a></sup> Those that form near the <a href="/wiki/Marshall_Islands" title="Marshall Islands">Marshall Islands</a> find their way to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jeju-do" title="Jeju-do">Jeju Island</a>, Korea.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<p>Typhoon paths follow three general directions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-enso_19-1"><a href="#cite_note-enso-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li>Straight track (or straight runner). A general westward path affects the <a href="/wiki/Philippines" title="Philippines">Philippines</a>, southern China, <a href="/wiki/Taiwan" title="Taiwan">Taiwan</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Vietnam" title="Vietnam">Vietnam</a>.</li>
<li>A parabolic recurving track. Storms recurving affect eastern Philippines, eastern China, Taiwan, Korea, Japan, and the Russian Far East.</li>
<li>Northward track. From point of origin, the storm follows a northerly direction, only affecting small islands.</li>
</ul>
<p>A rare few storms, like <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_John_(1994)" title="Hurricane John (1994)">Hurricane John</a>, were redesignated as typhoons as they originated in the Eastern/Central Pacific and moved into the western Pacific.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Basin_monitoring">Basin monitoring</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Typhoon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Basin monitoring">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Within the Western Pacific, <a href="/wiki/Regional_Specialized_Meteorological_Center" title="Regional Specialized Meteorological Center">RSMC Tokyo-Typhoon Center</a>, part of the <a href="/wiki/Japan_Meteorological_Agency" title="Japan Meteorological Agency">Japan Meteorological Agency</a> has had the official warning responsibility for the whole of the Western Pacific since 1989,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JMA2000_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-JMA2000-27">[27]</a></sup> and the naming responsibility for systems of tropical storm strength or greater since 2000.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HKO_10-2"><a href="#cite_note-HKO-10">[10]</a></sup> However each <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/WMO" title="WMO">National Meteorological and Hydrological Service</a> within the western Pacific has the responsibility for issuing warnings for land areas about tropical cyclones affecting their country, such as the Joint Typhoon Warning Center for United States agencies,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Philippine_Atmospheric,_Geophysical_and_Astronomical_Services_Administration" title="Philippine Atmospheric, Geophysical and Astronomical Services Administration">Philippine Atmospheric, Geophysical and Astronomical Services Administration</a> (PAGASA) for interests in the island archipelago nation,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> and the <a href="/wiki/Hong_Kong_Observatory" title="Hong Kong Observatory">Hong Kong Observatory</a> for storms that come close enough to cause the issuance of <a href="/wiki/Hong_Kong_tropical_cyclone_warning_signals" title="Hong Kong tropical cyclone warning signals">warning signals</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Name_sources">Name sources</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Typhoon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Name sources">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The list of names consists of entries from 17 Southeast and East Asian nations and the United States who have territories directly affected by typhoons. The submitted names are arranged into five lists, and each list is cycled with each year. Unlike tropical cyclones in other parts of the world, typhoons are not named after people. Instead, they generally refer to animals, flowers, astrological signs, and a few personal names. However, PAGASA retains its own naming list, which does consist of human names.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TyphoonBasic_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-TyphoonBasic-31">[31]</a></sup> Therefore, a typhoon can possibly have two names. Storms that cross the date line from the central Pacific retain their original name, but the designation of hurricane becomes typhoon. In Japan, people use the numerical designation of typhoons according to the sequence of their occurrence in the calendar year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JMA2000_27-1"><a href="#cite_note-JMA2000-27">[27]</a></sup></p>

<p>The most active Western Pacific typhoon season was in 1964,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> when 39 storms of tropical storm strength formed. Only 15 seasons had 30 or more storms developing since reliable records began. The least activity seen in the northwest Pacific Ocean was during the <a href="/wiki/2010_Pacific_typhoon_season" title="2010 Pacific typhoon season">2010 Pacific typhoon season</a>, when only 14 tropical storms and seven typhoons formed. In the Philippines, the most active season, since 1945, for tropical cyclone strikes was <a href="/wiki/1993_Pacific_typhoon_season" title="1993 Pacific typhoon season">1993</a> when nineteen tropical cyclones moved through the country.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> There was only one tropical cyclone that moved through the Philippines in <a href="/wiki/1958_Pacific_typhoon_season" title="1958 Pacific typhoon season">1958</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/2004_Pacific_typhoon_season" title="2004 Pacific typhoon season">2004 Pacific typhoon season</a> was the busiest for Okinawa since 1957.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> Within <a href="/wiki/Guangdong" title="Guangdong">Guangdong</a> in southern China, during the past thousand years, the most active decades for typhoon strikes were the 1660s and 1670s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup></p>
<p>The highest reliably-estimated <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Maximum_sustained_winds" title="Maximum sustained winds">maximum sustained winds</a> on record for a typhoon were those of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Typhoon_Haiyan_(2013)" title="Typhoon Haiyan (2013)">Typhoon Haiyan</a> at 195 miles per hour (314 km/h) shortly before its landfall in the central <a href="/wiki/Philippines" title="Philippines">Philippines</a> on Nov. 8, 2013.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> The most intense storm based on minimum pressure was <a href="/wiki/Typhoon_Tip" title="Typhoon Tip">Typhoon Tip</a> in the northwestern Pacific Ocean in 1979, which reached a minimum pressure of 870 hectopascals (26 inHg) and maximum sustained wind speeds of 165 knots (85 m/s, 190 mph, 310 km/h).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JTWC_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-JTWC-37">[37]</a></sup> The deadliest typhoon of the 20th century was <a href="/wiki/Typhoon_Nina_(1975)" title="Typhoon Nina (1975)">Typhoon Nina</a>, which killed nearly 100,000 in China in 1975 due to a flood that caused 12 reservoirs to fail.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> After <a href="/wiki/Typhoon_Morakot" title="Typhoon Morakot">Typhoon Morakot</a> landed in Taiwan at midnight on August 8, 2009, almost the entire southern region of Taiwan (<a href="/wiki/Chiayi_County" title="Chiayi County">Chiayi County</a>/<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chiayi_City" title="Chiayi City">Chiayi City</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tainan_County" title="Tainan County">Tainan County</a>/<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tainan_City" title="Tainan City">Tainan City</a> (now merged as Tainan), <a href="/wiki/Kaohsiung_County" title="Kaohsiung County">Kaohsiung County</a>/<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kaohsiung_City" title="Kaohsiung City">Kaohsiung City</a> (now merged as Kaohsiung), and <a href="/wiki/Pingtung_County" title="Pingtung County">Pingtung County</a>) and parts of <a href="/wiki/Taitung_County" title="Taitung County">Taitung County</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nantou_County" title="Nantou County">Nantou County</a> were flooded by record-breaking heavy rain. The rainfall in <a href="/wiki/Pingtung_County" title="Pingtung County">Pingtung County</a> reached 2,327 millimeters (91.6 in),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> breaking all rainfall records of any single place in Taiwan induced by a single typhoon,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> and making the cyclone the wettest known typhoon.</p>
