<p>The U.S. <a href="/wiki/National_Weather_Service" title="National Weather Service">National Weather Service</a> defines a gale as 34–47 <a href="/wiki/Knot_(unit)" title="Knot (unit)">knots</a> (<span class="nowrap">63–87 km/h</span>, <span class="nowrap">17.5–24.2 m/s</span> or <span class="nowrap">39–54 miles/hour</span>) of sustained surface winds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-noaa_glossary_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-noaa_glossary-1">[1]</a></sup> Forecasters typically issue <a href="/wiki/Gale_warning" title="Gale warning">gale warnings</a> when winds of this strength are expected.</p>
<p>Other sources use minima as low as 28 knots (52 km/h, 32 mph) and maxima as high as 90 knots (170 km/h, 100 mph). Through 1986, the <a href="/wiki/National_Hurricane_Center" title="National Hurricane Center">National Hurricane Center</a> used the term gale to refer to winds of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tropical" title="Tropical">tropical</a> force for coastal areas, between 33 knots (61 km/h, 38 mph) and 63 knots (117 km/h, 72 mph). The 90 knots (170 km/h, 100 mph) definition is very non-standard. A common alternative definition of the maximum is 55 knots (102 km/h, 63 mph).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>The most common way of measuring winds is with the <a href="/wiki/Beaufort_scale" title="Beaufort scale">Beaufort scale</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> which defines a gale as wind from 50 to 102 km/h. It is an empirical measure for describing wind speed based mainly on observed sea conditions. Its full name is the Beaufort Wind Force Scale.</p>
<p>On the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Beaufort_Wind_Scale" title="Beaufort Wind Scale">Beaufort Wind Scale</a>, a gale is classified as: 7: Moderate Gale (32–38 miles per hour), 8: Fresh Gale (39-46 mph), 9: Strong Gale (47-54 mph) and 10: Storm/Whole Gale (55-63 mph). A gale is a type of Wind Description preceded by 0: Calm, 1: Light Air, 2: Light Breeze, 3: Gentle Breeze, 4: Moderate Breeze, 5: Fresh Breeze, 6: Strong Breeze and succeeded by 11: Violent Storm and 12: Hurricane on a Beaufort Wind Scale. There is a unique Beaufort Scale number and a unique Arrow Indication for each type of Wind Description mentioned above.</p>
<p>The word <i>gale</i> is derived from the older <i>gail</i>, but its origin is uncertain.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
