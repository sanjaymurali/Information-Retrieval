<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Subtropical cyclone</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">

<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="thumb tright">

<a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Alex_(2016)" title="Hurricane Alex (2016)">Subtropical Storm Alex</a> in January 2016</div>

<p>A <b>subtropical cyclone</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Weather" title="Weather">weather</a> system that has some characteristics of a <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone" title="Tropical cyclone">tropical</a> and an <a href="/wiki/Extratropical_cyclone" title="Extratropical cyclone">extratropical cyclone</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NAtlSTClimo_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-NAtlSTClimo-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>As early as the 1950s, meteorologists were unclear whether they should be characterized as tropical or extratropical cyclones. They were officially recognized by the <a href="/wiki/National_Hurricane_Center" title="National Hurricane Center">National Hurricane Center</a> in 1972. Subtropical cyclones have begun to receive names from the official tropical cyclone lists in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/North_Atlantic_hurricane" title="North Atlantic hurricane">North Atlantic</a>, <a href="/wiki/South-West_Indian_Ocean_tropical_cyclone" title="South-West Indian Ocean tropical cyclone">South-west Indian Ocean</a>, and <a href="/wiki/South_Atlantic_tropical_cyclone" title="South Atlantic tropical cyclone">South Atlantic</a> basins.</p>
<p>There are two definitions currently used for subtropical cyclones. Across the north Atlantic and southwest Indian Ocean, they require central convection fairly near the center and a warming core in the mid-levels of the <a href="/wiki/Troposphere" title="Troposphere">troposphere</a>. Across the eastern half of the northern Pacific, they require a mid-tropospheric cyclone to cut off from the main belt of the <a href="/wiki/Westerlies" title="Westerlies">westerlies</a> and only a weak surface circulation. Subtropical cyclones have broad wind patterns with <a href="/wiki/Maximum_sustained_wind" title="Maximum sustained wind">maximum sustained winds</a> located farther from the center than typical tropical cyclones, and have no <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Weather_fronts" title="Weather fronts">weather fronts</a> linked into their center.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NAtlSTCases_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-NAtlSTCases-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Since they form from initially extratropical cyclones which have colder temperatures aloft than normally found in the tropics, the sea surface temperatures required for their formation are lower than the tropical cyclone threshold by 3°<a href="/wiki/Celsius" title="Celsius">C</a> (5°<a href="/wiki/Fahrenheit" title="Fahrenheit">F</a>), lying around 23 °C (73 °F). This also means that subtropical cyclones are more likely to form outside the traditional bounds of the <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="Atlantic hurricane season">North Atlantic hurricane season</a>. Subtropical cyclones are also observed to form in the South Atlantic; South Atlantic subtropical cyclones are observed in all months.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SAtlSTs_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-SAtlSTs-3">[3]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History_of_term">History of term</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Subtropical_cyclone&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History of term">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Throughout the 1950s and 1960s, the term semi-tropical and quasi-tropical were used for what would become known as subtropical cyclones.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> The term subtropical cyclone merely referred to any cyclone located in the subtropical belt near and just north of the <a href="/wiki/Horse_latitudes" title="Horse latitudes">horse latitudes</a>. Intense debate ensued in the late 1960s, after a number of hybrid cyclones formed in the Atlantic Basin. In 1972, the National Hurricane Center (NHC) finally designated these storms as subtropical cyclones in real-time,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NHC1972_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-NHC1972-5">[5]</a></sup> and updated the hurricane database to include subtropical cyclones from 1968 through 1971. The term "neutercane" began to be used for small subtropical cyclones which formed from mesoscale features, and the NHC began issuing public statements during the <a href="/wiki/1972_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="1972 Atlantic hurricane season">1972 Atlantic hurricane season</a> employing that classification. This name was not noted as controversial in contemporary news reports, but it was dropped less than a year later. Recent articles, published after the year 2000, have suggested that the name "neutercane" was considered sexist in the 1970s, but there do not appear to be any published reports from that period making this claim.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Naming">Naming</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Subtropical_cyclone&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Naming">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Gustav_09_sep_2002_1805Z.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="6000" data-file-width="6000" height="220" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fe/Gustav_09_sep_2002_1805Z.jpg/220px-Gustav_09_sep_2002_1805Z.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fe/Gustav_09_sep_2002_1805Z.jpg/330px-Gustav_09_sep_2002_1805Z.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fe/Gustav_09_sep_2002_1805Z.jpg/440px-Gustav_09_sep_2002_1805Z.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Gustav_09_sep_2002_1805Z.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Gustav_(2002)" title="Hurricane Gustav (2002)">Subtropical Storm Gustav</a> in 2002, the first system to be given a name as a subtropical cyclone</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>In the North Atlantic basin, subtropical cyclones were initially named from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/ICAO_spelling_alphabet" title="ICAO spelling alphabet">NATO phonetic alphabet</a> list in the early to mid-1970s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NHC1972_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-NHC1972-5">[5]</a></sup> In the intervening years of 1975–2001, subtropical storms were either named from the traditional list and considered tropical in real-time, or used a separate numbering system. Between 1992 and 2001, two different numbers were given to subtropical depressions or subtropical storms, one for public use, the other one for NRL and NHC reference. For example, <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Karen_(2001)" title="Hurricane Karen (2001)">Hurricane Karen</a> in 2001 was initially known as Subtropical Storm One as well as AL1301 (or 13L for short).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> In 2002, the NHC began giving numbers to subtropical depressions and names to subtropical storms from the same sequence as tropical cyclones. From 2002 onwards, Subtropical Depression 13L would be known as Subtropical Depression Thirteen instead. <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Gustav_(2002)" title="Hurricane Gustav (2002)">Hurricane Gustav</a> of 2002 was the first Subtropical Storm to receive a name but became tropical shortly after naming. <a href="/wiki/Subtropical_Storm_Nicole_(2004)" title="Subtropical Storm Nicole (2004)">Subtropical Storm Nicole</a>, from the <a href="/wiki/2004_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="2004 Atlantic hurricane season">2004 Atlantic hurricane season</a> was the first subtropical storm that did not become tropical since the policy change. A subtropical storm from the <a href="/wiki/2005_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="2005 Atlantic hurricane season">2005 Atlantic hurricane season</a> also did not become tropical, but was not named since it was not recognized until post-season analysis.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the southern Indian Ocean, subtropical cyclones are also named once winds reach tropical storm, or <a href="/wiki/Gale" title="Gale">gale</a>, force.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WMO2006_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-WMO2006-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>Since 2011, subtropical storms in the western South Atlantic Ocean are named by the <a href="/wiki/Brazilian_Navy" title="Brazilian Navy">Brazilian Navy Hydrographic Center</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Formation">Formation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Subtropical_cyclone&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Formation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Maystormvapor.gif"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="267" data-file-width="400" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/12/Maystormvapor.gif/220px-Maystormvapor.gif" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/12/Maystormvapor.gif/330px-Maystormvapor.gif 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/1/12/Maystormvapor.gif 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Maystormvapor.gif" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
A water vapour loop showing the formation of <a href="/wiki/Subtropical_Storm_Andrea_(2007)" title="Subtropical Storm Andrea (2007)">Subtropical Storm Andrea</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Subtropical cyclones form in a wide band of <a href="/wiki/Latitude" title="Latitude">latitude</a>, mainly south of the 50th parallel in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Northern_hemisphere" title="Northern hemisphere">northern hemisphere</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CLfaq_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-CLfaq-11">[11]</a></sup> Due to the increased frequency of cyclones which cut off from the main belt of the westerlies during the summer and fall, subtropical cyclones are significantly more frequent across the North Atlantic than the northwestern Pacific Ocean.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> In the eastern half of the north Pacific Ocean and north Indian Ocean, the older subtropical cyclone definition term is still used, which requires a weak circulation forming underneath a <a href="/wiki/Upper_tropospheric_cyclonic_vortex" title="Upper tropospheric cyclonic vortex">mid to upper-tropospheric low</a> which has cut off from the main belt of the westerlies during the cold season (<a href="/wiki/Winter" title="Winter">winter</a>). In the case of the north Indian Ocean, the formation of this type of vortex leads to the onset of <a href="/wiki/Monsoon" title="Monsoon">monsoon</a> rains during the <a href="/wiki/Wet_season" title="Wet season">wet season</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hast_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hast-13">[13]</a></sup> In the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Southern_hemisphere" title="Southern hemisphere">southern hemisphere</a>, subtropical cyclones are regularly observed across southern portions of the <a href="/wiki/Mozambique_Channel" title="Mozambique Channel">Mozambique Channel</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WMO2006_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-WMO2006-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>Most subtropical cyclones form when a deep <a href="/wiki/Cold-core_low" title="Cold-core low">cold-core</a> extratropical cyclone drops down into the subtropics. The system becomes blocked by a high latitude ridge, and eventually sheds its frontal boundaries as its source of cool and dry air from the high latitudes diverts away from the system. Temperature differences between the 500 hPa pressure level, or 5,900 meters (19,400 ft) above ground level, and the sea surface temperatures initially exceed the dry adiabatic <a href="/wiki/Lapse_rate" title="Lapse rate">lapse rate</a>, which causes an initial round of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thunderstorms" title="Thunderstorms">thunderstorms</a> to form at a distance east of the center. Due to the initial cold temperatures aloft, sea surface temperatures usually need to reach at least 20 °C (68 °F) for this initial round of thunderstorms. The initial thunderstorm activity moistens up the environment around the low, which destabilizes the atmosphere by reducing the <a href="/wiki/Lapse_rate" title="Lapse rate">lapse rate</a> needed for convection. When the next shortwave or upper level jet streak (wind maximum within the jet stream) moves nearby, convection reignites closer to the center and the system develops into a true subtropical cyclone. The average sea surface temperature that helps lead to subtropical cyclogenesis is 24 °C (75 °F).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NAtlSTClimo_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-NAtlSTClimo-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HistSubTropCyclones_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-HistSubTropCyclones-14">[14]</a></sup> If the thunderstorm activity becomes deep and persistent, allowing its initial low level warm core to deepen, <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclogenesis" title="Tropical cyclogenesis">tropical cyclogenesis</a> is possible.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CLfaq_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-CLfaq-11">[11]</a></sup> The locus of formation for North Atlantic subtropical cyclones is out in the open ocean; the island of Bermuda is regularly impacted by these systems.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BermudaSTs_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-BermudaSTs-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>The South Atlantic environment for formation of subtropical cyclones has both stronger vertical wind shear and lower sea surface temperatures, yet subtropical cyclogenesis is regularly observed in the open ocean in the South Atlantic. A second mechanism for formation has been diagnosed for South Atlantic subtropical cyclones: lee cyclogenesis in the region of the Brazil Current.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SAtlSTs_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-SAtlSTs-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Transition_from_extratropical">Transition from extratropical</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Subtropical_cyclone&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Transition from extratropical">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>By gaining tropical characteristics, an extratropical low may transit into a subtropical depression or storm. A subtropical depression/storm may further gain tropical characteristics to become a pure tropical depression or storm, which may eventually develop into a hurricane, and there are at least three cases of tropical storms transforming into a subtropical storm (<a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Klaus_(1984)" title="Hurricane Klaus (1984)">Hurricane Klaus</a> in 1984, <a href="/wiki/Tropical_Storm_Allison" title="Tropical Storm Allison">Tropical Storm Allison</a> in 2001 and <a href="/wiki/Tropical_Storm_Lee_(2011)" title="Tropical Storm Lee (2011)">Tropical Storm Lee</a> in 2011). Generally, a tropical storm or tropical depression is not called subtropical while it is becoming extratropical, after hitting either land or colder waters. This transition normally requires significant instability through the atmosphere, with temperature differences between the underlying ocean and the mid-levels of the <a href="/wiki/Troposphere" title="Troposphere">troposphere</a> requiring over 20 °C, or 72 °F, of contrast in this roughly 5,900 meters (19,400 ft) layer of the lower atmosphere. The <a href="/wiki/Mode_(statistics)" title="Mode (statistics)">mode</a> of the sea surface temperatures that subtropical cyclones form over is 23 °C (73 °F).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HistSubTropCyclones_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-HistSubTropCyclones-14">[14]</a></sup> Transition from subtropical cyclones into tropical cyclones occurs only in very rare cases over the South Atlantic Ocean, such as <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Catarina" title="Hurricane Catarina">Hurricane Catarina</a> in 2004.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SAtlSTs_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-SAtlSTs-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Characteristics">Characteristics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Subtropical_cyclone&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Characteristics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:01L_2017-04-19_1530Z.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="5040" data-file-width="3780" height="293" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8e/01L_2017-04-19_1530Z.jpg/220px-01L_2017-04-19_1530Z.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8e/01L_2017-04-19_1530Z.jpg/330px-01L_2017-04-19_1530Z.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8e/01L_2017-04-19_1530Z.jpg/440px-01L_2017-04-19_1530Z.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:01L_2017-04-19_1530Z.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Subtropical Depression One in April 2017</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>These storms can have maximum <a href="/wiki/Wind" title="Wind">winds</a> extending farther from the center than in a purely tropical cyclone and have no weather fronts linking directly to the center of circulation. In the <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Ocean" title="Atlantic Ocean">Atlantic</a> Basin, the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> <a href="/wiki/National_Oceanic_and_Atmospheric_Administration" title="National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration">NOAA</a> classifies subtropical cyclones similarly to their tropical cousins, based on maximum sustained surface winds. Those with winds below 18 <a href="/wiki/Metre_per_second" title="Metre per second">m/s</a>, (65 km/h, 35 <a href="/wiki/Knot_(unit)" title="Knot (unit)">knots</a>, or 39 mph) are called subtropical depressions, while those at or above this <a href="/wiki/Speed" title="Speed">speed</a> are referred to as subtropical storms.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>Subtropical cyclones with hurricane-force winds of greater than 33 <a href="/wiki/Metre_per_second" title="Metre per second">m/s</a>, (119 km/h, 64 <a href="/wiki/Knot_(unit)" title="Knot (unit)">knots</a>, or 74 mph) are not officially recognized by the National Hurricane Center. Once a subtropical storm intensifies enough to have hurricane-force winds, then it is automatically assumed to have become a fully tropical hurricane.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wunderground_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-Wunderground-17">[17]</a></sup> Despite this however, prior to the start of modern policies two systems in the Atlantic hurricane database attained hurricane-force winds as a subtropical cyclone; a <a href="/wiki/1968_Atlantic_hurricane_season#Subtropical_Storm_One" title="1968 Atlantic hurricane season">subtropical storm</a> in <a href="/wiki/1968_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="1968 Atlantic hurricane season">1968</a>, and a <a href="/wiki/1979_Atlantic_hurricane_season#Subtropical_Storm_One" title="1979 Atlantic hurricane season">subtropical storm</a> in <a href="/wiki/1979_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="1979 Atlantic hurricane season">1979</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HURDAT_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-HURDAT-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>Subtropical cyclones are also more likely than tropical cyclones to form outside of a region's designated hurricane season. Recent North Atlantic examples of this include the following storms:</p>
<ul>
<li>Subtropical Storm Ana (which became <a href="/wiki/Tropical_Storm_Ana_(2003)" title="Tropical Storm Ana (2003)">Tropical Storm Ana</a>) in late-April of the <a href="/wiki/2003_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="2003 Atlantic hurricane season">2003 hurricane season</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HURDAT2_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-HURDAT2-19">[19]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Subtropical_Storm_Andrea_(2007)" title="Subtropical Storm Andrea (2007)">Subtropical Storm Andrea</a> in early-May of the <a href="/wiki/2007_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="2007 Atlantic hurricane season">2007 hurricane season</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HURDAT2_19-1"><a href="#cite_note-HURDAT2-19">[19]</a></sup></li>
<li>Subtropical Storm Olga (which became <a href="/wiki/Tropical_Storm_Olga_(2007)" title="Tropical Storm Olga (2007)">Tropical Storm Olga</a>) in mid-December of the 2007 hurricane season.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HURDAT2_19-2"><a href="#cite_note-HURDAT2-19">[19]</a></sup></li>
<li>Subtropical Storm Beryl (which became <a href="/wiki/Tropical_Storm_Beryl_(2012)" title="Tropical Storm Beryl (2012)">Tropical Storm Beryl</a>) in late-May of the <a href="/wiki/2012_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="2012 Atlantic hurricane season">2012 hurricane season</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HURDAT2_19-3"><a href="#cite_note-HURDAT2-19">[19]</a></sup></li>
<li>An <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/December_2013_Azores_subtropical_storm" title="December 2013 Azores subtropical storm">unnamed subtropical storm</a> in early-December of the <a href="/wiki/2013_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="2013 Atlantic hurricane season">2013 hurricane season</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HURDAT2_19-4"><a href="#cite_note-HURDAT2-19">[19]</a></sup></li>
<li>Subtropical Storm Ana (which became <a href="/wiki/Tropical_Storm_Ana_(2015)" title="Tropical Storm Ana (2015)">Tropical Storm Ana</a>) in early-May of the <a href="/wiki/2015_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="2015 Atlantic hurricane season">2015 hurricane season</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HURDAT2_19-5"><a href="#cite_note-HURDAT2-19">[19]</a></sup></li>
<li>Subtropical Storm Alex (which became <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Alex_(2016)" title="Hurricane Alex (2016)">Hurricane Alex</a>) in mid-January of the <a href="/wiki/2016_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="2016 Atlantic hurricane season">2016 hurricane season</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HURDAT2_19-6"><a href="#cite_note-HURDAT2-19">[19]</a></sup></li>
<li>Subtropical Depression One (which became <a href="/wiki/2017_Atlantic_hurricane_season#Tropical_Storm_Arlene" title="2017 Atlantic hurricane season">Tropical Storm Arlene</a>) in mid-April of the <a href="/wiki/2017_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="2017 Atlantic hurricane season">2017 hurricane season</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HURDAT2_19-7"><a href="#cite_note-HURDAT2-19">[19]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<p>Diagrams which depict a <a href="/wiki/Cyclone" title="Cyclone">cyclone</a>'s phase depict subtropical cyclones with a shallow warm core and as asymmetric systems, similar to tropical cyclones which have begun the transition to an extratropical cyclone.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CPS1_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-CPS1-20">[20]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NAtlSTCases_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-NAtlSTCases-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Types">Types</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Subtropical_cyclone&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Types">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Upper-level_low">Upper-level low</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Subtropical_cyclone&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Upper-level low">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The most common type of subtropical storm is an upper-level cold low with circulation extending to the surface layer and maximum sustained winds generally occurring at a radius of about 160 kilometers (99 mi) or more from the center. In comparison to tropical cyclones, such systems have a relatively broad zone of maximum winds that is located farther from the center, and typically have a less symmetric wind field and distribution of convection.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mesoscale_low">Mesoscale low</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Subtropical_cyclone&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Mesoscale low">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A second type of subtropical cyclone is a mesoscale low originating in or near a frontolyzing zone of horizontal wind shear, also known as a dying frontal zone, with radius of maximum sustained winds generally less than 50 kilometers (31 mi). The entire circulation may initially have a diameter of less than 160 kilometers (99 mi). These generally short-lived systems may be either <a href="/wiki/Cold-core_low" title="Cold-core low">cold core</a> or warm core, and in 1972 this type of subtropical cyclone was referred to as a "neutercane".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Kona_storm">Kona storm</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Subtropical_cyclone&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Kona storm">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Kona_storm" title="Kona storm">Kona storm</a></div>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Omeka_2010-12-19_0057Z.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3950" data-file-width="3160" height="213" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/19/Omeka_2010-12-19_0057Z.jpg/170px-Omeka_2010-12-19_0057Z.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/19/Omeka_2010-12-19_0057Z.jpg/255px-Omeka_2010-12-19_0057Z.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/19/Omeka_2010-12-19_0057Z.jpg/340px-Omeka_2010-12-19_0057Z.jpg 2x" width="170"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Omeka_2010-12-19_0057Z.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
A <a href="/wiki/Tropical_Storm_Omeka" title="Tropical Storm Omeka">subtropical storm</a> in December 2010, originally a Kona storm</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Kona storms (or Kona lows) are deep cyclones that form during the cool season of the central Pacific Ocean. A definition change in the term during the early 1970s makes categorization of the systems more complex, as many kona lows are extratropical cyclones, complete with their own weather fronts. Those across the northeast Pacific Ocean consider them subtropical cyclones as long as a weak surface circulation is present.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hast_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-Hast-13">[13]</a></sup> <i>Kona</i> is a Hawaiian term for <i>leeward</i>, which explains the change in wind direction for the Hawaiian Islands from easterly to southerly when this type of cyclone is present.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Australian_East_Coast_Lows">Australian East Coast Lows</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Subtropical_cyclone&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Australian East Coast Lows">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Australian_east_coast_low" title="Australian east coast low">Australian east coast low</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:ECL25_06_13.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="4145" data-file-width="4497" height="157" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1d/ECL25_06_13.jpg/170px-ECL25_06_13.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1d/ECL25_06_13.jpg/255px-ECL25_06_13.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1d/ECL25_06_13.jpg/340px-ECL25_06_13.jpg 2x" width="170"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:ECL25_06_13.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
An Australian East Coast Low in June 2013</div>
</div>
</div>
<p><b>Australian east coast lows</b> (known locally as <b>east coast lows</b> and sometimes as east coast cyclones<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Leslie_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-Leslie-25">[25]</a></sup>) are <a href="/wiki/Extratropical_cyclone" title="Extratropical cyclone">extratropical cyclones</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> the most intense of these systems have many of the characteristics of subtropical cyclones.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dowdy11_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-Dowdy11-27">[27]</a></sup> They develop between 25˚south and 40˚south and within 5˚ of the Australian coastline,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Leslie_25-1"><a href="#cite_note-Leslie-25">[25]</a></sup> typically during the winter months.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Holland_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-Holland-28">[28]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lim_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-Lim-29">[29]</a></sup> Each year there are about ten "significant impact" maritime lows.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BOMECL_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-BOMECL-30">[30]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bomb_(meteorology)" title="Bomb (meteorology)">Explosive cyclogenesis</a> is seen on average just once per year, but these storms cause significant wind and flood damage when they occur.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Holland_28-1"><a href="#cite_note-Holland-28">[28]</a></sup> Australian east coast cyclones vary in size from <a href="/wiki/Mesoscale_meteorology" title="Mesoscale meteorology">mesoscale</a> (approximately 10 km to 100 km) to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Synoptic_meteorology" title="Synoptic meteorology">synoptic scale</a> (approximately 100 km to 1,000 km).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GuyCarp_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-GuyCarp-31">[31]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hopkins_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hopkins-32">[32]</a></sup> These storms which mostly affect the south east coast should not be confused with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Australian_region_tropical_cyclone_climatology" title="Australian region tropical cyclone climatology">Australian region tropical cyclones</a> which typically affect the northern half of the continent.</p>
