<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">South-West Indian Ocean tropical cyclone</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>

<p>In the south-west <a href="/wiki/Indian_Ocean" title="Indian Ocean">Indian Ocean</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone" title="Tropical cyclone">tropical cyclones</a> form south of the <a href="/wiki/Equator" title="Equator">equator</a> and west of <a href="/wiki/90th_meridian_east" title="90th meridian east">90° E</a> to the coast of <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Warnings_and_nomenclature">Warnings and nomenclature</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=South-West_Indian_Ocean_tropical_cyclone&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Warnings and nomenclature">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Each year, the <a href="/wiki/M%C3%A9t%C3%A9o-France" title="Météo-France">Météo-France</a> office (MFR) based on <a href="/wiki/R%C3%A9union" title="Réunion">Réunion</a> island issues warnings on tropical cyclones within the basin, which is defined as the waters of the <a href="/wiki/Indian_Ocean" title="Indian Ocean">Indian Ocean</a> from the coast of Africa to 90° <a href="/wiki/Longitude" title="Longitude">E</a>, south of the <a href="/wiki/Equator" title="Equator">equator</a>. The agency issues the warnings as part of its role as a <a href="/wiki/Regional_Specialized_Meteorological_Center" title="Regional Specialized Meteorological Center">Regional Specialized Meteorological Center</a>, designated as such in 1993 by the <a href="/wiki/World_Meteorological_Organization" title="World Meteorological Organization">World Meteorological Organization</a>. Intensities are estimated through the <a href="/wiki/Dvorak_technique" title="Dvorak technique">Dvorak technique</a>, which utilizes images from satellites by the American <a href="/wiki/National_Oceanic_and_Atmospheric_Administration" title="National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration">National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ss_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-ss-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-caroff_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-caroff-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Joint_Typhoon_Warning_Center" title="Joint Typhoon Warning Center">Joint Typhoon Warning Center</a> – a joint <a href="/wiki/United_States_Navy" title="United States Navy">United States Navy</a> – <a href="/wiki/United_States_Air_Force" title="United States Air Force">United States Air Force</a> task force – also issues tropical cyclone warnings for the region.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-atcr_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-atcr-3">[3]</a></sup> Wind estimates from Météo-France and most other basins throughout the world are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Maximum_sustained_winds" title="Maximum sustained winds">sustained</a> over 10 minutes, while estimates from the United States-based Joint Typhoon Warning Center are sustained over 1 minute. 1-minute winds are about 1.12 times the amount of 10-minute winds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FAQD4_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-FAQD4-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>If a tropical storm in the basin strengthens to attain 10 minute sustained winds of at least 118 km/h (74 mph), the MFR classifies it as a <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone_scales#South-Western_Indian_Ocean" title="Tropical cyclone scales">tropical cyclone</a>, equivalent to a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saffir-Simpson_Hurricane_Wind_Scale" title="Saffir-Simpson Hurricane Wind Scale">hurricane</a> or <a href="/wiki/Typhoon" title="Typhoon">typhoon</a> (a use of "tropical cyclone" which is more restrictive than the usual definition).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="History_of_the_basin">History of the basin</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=South-West_Indian_Ocean_tropical_cyclone&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History of the basin">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The first storm in the database for MFR originated on January 11, 1848. In January 1960, the first <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone_naming" title="Tropical cyclone naming">named storm</a> was Alex, and each subsequent year had a list of storm names. Beginning in 1967, satellites helped locate cyclones in the basin, and in the following year, the MFR began estimating storm intensities from the satellite images. By 1977, the agency was using the <a href="/wiki/Dvorak_technique" title="Dvorak technique">Dvorak technique</a> on an unofficial basis, but officially adopted it in 1981. Originally, the basin only extended to 80° E, and while it was extended eastward to the current 90° E, a lack of satellite imagery initially made data uncertain east of 80° E. The World Meteorological Organization designated the MFR as a Regional Tropical Cyclones Advisory Centre in 1988, and upgraded it to a <a href="/wiki/Regional_Specialized_Meteorological_Center" title="Regional Specialized Meteorological Center">Regional Specialized Meteorological Center</a> in 1993. In May 1998, two Europe-based <a href="/wiki/Meteosat" title="Meteosat">Meteosat</a> satellites began providing complete coverage of the basin. On July 1, 2002, the MFR shifted the cyclone year to begin on this date and end on June 30 of the following year; previously, the cyclone year began on August 1 and ended on the subsequent July 31. In 2003, the MFR extended their area of warning responsibility to 40° <a href="/wiki/Latitude" title="Latitude">S</a>, having previously been limited at 30° S.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-caroff_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-caroff-2">[2]</a></sup> During 2011, MFR started a reanalysis project of all tropical systems between 1978–98, with methods such as a Dvorak technique reanalysis and use of microwave imagery.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RSMC_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-RSMC-6">[6]</a></sup> Preliminary results from this reanalysis project include correcting an increasing trend in the number of very intense tropical cyclones in the basin since 1978.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RSMC_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-RSMC-6">[6]</a></sup> This also revealed a seemingly systematic underestimation of tropical cyclone intensities in the past.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RSMC_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-RSMC-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Statistics">Statistics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=South-West_Indian_Ocean_tropical_cyclone&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Statistics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>From the <a href="/wiki/1980%E2%80%9381_South-West_Indian_Ocean_cyclone_season" title="1980–81 South-West Indian Ocean cyclone season">1980–81</a> to the <a href="/wiki/2010%E2%80%9311_South-West_Indian_Ocean_cyclone_season" title="2010–11 South-West Indian Ocean cyclone season">2010–11</a> season, there was an average of 9.3 tropical storms each year in the basin. A tropical storm has 10 minute winds of at least 65 km/h (40 mph). There are an average of five storms that become tropical cyclones, which have 10  minute winds of at least 120 km/h (75 mph).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> As of 2002, there was an average of 54 days when tropical systems were active in the basin, of which 20 had <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone_scales#South-Western_Indian_Ocean" title="Tropical cyclone scales">tropical cyclones</a> active, or a system with winds of over 120 km/h (75 mph). The median start date for the season was November 17, and the median end date was April 20.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ss_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-ss-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Climatology">Climatology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=South-West_Indian_Ocean_tropical_cyclone&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Climatology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Southwest_Indian_Ocean_cyclone_tracks_1980-2005.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3000" data-file-width="4000" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bb/Southwest_Indian_Ocean_cyclone_tracks_1980-2005.jpg/220px-Southwest_Indian_Ocean_cyclone_tracks_1980-2005.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bb/Southwest_Indian_Ocean_cyclone_tracks_1980-2005.jpg/330px-Southwest_Indian_Ocean_cyclone_tracks_1980-2005.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bb/Southwest_Indian_Ocean_cyclone_tracks_1980-2005.jpg/440px-Southwest_Indian_Ocean_cyclone_tracks_1980-2005.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Southwest_Indian_Ocean_cyclone_tracks_1980-2005.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Tracks of storms in the basin from 1980 to 2005</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Generally, the <a href="/wiki/Monsoon_trough" title="Monsoon trough">monsoon</a> does not cross into the <a href="/wiki/Mozambique_Channel" title="Mozambique Channel">Mozambique Channel</a> until December; as a result, storms rarely form there before that time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ss_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-ss-1">[1]</a></sup> From 1948 to 2010, 94 tropical systems developed in the small body of water, of which about half made <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Landfall_(meteorology)" title="Landfall (meteorology)">landfall</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Occasionally, small storms form in the Mozambique Channel that resemble <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mediterranean_tropical_cyclone" title="Mediterranean tropical cyclone">Mediterranean tropical cyclones</a> or storms in the northeastern <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atlantic_hurricanes" title="Atlantic hurricanes">Atlantic Ocean</a>; these systems are well-organized but have weaker <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Convection_(meteorology)" title="Convection (meteorology)">convection</a> than typical tropical cyclones, and originate over cooler than normal <a href="/wiki/Sea_surface_temperature" title="Sea surface temperature">water temperatures</a> of less than 26 °C (79 °F). A survey in 2004 conducted by weather expert Gary Padgett found meteorologists split whether these storms should be classified as tropical or <a href="/wiki/Subtropical_cyclone" title="Subtropical cyclone">subtropical</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>In an average year, ten tropical depressions or storms strike Madagascar, and most generally do not cause much damage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wb2_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-wb2-10">[10]</a></sup> Occasionally, storms or their remnants enter the interior of southeastern Africa, bringing heavy rainfall to <a href="/wiki/Zimbabwe" title="Zimbabwe">Zimbabwe</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
